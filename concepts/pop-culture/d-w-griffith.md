# D. W. Griffith

## summary

*David Wark* (D. W.) *Griffith* (1875–1948) was an American director and pioneer of cinematic language who, more than any other single figure, established the formal vocabulary of film — close-ups, cross-cutting, camera movement, and montage. His films, particularly ***The Birth of a Nation*** (1915), revolutionized cinema's technical and narrative possibilities while simultaneously embedding racist ideology, making him cinema's founding father and a troubling historical figure.

## you gotta know

- Griffith's ***The Birth of a Nation*** (1915) was the first feature-length narrative film and a *commercial and technical triumph* — it grossed $10 million (unprecedented), pioneered film techniques, and was screened at the White House, yet it depicts the Ku Klux Klan as heroic defenders against Black Reconstruction, a grotesque falsification that influenced real-world racial violence.
- ***Intolerance*** (1916), Griffith's response to censure, is an *ambitious four-narrative montage* spanning ancient Babylon, Renaissance France, modern America, and first-century Judea, attempting to show intolerance across history — a technical marvel even as it reflects his political confusion.
- Griffith pioneered the **close-up** — framing an actor's face in tight focus — as an *emotional tool*; before Griffith, cameras rarely moved and characters were filmed full-length from a distance.
- He systematized **cross-cutting** (cutting between simultaneous events) to build *tension and parallel meaning*, a technique that became cinema's primary syntax for linking actions and ideas.
- The **tracking shot** — a camera that moves alongside or around action rather than remaining static — appears throughout his work, giving cinema the capacity to *explore space* rather than merely record it.
- Griffith's editing was revolutionary: he understood that *meaning emerges from juxtaposition*, prefiguring Soviet montage theory (Eisenstein, Pudovkin) and modernist collage by decades.
- In the 1920s, Griffith's career declined as other directors (Lubitsch, Sternberg, Murnau) surpassed him; he died embittered, his legacy permanently shadowed by the racism of his most influential film.

- [[montage]] — Griffith's editing philosophy that influenced Soviet theory and shaped twentieth-century visual culture.
