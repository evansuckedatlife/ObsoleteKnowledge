# The Last Laugh

## summary

***The Last Laugh*** (1924) is F. W. Murnau's *camera-movement masterpiece* and one of the first films to use the camera itself as an *emotional instrument*. The film, set in a Berlin hotel, follows an elderly doorman stripped of his uniform and dignity — a *character study* in humiliation and resilience. Murnau's innovative use of camera movement, reflection, and subjective cinematography made it a turning point in silent film aesthetics.

## you gotta know

- The film follows *Professor Unrath* (played by *Emil Jannings*), a distinguished hotel doorman whose job defines his identity; when he is demoted to bathroom attendant due to age and injury, his *social humiliation* becomes the film's tragic core.
- Murnau pioneered **moving-camera cinematography**: the camera is strapped to dancers, vehicles, and operators, creating the effect of *drunk perception* or *emotional vertigo* — the camera itself becomes a character expressing the protagonist's psychological state.
- The film contains *almost no intertitles* (text cards), a radical choice that forced viewers to read emotion from *facial expression, gesture, and visual composition alone* — Murnau's faith in cinema's visual language.
- ***The Last Laugh***'s **ending** is deliberately *artificial* and *anti-tragic*: a superimposed text card reveals the doorman has won the lottery, contradicting the film's accumulated pathos — Murnau's comment on cinema's capacity to *overturn reality through artifice*.
- The film's *mise-en-scène* (production design and camera placement) uses **reflections in mirrors, distorted perspectives, and extreme angles** to visualize the protagonist's psychological fragmentation and shame.
- ***The Last Laugh*** was *hugely influential* on Soviet montage theory and modernist cinema generally, showing that *subjective camera movement* could express interiority (inner experience) as powerfully as dialogue.
- Emil Jannings' performance is *remarkably subtle*: his face registers humiliation and resilience through microexpressions, the elderly actor conveying decades of dignity-maintenance under impossible circumstances.
