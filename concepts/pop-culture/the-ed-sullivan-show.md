# The Ed Sullivan Show

## summary

*The Ed Sullivan Show* is a legendary variety television program that aired on *CBS* from 1948 to 1971, becoming the nation's premier venue for live entertainment. Hosted by the shrewd, gravelly-voiced *Ed Sullivan*, the show introduced mainstream American audiences to an astounding breadth of performers—from opera singers and comedians to rock musicians and acrobats—and served as a cultural barometer for what America wanted to see.

## you gotta know

- Originally called "Toast of the Town" (1948–1955) before becoming *The Ed Sullivan Show*, it ran for 23 seasons on Sunday nights at 8 p.m., claiming an audience of up to 50 million viewers at its peak.
- *Ed Sullivan*, a former journalist and gossip columnist with no formal show-business background, became the show's draw through his earnest, deadpan demeanor and his uncanny ability to book cutting-edge talent alongside vaudeville veterans.
- The show's most famous moment: *Elvis Presley's* appearance in September 1956, filmed from the waist up to avoid showing his provocative hip movements, which drew 82.6% of the television audience.
- Featured breakthrough American television performances by *The Beatles* (February 1964, 73 million viewers), launching them to superstardom in the U.S. and establishing the show's role in pop-music history.
- Showcased an unparalleled range: opera stars (*Maria Callas*), comedians (*Jackie Mason*, *Rodney Dangerfield*), circus acts, ballet companies, and novelty performers sat side-by-side in the show's eclectic format.
- The show pioneered the live-broadcast variety format and set the standard for what television could offer: immediacy, surprise, and the possibility of witnessing a genuine moment of breakthrough or failure.
- Sullivan's eye for talent and his willingness to book controversial or avant-garde acts (despite network pressure) made the show a proving ground for careers and a mirror of cultural change.
