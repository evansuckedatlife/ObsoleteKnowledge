# 60 Minutes

## summary

*60 Minutes* is the longest-running primetime television program in American history, an investigative news magazine that debuted on *CBS* in 1968 and revolutionized television journalism by bringing serious, in-depth reporting to entertainment-time slots. The show's signature format—three to four long-form investigative segments per episode, anchored by distinctive correspondent personalities—proved that audiences would watch challenging journalism when presented with compelling storytelling and production values.

## you gotta know

- Premiered on *CBS* in September 1968 as an experiment in using the 7 p.m. Sunday slot for news rather than entertainment; it succeeded beyond expectation and remains on the air.
- Created by *Don Hewitt*, the legendary news producer who pioneered the magazine format and insisted on maintaining journalistic rigor while employing cinematic visual techniques.
- Featured a rotating roster of correspondents, most famously *Mike Wallace*, *Morley Safer*, *Dan Rather*, and *Andy Rooney*, each bringing distinctive interview styles and investigating different stories each week.
- Segments ranged from corporate malfeasance and political corruption to human-interest stories and international reporting, setting the standard for investigative television journalism.
- The show's opening—the iconic *Stopwatch*, ticking off the seconds—became one of television's most recognizable branding elements, signaling the urgency and precision of the reporting.
- *Andy Rooney's* weekly essays became a cultural fixture, offering folksy commentary on modern life and frequently sparking national debate.
- The show won numerous *Peabody* and *Emmy* awards and set the template for news magazines that followed (*20/20*, *Dateline NBC*), though *60 Minutes* remained the ratings and cultural leader for decades.
