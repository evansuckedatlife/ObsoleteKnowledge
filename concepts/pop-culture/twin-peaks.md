# Twin Peaks

## summary

*Twin Peaks* is a groundbreaking television drama that aired on *ABC* from 1990 to 1991 and later returned in limited form, establishing itself as one of the most influential and aesthetically daring series in television history. Created by filmmaker *David Lynch* and writer *Mark Frost*, the show blended murder-mystery procedural with surrealism, horror, and absurdist humor, demonstrating that television could support complex, unconventional storytelling that challenged traditional narrative structures and audience expectations.

## you gotta know

- Premiered on *ABC* in April 1990 and ran for two seasons (30 episodes), becoming a massive cultural phenomenon and an instant cultural touchstone despite its unconventional storytelling.
- Centered on the murder of homecoming queen *Laura Palmer* in the fictional small town of *Twin Peaks*, Washington; the investigation unfolds in ways both procedurally rigorous and increasingly surreal and supernatural.
- Starred *Kyle MacLachlan* as FBI Special Agent *Dale Cooper*, whose fastidious manners, love of cherry pie and coffee, and spiritual intuition made him television's most distinctive and memorable detective.
- The show's second season, responding to pressure to solve the central mystery, declined in quality when the network demanded resolution of *Laura Palmer's* murder; the show rebounded artistically with a *David Lynch* film continuation and later a 2017 limited series.
- *Lynch* directed much of the material personally and introduced a visual and tonal vocabulary combining noir detective conventions, horror, magical realism, and performance-art aesthetics rarely seen on American network television.
- Featured an ensemble cast of town eccentrics (a one-armed man with premonitions, Log Lady obsessed with her Douglas fir log, a gentle-hearted FBI receptionist) that turned the small town into a hall of quirky archetypes.
- The show influenced subsequent television series to attempt more ambitious, serialized, and unconventional narratives, demonstrating that audiences would follow complex, non-linear storytelling if it was compelling and stylistically distinctive.
