# M*A*S*H

## summary

*M*A*S*H* aired on CBS from 1972 to 1983 and followed a unit of American surgeons and support staff at a Mobile Army Surgical Hospital (MASH) during the Korean War. Balancing dark humor, antiwar sentiment, and genuine pathos, the show proved that sitcoms could address the absurdity and tragedy of military conflict while building ensemble camaraderie and character depth. The show's 11-season run made it a cultural institution; its finale remains one of the most-watched television episodes ever, and its final episode shaped how Americans processed the Vietnam War's aftermath.

## you gotta know

- Starred *Alan Alda* as Captain Benjamin "Hawkeye" Pierce, the show's conscience and comedic heart, a surgeon using humor to cope with wartime trauma.
- The title stood for Mobile Army Surgical Hospital, a real U.S. military unit that served during the Korean War (1950–1953).
- Shifted from broad comedy in early seasons to increasingly dramatic storytelling, blending sitcom tropes with genuine emotional weight.
- The antiwar message grew stronger as the Vietnam War dragged on in reality; the show became a cultural mirror of American ambivalence about military intervention.
- Final episode, "Goodbye, Farewell and Amen" (1983), drew 106 million viewers—one of the largest television audiences in history.
- *Alan Alda* won four Emmy Awards and became the show's creative force as director and writer.
- Introduced the "dramedy" format, proving sitcoms could hold both laughs and tears simultaneously.
