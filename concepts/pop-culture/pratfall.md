## summary

A *pratfall* is a *exaggerated tumble, stumble, or fall performed for comedic effect* — one of the basic units of physical comedy in slapstick and silent film. The term derives from the commedia dell'arte tradition and specifically from the "slap" sound made by a prop stick (slapstick) striking the stage; in cinema, the pratfall became a fundamental gag, requiring precise control of timing, positioning, and camera framing to land the joke.

## you gotta know

- Pratfalls appear in **commedia dell'arte** (Renaissance Italian street theater) and music-hall traditions, where they were *improvised responses to audience energy*, but cinema rendered them *precisely choreographed and reproducible*.
- In silent film, the pratfall's effectiveness depends on *three elements*: the performer's apparent loss of control (the *appearance* of accident), the camera's framing (showing the full body or the impact point), and the *audience's complicity* in recognizing the physical effort required to make it look unintentional.
- **Buster Keaton** was the *master of the pratfall*: his deadpan expression while falling made the gag sublime — the audience laughs not at falling but at the contrast between his impassive face and the chaos of his body.
- Pratfalls require *extraordinary physical control*: a performer must absorb impact safely while appearing to surrender to gravity, and the "fall" must be reproducible across multiple takes for editing.
- The **vertical pratfall** (falling straight backward or forward) differs from the *horizontal pratfall* (a sliding tumble across ground or furniture), each requiring different technical skill and timing.
- Silent film elevated pratfalls from improvisational theater into *precisely choreographed sequences*: Keaton could design an entire scene around a single, complex pratfall, where every element (furniture placement, camera angle, actor position) was predetermined.
- The pratfall's decline coincides with sound cinema's rise: dialogue-based comedy made physical gags seem crude, and performers trained in dialogue acting often lacked the bodily control pratfalls required.
