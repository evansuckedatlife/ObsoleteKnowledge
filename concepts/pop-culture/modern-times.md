## summary

***Modern Times*** (1936) is Charlie Chaplin's last fully silent feature film — made five years after the arrival of synchronized sound — and a *satirical masterpiece* about industrial dehumanization. The film depicts the Tramp as a factory worker driven to nervous breakdown by assembly-line monotony and surveillance, a critique of mechanization that remains urgent in the twenty-first century.

## you gotta know

- ***Modern Times*** is set in a *dystopian factory* where the Tramp works on an endless assembly line, his rhythms dictated by a tyrannical efficiency expert; the film opens with a shot of clock gears intercut with workers, literalizing Chaplin's metaphor of human mechanization.
- The film's *central scene* — the Tramp in a mechanical feeding device that malfunctions and spins out of control — is a *masterwork of visual gag and satire combined*, the machinery consuming and ejecting the worker like an animal.
- Chaplin made ***Modern Times*** as a *conscious act of artistic defiance*: sound had arrived years earlier, yet he chose silence, trusting pantomime and visual language to express his critique in a way dialogue could not.
- The film includes a **dream sequence** (a ballet where the Tramp and his love interest, *Paulette Goddard*, waltz through a consumer paradise) that provides Romantic respite from industrial harshness — one of Chaplin's most poetic moments.
- ***Modern Times*** was immediately recognized as *social commentary*, a film about economic vulnerability and the dehumanization of labor during the Great Depression; it remains a touchstone for films about technology and alienation.
- Chaplin's performance is *physical perfection*: every gesture conveys the Tramp's psychological fragility and dignity under inhumane conditions; his vacant stare during the machine-feeding sequence is simultaneously absurd and tragic.
- The film's ending — the Tramp and his companion walking down a rural road toward the sunrise, singing wordlessly — offers *ambiguous hope*, neither affirming nor denying the possibility of escape from industrial capitalism.
