# Intolerance

## summary

*Intolerance* is D.W. Griffith's 1916 epic response to accusations of bigotry, interweaving four stories across four historical eras—ancient Babylon, Judea in the time of Christ, 16th-century France, and contemporary America—to argue that prejudice and bigotry are humanity's eternal curse. Technically breathtaking but thematically confused, it remains a monument to Griffith's ambition and the limits of his progressive vision.

## you gotta know

- Directed by *D.W. Griffith*, released in 1916; Griffith's first major film after *The Birth of a Nation*, intended as a philosophical rebuttal to critics.
- Four intercut narratives: the fall of Babylon (religious persecution), the St. Bartholomew's Day Massacre (sectarian violence), Christ's trial and crucifixion (institutional power over compassion), and a contemporary labor strike (capitalist intolerance toward workers).
- The *Babylonian sequences* feature a monumental set (the reconstruction of Babylon's walls) that remains stunning; crowds of thousands, chariots, and exotic design create spectacle that dwarfs earlier epics.
- Uses sophisticated parallel editing to interweave the four timelines—a technical innovation that influenced narrative cinema for decades.
- The *Eternal Mother* figure appears between segments, rocking a cradle to represent continuity across time—Griffith's effort to unify the philosophical theme.
- The film criticizes industrial capitalism, religious dogmatism, and mob hysteria, but its critique of *Birth of a Nation*'s racism is muddled and implicit rather than clear.
- At nearly four hours, it was commercially disappointing and artistically overstuffed; modern revival has recognized its formal innovation while noting its thematic confusion.
- Despite its flaws, it influenced epics and prestige cinema's ambitious structural ambitions.
