# Vaudeville

## summary

*Vaudeville* was an American form of live entertainment (roughly 1880s–1930s) featuring a rapid succession of *variety acts* — singers, dancers, comedians, acrobats, magicians, trained animals — in dedicated theaters. It was the dominant form of theatrical entertainment in the United States before cinema and supplied early film with most of its comedians and physical performers, including Buster Keaton.

## you gotta know

- Vaudeville circuits (booking networks like the *Keith-Albee circuit* and *Pantages*) made entertainment *accessible nationwide* and developed standardized 15–20 minute "acts" designed to keep audiences engaged through rapid-fire variety.
- **Buster Keaton's family** — his parents, *Joe and Myra*, and siblings — were *vaudeville performers*; Keaton appeared on stage from early childhood ("Buster Keaton" became his stage name after a famous pratfall as a toddler), and his parents' act shaped his approach to physical comedy.
- Vaudeville acts typically included *comedy sketches with strong physical elements*: slapstick, pratfalls, and absurdist situations predominated because they worked across language and class barriers.
- The **transition from vaudeville to cinema** (1910s–1930s) saw vaudeville's greatest stars — Chaplin, Keaton, and *Laurel and Hardy* — transfer to film, while cinema's rising popularity gradually starved vaudeville of audiences and financing.
- Vaudeville's *high turnover rate* (new bills weekly) trained performers in *rapid-fire gag density*, precision timing, and the ability to read and respond to live audience energy — skills that translated directly to silent-film's visual precision.
- Unlike British music hall, which thrived in working-class venues, American vaudeville *served a broader demographic* and emphasized "family entertainment," which shaped its humor style — slapstick over bawdiness.
- The decline of vaudeville was swiftly complete: by 1935–1940, the form was essentially extinct, with only a handful of circuits surviving into the 1950s.
