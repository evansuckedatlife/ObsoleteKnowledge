## summary

*Battleship Potemkin* is Sergei Eisenstein's 1925 Soviet propaganda epic depicting a 1905 naval mutiny against the Tsarist regime. Using revolutionary montage—the collision and juxtaposition of shots to create meaning—it transcends propaganda to become a technical and artistic masterpiece that taught the world how cinema could shape consciousness and emotion through editing alone.

## you gotta know

- Directed by *Sergei Eisenstein*, released in 1925; commissioned as Soviet propaganda for the 20th anniversary of the failed 1905 revolution.
- Pioneered *montage theory*: Eisenstein believed meaning emerges not from individual shots but from their collision—a cut from one image to another creates emotional and intellectual impact.
- The Odessa Steps sequence (a massacre of civilians by Tsarist troops) is cinema's most famous demonstration of montage power: a baby carriage tumbling down endless steps, intercut with soldiers, horses, and panic, conveys horror and outrage through rhythm and editing rather than narrative.
- The film is structured as five acts, each representing a stage of revolutionary consciousness: injustice, mutiny, solidarity, repression, hope.
- Employs visual symbolism: the battleship itself as the people's will, the Tsar's portrait torn down as revolution manifested, geometric composition to suggest collective order.
- Was hailed by Soviet authorities and intellectuals but faced censorship elsewhere; Western audiences only gradually recognized its artistic achievement beneath its ideology.
- Remains a technical and aesthetic text studied in film schools worldwide; its editing principles influenced decades of cinema and advertising.
