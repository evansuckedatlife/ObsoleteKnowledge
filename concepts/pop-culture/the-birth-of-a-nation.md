## summary

*The Birth of a Nation* is D.W. Griffith's 1915 silent epic depicting the American Civil War and Reconstruction, technically groundbreaking but infamous for its racist narrative that lionizes the Ku Klux Klan and portrays Black citizens and Reconstruction efforts as threats to white Southern order. Despite its moral bankruptcy, it shaped cinema as an art form through innovative editing, camera work, and cross-cutting techniques.

## you gotta know

- Directed by *D.W. Griffith* and released in 1915; at 3 hours, the longest and most expensive American film made to that point.
- Uses groundbreaking cinematic techniques including parallel editing, iris shots, and close-ups to tell an epic narrative across two acts (the war itself, then Reconstruction).
- Depicts the Reconstruction period as a catastrophe engineered by carpetbaggers and Black politicians, romanticizing the KKK as heroic defenders of Southern white supremacy.
- Sparked organized protests from the *NAACP* upon release and was banned in some cities—the first major film censorship battle in America.
- Screened at the White House for President *Woodrow Wilson*, reportedly the first film shown there; Wilson allegedly praised it, cementing its cultural weight.
- The film's narrative lies hardened into a mythology of Reconstruction (the "Lost Cause") that poisoned American historical understanding for decades.
- Inspired a resurgent real Ku Klux Klan in the 1920s, which used the film for recruitment and propaganda.
