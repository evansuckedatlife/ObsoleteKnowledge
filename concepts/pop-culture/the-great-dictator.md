# The Great Dictator

## summary

***The Great Dictator*** (1940) is Charlie Chaplin's *first true talking picture* — his bold satirical response to the rise of fascism in Europe. Chaplin plays dual roles: the *Tramp* (a Jewish barber) and *Adenoid Hynkel*, a Hitler-like dictator. The film is a *political manifesto* disguised as comedy, culminating in Chaplin's famous monologue denouncing fascism and appealing to human dignity.

## you gotta know

- Chaplin plays **two characters**: the gentle, bemused Jewish barber and the *ranting, infantile dictator*, a doubling that allows Chaplin to explore tyranny through parody — the dictator as a grotesquely overgrown child throwing tantrums.
- The film's *central satire* targets Hitler by name (unusual for a mainstream 1940 film) and depicts fascism as aesthetically and morally ridiculous, a *refusal to grant tyranny gravitas*.
- The **final monologue** — Chaplin's barber, mistaken for Hynkel, addresses a rally and delivers a *passionate speech for human dignity, reason, and brotherhood* — remains one of cinema's greatest moments of moral eloquence, breaking character entirely to speak as *Chaplin himself*.
- ***The Great Dictator*** was made *while World War II was ongoing* (though before the U.S. entered), making it a *daring political intervention* that many feared would invite censorship or backlash.
- Chaplin's decision to *finally embrace dialogue* in this film was strategic: the Tramp's pantomime could not express political anger; speech was necessary, though the barber character's gentle voice contrasts with the dictator's bombastic ranting.
- The film was a *commercial and critical success*, earning $5 million domestically (huge for 1940) and establishing Chaplin as not merely an entertainer but a *moral voice*, a status he would maintain throughout his remaining career.
- The monologue's *rejection of both fascism and capitalism's dehumanization* gave the film leftist resonance that would later haunt Chaplin during the McCarthy era.
