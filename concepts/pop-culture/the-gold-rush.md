## summary

*The Gold Rush* is Charlie Chaplin's 1925 masterpiece following a hapless prospector during the Yukon gold rush who endures privation, freezing weather, and romantic longing with ingenuity and pathos. Blending slapstick and sentiment, it remains the quintessential Chaplin—a comedy about human dignity in the face of hardship that can shift within frames from hilarious to heartbreaking.

## you gotta know

- Directed by and starring *Charlie Chaplin*, released in 1925; Chaplin spent $957,000 to make it, an enormous sum.
- The "Tramp" character (Chaplin's signature everyman persona) survives by his wits and good heart amid impossible conditions: hunger, cold, and competition from brutal prospectors.
- Features the iconic *Thanksgiving scene*: starving and delirious, Chaplin cooks and eats his own boot, savoring it as a gourmet meal—comedy and pathos fused.
- The *dance of the dinner rolls*: Chaplin's character performs an improvised waltz using two bread rolls and forks as imaginary legs, conveying hope and loneliness simultaneously.
- Employs extreme close-ups on Chaplin's face to convey inner emotion without words, pioneering the expressive use of the silent actor's visage.
- The ending is deliberately ambiguous about love and fortune, allowing each viewer to see hope or loss depending on their interpretation.
- One of the highest-grossing and most beloved films of the silent era; Chaplin famously said it was his personal favorite of his own works.
