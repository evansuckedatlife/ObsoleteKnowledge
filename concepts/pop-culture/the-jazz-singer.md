# The Jazz Singer

## summary

***The Jazz Singer*** (1927) is a landmark film directed by Alan Crosland and starring *Al Jolson* that introduced synchronized sound (**Vitaphone** technology) to mainstream American cinema. Though not the first sound film, it was the first feature-length narrative film with synchronized dialogue and singing, and its commercial success catalyzed the rapid conversion of Hollywood from silent to talking pictures, effectively ending the silent-film era within three years.

## you gotta know

- ***The Jazz Singer*** starred *Al Jolson*, a popular vaudeville and stage performer, as *Jakie Rabinowitz*, a Jewish cantor's son who dreams of becoming a popular singer, creating a narrative about assimilation and generational conflict that reflected real American anxieties.
- The film's **Vitaphone system** was a *disc-based synchronized sound* technology: a pre-recorded phonograph disc played in sync with the projected film — primitive by later standards but revolutionary at the time.
- Most of the film was *still silent*, with only a few scenes featuring synchronized dialogue and Jolson's *singing*; the bulk of exposition and plot remained conveyed through intertitles, reflecting the hybrid technology's limitations.
- Jolson's *ad-libbed line* "Wait a minute, wait a minute, you ain't heard nothin' yet!" became cinema's symbolic first words, signaling the arrival of the speaking picture.
- The film's release in October 1927 shocked the industry: within 18 months, the vast majority of American theaters had converted to sound equipment; by 1930, silent films were commercially extinct in the U.S.
- Many acclaimed silent filmmakers *resisted sound*: Charlie Chaplin continued making silent films into the late 1930s; others like *D. W. Griffith* saw their careers decline as they struggled with the new medium.
- The transition was *devastating* for international cinema: sound made films *language-specific*, reducing the global reach silent films had enjoyed; American dominance intensified as only major studios could afford rapid retooling.
