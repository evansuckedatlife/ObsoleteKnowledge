# Our Hospitality

## summary

***Our Hospitality*** (1923) is Buster Keaton's *early masterpiece*, a comedy-thriller about a young man caught in a feud between two mountain families. The film is celebrated for its *innovative use of location cinematography* (filmed on the Truckee River in California) and its integration of danger-laden stunts with comedic narrative — Keaton performs his own dangerous water sequences, establishing the template for action-comedy.

## you gotta know

- ***Our Hospitality*** follows *William "Willie" McKay* (Keaton), who returns to the Appalachian mountains to claim his inherited property and falls in love with *Virginia*, the daughter of his family's enemy — a romantic plot complicated by a *blood feud*.
- The film's central conceit is the **code of Appalachian hospitality**: once a guest enters a house, he cannot be harmed; Keaton's character exploits this rule to survive the feud, a premise that generates both comedy and suspense.
- Keaton performs **genuinely dangerous stunts**: being dragged through river rapids, falling from a cliff (a stunt double briefly), and navigating treacherous water sequences — this was *actual peril on camera*, not special effects, establishing Keaton's reputation for physical fearlessness.
- The **water-rescue sequence** (where Willie saves Virginia from a waterfall) is a masterwork of *integrated stunt-work and pathos*, Keaton's deadpan expression persisting even as he risks drowning to save his love interest.
- ***Our Hospitality*** is structured as a *romantic adventure* rather than pure comedy, the feud narrative providing genuine stakes that elevate slapstick into something more ambitious — an early example of *genre synthesis* in silent film.
- The film's use of **actual landscape** (the Truckee River, California mountains) instead of studio sets pioneered *location cinematography*, influencing how filmmakers would use natural environments for visual storytelling.
- Keaton's *perpetual deadpan* in moments of actual physical danger — filmed from multiple angles to establish the reality of the stunts — creates a *unique comic-dramatic tension* that audiences find both hilarious and heartbreaking.
