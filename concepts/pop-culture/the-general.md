# The General

## summary

*The General* is Buster Keaton's 1926 silent comedy set during the American Civil War, in which a railroad engineer pursues a stolen locomotive across enemy lines to reclaim both his train and his girlfriend. Keaton's deadpan genius and the film's precise timing and visual gags—all executed with dangerous practical stunts—make it a masterclass in silent physical comedy and one of the era's most entertaining films.

## you gotta know

- Directed by *Buster Keaton* and *Clyde Bruckman*, released in 1926; the most expensive comedy of the silent era at the time.
- *Keaton*'s character, *Johnnie Gray*, pursues the locomotive *General* (a real 1880s engine) with stone-faced determination, never cracking a smile despite increasingly absurd situations.
- The train chase across Georgia is the film's centerpiece: Keaton performs dangerous stunts (riding on the engine, jumping between cars, a real locomotive hitting a bridge) with meticulous choreography.
- Every gag is a miniature machine: precisely timed action, careful framing, and the locomotive's mechanics themselves become comedy—smoke from the engine obscuring Johnnie's vision, water spraying his face.
- Visual puns and sight gags rely entirely on composition and timing; dialogue cards are minimal, proving that comedy's structure transcends words.
- The Civil War backdrop allows for a satirical historical comedy: Johnnie's loyalty to his train supersedes ideology, and he ends up rescuing Southern soldiers—a deadpan subversion of war movie conventions.
- Often cited as Keaton's masterpiece and one of the greatest silent comedies ever made, rivaling *Metropolis* and *Potemkin* in technical achievement.
