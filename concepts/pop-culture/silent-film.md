# Silent Film

## summary

Silent film is cinema without synchronized dialogue, relying on pantomime, acting intensity, and visual storytelling to convey narrative — the dominant form of movies from the 1890s until the arrival of sound in the late 1920s. Born from technological innovation and perfected by visionary directors and performers, silent film established the grammar of cinema itself and remains a cornerstone of film history.

## you gotta know

- Silent films relied entirely on *physical acting*, *facial expression*, and *body language* to convey emotion and plot, forcing performers to develop extraordinary precision and exaggeration.
- **Intertitles** — text cards inserted between scenes — carried dialogue, explanation, and narration; their placement and design became an art form in themselves.
- The medium *standardized at 16–24 frames per second*, a speed that became the industry norm and shaped the visual rhythm of cinema for decades.
- Silent film's golden age (roughly 1915–1927) produced masterworks of *formal innovation*: close-ups, cross-cutting, montage, and camera movement that redefined how stories could be told.
- The **Jazz Singer** (1927) introduced synchronized sound (**Vitaphone** system) and spelled the end of silent film's commercial dominance within three years — though some directors continued the form well into the 1930s.
- Silence forced filmmakers to become *visual poets*: German Expressionism, Soviet montage theory, and American slapstick comedy all emerged as distinctly cinematic responses to the absence of dialogue.

## connections

- [[charlie-chaplin]] — the most famous silent comedian, whose *Tramp* character became a global icon of pathos and resilience.
- [[buster-keaton]] — the stone-faced master of physical comedy and innovative visual gags, rivaling Chaplin in technical brilliance.
- [[d-w-griffith]] — the foundational director who established the grammar of cinema (cross-cutting, close-ups, tracking shots) and dominated 1910s–1920s American film.
- [[f-w-murnau]] — the German Expressionist visionary whose *Nosferatu* and *Sunrise* redefined psychological horror and romantic cinema.
- [[the-gold-rush]] — Chaplin's 1925 masterpiece, blending comedy, pathos, and survival in the Yukon, a canonical silent film.
- [[metropolis-film]] — Fritz Lang's 1927 science-fiction epic, the most expensive silent film ever made, showcasing the form's technological ambitions.
- [[the-general]] — Buster Keaton's 1926 masterpiece of physical comedy, a Civil War chase film praised for its integration of gag and narrative.
- [[sherlock-jr]] — Keaton's 1924 comedy-thriller featuring revolutionary special effects and self-reflexive film-within-film storytelling.
- [[german-expressionism]] — an art movement (visual arts, theater, film) that shaped the *aesthetic* of silent cinema, especially horror and psychological drama.
- [[intertitles]] — the text insertions that carried spoken language, evolving from functional labels to art.
- [[the-jazz-singer]] — the 1927 Al Jolson film that introduced synchronized sound and hastened silent film's commercial extinction.
- [[edison-kinetoscope]] — the 1890s-era viewing device that pioneered *peephole cinema*, the precursor to projected motion pictures.
