# Buster Keaton

## summary

*Buster Keaton* (1895–1966), born Joseph Frank Keaton, was an American actor, filmmaker, and acrobat who perfected the *stone-faced* style of physical comedy in silent film. Rivaling Charlie Chaplin as the era's greatest comedian, Keaton's films are celebrated for their mathematical precision, innovative camera work, and breathtaking stunts — all performed with an inscrutable facial expression that became his trademark.

## you gotta know

- Keaton's signature **deadpan expression** — an immobile, sorrowful face maintained even during extreme physical comedy — became iconic; he rarely smiled or winked at the audience.
- ***The General*** (1926) is regarded as one of the finest comedies ever made: Keaton plays a railroad engineer in the Civil War pursuing a stolen locomotive, blending slapstick with genuine suspense and narrative logic.
- ***Sherlock Jr.*** (1924) features Keaton as a film projectionist who dreams himself *into* a movie and engages in surreal interactions with the film itself — an early *mise en abîme* that influenced modernist cinema.
- Keaton performed virtually all his own *dangerous stunts*: in ***Steamboat Bill, Jr.*** (1928), a two-ton building facade fell around him (he passed through a window opening), a moment that nearly killed him.
- ***Our Hospitality*** (1923) integrates *location cinematography* (filmed on the Truckee River) with narrative coherence, pioneering the use of natural settings for comedic effect.
- Keaton was a *mathematician of gags*: his films are structured like proofs, each scene building logically to the next; he contrasted this with Chaplin's emotional, intuitive style.
- Unlike Chaplin's Tramp (a dignified character), Keaton's protagonists are often *ordinary men* trapped in absurd situations — a hotel bellhop, a soldier, a railroad worker — making his comedy feel defiantly existential.

- [[silent-film]] — the medium Keaton mastered, demonstrating that wordless cinema could achieve maximum sophistication.
- [[charlie-chaplin]] — his great rival; the contrast between their styles shaped 1920s comedy.
- [[the-general]] — Keaton's acknowledged masterpiece, balancing visual gag density with an emotionally coherent narrative.
- [[sherlock-jr]] — a meta-cinematic experiment that influenced later modernist and self-reflexive filmmaking.
- [[slapstick-comedy]] — the tradition Keaton elevated through engineering precision and stunt-work rather than Chaplin's sentimentality.
- [[vaudeville]] — Keaton's family background; his parents were vaudeville performers, and he appeared on stage before entering film.
- [[our-hospitality]] — one of his finest early features, showcasing the integration of romantic plot and physical comedy.
- [[pratfall]] — the comic tumble or fall that became Keaton's signature physical vocabulary.
