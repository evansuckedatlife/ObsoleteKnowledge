## summary

*Intertitles* (also called *title cards* or *inter-titles*) are text cards inserted between scenes in silent films to convey dialogue, exposition, emotional commentary, or dramatic emphasis. As cinema's sole linguistic medium before synchronized sound, intertitles evolved from utilitarian labels into an art form, their typography, placement, and pacing integral to the film's emotional impact.

## you gotta know

- Intertitles emerged in early cinema (1900s) as a *practical necessity*: without audible dialogue, filmmakers needed text to establish plot, convey speech, and guide viewers.
- Their *design and pacing* became a filmmaker's tool: large, dramatic cards could punctuate jokes, create suspense, or signal emotional shifts — timing was everything.
- The **typography of intertitles** — font choice, color, size, ornament — became a *visual dimension of storytelling*, with different films developing distinct graphic styles.
- Intertitles typically appeared in three categories: **dialogue** (what characters "say"), **exposition** (plot setup or narrator commentary), and **emotional emphasis** (a single word, like "ANGUISH" or "DESIRE," to underscore a moment).
- The *ratio of image to text* varied widely: some directors (D. W. Griffith, for example) used intertitles sparingly, trusting visual language; others (European avant-garde) used text as a quasi-literary device.
- Intertitles created a *rhythm* that shaped viewer experience: too many interrupted flow; too few left audiences confused; mastering their placement was a mark of directorial sophistication.
- With the arrival of sound cinema in 1927–1929, intertitles vanished almost overnight, making silent film a visually distinct and instantly recognizable form.

## connections

- [[silent-film]] — the medium that made intertitles essential and elevated them to art.
- [[charlie-chaplin]] — used intertitles sparingly and poetically, trusting his physical acting to carry meaning.
- [[buster-keaton]] — similarly minimalist in intertitle use, preferring visual gag to text.
- [[d-w-griffith]] — pioneered the use of intertitles as *dramatic punctuation* and emotional heightening.
- [[typography]] — the visual art of lettering that intertitles made cinematic.
- [[film-editing]] — intertitles were edited elements, their placement determining pacing and emphasis.
- [[narrative-cinema]] — intertitles were cinema's first *narrative mode*, establishing conventions still used in modern film (title sequences, captions).
