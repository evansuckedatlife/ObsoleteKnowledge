# Charlie Chaplin

## summary

Charles Spencer *Chaplin* (1889–1977) was a British-American actor, director, and composer who became the most iconic figure of silent cinema through the creation and refinement of the *Tramp* character — a bumbling, dignified everyman whose physical grace and emotional vulnerability captivated global audiences. His films, blending slapstick with pathos, redefined what cinema could express and established him as one of the greatest artists of the twentieth century.

## you gotta know

- Chaplin's signature character, **the Tramp**, wore a *bowler hat, baggy pants, oversized shoes, and a cane*, and spoke through gesture and pantomime with an uncanny blend of comedy and poignancy.
- His most celebrated film, ***The Gold Rush*** (1925), follows the Tramp through the Yukon gold rush; its *dinner-roll scene* (where he performs a ballet with two dinner rolls as feet) is regarded as one of cinema's greatest moments.
- ***Modern Times*** (1936), made after sound had arrived, was Chaplin's *last fully silent film*, a critique of industrial dehumanization featuring the Tramp as a factory worker destroyed by mechanization.
- ***City Lights*** (1927) starred Chaplin as the Tramp falling in love with a blind flower-seller; though sound was introduced that year, Chaplin released it as a silent film, refusing to abandon the form.
- ***The Great Dictator*** (1940) was Chaplin's *first true talkie*; he played both the Tramp and a Hitler-like dictator, and the final monologue against fascism became one of cinema's most quoted speeches.
- Chaplin was a virtuoso of *silent-film technique*: innovating camera placement, editing rhythm, and the use of close-ups to convey emotional nuance, influencing generations of filmmakers.
- Off-screen, Chaplin's life was marked by *exile*: he was deported from the United States in 1952 during the McCarthy era due to suspected communist sympathies and personal scandals.
