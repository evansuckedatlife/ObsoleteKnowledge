# Dmitri Shostakovich

## summary

Dmitri Shostakovich (1906–1975) was a Soviet Russian composer and pianist who was one of the most celebrated and scrutinized figures of 20th-century music. Operating under the highly restrictive censorship of Joseph Stalin's regime, he developed a unique musical language characterized by sharp contrasts, dark irony, grotesque humor, and hidden political subversion. His vast output includes 15 symphonies, 15 string quartets, and multiple operas, which alternate between public loyalty to the Soviet state and private grief and defiance.

## you gotta know

- **Symphony No. 5 in D minor, Op. 47** (1937) is his most famous work, composed as a response to official condemnation and subtitled by a journalist as "a Soviet artist's creative response to just criticism"; its apparently triumphant finale is often interpreted as forced, hollow jubilation.
- **Symphony No. 7 in C major, Op. 60** ("Leningrad", 1941) was written during the German siege of the city and became a global symbol of resistance to fascism, featuring a repetitive, mechanical *invasion theme* in the first movement that builds to a colossal climax.
- **Lady Macbeth of the Mtsensk District** (1934) is a dark opera whose depiction of murder and raw sexuality outraged Stalin, provoking the infamous 1936 *Pravda* editorial "Muddle Instead of Music" that began Shostakovich's lifelong fear of arrest and execution.
- **String Quartet No. 8 in C minor, Op. 110** (1960) was written in Dresden after he reluctantly joined the Communist Party and is dedicated "to the victims of fascism and war"; it heavily incorporates his personal musical signature motif, **DSCH** (D - E-flat - C - B natural).
- **Symphony No. 13 in B-flat minor, Op. 113** ("Babi Yar", 1962) sets the poetry of Yevgeny Yevtushenko to commemorate the Nazi massacre of Jews at Babi Yar and openly criticize Soviet antisemitism and bureaucracy.
- **Testimony** is a highly controversial memoir published posthumously in 1979 by Solomon Volkov, which portrays the composer as a secret dissident who hid anti-Soviet double meanings in his music.
