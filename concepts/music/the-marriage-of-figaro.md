# The Marriage of Figaro

## summary

Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart's *The Marriage of Figaro* (1786) is a four-act *opera buffa* (comic opera) widely considered one of the greatest masterpieces in musical history. Featuring a brilliant Italian libretto by Lorenzo Da Ponte, it is based on Pierre Beaumarchais's controversial French play *La Folle Journée, ou Le Mariage de Figaro*. The plot follows the clever servants Figaro and Susanna as they prepare for their wedding while scheming to thwart their philandering employer, Count Almaviva, who intends to seduce Susanna and assert his archaic feudal right of *droit du seigneur*. Through a series of intricate disguises, nocturnal trysts, and comic misunderstandings, the servants expose the Count's hypocrisy, ultimately forcing him to beg his long-suffering wife for forgiveness. 

## you gotta know

### plot & characters

- **Figaro** is the Count's valet and former barber of Seville who seeks to marry Susanna and coordinates the schemes to outwit the Count.
- **Susanna** is Countess Almaviva's maid and Figaro's fiancée, whose quick wit and resourcefulness drive the comic intrigues.
- **Count Almaviva** is the aristocratic governor of Aguas Frescas near Seville, whose jealousy and wandering eye make him the target of the household's schemes.
- **Rosina (Countess Almaviva)** is the neglected Countess who conspires with her servants to win back her husband's affection by swapping clothes with Susanna.
- **Cherubino** is the Count's adolescent page boy whose hormone-driven infatuation with all women, especially the Countess, causes constant chaos.
- **Marcellina** and **Doctor Bartolo** initially plot to force Figaro to marry Marcellina to resolve an unpaid debt, but they are dramatically revealed in Act III to be Figaro's long-lost parents.

### musical numbers

- **Se vuol ballare** (*If you want to dance*) is Figaro's Act I cavatina, sung after learning of the Count's designs on Susanna, declaring his intent to play the tune and outmaneuver his master.
- **Non più andrai** (*No more gallivanting*) is Figaro's famous Act I aria, where he mockingly describes military life to Cherubino, whom the Count has ordered to join the army.
- **Porgi, amor** (*Grant, love, some comfort*) is the Countess's melancholic Act II entrance aria, lamenting her husband's infidelity.
- **Voi che sapete** (*You who know what love is*) is Cherubino's Act II arietta, sung to the Countess and Susanna, describing the dizzying sensations of his first love.
- **Dove sono i bei momenti** (*Where are the beautiful moments*) is the Countess's reflective Act III aria, expressing hope that she can recapture her husband's love.
- **Sull'aria... che soave zeffiretto** (*On the breeze... what a gentle zephyr*) is the Act III "letter duet" where Countess Rosina dictates a letter to Susanna, setting a trap for the Count under the pine trees.

## context

*The Marriage of Figaro* was revolutionary for its sharp satire of the nobility and its empathetic, humanistic portrayal of working-class servants outsmarting their aristocratic masters. Coming just three years before the French Revolution, Beaumarchais's original play had been banned in Paris and Vienna, but Mozart and Da Ponte successfully softened the explicit political diatribes into a universal comedy of manners that sailed past Austrian censors. The opera is celebrated for its complex ensemble numbers, which seamlessly advance the dramatic action through musical structure.
