# Thomas Tallis

## summary

*Thomas Tallis* (c. 1505–1585) was the greatest English composer of the Tudor period, a versatile master equally skilled in Catholic Latin motets and Protestant English anthems who navigated the treacherous religious shifts of his era with uncompromising artistry. His seamless vocal writing and harmonic sophistication established the foundation for English cathedral music.

## you gotta know

- Composed the 40-voice motet *Spem in Deum* (sometimes titled *Tallis Motets*), an extraordinary feat of polyphonic density and tonal balance that stands among the most complex vocal works of the Renaissance.
- Created an extensive English service repertoire: settings of the Morning and Evening Prayer from the Book of Common Prayer, defining the Anglican choral tradition.
- Served at the Chapel Royal in London for nearly 40 years, surviving religious upheavals under Henry VIII, Edward VI, Mary I, and Elizabeth I by his artistic indispensability.
- Master of modal counterpoint with an instinctive sense of harmonic color and cadential clarity unusual for his era.
- His Latin motets, including works on penitential and Marian texts, suggest his private Catholic conviction while his English anthems served the established church.
- Composed for all available vocal combinations: from simple three-voice works to massive polyphonic canvases, always with singability and clarity.
