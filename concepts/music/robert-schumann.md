# Robert Schumann

## summary

Robert Schumann (1810–1856) was a German composer, pianist, and one of the most influential music critics of the Romantic era. His music is characterized by its intense subjectivity, literary allusions, and flights of fantasy, particularly in his piano works and art songs (*Lieder*). Schumann's career as a virtuoso pianist ended early after a hand injury, leading him to focus on composition and writing. He married the brilliant pianist and composer Clara Wieck, who became the champion and performer of his music. Tormented by severe mental illness, Schumann attempted suicide in 1854 and spent his final years in an asylum, where he died at age 46.

## you gotta know

- **Clara Schumann** (née Wieck) was his wife, a child prodigy and one of the most distinguished pianists of the 19th century, who married Robert against her father's bitter opposition.
- **Neue Zeitschrift für Musik** (*New Journal for Music*) was the highly influential journal Schumann edited, in which he wrote reviews under the pseudonyms **Florestan** (representing his passionate, impetuous side) and **Eusebius** (his introspective, dreamy side).
- **Carnaval, Op. 9** is a set of 21 short piano pieces depicting a masked ball, containing musical portraits of commedia dell'arte characters, his alter egos, Chopin, Paganini, and his beloved Clara.
- **Dichterliebe** (*A Poet's Love*) is his celebrated 1840 song cycle setting sixteen poems by Heinrich Heine, known for its psychological depth and extensive piano postludes.
- **Symphony No. 3 in E-flat major, Op. 97** is known as the **Rhenish** Symphony, inspired by a trip to the Rhineland; it has five movements, including a solemn fourth movement reflecting a cardinal's elevation inside Cologne Cathedral.
- **Johannes Brahms** was the young composer whom Schumann took in as a protégé, famously proclaiming him the future of music in his 1853 article *Neue Bahnen* ("New Paths").
