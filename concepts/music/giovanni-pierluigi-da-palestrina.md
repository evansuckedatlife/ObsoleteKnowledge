# Giovanni Pierluigi da Palestrina

## summary

*Palestrina* (c. 1525–1594) was an Italian Renaissance composer revered for his crystalline polyphonic masses and motets that epitomized the *stile palestriniano*—a modal, thoroughly singable sacred idiom. His mastery of vocal counterpoint and clarity of text delivery made him the model for church music composition for centuries.

## you gotta know

- Composed over 100 masses, many employing parody technique (reworking earlier secular songs into sacred forms), and his *Missa Papae Marcelli* was celebrated for the clarity of its Latin text.
- Served as *maestro di cappella* of the Julian Chapel (the papal choir) in Rome, a position of immense prestige and influence.
- Master of the *prima prattica*—the strict counterpoint rules of the Renaissance, prioritizing harmonic perfection and strict voice-leading over dramatic expression.
- His motet repertoire includes over 250 works, many on liturgical texts, characterized by modal harmony and seamless vocal lines.
- Tradition holds that his *Missa Papae Marcelli* saved polyphonic music from a church ban during the Counter-Reformation by demonstrating that complex counterpoint could preserve text intelligibility.
- Established the template for *a cappella* sacred music: unaccompanied voices in balanced harmony, no instrumental doubling, deeply spiritual restraint.
