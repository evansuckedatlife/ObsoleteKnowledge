# Richard Wagner

## summary

Richard Wagner (1813–1883) was a revolutionary German composer, conductor, and theatre director who fundamentally transformed Western classical music and opera. He is best known for his massive "music dramas," most notably the four-opera cycle *The Ring of the Nibelung* (*Der Ring des Nibelungen*). Wagner pioneered the concept of the *Gesamtkunstwerk* ("total work of art"), which synthesized music, poetry, drama, and visual arts into a single cohesive experience. He designed and built his own opera house, the Bayreuth Festspielhaus, specifically to stage his works under ideal acoustic conditions. His advanced use of chromaticism and *leitmotifs* (recurring themes associated with specific characters or ideas) pushed tonal music to its limits, paving the way for 20th-century modernism.

## you gotta know

### major works & concepts

- **The Ring of the Nibelung** (*Der Ring des Nibelungen*) is a monumental fifteen-hour, four-opera cycle comprising *Das Rheingold*, *Die Walküre*, *Siegfried*, and *Götterdämmerung*, based on Norse sagas and the Middle High German epic *Nibelungenlied*.
- **Tristan und Isolde** (1865) is a landmark music drama famous for the **Tristan chord** (F-B-D-sharp-G-sharp) in its opening measures, which avoids traditional resolution and is widely seen as the beginning of the breakdown of classical tonality.
- **Bayreuth Festspielhaus** is the custom-designed opera house in Bavaria, Germany, funded in part by King Ludwig II of Bavaria, featuring a hidden orchestra pit that blends the sound of the instruments with the singers' voices.
- **Leitmotif** is the musical technique Wagner perfected, assigning specific melodic phrases to represent individual characters, objects (like the Ring or the sword Nothung), or abstract concepts, which develop and intertwine throughout the drama.
- **Gesamtkunstwerk** is his philosophical ideal of the "total work of art," which he outlined in essays like *The Art-Work of the Future* and *Opera and Drama*.

### individual operas & pieces

- **Ride of the Valkyries** is the thunderous, brass-heavy prelude to Act III of *Die Walküre*, famously used in popular culture (such as the helicopter scene in *Apocalypse Now*).
- **Bridal Chorus** (*Treulich geführt*, popularly known as "Here Comes the Bride") is the wedding march from his 1850 romantic opera **Lohengrin**.
- **Parsifal** (1882) is his final completed music drama, centered on the Arthurian quest for the Holy Grail, which he designated as a sacred festival play (*Bühnenweihfestspiel*) and forbade from being performed outside Bayreuth for thirty years.
- **Siegfried Idyll** is a chamber symphonic poem composed as a surprise birthday gift for his second wife, **Cosima**, performed on the stairs of their villa at Tribschen.

## context

Wagner remains one of the most controversial figures in cultural history. While his musical genius is undisputed, his aggressive German nationalism and virulent anti-Semitic writings, particularly his essay *Das Judenthum in der Musik* ("Jewishness in Music"), have cast a dark shadow over his legacy. Decades after his death, his music was championed by Adolf Hitler and the Nazi regime, leading to an informal, ongoing ban on public performances of his works in Israel. He was the son-in-law of fellow composer Franz Liszt, having married Liszt's daughter Cosima.
