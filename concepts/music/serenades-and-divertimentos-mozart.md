# Mozart serenades and divertimentos

## summary

Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart composed numerous serenades and divertimentos throughout his career, originally intended as light, entertaining background music for outdoor banquets, weddings, and social gatherings of the nobility. Despite their functional origins, these works contain some of his most sophisticated craftsmanship and his most universally recognized melodies. The most famous among them is the Serenade No. 13 in G major, *Eine kleine Nachtmusik* (K. 525), which stands as an icon of the Classical era, and *A Musical Joke* (K. 522), a brilliant satire of bad composition.

## you gotta know

- **Eine kleine Nachtmusik (Serenade No. 13 in G major, K. 525)** translates to "A Little Night Music" or "A Little Serenade." Written for a string chamber ensemble (or string orchestra), its brisk, ascending opening theme is one of the most famous melodies in music history.
- **A Musical Joke (Ein musikalischer Spass, K. 522)** is a satirical divertimento composed shortly after the death of Mozart's father Leopold. It parodies incompetent composers by using awkward rhythms, redundant phrases, parallel fifths, and concluding with a series of discordant, polytonal horn blasts that mock amateur writing.
- **Gran Partita (Serenade No. 10 in B-flat major, K. 361)** is a massive, highly expressive work scored for twelve wind instruments and a double bass. Its slow *Adagio* movement features a famous dialogue between oboe and basset horn that is dramatized in the play and film *Amadeus*.
- **Posthorn Serenade (Serenade No. 9 in D major, K. 320)** is named for the inclusion of a post horn solo—a valveless brass instrument used by mail coaches—in its second minuet.
- **Haffner Serenade (Serenade No. 8 in D major, K. 250)** was written for the wedding of Marie Elisabeth Haffner, daughter of a prominent Salzburg merchant. Mozart later adapted some of its movements into his famous *Haffner* Symphony (No. 35).
