# Polyphony

## summary

*Polyphony* is the texture of simultaneous independent melodic lines, each with its own rhythmic and pitch identity, that interweave to create harmony. It is the opposite of *monophony* (a single line) and *homophony* (chords supporting a melody), and is the foundation of much Western classical music, from medieval organum through Bach and beyond.

## you gotta know

- *Polyphonic* texture requires each voice to be relatively independent and interesting on its own; if voices move entirely together in the same rhythm, the result is *homophonic*, not polyphonic.
- *Counterpoint* is the disciplined art of writing polyphony according to historical rules; *free polyphony* loosens those rules for contemporary or expressive freedom.
- Medieval *organum* was one of the earliest polyphonic forms, pairing a chant with an added voice; Renaissance *motets* expanded the technique to 4–6 voices.
- *Fugue*, *canon*, and *round* are polyphonic forms; so are many passages in symphonies (the development section of a sonata) and chamber music.
- The *Renaissance* (16th century) and *Baroque* (17th–early 18th) eras prioritized polyphony; *Classical* and *Romantic* composers emphasized *homophony* but used polyphony selectively.

## connections

- [[counterpoint]] — the formal study of how to write polyphonic lines properly.
- [[fugue]] — a highly organized polyphonic form using strict imitation.
- [[homophony]] — the textural counterpart; most popular classical music is essentially homophonic.
- [[renaissance-music]] — the period that perfected polyphonic vocal music.
