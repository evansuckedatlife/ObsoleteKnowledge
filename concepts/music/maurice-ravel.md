# Maurice Ravel

## summary

Maurice Ravel (1875–1937) was a French composer, pianist, and conductor who is widely regarded as one of France's greatest composers. Often associated with Claude Debussy as a pioneer of Impressionist music (though both rejected the term), Ravel was a meticulous craftsman who combined traditional formal structures with novel harmonic language and brilliant orchestration. His work is celebrated for its lush textures, Spanish influence, and virtuosic piano writing. 

## you gotta know

- **Boléro** (1928) is his most famous and commercially successful work, a one-movement orchestral piece featuring an unchanging 18-bar melody repeated 18 times in a gradual, massive crescendo over a relentless snare drum ostinato.
- **Daphnis et Chloé** (1912) is a massive, lush ballet commissioned by Sergei Diaghilev for the **Ballets Russes**, featuring a wordless chorus and widely considered Ravel's orchestral masterpiece.
- **La Valse** (1920) is a choreographic poem conceived as an homage to the Viennese waltz; it features increasingly chaotic, swirling themes that are often interpreted as a metaphor for the destruction of pre-war European civilization.
- **Gaspard de la nuit** (1908) is a devilishly difficult three-movement piano suite based on poems by Aloysius Bertrand; its final movement, *Scarbo*, was deliberately written to be more technically demanding than Balakirev's *Islamey*.
- **Pavane for a Dead Princess** (*Pavane pour une infante défunte*, 1899) is a brief, wistful piece originally written for solo piano and later orchestrated, designed to evoke a slow court dance that a young Spanish princess might have danced.
- **Pictures at an Exhibition** is Ravel's celebrated 1922 orchestration of **Modest Mussorgsky**'s piano suite; it became the definitive version of the work and cemented Ravel's reputation as a master orchestrator.
