# Japanese Gagaku

## summary

*Gagaku* is Japan's ancient imperial court music, performed continuously for over 1,200 years. A refined ensemble tradition featuring winds (shawm, flute), strings (koto, biwa), and percussion, gagaku is organized into instrumental dances and processional pieces that marked state ceremonies, religious rites, and entertainment for the imperial household.

## you gotta know

- Gagaku originated as a fusion of Japanese indigenous music, Tang Dynasty Chinese music, and Korean and Central Asian influences; each layer left distinct stylistic traces.
- Ensembles feature the *sho* (free-reed mouth organ), *ryuteki* (transverse flute), *hichiriki* (double-reed shawm), alongside *koto* (long zither) and *biwa* (plucked lute); percussion includes drums and gongs.
- Pieces are organized into three categories: *bugaku* (instrumental dances with masked dancers), *kangen* (purely instrumental versions), and *roei* (vocal pieces with instrumentals).
- Many pieces bear Chinese titles and were adopted from Tang court tradition; the repertoire includes works over a millennium old, preserved through strict oral transmission.
- The imperial court maintains an official gagaku ensemble that performs at state ceremonies; transmission is jealously guarded, with apprenticeship beginning in childhood.
- Unlike many Asian music traditions, gagaku was never significantly interrupted—it is the world's oldest continuously practiced court music ensemble tradition.
- Modern performances evoke an austere, meditative aesthetic; the slow tempo and timbral subtlety demand intense listener concentration.
