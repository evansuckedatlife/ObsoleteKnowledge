# Persian Dastgah

## summary

The *dastgah* is the modal-melodic framework of Persian classical music, comprising a system of hierarchical melodic formulas and emotional associations within which musicians improvise. Like the Indian raga and Arabic maqam, each of the twelve main dastgahs organizes a repertoire of *gushehs* (melodic phrases) into a coherent aesthetic universe; the tradition prizes subtle emotional expression and technical mastery over virtuosic display.

## you gotta know

- The system includes twelve primary dastgahs (corresponding roughly to Western keys) and five secondary modes (*afshari*, *bayati*, *rehavi*, *setar*, *chahargah*); each has a distinct emotional character and associated melodic motifs.
- The *ney* (reed flute) is the most revered instrument, prized for its soulful, human-like voice; the *tar* (plucked lute) and *setar* (smaller lute) are also central, while the *kamancheh* (bowed string) and *santur* (hammered dulcimer) provide accompaniment.
- Performances begin with a free-rhythm section (*daraamad*), progress through progressively faster *gushehs*, and may culminate in a rhythmic piece (*reng*); the structure is hierarchical and emotional rather than formally prescribed.
- The tradition is transmitted orally, typically from master to student; notation is a modern intrusion, resisted by purists who argue it cannot capture the microtonal subtleties and expressive timing.
- Dastgah music was historically tied to the Persian court and aristocracy; it remains a symbol of cultural refinement and national identity in modern Iran.
- The poetic texts often sung in dastgah pieces frequently reference mystical themes, love, and longing—shared with the Sufi spiritual tradition.
- Modern performers like Farid Farjadi and Shajarian have revitalized the tradition globally, though it remains less internationally known than Indian or Arabic classical music.
