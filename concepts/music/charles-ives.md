# Charles Ives

## summary

Charles Ives (1874–1954) was an American modernist composer who is now recognized as one of the first American composers of international significance. Believing that a professional musician could be forced to compromise their artistic freedom, Ives pursued a highly successful career in the insurance business, composing in his spare time. His music was decades ahead of its time, incorporating radical experiments with polytonality, polyrhythms, quarter tones, and tone clusters, often overlaid with nostalgic quotations of American hymns, folk songs, and marching bands.

## you gotta know

- **The Unanswered Question** (1908) is a short, philosophical chamber work in which a trumpet plays a phrase representing "The Perennial Question of Existence" over a slow string background representing "The Silences of the Druids," while a woodwind quartet tries and fails to answer the question, becoming increasingly chaotic.
- **Concord Sonata** (Piano Sonata No. 2, 1915) is a monumental work dedicated to transcendentalist writers associated with Concord, Massachusetts (*Emerson*, *Hawthorne*, *The Alcotts*, and *Thoreau*); it famously requires the pianist to use a 14.75-inch wooden block to play massive tone clusters.
- **Three Places in New England** (1914) is a brilliant orchestral suite depicting regional historic sites; its second movement (*Putnam's Camp*) famously recreates the acoustic experience of two marching bands playing completely different tunes as they march past each other.
- **Symphony No. 4** (completed 1916) is a colossal, multi-layered work that is so rhythmically complex it often requires two conductors to perform, serving as a summation of his philosophical and musical ideals.
- **Central Park in the Dark** (1906) is an atmospheric chamber piece that portrays a quiet night in New York City interrupted by distant streetcars, ragtime music, and rowdy crowds before settling back into a quiet string drone.
- **Ives & Myrick** was the highly successful insurance agency he co-founded in New York; he pioneered the modern concept of estate planning and wrote a widely read pamphlet on insurance sales before his health forced him to retire.
