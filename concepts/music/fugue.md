# Fugue

## summary

A *fugue* is a polyphonic composition where two or more voices develop the same thematic material (the subject) through imitation and counterpoint, each voice entering at staggered points. The form is the pinnacle of contrapuntal art, combining strict compositional rules with emotional and intellectual depth.

## you gotta know

- The structure begins with exposition: each voice (typically 2–4) enters with the subject, alternating with subject and *answer* (a transposed repetition).
- After the exposition, the fugue moves through *middle entries* and *episodes* (modulatory passages that develop motives) before a final section restates the subject.
- The *subject* is the governing melody; the *countersubject* is a melody that accompanies it; both are woven throughout.
- *Stretto* is a climactic section where the subject enters in rapid succession across all voices, compressed in time, often in the final pages.
- *Bach's Art of Fugue* (incomplete 14-part work) and *The Well-Tempered Clavier* (24 preludes and fugues) are the supreme exemplars.
- A fugue may be marked by the number of its voices: "three-part fugue," "four-part fugue," etc.

## connections

- [[counterpoint]] — the foundational technique that governs fugal construction.
- [[bach]] — the master who perfected the form and wrote the most studied fugues in Western music.
- [[sonata-form]] — often incorporates fugal techniques, especially in development sections.
- [[polyphony]] — the broader texture in which fugue is the most disciplined instance.
