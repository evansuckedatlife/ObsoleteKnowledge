# The Magic Flute

## summary

Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart's *The Magic Flute* (1791) is a two-act *Singspiel* (a German opera form featuring spoken dialogue) that ranks among his most popular and symbolic works. With a libretto by Emanuel Schikaneder, the opera follows Prince Tamino, who is rescued from a giant serpent by the three ladies of the Queen of the Night. The Queen commissions Tamino to rescue her daughter Pamina, who has been captured by the high priest Sarastro. Accompanied by the comic bird-catcher Papageno, Tamino is gifted a magical flute and bells to protect them. Upon arriving at Sarastro's temple, Tamino realizes that Sarastro represents wisdom and light, while the Queen of the Night represents darkness and tyranny. Tamino and Pamina undergo trials of silence, fire, and water to achieve spiritual enlightenment and join Sarastro's order, while the Queen is defeated.

## you gotta know

### plot & characters

- **Tamino** is a foreign prince who falls in love with Pamina's portrait and undergoes initiation trials to prove himself worthy of her.
- **Pamina** is the Queen of the Night's daughter, captured by Sarastro for her own protection, who joins Tamino in his final initiation trials.
- **Papageno** is a simple, gluttonous bird-catcher clad in feathers who seeks only food, wine, and a "Papagena" to love, failing his trials but rewarded with a wife.
- **Sarastro** is the high priest of Isis and Osiris who governs a temple of wisdom and reason, acting as a benevolent guide to Tamino and Pamina.
- **The Queen of the Night** (*Königin der Nacht*) is Pamina's mother, a personification of dark vengeance who plots to destroy Sarastro's temple.
- **Monostatos** is a Moorish temple slave who repeatedly attempts to force himself on Pamina, later betraying Sarastro to join the Queen of the Night.

### musical numbers & key elements

- **Der Hölle Rache kocht in meinem Herzen** (*Hell's vengeance boils in my heart*) is the Queen of the Night's famous Act II coloratura aria, demanding that Pamina assassinate Sarastro, notorious for its extremely high range (hitting high F6).
- **Der Vogelfänger bin ich ja** (*The birdcatcher am I indeed*) is Papageno's Act I entrance song, introduced by a simple panpipe motif.
- **Dies Bildnis ist bezaubernd schön** (*This image is enchantingly beautiful*) is Tamino's Act I lyrical aria expressing his immediate love for Pamina upon seeing her portrait.
- **O Isis und Osiris** is Sarastro's Act II solemn bass aria, calling upon the Egyptian gods to grant wisdom and strength to the new initiates.
- **Pa-pa-pa** is the joyful comic duet in Act II where Papageno and Papagena celebrate their reunion and fantasize about raising a large family of "little Papagenos."
- **Magical Instruments** include the **Magic Flute** (given to Tamino, which can tame wild beasts and protect during trials) and **Magic Bells** (given to Papageno, which make Monostatos and his guards dance involuntarily).

## context

*The Magic Flute* is famously saturated with Freemasonry themes and rituals (both Mozart and Schikaneder were Masons). The opera opens with an overture featuring three solemn chords, reflecting the Masonic importance of the number three, and the trials of the initiates mirror Masonic rites of passage. The Singspiel format allowed the work to reach a broad, popular audience in Vienna's suburban Theater auf der Wieden, rather than the aristocratic court. It was Mozart's last completed opera, premiering just over two months before his death.
