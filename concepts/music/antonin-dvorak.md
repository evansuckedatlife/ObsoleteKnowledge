# Antonín Dvořák

## summary

Antonín Dvořák (1841–1904) was a Czech composer who achieved international fame by integrating Czech and Slavic folk rhythms into Classical structures. He was a leading figure of musical nationalism, composing the *Slavonic Dances* and the folklore-inspired opera *Rusalka*. In 1892, Dvořák traveled to the United States to serve as the director of the National Conservatory of Music of America in New York City. During his American stay, he was deeply inspired by African-American spirituals and Native American music, leading him to compose his most famous work, the *New World* Symphony, and his popular *American* String Quartet.

## you gotta know

- **Symphony No. 9 in E minor, Op. 95** (subtitled **"From the New World"**) was composed in New York in 1893, weaving together melodies inspired by African-American spirituals (such as *Swing Low, Sweet Chariot*) and Henry Wadsworth Longfellow's epic poem *The Song of Hiawatha*.
- **Largo** (second movement) of the *New World* Symphony features a famous, nostalgic solo for the **English horn**, which was later set to lyrics by William Arms Fisher as the song **Goin' Home**.
- **Cello Concerto in B minor, Op. 104** is widely considered the greatest concerto for the instrument, written in America but incorporating a melody from his own song *Lasst mich allein* ("Leave me alone") to mourn the terminal illness of his sister-in-law Josefina.
- **Slavonic Dances** (published in two sets, Op. 46 and Op. 72) are highly popular orchestral works inspired by traditional Czech dances like the *furiant* and *dumka*, which launched his career.
- **American String Quartet** (String Quartet No. 12 in F major, Op. 96) was composed in just three days during a summer holiday in the Czech-immigrant community of Spillville, Iowa.
- **Song to the Moon** (*Měsíčku na nebi hlubokém*) is the famous soprano aria from his fairy-tale opera **Rusalka**, in which the water nymph Rusalka asks the moon to tell a human prince of her love.
