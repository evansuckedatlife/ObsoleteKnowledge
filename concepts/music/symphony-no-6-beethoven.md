# Symphony No. 6 in F major

## summary

Ludwig van Beethoven's *Symphony No. 6 in F major*, Op. 68 (1808), known as the **Pastoral Symphony**, is one of the most famous examples of early program music. Written during the same period as the Fifth Symphony, it reflects Beethoven's deep love of nature and his frequent walks in the Viennese countryside. Unlike traditional Classical symphonies, it has five movements, each with a descriptive program title. The work is celebrated for its musical depictions of flowing water, birdcalls, rustic dances, and a violent thunderstorm, concluding with a hymn of thanksgiving.

## you gotta know

- **Descriptive titles** were provided by Beethoven for each movement, starting with "Awakening of cheerful feelings upon arrival in the countryside" for the first movement.
- **Scene by the Brook** (second movement, *Andante molto mosso*) features a famous, literal woodwind cadenza imitating birdcalls: a **nightingale** (flute), a **quail** (oboe), and a **cuckoo** (clarinets).
- **Merry Assembly of Country Folk** (third movement) is a lively folk dance of local peasants, which is cut short by the sudden onset of rain.
- **Thunderstorm** (fourth movement) is a dramatic, through-composed movement that acts as an transition, using the piccolo and timpani to evoke wind, lightning, and thunder.
- **Shepherd's Song** (fifth movement, *Allegretto*) resolves the storm into a joyful, hymn-like thanksgiving melody sung by the shepherds.
