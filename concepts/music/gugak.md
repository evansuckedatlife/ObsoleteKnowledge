# Korean Gugak

## summary

*Gugak* ("national music") is the indigenous music tradition of Korea, encompassing court music, folk instrumental pieces, and vocal traditions. Like other East Asian classical traditions, gugak emphasizes melodic subtlety, refined timbre, and the emotional expression of the performer; ensemble and solo pieces showcase instruments uniquely Korean and often preserve musical styles lost in neighboring traditions.

## you gotta know

- Court music (*jeongak*) includes ancient instrumental pieces (*sanjo*, *pansori*) and orchestral works performed at court and in contemporary concert halls; the tradition was nearly lost during Japanese occupation and has been actively revived.
- Primary instruments include the *gayageum* (zither-like plucked instrument with 12 or more strings), *geomungo* (angular zither played with a plectrum), *daegeum* (large transverse flute), and *janggu* (double-headed hourglass drum).
- *Pansori* is a narrative vocal tradition where a single singer, accompanied only by a drummer, performs long epic tales with dramatic vocal effects and emotional intensity; it was designated UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage in 2003.
- The *sanjo* form is an instrumental solo (or small ensemble) that begins slowly and accelerates rhythmically, resembling the *alap-jor-jhala* progression in Hindustani music.
- Gugak uses a five-note pentatonic scale similar to Chinese music; however, the microtonal bending and ornamentation are distinctly Korean, producing a unique timbral character.
- Rhythmic cycles called *jangdan* structure many pieces; the *janggu* drummer is often a virtuoso soloist, featured prominently in ensemble contexts.
- The Korean National Classical Music Institute and modern concert halls have established gugak as a living tradition; contemporary composers blend gugak with Western forms.
