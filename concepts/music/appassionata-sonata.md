# Appassionata Sonata

## summary

Ludwig van Beethoven's *Piano Sonata No. 23 in F minor*, Op. 57 (1805), widely known as the **Appassionata**, is one of the most stormy and emotionally intense keyboard works of his middle period. Composed during the same era as his *Eroica* Symphony, it represents his mastery of dramatic contrast and large-scale sonata form. The nickname *Appassionata* (meaning "passionate") was not coined by Beethoven but was added by a Hamburg publisher in 1838. Beethoven dedicated the work to his friend and patron, Count Franz von Brunsvik.

## you gotta know

- **First Movement** (*Allegro assai*) begins with a quiet, ominous unison theme in F minor that spans two octaves, which is immediately repeated a half-step higher in G-flat major, punctuated by explosive fortissimo outbursts.
- **Second Movement** (*Andante con moto*) is a serene, chorale-like theme and variations in D-flat major. It concludes with an unresolved, dissonant diminished seventh chord that transitions directly (*attacca*) into the finale.
- **Third Movement** (*Allegro ma non troppo*) is a driving, restless perpetual motion finale in F minor, characterized by continuous sixteenth-note runs and dramatic sweep, ending with a frantic, prestissimo dance-like coda.
- **Count Franz von Brunsvik** is the Hungarian nobleman to whom the work is dedicated.
- **Lenore** was the name of his opera *Fidelio* in its early versions, which shared the sonata's high-drama, tragic musical atmosphere.
