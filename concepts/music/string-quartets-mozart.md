# Mozart string quartets

## summary

Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart composed 23 string quartets, which represent a cornerstone of Classical chamber music. His quartets trace a path from his early Italian-style works to the highly sophisticated, contrapuntally rich masterpieces of his maturity in Vienna. The peak of this output is the set of six "Haydn Quartets" (Nos. 14–19), dedicated to his close friend and mentor Joseph Haydn. These works are celebrated for their structural balance, wit, and chamber dialogue among the four instruments.

## you gotta know

- **Haydn Quartets (Nos. 14–19)** are a set of six quartets published in 1785 with a warm, personal dedication to Joseph Haydn, who had recently revolutionized the genre with his own Op. 33 quartets.
- **Dissonance Quartet (No. 19 in C major, K. 465)** is the most famous of the Haydn set, renowned for its slow, eerie opening adagio that utilizes unexpected chromaticism and shifting tonalities that highly disturbed contemporary listeners.
- **Hunt Quartet (No. 17 in B-flat major, K. 458)** is another member of the Haydn set, nicknamed for its cheerful, lilting opening theme which resembles the calls of hunting horns.
- **Prussian Quartets (Nos. 21–23)** are Mozart's final three quartets, dedicated to King Friedrich Wilhelm II of Prussia. Because the king was a skilled amateur cellist, these works feature unusually prominent and technically demanding cello lines.
- **Hoffmeister Quartet (No. 20 in D major, K. 499)** is a standalone quartet named after Franz Anton Hoffmeister, Mozart's friend and publisher who lent him money in exchange for the work.
