# Piano Concerto No. 5 in E-flat major

## summary

Ludwig van Beethoven's *Piano Concerto No. 5 in E-flat major*, Op. 73 (1811), popularly known as the **Emperor Concerto**, is his final completed piano concerto. Composed during the bombardment of Vienna by Napoleon's troops, the work is characterized by its heroic scale, military rhythms, and symphonic grandeur. The nickname "Emperor" was not given by Beethoven (who despised Napoleon's imperial ambitions) but likely by the English publisher Johann Baptist Cramer. The concerto is celebrated for its virtuosic piano writing and its innovative structure, including a bold opening that bypasses the traditional orchestral exposition.

## you gotta know

- **Written-out opening cadenza** begins the first movement immediately, as the soloist executes rapid arpeggios and runs following three massive chords from the orchestra, reversing the classical convention of placing the cadenza at the end.
- **No improvised cadenza** is permitted in the first movement; Beethoven explicitly noted in the score that the soloist should not improvise but immediately play his written-out solo passage.
- **Siege of Vienna** in 1809 forced Beethoven to write the work while sheltering in his brother Karl's basement, covering his ears with pillows to protect his failing hearing from the French artillery fire.
- **Adagio un poco mosso** (second movement) is a serene, lyrical movement in the remote key of B major, which transitions directly (*attacca*) into the rondo finale via a quiet horn note that drops a half-step.
- **E-flat major** was Beethoven's preferred key for heroic themes, shared with his Third Symphony (*Eroica*) and the *Les Adieux* Piano Sonata.
