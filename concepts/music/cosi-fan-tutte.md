# Così fan tutte

## summary

Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart's *Così fan tutte, ossia La scuola degli amanti* (1790) is a two-act Italian *opera buffa* (comic opera) that represents the peak of ensemble writing in Classical music. With a libretto by Lorenzo Da Ponte, the title translates to "Thus do they all" or "The School for Lovers," implying that all women are fickle in love. The story follows two young soldiers, Ferrando and Guglielmo, who brag of their fiancées' (sisters Dorabella and Fiordiligi) absolute fidelity. The cynical philosopher Don Alfonso bets the soldiers that the women can be seduced within twenty-four hours. Alfonso forces the soldiers to pretend to be called to war, only to return disguised as exotic "Albanians" who immediately woo each other's partner. Guided by the mercenary maid Despina, both sisters eventually succumb to the new suitors, exposing the fragility of romantic idealism before the deception is revealed.

## you gotta know

### plot & characters

- **Fiordiligi** and **Dorabella** are the two sisters from Ferrara living in Naples; Fiordiligi is initially serious and steadfast, while Dorabella is more impulsive.
- **Ferrando** and **Guglielmo** are the two officers betrothed to Dorabella and Fiordiligi, respectively, who participate in Don Alfonso's bet and find themselves seduced by the experiment.
- **Don Alfonso** is the cynical, elderly bachelor and philosopher who masterminds the bet and coordinates the elaborate deception to prove his view of human nature.
- **Despina** is the sisters' cunning, pragmatic maid who is bribed by Don Alfonso to encourage the sisters to flirt, later disguising herself as a doctor (curing the men's "poisoning" with a giant magnet) and a notary to draw up a fake marriage contract.

### musical numbers

- **Soave sia il vento** (*Gentle be the breeze*) is a famous, serene Act I trio sung by Fiordiligi, Dorabella, and Don Alfonso, wishing the soldiers a safe voyage, accompanied by undulating muted strings.
- **Come scoglio** (*Like a rock*) is Fiordiligi's Act I aria, a demanding coloratura piece in which she declares her fidelity is as immovable as a rock in a storm, featuring dramatic octave leaps.
- **Un'aura amorosa** (*A loving breath*) is Ferrando's Act I lyric tenor aria, in which he praises the power of love to sustain him during the trial.
- **Una donna a quindici anni** (*A fifteen-year-old woman*) is Despina's Act II aria in which she advises the sisters that a young girl must know how to flirt, lie, and manage multiple lovers.
- **Per pietà, ben mio, perdona** (*For mercy, my love, forgive*) is Fiordiligi's Act II rondo expressing her deep shame and internal conflict as she realizes she is falling for her disguised brother-in-law.

## context

For much of the 19th and early 20th centuries, *Così fan tutte* was dismissed as immoral, vulgar, and unworthy of Mozart's genius due to its frank depiction of infidelity and wife-swapping. As a result, it was rarely performed or was staged with heavily modified, sanitized plots. In the mid-20th century, the opera underwent a major critical revival, with musicologists praising its brilliant psychological depth, its use of parody, and its exquisite vocal writing.
