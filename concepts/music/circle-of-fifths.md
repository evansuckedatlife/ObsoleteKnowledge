# Circle of Fifths

## summary

The *circle of fifths* is a geometric diagram organizing all twelve pitches (in their major and minor keys) in a circle, ordered by their distance in perfect fifths. It is an indispensable tool for understanding key relationships, chord progressions, and modulation, and appears on nearly every musician's wall.

## you gotta know

- Clockwise from C, each step moves up a perfect fifth (C → G → D → A → E → B → F#), adding one sharp to the key signature; counterclockwise moves down a fifth (adding flats).
- Adjacent keys on the circle share the most pitches and are the closest in harmonic color; moving around the circle increases harmonic distance.
- The outer ring shows major keys; the inner ring shows relative minor keys (each sharing the same key signature).
- Key signatures follow the circle: zero sharps/flats at C and Am; one sharp (F#) at G and Em; one flat (Bb) at F and Dm.
- Musicians use it to quickly find closely related keys for modulation, predict the next chord in a progression (many pop/jazz songs follow a clockwise path: IV–I–V or Dm–G–C), and memorize key signatures.

## connections

- [[key-signature]] — the circle of fifths is the mnemonic for understanding key signature patterns.
- [[tonality]] — the circle represents the harmonic space of the major/minor tonal system.
- [[modulation]] — adjacent keys on the circle are the easiest to modulate into.
- [[chord-progression]] — many canonical progressions (I–IV–V–I, vi–IV–I–V) move in patterns readable from the circle.
