## summary

*Vesti la giubba* (*Put on the costume*) is the heart-wrenching tenor aria sung by Canio in Ruggero Leoncavallo's opera *Pagliacci* (*The Clowns*). In this dramatic moment, Canio—a traveling performer who has just discovered his wife's infidelity—must set aside his personal anguish and prepare for that evening's commedia dell'arte performance. The aria captures the bitter irony of an actor forced to smile and entertain while his world crumbles, making it one of operatic theater's most poignant meditations on the mask between performer and person.

## you gotta know

- Composed by *Ruggero Leoncavallo* for tenor voice, featuring one of the most emotionally devastating moments in all of opera.
- The title translates to "Put on the costume," and Canio must literally don his clown's costume and makeup while grappling with unbearable jealousy and rage.
- *Pagliacci* (1892) is a *verismo* opera, a style emphasizing raw realism and contemporary (rather than historical or mythological) subject matter, populated by working-class characters.
- The aria's melody begins tenderly but builds to an anguished outburst before returning to resignation, mirroring Canio's emotional volatility and his forced acceptance of his duty.
- Leoncavallo's orchestration is spare but pointed, with pizzicato strings and mournful woodwinds underscoring the actor's isolation and existential crisis.
- The aria's famous final lines, *Ridi, pagliaccio!* (*Laugh, clown!*), encapsulate the entire human tragedy: the performer must entertain despite personal devastation.
- This aria gained particular fame through the recordings of tenor *Enrico Caruso*, whose emotional intensity became the standard interpretation.
