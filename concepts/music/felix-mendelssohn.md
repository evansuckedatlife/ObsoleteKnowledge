# Felix Mendelssohn

## summary

Felix Mendelssohn (1809–1847) was a German composer, pianist, and conductor of the early Romantic period. A child prodigy born into a wealthy intellectual Jewish family (grandson of philosopher Moses Mendelssohn), he displayed a mastery of Classical structure and Romantic expression at an early age. Mendelssohn was a key figure in musical history, conducting a landmark 1829 performance of J.S. Bach's *St Matthew Passion* that sparked a major European Bach revival. He is celebrated for his travel-inspired orchestral works, such as the *Italian* and *Scottish* symphonies, and his incidental music for Shakespeare's *A Midsummer Night's Dream*.

## you gotta know

- **A Midsummer Night's Dream** is his most famous theatrical work. He composed the brilliant concert overture (Op. 21) at age 17 and later added incidental music (Op. 61), which contains the ubiquitous **Wedding March** used at marriages.
- **Symphony No. 4 in A major, Op. 90** is known as the **Italian** Symphony, reflecting the sunshine and folk dances of Italy, concluding with a rapid, minor-key *saltarello* and *tarantella*.
- **Symphony No. 3 in A minor, Op. 56** is the **Scottish** Symphony, inspired by a visit to the ruins of Holyrood Chapel in Edinburgh, where Mary, Queen of Scots was crowned.
- **Hebrides Overture, Op. 26** (also called **Fingal's Cave**) is a concert overture depicting the rolling waves and echoing acoustics of Fingal's Cave on the Scottish island of Staffa.
- **Violin Concerto in E minor, Op. 64** is a cornerstone of the violin repertoire, noted for having the soloist enter in the very first measure and utilizing a bassoon note to connect the first and second movements without pause.
- **Songs Without Words** (*Lieder ohne Worte*) is a collection of 48 short, lyrical piano pieces published in eight volumes, which popularized the Romantic character piece.
