# Duke Ellington

## summary

*Duke Ellington* was an American jazz composer, bandleader, and pianist whose sophisticated orchestrations, extended compositions, and thirty-year tenure with his orchestra established him as one of the most important musicians in twentieth-century American music. His prolific output bridged jazz and concert music, elevating the genre's artistic status.

## you gotta know

- Bandleader of the *Duke Ellington Orchestra*, one of the longest continuously performing orchestras in history, which served as a laboratory for complex harmonic arrangements.
- Composed thousands of works including suite-length pieces like *Black, Brown and Beige* (1943), which explored African-American history through orchestral jazz.
- Created signature arrangements that showcased individual band members' timbres—the "Ellington Sound" featuring growling trumpets and muted brass effects.
- Iconic compositions include "Mood Indigo," "Take the A Train," "It Don't Mean a Thing (If It Ain't Got That Swing)," and "Satin Doll."
- Pioneered jazz education through concert tours at universities and demonstrated that jazz could achieve the artistic weight of classical symphonic music.
- Performed at Carnegie Hall (1943) and represented American jazz internationally for over fifty years.
