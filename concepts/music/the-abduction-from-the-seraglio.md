# The Abduction from the Seraglio

## summary

Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart's *The Abduction from the Seraglio* (1782) is a three-act German *Singspiel* (comic opera with spoken dialogue) that popularized the late 18th-century European fashion for "Turkish" themes. The plot follows the Spanish nobleman Belmonte and his servant Pedrillo as they attempt to rescue Belmonte's fiancée, Konstanze, and her maid, Blonde, from the seraglio (harem) of Pasha Selim in Turkey. Though their escape plan is discovered, the Pasha displays unexpected magnanimity by releasing the lovers, refusing to resort to violence. The opera is celebrated for its demanding vocal roles, its lively "Janissary" percussion, and its characterization of Osmin, the comical but threatening palace overseer.

## you gotta know

- **Konstanze** is the noble Spanish captive who remains fiercely faithful to Belmonte, rejecting the Pasha's romantic advances despite his threats of torture.
- **Belmonte** is the young Spanish nobleman who travels to Turkey disguised as an architect to rescue his captured fiancée.
- **Osmin** is the hot-tempered, xenophobic overseer of Pasha Selim's palace who guards the harem and sings deep, bombastic bass arias detailing his plans to execute foreigners.
- **Pasha Selim** is the Turkish ruler who bought the captives; unusually for an opera character, his role is entirely spoken, and he ultimately grants the lovers their freedom to show that mercy is superior to revenge.
- **Martern aller Arten** (*Tortures of all kinds*) is Konstanze's monumental Act II coloratura aria, a twenty-minute showpiece featuring a long orchestral introduction with four solo instruments.
- **Solche hergelauf'ne Laffen** (*Such run-about puppies*) is Osmin's Act I aria in which he vents his anger at Pedrillo, utilizing exceptionally low bass notes.
- **Janissary Music** is utilized throughout the score, incorporating triangles, cymbals, and bass drums to imitate the traditional military music of the Ottoman Empire.
