# Mozart last three symphonies

## summary

Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart's final three symphonies—No. 39 in E-flat major (K. 543), No. 40 in G minor (K. 550), and No. 41 in C major (K. 551, "Jupiter")—represent the absolute peak of the Classical symphony. Composed in rapid succession in less than three months during the summer of 1788, these works display a dramatic range and structural complexity that transformed the symphonic genre. There is no historical record of whether Mozart ever heard these symphonies performed, as he composed them during a period of financial hardship when his subscription concerts were failing.

## you gotta know

- **The summer of 1788** saw Mozart complete Symphony No. 39 on June 26, No. 40 on July 25, and No. 41 on August 10, a remarkable feat of rapid composition.
- **Symphony No. 41 in C major, K. 551** is universally known by its nickname **Jupiter**, likely coined by the publisher Johann Baptist Cramer to evoke the symphony's grand, godlike proportions.
- **Five-voice coda** of the *Jupiter* Symphony's fourth movement is one of the greatest contrapuntal achievements in music history, in which Mozart combines five separate melodic themes simultaneously in double counterpoint.
- **Symphony No. 40 in G minor, K. 550** is one of only two minor-key symphonies he wrote, often referred to as the "Great G minor" (to distinguish it from No. 25). Its restless, driving opening theme in the violins is one of the most famous in the classical repertoire.
- **Symphony No. 39 in E-flat major, K. 543** is a warm and lyrical work that opens with a grand, slow introduction and is notable for completely omitting oboes, replacing their voice with clarinets.
