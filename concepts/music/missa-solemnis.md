# Missa solemnis

## summary

Ludwig van Beethoven's *Missa solemnis in D major*, Op. 123 (1823) is a monumental setting of the Catholic Mass ordinary. Composed during his late period alongside the Ninth Symphony, it represents one of his most profound, complex, and demanding compositions. Beethoven intended it for the installation of his patron and pupil, Archduke Rudolf of Austria, as the Archbishop of Olomouc, though its massive scale and length delayed its completion past the ceremony. Marked by its intense vocal difficulty, it is a deeply personal and dramatic statement of faith, inscribed by Beethoven with the words: "From the heart—may it go again to the heart."

## you gotta know

- **Archduke Rudolf of Austria** was Beethoven's aristocratic patron, student, and friend to whom the mass is dedicated.
- **Vocal Demands** on the chorus are notoriously high, requiring singers to sustain extremely high pitches at loud volumes for long durations, similar to the vocal challenges of the Ninth Symphony.
- **Benedictus** contains a famous, exceptionally long and expressive violin solo that plays alongside the vocalists, symbolizing the Holy Spirit descending to earth.
- **Agnus Dei** concludes with the prayer **Dona nobis pacem** (*Grant us peace*), which is dramatically interrupted by distant drum rolls and trumpet fanfares, depicting the terrors of war and contrasting them with a prayer for inner and outer peace.
- **Kyrie**, **Gloria**, **Credo**, **Sanctus**, and **Agnus Dei** comprise the five traditional divisions of the Latin Mass ordinary that structure the work.
