# Sonata Form

## summary

*Sonata form* is the most important structural principle in instrumental music since the Classical period, organizing a movement into three main sections: exposition (introducing two contrasting themes), development (fragmenting and recombining them through harmonic exploration), and recapitulation (restating both themes, typically in the home key). It is the architecture underlying symphonies, concertos, chamber music, and solo works for nearly 300 years.

## you gotta know

- The *exposition* presents a first theme in the tonic key and a second theme in a related key (typically dominant or relative major), often with a codetta (closing material).
- A *development* section dismantles the themes, sequences them, modulates through distant keys, and generates tension, culminating in a buildup toward the reprise.
- The *recapitulation* restates both themes, but crucially, both are now in the tonic key, providing harmonic closure; sometimes a *coda* extends the ending.
- First movements of symphonies (e.g., *Beethoven's Fifth*) are the most visible example; fast movements of concertos and chamber works often use it.
- *Haydn*, *Mozart*, and *Beethoven* refined the form during the Classical era; *Brahms* and *Mahler* stretched it far beyond.

## connections

- [[modulation]] — the essential technique for moving between keys in the development and recapitulation.
- [[symphony]] — the form that made sonata form the cornerstone of instrumental music.
- [[tonality]] — the harmonic system (major/minor keys) that gives sonata form its architecture.
- [[counterpoint]] — often woven into development sections for textural richness.
