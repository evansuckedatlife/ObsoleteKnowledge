# Leitmotif

## summary

A *leitmotif* (German: "leading motif") is a recurring melodic or harmonic phrase associated with a particular character, idea, emotion, or event in a dramatic work, notably opera. When that element reappears, so does its motif, creating symbolic continuity and allowing composers to comment on the action through subtle musical references.

## you gotta know

- *Wagner* systematically developed the leitmotif system in his operas, particularly the *Ring Cycle* (*Das Rheingold*, *Die Walküre*, *Siegfried*, *Götterdämmerung*), assigning distinct motifs to gods, heroes, and concepts (love, power, death).
- A leitmotif remains recognizable even when transposed, inverted, fragmented, or combined with other motifs, allowing for psychological and dramatic commentary.
- *Example:* the *Eros* motif in *Tristan und Isolde* represents passion and yearning, recurring and evolving as the characters' fates unfold.
- Leitmotifs allow a composer to convey character and story without explicit sung text, making them powerful in instrumental interludes or overtures.
- Modern film scoring (John Williams, Hans Zimmer) inherited the leitmotif technique, assigning themes to main characters and story arcs.

## connections

- [[wagner]] — the composer who perfected and systematized the leitmotif technique.
- [[opera]] — the dramatic form most suited to leitmotif development.
- [[motif]] — the shorter unit of which a leitmotif is a specific, narrative application.
- [[ring-cycle]] — the supreme example of leitmotif orchestration across a multi-opera sequence.
