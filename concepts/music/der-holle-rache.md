# Der Hölle Rache

## summary

*Der Hölle Rache kocht in meinem Herzen* (*Hell's vengeance boils in my heart*) is the infamous coloratura soprano aria sung by the Queen of the Night in Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart's *The Magic Flute*. Occurring in Act II when the Queen commands her daughter Pamina to murder Sarastro, this aria has become legendary for its fiendish technical demands—piercing high notes, rapid-fire scales, and treacherous leaps that test the limits of the soprano voice. The aria perfectly captures the Queen's supernatural rage and malevolence, transforming her into an operatic archetype of vengeful fury.

## you gotta know

- Composed by *Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart* for soprano voice, demanding a vocal range extending into the highest registers and exceptional technical facility.
- The aria features two F6s (the highest note written by Mozart for opera), requiring the soprano to sustain and project notes at the absolute upper limit of the soprano range.
- The Queen's rage at being thwarted by Sarastro—who has taken her daughter Pamina—drives an aria filled with lightning-fast coloratura passages and accusatory outbursts.
- The aria opens with the words *Der Hölle Rache kocht in meinem Herzen* before erupting into the famous high note passage, a structural and dramatic pivot point.
- Mozart uses orchestration to underscore the Queen's supernatural nature, employing rapid violins, pointed woodwinds, and sparse accompaniment that makes the soprano's voice the undisputed focus.
- This aria is considered one of the most difficult in all of opera; not all sopranos can sing it, and those who do often make it a career-defining role.
- The aria's fame has extended well beyond opera circles; it appears in films, cartoons, and popular culture as the sonic embodiment of operatic dramatics and vocal impossibility.
