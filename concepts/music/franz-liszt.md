# Franz Liszt

## summary

Franz Liszt (1811–1886) was a Hungarian composer, teacher, and the most celebrated piano virtuoso of the 19th century. Renowned for his unparalleled technical brilliance and dramatic showmanship, he generated a frenzy among audiences known as **Lisztomania**, during which fans fought over his discarded gloves and locks of hair. As a composer, Liszt was a key figure of the New German School, inventing the **symphonic poem** (a single-movement programmatic orchestral work) and pioneering the technique of thematic transformation. His keyboard works, including the monumental Sonata in B minor and the *Transcendental Études*, pushed piano technique to its physical limits.

## you gotta know

### virtuosity & compositions

- **Lisztomania** was the intense public hysteria surrounding his concerts in the 1840s, an early precursor to modern celebrity culture.
- **Piano Sonata in B minor, S. 178** is his single-movement masterpiece, celebrated for its structural innovation where a handful of short motifs are transformed to serve as the themes for all the traditional sections of a sonata.
- **Symphonic Poem** is the genre Liszt invented, writing thirteen works (such as *Les Préludes*) to translate literary and philosophical programs into orchestral sound without classical symphonic divisions.
- **La Campanella** (*The Little Bell*) is his notoriously difficult étude in G-sharp minor, adapting the bell-like violin harmonics of Niccolò Paganini's Second Violin Concerto.
- **Hungarian Rhapsodies** is a set of nineteen piano works based on Hungarian folk and Romani melodies, most notably the highly popular and virtuoso **Hungarian Rhapsody No. 2**.
- **Transcendental Études** (*Études d'exécution transcendante*) is a set of twelve extremely challenging studies, including *Mazeppa* and *Feux follets*.

### patronage & religious life

- **Abbé Liszt** was the title he received in 1865 after moving to Rome, taking minor orders in the Catholic Church, and composing sacred choral music.
- **Weimar period** was his mid-career residency as the court director of music, where he championed the works of contemporary composers and mentored pupils from all over Europe for free.

## context

Liszt was a generous patron and friend to many composers, helping to establish the careers of Hector Berlioz, Edvard Grieg, and Camille Saint-Saëns. His personal relationships were highly publicized, including a long affair with the author Countess Marie d'Agoult (with whom he had three children) and Princess Carolyne zu Sayn-Wittgenstein. His daughter Cosima eventually married his close friend and protégé, Richard Wagner, creating a complex musical dynasty.
