# Boris Godunov

## summary

Modest Mussorgsky's *Boris Godunov* (1874) is a monumental Russian opera based on Alexander Pushkin's historical drama and Nikolay Karamzin's history. Set during the chaotic Time of Troubles (1598–1605), the opera tells the story of Tsar Boris Godunov, who takes the Russian throne after the mysterious assassination of the young Tsarevich Dmitry (the rightful heir of Ivan the Terrible). Tormented by guilt, Boris's reign is undermined by a massive famine, boyar conspiracies, and the rise of a False Dmitry—a runaway monk named Grigori who claims to be the escaped prince. Boris is eventually driven to madness and dies, leaving Russia in political turmoil. The work is celebrated for its powerful choral writing, portraying the Russian populace as the true protagonist of the drama.

## you gotta know

- **Boris Godunov** is the Tsar of Russia whose guilt over the murder of the child heir Dmitry leads to his descent into paranoia and final collapse.
- **Grigori Otrepyev** (the False Dmitry) is a young novice monk who learns of Dmitry's murder from his mentor, escapes his monastery, and claims the heir's identity to launch a rebellion supported by Poland.
- **Pimen** is an elderly monk and chronicler whose writing of Russia's history, including the murder of the Tsarevich, inadvertently inspires Grigori to impersonate the prince.
- **Prince Shuisky** is a deceptive boyar who acts as Boris's chief advisor but secretly plots to overthrow him, feeding Boris's paranoia with details of Dmitry's death.
- **The Holy Fool** (*Yurodivy*) is a peasant who speaks truth to power, famously confronting Boris in public and refusing to pray for him because "Tsar Herod" murdered the young prince.
- **Coronation Scene** is a spectacular scene in the Prologue, characterized by the ringing of giant Moscow cathedral bells and Boris's solemn, foreboding prayer as he is crowned.
- **Clock Scene** is Boris's Act II monologue in which he hallucinates the ghost of the murdered Tsarevich, begging God for mercy while a chiming clock drives him mad.
- **Death of Boris** occurs in Act IV, where he collapses during a boyar council meeting after warning his young son Fyodor to protect the state, accompanied by a tolling bell and mourning monks.
