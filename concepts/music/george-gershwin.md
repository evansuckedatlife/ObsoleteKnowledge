# George Gershwin

## summary

George Gershwin (1898–1937) was a monumental American composer and pianist who successfully bridged the gap between popular Tin Pan Alley song and classical symphonic music. Working in close partnership with his lyricist brother, Ira Gershwin, he composed dozens of Broadway hit songs and musicals before achieving classical acclaim. His masterpiece, *Rhapsody in Blue* (1924), integrated jazz rhythms and blues inflections into a concertante work for piano and jazz band, establishing a unique American symphonic voice. His ambitious "folk opera," *Porgy and Bess* (1935), became a cornerstone of American theater, giving birth to standards like "Summertime." Gershwin's brilliant career was tragically cut short when he died of a brain tumor at the age of 38.

## you gotta know

### major symphonic & theatrical works

- **Rhapsody in Blue** (1924) is a concertante work for piano and jazz band (later orchestrated by Ferde Grofé), famous for its opening **seventeen-note clarinet glissando** performed by Ross Gorman at the premiere.
- **Porgy and Bess** (1935) is his landmark American opera set in the fictional tenement of Catfish Row, Charleston, depicting the lives of Black Americans. It contains famous numbers like **Summertime** and *I Got Plenty o' Nuttin'*.
- **An American in Paris** (1928) is a programmatic symphonic poem depicting a homesick American walking through Paris, famous for incorporating literal **French taxi horns** into the orchestra's percussion section.
- **Piano Concerto in F** (1925) is a three-movement classical concerto commissioned by Walter Damrosch, which integrates blues harmonies and the Charleston dance rhythm.

### popular songs & musicals

- **Ira Gershwin** was his older brother and lyricist, with whom he wrote classic American songbook standards like *I Got Rhythm*, *Embraceable You*, and *Someone to Watch Over Me*.
- **Swanee** (1919) was George's first massive commercial success, popularized by singer Al Jolson, which made him a wealthy man and Tin Pan Alley star.
- **Of Thee I Sing** (1931) was a satirical political musical written with Ira, which became the first musical to win the Pulitzer Prize for Drama.

## context

Gershwin was a key figure of the Roaring Twenties, capturing the energy of New York City in his syncopated rhythms. In 1928, he traveled to Europe, where he met prominent composers like Maurice Ravel, Igor Stravinsky, and Alban Berg. Ravel famously refused to teach Gershwin composition, writing to him: "Why should you be a second-rate Ravel when you can be a first-rate Gershwin?" He died in Los Angeles in 1937, shortly after experiencing severe olfactory hallucinations of burning rubber, which were later diagnosed as symptoms of a glioblastoma.
