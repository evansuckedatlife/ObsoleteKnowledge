# Motet

## summary

The motet is a polyphonic vocal composition on a sacred Latin text, originating in the medieval period and reaching its height during the Renaissance (15th–16th centuries). Unlike the mass, which sets fixed liturgical texts, the motet is a flexible form that could celebrate saints, mark feast days, or express personal devotion. It stands as the apex of Renaissance polyphonic craft, where multiple voice-parts interweave in intricate counterpoint.

## you gotta know

- The motet evolved from the organum of medieval cathedrals, where singers added new text and melody above existing liturgical chant; by the Renaissance, it had become a completely original polyphonic composition.
- Motets are almost always **unaccompanied vocal music** (a cappella) set to Latin sacred texts; the text can be a verse from the Bible, a liturgical antiphon, or a poetic meditation on faith.
- Structurally, motets typically have 4–6 voice-parts that often enter one by one in **imitative counterpoint**, each voice stating a melodic idea and other voices echoing or combining with it—a technique perfected in the Renaissance.
- Major Renaissance motet composers include [[josquin-des-prez]] (Franco-Flemish, circa 1450–1521), [[orlando-di-lasso]] (1530–1594, incredibly prolific), and [[giovanni-pierluigi-da-palestrina]] (Italian, 1525–1594).
- **Palestrina's motets** exemplify the clarity and flowing counterpoint that defined late Renaissance style; his work was seen as the model for liturgically correct polyphonic music and influenced centuries of church music.
- The motet was the primary form for ambitious sacred polyphonic composition before the Baroque era elevated the mass and the cantata.
