# Qawwali

## summary

*Qawwali* is a form of Sufi devotional music from South Asia, most strongly associated with Pakistan but also performed in India and diaspora communities worldwide. Male singers (often performing in groups) lead audiences in ecstatic spiritual experiences through elaborate vocal improvisations, poetic devotional texts, and rhythmic hand-clapping; qawwali represents the intersection of music, spirituality, and emotional intensity within Islamic mystical practice.

## you gotta know

- The word derives from an Arabic root meaning "to speak" or "to say"; qawwals are professional singers of sacred poetry, traditionally attached to Sufi shrines and honored as spiritual intermediaries.
- Performances are centered on devotional poetry, often praising the Prophet Muhammad, Sufi saints, or God (*Allah*); texts are repeated, elaborated, and varied across long performances.
- The *harmonium* (hand-pumped keyboard) and *tabla* (drums) accompany the singers; the audience participates through hand-clapping and swaying, creating a collective spiritual trance state (*wajad*).
- Vocal technique emphasizes elaborate ornamentation, microtonal bending, and the ability to sustain notes while navigating complex emotional states; the style shares some features with Indian classical vocal traditions.
- Qawwali is traditionally performed at *dargahs* (Sufi shrine shrines) during religious celebrations or weekly gatherings; live performance is essential, as recorded versions are considered spiritually diminished.
- The tradition nearly disappeared in the mid-20th century due to modernization and religious conservatism, but was revived by legendary singers like Nusrat Fateh Ali Khan, who brought qawwali to global audiences.
- UNESCO designated qawwali as Masterpiece of the Oral and Intangible Heritage of Humanity in 2001.
