# Madrigal

## summary

The madrigal is a secular vocal form that flourished in Italy during the Renaissance (roughly 1530–1630), setting Italian poetry (often sonnets and other lyric verse) to polyphonic music. Distinct from the motet's sacred context and Latin text, madrigals are intimate, expressive works for small ensembles that pair poetic subtlety with harmonic innovation and word-painting—musical illustration of poetic imagery.

## you gotta know

- The madrigal emerged in Italy in the 1530s as a musical response to the renewed interest in the vernacular language and classical poetry fostered by humanism and Petrarchism.
- Madrigals are set for small vocal ensembles (typically 3–6 unaccompanied singers) performing secular poetry, often about love, nature, or myth; the text is the primary driver of the music.
- **Word-painting** (musical illustration of textual meaning) is central: composers match rhythm, melody, and harmony to poetic imagery—for example, ascending lines for "heaven," chromatic descent for "suffering."
- The madrigal evolved from simple chordal settings (early madrigals) to complex polyphonic works (late Renaissance madrigals) where all voices participate equally in a dense web of imitative counterpoint.
- Major madrigal composers include [[orlando-di-lasso]] (Franco-Flemish), [[carlo-gesualdo]] (Neapolitan), and [[luca-marenzio]] (Roman), each bringing distinctive harmonic adventurousness.
- **Gesualdo's madrigals** are famous for radical chromatic harmony—unexpected dissonance and unusual chord progressions that sound almost modern; he was also a murderer and prince, making his music historically colorful.
- The madrigal reached England in the late 16th century, where composers like [[william-byrd]] and [[thomas-tallis]] adapted it to English verse and gave it a slightly different character.
