# Samuel Barber

## summary

Samuel Barber (1910–1981) was an American composer of orchestral, choral, and dramatic music who was one of the most celebrated classical figures of the 20th century. Unlike many of his contemporaries who embraced experimental atonality or avant-garde systems, Barber remained committed to a deeply expressive, lyrical, and neo-Romantic musical language. His legendary *Adagio for Strings* has become a universal anthem for grief and public mourning, representing the pinnacle of his emotional intensity.

## you gotta know

- **Adagio for Strings** (1936) is his signature work, originally the slow second movement of his *String Quartet, Op. 11*, which was arranged for string orchestra and premiered under conductor **Arturo Toscanini**; it has since become the definitive classical elegy, played at memorials and in films like *Platoon*.
- **Knoxville: Summer of 1915** (1947) is a lush, nostalgic work for soprano and orchestra that sets a prose poem by **James Agee** depicting a peaceful, warm summer evening in Tennessee.
- **Hermit Songs** (1953) is a cycle of ten songs setting marginalia and poems written by anonymous Irish monks between the 8th and 13th centuries, famously premiered by soprano **Leontyne Price** with Barber at the piano.
- **Violin Concerto, Op. 14** (1939) is a brilliant three-movement concerto whose first two movements are intensely lyrical, while the final movement is a breakneck, technically grueling *presto in moto perpetuo* (perpetual motion) section.
- **Vanessa** (1958) is his highly successful first opera, featuring a libretto written by his lifelong partner **Gian Carlo Menotti**, which won the Pulitzer Prize for Music.
- **Antony and Cleopatra** (1966) is an opera commissioned for the grand opening of the new Metropolitan Opera House at Lincoln Center; the premiere was a disastrous, over-produced failure that sent Barber into a deep depression and creative isolation.
