# Mozart piano concertos

## summary

Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart composed 27 numbered piano concertos between 1767 and 1791, creating what is widely regarded as one of the greatest bodies of work in the concerto genre. Mozart established the Classical concerto structure as a dramatic dialogue between the soloist and the orchestra, moving away from simple soloist accompaniment. He composed most of these works to perform himself at his subscription concerts in Vienna. Major highlights include the stormy No. 20 in D minor (K. 466) and the lyrical No. 21 in C major (K. 467).

## you gotta know

- **Concertos 1–4** are childhood arrangements of keyboard movements by other composers (such as C.P.E. Bach), meaning only 23 are entirely original.
- **Piano Concerto No. 21 in C major, K. 467** is celebrated for its lyrical, dreamlike second-movement *Andante*, which became globally famous after its use in the 1967 Swedish film *Elvira Madigan*.
- **Piano Concerto No. 20 in D minor, K. 466** is the first of only two minor-key concertos Mozart wrote. It is a highly dramatic and turbulent work that was a favorite of Ludwig van Beethoven, who wrote the most frequently performed cadenzas for its outer movements.
- **Piano Concerto No. 24 in C minor, K. 491** is the other minor-key concerto, featuring the largest orchestra of any of his concertos. Upon hearing it, Beethoven reportedly remarked to a colleague that they would never be able to compose anything so grand.
- **Piano Concerto No. 9 in E-flat major, K. 271** is nicknamed the **Jeunehomme** (properly written as *Jenamy*, after the French virtuoso Victoire Jenamy). It was a breakthrough work in which the piano boldly enters in the second measure of the piece, defying the classical convention of a long opening orchestral exposition.
