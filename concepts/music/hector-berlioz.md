# Hector Berlioz

## summary

Hector Berlioz (1803–1869) was a French Romantic composer, conductor, and critic who made monumental contributions to modern orchestration and program music. Unlike most composers of his era, Berlioz did not play the piano, instead playing the flute and guitar. His most famous work is the *Symphonie fantastique* (1830), a radical program symphony depicting an artist's drug-induced hallucinations about his beloved. Berlioz was a literary-minded composer, frequently basing works on Shakespeare, Byron, and Virgil, and his influential treatise on instrumentation remains a classic study of the orchestra.

## you gotta know

- **Symphonie fantastique, Op. 14** is his five-movement masterpiece, which depicts an artist poisoning himself with opium out of unrequited love, featuring the famous movements "March to the Scaffold" and "Dream of a Witches' Sabbath."
- **Idée fixe** (*Fixed idea*) is the term Berlioz coined for the recurring melody that represents the beloved woman, which appears in various guises in all five movements of the *Symphonie fantastique*.
- **Harriet Smithson** was the Irish actress whom Berlioz saw playing Ophelia in Paris, sparking the obsessive infatuation that inspired the *Symphonie fantastique*; they later married, though the union was unhappy.
- **Treatise on Instrumentation** (*Grand traité d'instrumentation et d'orchestration modernes*) is his 1844 book that codified modern orchestral writing, later updated by Richard Strauss.
- **Harold in Italy, Op. 16** is a symphonic work with a prominent solo viola part, based on Lord Byron's *Childe Harold's Pilgrimage*, commissioned by Niccolò Paganini.
- **Dies irae** is the medieval Gregorian chant for the dead, which Berlioz famously quotes in the tubas and bassoons during the witches' sabbath finale to signify death and damnation.
