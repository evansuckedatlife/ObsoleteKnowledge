# Other Mozart symphonies

## summary

Before composing his final three masterpieces in 1788, Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart wrote dozens of symphonies (officially numbered 1 to 38, though some are spurious and others exist outside the numbering). These works track his growth from a child prodigy writing Italian-style symphonies to a mature artist in Salzburg and Vienna. Among the most celebrated are his stormy "Little" G minor Symphony (No. 25) and his mature Viennese symphonies named after the cities of Prague (No. 38), Linz (No. 36), and Haffner (No. 35).

## you gotta know

- **Symphony No. 25 in G minor, K. 183** is known as the "Little" G minor. Written when he was 17 under the influence of the *Sturm und Drang* (Storm and Stress) movement, its syncopated opening horn blasts are famous for scoring the opening sequence of the film *Amadeus*.
- **Symphony No. 38 in D major, K. 504** is nicknamed the **Prague** to honor the city where it premiered in 1787. Unusually for a late classical symphony, it has only three movements, completely omitting the standard minuet and trio.
- **Symphony No. 36 in C major, K. 425** is called the **Linz** because Mozart composed it in just four days during a stopover in the town of Linz to prepare for an unexpected concert. It is his first symphony to feature a slow opening introduction.
- **Symphony No. 35 in D major, K. 385** is the **Haffner**, commissioned by the Salzburg Haffner family to celebrate Sigmund Haffner's ennoblement.
- **Symphony No. 29 in A major, K. 201** is an elegant chamber-like symphony written in Salzburg in 1774, marked by a soaring, octave-leaping opening theme in the violins.
- **Symphony No. 31 in D major, K. 297** is the **Paris**, composed during a job-seeking trip to the French capital in 1778, notable for its large orchestra and being the first time Mozart incorporated clarinets in a symphony.
