# Symphony No. 5 in C minor

## summary

Ludwig van Beethoven's *Symphony No. 5 in C minor*, Op. 67 (1808), is perhaps the most famous work in the classical music repertoire. It is universally recognized by its dramatic four-note opening motif (three short notes followed by one long note), which repeats and builds throughout the entire first movement. The symphony traces a psychological journey from the dark, turbulent key of C minor to a triumphant, brass-heavy C major in the finale. Premiering at a marathon concert in Vienna in December 1808, the work established Beethoven as a revolutionary figure who expanded the emotional and structural limits of the symphonic form.

## you gotta know

### musical elements & structure

- **Opening Motif** consists of a short-short-short-long pattern (G-G-G-E-flat in the first statement) played fortissimo in unison by the strings and clarinets.
- **Fate Knocking at the Door** is a famous description of the opening motif attributed to Beethoven by his secretary Anton Schindler, though this anecdote is widely doubted by modern musicologists.
- **First Movement** (*Allegro con brio*) is written in sonata-allegro form, entirely generated from the opening four-note cells, featuring a sudden, expressive solo oboe cadenza in the recapitulation.
- **Third Movement** is a Scherzo in C minor that features a dramatic horn call and a trio with fugal cellos, ending with a soft, mysterious bridge led by a tapping timpani.
- **Attacca transition** connects the third movement directly to the fourth without pause, resolving the tension into C major.
- **Fourth Movement** (*Allegro*) is a triumphant march that expands the Classical orchestra by introducing the **piccolo**, **contrabassoon**, and **trombones** into a symphony for the first time.

## context

During World War II, the BBC popularized the opening motif of the Fifth Symphony as a symbol of Allied resistance and victory. The rhythm of three short beats and one long beat matches the Morse code for the letter **V** (dot-dot-dot-dash), representing "V for Victory." In 1977, the first movement was recorded and included on the Voyager Golden Record, sent into deep space to represent human achievement.
