# Symphony No. 9 in D minor

## summary

Ludwig van Beethoven's *Symphony No. 9 in D minor*, Op. 125 (1824), also known as the **Choral Symphony**, is one of the supreme achievements in human culture. It was the first symphony by a major composer to incorporate vocal soloists and a chorus, which enter in the monumental fourth movement to sing a setting of Friedrich Schiller's poem *An die Freude* (Ode to Joy). Completed when Beethoven was almost entirely deaf, the symphony's scale, complexity, and humanistic message of universal brotherhood transformed the symphonic genre. Its premiere in Vienna was met with ecstatic applause, requiring the soloist Caroline Unger to turn the deaf composer around to witness the audience's standing ovation.

## you gotta know

### movements & musical structure

- **First Movement** (*Allegro ma non troppo*) opens quietly with open fifths (A and E) played tremolo in the strings, sounding like an orchestra tuning up, before exploding into a thunderous D-minor main theme.
- **Second Movement** (*Molto vivace*) is a fast-paced Scherzo, placed unusually before the slow movement, which is famous for its sudden, dramatic solo timpani octaves.
- **Third Movement** (*Adagio molto e cantabile*) is a deeply lyrical, slow movement structured as a double theme and variations.
- **Fourth Movement** (*Presto*) opens with a harsh dissonance (the "horror fanfar") followed by instrumental recitatives in the cellos and basses that reject the themes of the previous three movements, before introducing the *Ode to Joy* melody.

### vocal entry & lyrics

- **O Freunde, nicht diese Töne!** (*O friends, not these tones!*) are the opening baritone solo words, written by Beethoven himself to bridge the instrumental music to the vocal setting of Schiller's poem.
- **Vocal Quartet & Chorus** consists of four soloists (soprano, alto, tenor, bass) and a full mixed chorus. The tenor soloist sings a famous military-style march version of the theme (*Alla marcia*) accompanied by triangle, cymbals, and bass drum.

## context

The Ninth Symphony stands as a universal symbol of hope and freedom. Its melody was adapted into the official anthem of the European Union, and it was famously performed in 1989 to celebrate the fall of the Berlin Wall, where conductor Leonard Bernstein substituted the word *Freiheit* (Freedom) for *Freude* (Joy). In Japan, performing the "Daiku" (Great Ninth) is an annual year-end tradition. The work influenced generations of Romantic composers, particularly Gustav Mahler and Anton Bruckner, who inherited both the model of the choral finale and the superstitious "curse of the ninth" (the fear of dying after completing a ninth symphony).
