# Wellington's Victory

## summary

Ludwig van Beethoven's *Wellington's Victory, or the Battle of Vitoria*, Op. 91 (1813), also known as the **Battle Symphony**, is a highly programmatic orchestral work. It was composed to celebrate the British Duke of Wellington's victory over Napoleon's French forces at the Battle of Vitoria in Spain. Although regarded by music critics and even Beethoven himself as a popular novelty rather than serious art, it was one of the most lucrative and popular successes of his lifetime. The piece is notable for its literal sound effects representing battle and its usage of patriotic folk tunes to signify the opposing armies.

## you gotta know

- **Battle of Vitoria** (1813) in Spain is the battle depicted in the work, in which Arthur Wellesley defeated Joseph Bonaparte; it specifically does *not* depict the Battle of Waterloo.
- **Panharmonicon** was the mechanical automated orchestra invented by **Johann Nepomuk Mälzel** (the inventor of the metronome) for which Beethoven originally wrote the piece, before adapting it for live orchestra.
- **Patriotic Melodies** are used to represent the nations: **Rule, Britannia!** represents the British, **Marlbrough s'en va-t-en guerre** (popularly known as *For He's a Jolly Good Fellow*) represents the French, and the work concludes with a fugal fantasy on **God Save the King**.
- **Special Effects** include literal artillery simulation, calling for percussionists to fire muskets offstage and strike large bass drums on opposite sides of the stage to represent British and French cannon fire.
- **Financial Windfall** from the piece was massive, outshining the public reception of his Seventh Symphony, which premiered at the same concert in Vienna in December 1813.
