# Jazz

## summary

Jazz is an American musical genre born in early 20th-century New Orleans, fusing African American blues, ragtime, and European harmonic traditions with a culture of collective improvisation. Centered on swing rhythm and syncopation, it became the defining popular music of the mid-century and one of America's most profound cultural exports. Jazz transformed music performance itself—turning the soloist's spontaneous invention into an art form equal to composed melody.

## you gotta know

### origins and early history

- Jazz emerged in New Orleans around 1900, where Caribbean, African, and European musical traditions collided in a port city of unusual cultural hybridity; early practitioners adapted European instruments (trumpet, trombone, clarinet) and classical harmonic structures to play blues and ragtime idioms.
- The birth of jazz coincides with the end of American slavery's legal structures and the rise of Black autonomous cultural institutions: dance halls, saloons, and brass bands where self-taught and formally trained musicians improvised together.
- The *Original Dixieland Jazz Band*, a white ensemble, made the first jazz recordings in 1917, but Black New Orleans musicians—including *Buddy Bolden* (cornet), *Jelly Roll Morton* (piano), and *King Oliver* (cornet)—had already established the style in live performance.

### core features

- **Improvisation** is the essence: unlike classical music, jazz is partly composed and partly invented in real time, with musicians responding to harmonic changes and one another's ideas.
- **Syncopation** and **swing rhythm**—an off-beat accent and elasticity that defies strict metronomic time—are jazz's rhythmic signature, derived from African polyrhythm and blues phrasing.
- **Blues scale and blue notes** (the flatted 3rd and 7th degrees) permeate jazz tonality and give the music its characteristic "bent" quality, distinct from European diatonic scales.
- The typical jazz **ensemble** was small: a "front line" (trumpet, trombone, clarinet) played melodic ideas over a rhythm section (piano, upright bass, drums), with all voices improvising simultaneously in a technique called *New Orleans polyphony*.

### major eras and styles

- **Early jazz** (1900–1920s): collective improvisation, New Orleans style, focus on blues and ragtime idioms; [[jelly-roll-morton]] was a master composer in this era.
- **Swing era** (1930s–1940s): big bands with arranged harmonies provided frameworks for soloists; [[duke-ellington]] and [[count-basie]] led orchestras that swung; [[louis-armstrong]] rose to fame as a trumpet soloist and vocalist.
- **Bebop** (1940s): a revolutionary style led by [[charlie-parker]], [[dizzy-gillespie]], and [[thelonious-monk]], with faster tempos, complex chord changes, and asymmetrical melodic lines that shifted focus from ensemble swing to individual virtuosity.
- **Cool jazz** (1950s): a reaction to bebop's intensity, emphasizing cerebral arrangement and modal harmony; [[miles-davis]] recorded *Kind of Blue* (1959), a landmark [[modal-jazz]] album that influenced generations.
- **Free jazz** and **modal jazz** (1960s onward): [[john-coltrane]] and [[ornette-coleman]] pushed past fixed chord changes; the genre fragmented into countless substyles—fusion, soul jazz, avant-garde.

### influence and legacy

- Jazz reshaped Western music itself: its emphasis on individual voice and spontaneous dialogue influenced classical composers ([[george-gershwin]], [[aaron-copland]]), popular song, and global music traditions.
- It became a symbol of American freedom and creativity during the Cold War, despite (and because of) its roots in Black struggle; jazz tours were sponsored as cultural diplomacy.
- Today, jazz is a global music, with thriving scenes in Europe, Japan, Brazil, and beyond; it is taught in universities as both history and performance practice, a canonized high art.

## context

Jazz stands unique in 20th-century culture as a democratizing force: a music created by people denied access to formal conservatory training, yet so powerful it redefined how Western music is made. Unlike the composer-performer divide of classical music (where a Beethoven writes, and conductors/soloists execute), jazz insisted on real-time creation—the performer as a co-composer. This challenged the bourgeois notion that true art requires advance composition and rigid rehearsal, and it emerged from the Blues tradition, itself a repository of African American resilience and vernacular wisdom. The swing rhythm, the blue note, the soloist's primacy—these features represent a different conception of musical beauty: one that values spontaneity, communal response, and the unique voice of the individual player. Jazz also became a vehicle for social commentary: in an era of segregation, it crossed racial lines through its very practice, and its greatest figures (Armstrong, Coltrane, Gillespie) used their art as a form of human dignity in the face of systemic racism.

## connections

- [[louis-armstrong]] — the first great jazz soloist, whose trumpet and vocals defined the genre's expressive range.
- [[duke-ellington]] — composer-bandleader who elevated jazz composition to symphonic sophistication.
- [[charlie-parker]] — the bebop revolutionary whose harmonic innovations reshaped the entire idiom.
- [[miles-davis]] — the modal jazz pioneer and electric fusion visionary; constantly reinvented.
- [[the-jazz-age]] — the cultural epoch that jazz music defined; synonymous with 1920s modernity and speed.
- [[blues]] — the harmonic and emotional root of jazz; the 12-bar blues form is jazz's foundational harmonic template.
- [[improvisation]] — the core practice that distinguishes jazz from all prior Western art music.
- [[syncopation]] — the rhythmic offset that gives jazz its swing and energy.
- [[harlem-renaissance]] — the Black cultural awakening of the 1920s–30s, centered on jazz, literature, and visual art.
- [[george-gershwin]] — a classical composer who synthesized jazz idioms in concert music like *Rhapsody in Blue*.
- [[raga]] — jazz shares with Indian classical music an emphasis on improvisation within a harmonic framework.
