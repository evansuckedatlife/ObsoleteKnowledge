# Arabic Maqam

## summary

The *maqam* is a melodic system fundamental to Arabic classical music, analogous to the Indian raga. Each maqam is a framework of notes, characteristic phrases, and emotional associations within which musicians improvise and perform composed pieces. With its microtonal intervals (quarter-tones unavailable in Western notation), ornamented phrasing, and emotional intensity, maqam music expresses the aesthetic values of the Arab world across the Levant, Egypt, Iraq, and the Gulf.

## you gotta know

- The maqam system includes numerous named modes (over 70 major maqams exist, with hundreds of variants), each associated with emotional states and seasons or times of day.
- Intervals smaller than the Western semitone—*quarter-tones*—are central; the same note can be played flat or natural, creating a tonal palette foreign to Western equal temperament.
- The *oud* (lute) and *qanun* (zither) are the primary maqam instruments; the human voice is equally valued, and singers must master the microtonal inflections and ornamentation integral to the tradition.
- Performances typically begin with a free-rhythm improvisation (*taqsim*), progress to a slower composed piece, and culminate in rapid rhythmic sections; the structure mirrors Hindustani *alap-jor-jhala* progression.
- The maqam tradition was significantly documented and standardized during the Ottoman Empire; the *Bagdad School* of the 19th century produced notable systematizations and famous composers like *Mulla Othman*.
- Urban court music, folk traditions, and religious music all employ the maqam system, making it a unifying musical language across social contexts.
- Modern maqam players like Oum Kulthum became icons; the tradition remains vital in contemporary Arab music and cinema.
