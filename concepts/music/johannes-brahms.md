# Johannes Brahms

## summary

Johannes Brahms (1833–1897) was a German composer, pianist, and conductor who was one of the leading musicians of the Romantic period. Often grouped with Bach and Beethoven as one of the "Three Bs" of classical music, Brahms was a musical traditionalist. He championed Classical structures, sonata form, and strict counterpoint, placing himself in opposition to the progressive "New German School" of Franz Liszt and Richard Wagner. Highly self-critical, Brahms destroyed many of his early drafts and spent over twenty years perfecting his First Symphony, which was immediately hailed as a masterpiece.

## you gotta know

- **Symphony No. 1 in C minor, Op. 68** was famously dubbed **"Beethoven's Tenth"** by the conductor Hans von Bülow, referencing both the shadow of Beethoven under which Brahms composed and the similarity of the finale's grand major-key theme to the *Ode to Joy*.
- **A German Requiem** (*Ein deutsches Requiem*, Op. 45) is a massive sacred choral work setting German Lutheran biblical texts rather than the traditional Latin Catholic liturgy, written following the deaths of his mother and his mentor Robert Schumann.
- **Academic Festival Overture, Op. 80** is a boisterous concert overture composed as a thank-you to the University of Breslau for awarding him an honorary doctorate, which weaves together popular 19th-century student drinking songs, concluding with *Gaudeamus igitur*.
- **Hungarian Dances** is a highly popular set of 21 dance tunes based on Hungarian Romani melodies, originally written for piano four-hands, the most famous being No. 5 in F-sharp minor.
- **Clara Schumann** was the widow of Robert Schumann, with whom Brahms maintained a lifelong, deeply affectionate relationship, acting as her confidant and assistant.
- **Lullaby** (*Wiegenlied*, Op. 49, No. 4) is his universally recognized cradle song, composed to celebrate the birth of a friend's child.
