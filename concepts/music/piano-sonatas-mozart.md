# Mozart piano sonatas

## summary

Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart composed 18 numbered piano sonatas between 1774 and 1789. Written primarily for the fortepiano rather than the harpsichord, these works track the evolution of Classical keyboard style and Mozart's personal development. They range from pedagogical pieces written for his students to complex concert works. The most famous of these is the Sonata No. 11 in A major (K. 331), celebrated for its final "Turkish Rondo" movement, and the Sonata No. 16 in C major (K. 545), known as the *Sonata facile* due to its beginner-friendly layout.

## you gotta know

- **Piano Sonata No. 11 in A major, K. 331** is famous for its third-movement finale, the **Rondo alla Turca** (Turkish March), which mimics the clanging percussion of Ottoman Janissary music. Unusually, its first movement is a theme and variations rather than a sonata-allegro movement.
- **Piano Sonata No. 16 in C major, K. 545** was entered in Mozart's own catalog as "for beginners" (*eine kleine Klaviersonata für Anfänger*) and is commonly referred to as the *Sonata facile* or *Simple Sonata*.
- **Piano Sonata No. 14 in C minor, K. 457** is one of only two minor-key sonatas Mozart wrote. It is a dark, dramatic, and highly expressive work that prefigured Romanticism and directly influenced Beethoven's *Pathétique* Sonata.
- **Fantasia in C minor, K. 475** is a structurally free piece that Mozart published alongside Sonata No. 14 as a unified prelude and sonata.
- **Piano Sonata No. 8 in A minor, K. 310** is the other minor-key sonata, composed in Paris in 1778 around the time of his mother's death, characterized by its tragic, driving intensity.
