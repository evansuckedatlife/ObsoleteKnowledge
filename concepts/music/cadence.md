# Cadence

## summary

A *cadence* is a concluding harmonic phrase that signals the end of a musical phrase, section, or entire piece. It is the melodic and harmonic equivalent of punctuation, with different types conveying finality, pause, or continuity, and serving as a fundamental punctuation mark in tonal music.

## you gotta know

- The *authentic cadence* (or perfect cadence) moves from the dominant chord (V) to the tonic (I), the strongest closure; a *half cadence* ends on the dominant, creating pause or question.
- The *plagal cadence* (IV to I) is gentler, sometimes called the "Amen cadence" because of its use in hymns; it feels less conclusive than authentic.
- The *deceptive cadence* moves from V to vi (the relative minor), subverting expectation; widely used for surprise or prolonging tension.
- A *cadence* may be *perfect* (both chords in root position, strongest) or *imperfect*, affecting its sense of finality.
- Composers use cadences to shape the listener's sense of phrasing, article, and motion—a key tonal navigation tool.

## connections

- [[harmony]] — cadences are the fundamental units of harmonic grammar.
- [[chord-progression]] — cadences are standardized progressions used to end phrases.
- [[tonality]] — the major/minor system in which cadences acquire their meaning and strength.
- [[sonata-form]] — cadences mark structural boundaries in expositions, developments, and recapitulations.
