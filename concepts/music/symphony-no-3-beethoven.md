# Symphony No. 3 in E-flat major

## summary

Ludwig van Beethoven's *Symphony No. 3 in E-flat major*, Op. 55 (1804), widely known as the **Eroica**, is one of the most pivotal works in music history. It marks the boundary between the Classical and Romantic eras, twice as long as previous symphonies and far more emotionally intense. Beethoven originally dedicated the symphony to Napoleon Bonaparte, whom he admired as a hero of the French Revolution's democratic ideals. However, upon hearing that Napoleon had crowned himself Emperor of the French, a furious Beethoven scratched out the dedication on the title page so violently that he tore the paper. When published, it was retitled *Sinfonia Eroica* ("Heroic Symphony"), re-dedicated "to celebrate the memory of a great man."

## you gotta know

### movements & musical features

- **First Movement** (*Allegro con brio*) begins with two abrupt, fortissimo chords in E-flat major, followed by a cello-led main theme that unexpectedly dips to a chromatic C-sharp, creating harmonic tension.
- **Horn False Entry** is a famous moment in the first movement's transition to the recapitulation, where the horn solo enters with the main theme four measures before the rest of the orchestra, a detail that led Beethoven's pupil Ferdinand Ries to mistakenly criticize the player for miscounting.
- **Second Movement** (*Marcia funebre*) is a solemn, tragic Funeral March in C minor, featuring a sobbing oboe melody and a dramatic fugal section.
- **Third Movement** (*Scherzo: Allegro vivace*) is a rapid, energetic movement that features a trio with three French horns playing hunting calls.
- **Fourth Movement** (*Finale*) is a theme and variations based on a melody from Beethoven's own 1801 ballet *The Creatures of Prometheus*. The movement begins by exposing only the bass line of the theme before building the full melody.

## context

*Eroica* was a radical departure from the chamber-like symmetry of Haydn and Mozart, introducing a massive development section and a programmatic narrative of heroic struggle, death, and rebirth. It was first performed publicly at the Theater an der Wien in 1805. The Funeral March was performed at the memorial ceremonies of many public figures, including the state funeral of Felix Mendelssohn, and was broadcast by German radio to announce the defeat at the Battle of Stalingrad.
