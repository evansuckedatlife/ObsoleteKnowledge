# Counterpoint

## summary

*Counterpoint* is the art of combining two or more independent melodic lines that sound simultaneously, each with its own rhythmic and harmonic character. The lines move in relation to each other (parallel, contrary, or oblique motion) while maintaining harmonic unity, creating texture and complexity that defines much of Western classical music.

## you gotta know

- The term comes from the Latin "punctus contra punctum" (point against point), referring to note-against-note composition.
- In *strict* counterpoint, every interval between simultaneous notes follows codified rules (perfect consonances in certain positions, forbidden parallels of fifths and octaves).
- *Free* counterpoint relaxes these rules in favor of musical expressiveness; used in much Baroque and later music.
- *Bach* was the master of counterpoint, particularly in his *Art of Fugue* and *Musical Offering*, which explore extreme contrapuntal techniques.
- *Fugue*, *canon*, and *round* are the primary forms built on contrapuntal principle.
- The technique requires each line to be singable and interesting in its own right, not merely harmonic filler.

## connections

- [[fugue]] — the most sophisticated contrapuntal form, built on strict subject-and-answer imitation.
- [[polyphony]] — the broader texture of multiple simultaneous melodic lines, of which counterpoint is the disciplined subset.
- [[bach]] — the ultimate contrapuntal master; his works are the gold standard for study.
- [[sonata-form]] — uses countrapuntal techniques in its development sections.
- [[canon]] — a form of strict counterpoint where one melody chases itself at fixed intervals.
