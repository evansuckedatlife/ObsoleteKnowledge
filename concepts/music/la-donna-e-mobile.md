## summary

*La donna è mobile* (*Woman is fickle*) is the swaggering tenor aria sung by the Duke of Mantua in Act III of Giuseppe Verdi's opera *Rigoletto*. Callous and cynical, the Duke sings of women's inconstancy while plotting seduction, his carefree melody belying his moral emptiness. This aria became instantly popular upon the opera's 1851 premiere in Venice and remains one of the most frequently performed and recognizable opera melodies, beloved for its infectious tune yet forever associated with a character of supreme villainy and depravity.

## you gotta know

- Composed by *Giuseppe Verdi* for tenor voice; the aria's irresistible melody has made it one of the most performed and popular opera arias of all time.
- The Duke of Mantua is the opera's antagonist—dissolute, heartless, and utterly indifferent to the suffering he causes, especially to Rigoletto's daughter Gilda, whom he has seduced and abandoned.
- The aria is structured as a light, folk-like melody in major key, almost cheerful in its simplicity, creating a dark irony: such evil is expressed in such an innocent-sounding tune.
- *La donna è mobile* translates to "Woman is fickle" or "Woman is changeable," the Duke's misogynistic philosophy sung without a shred of self-awareness or irony.
- The melody is so catchy and ubiquitous that it has entered popular culture, appearing in films, cartoons, and street performances worldwide, often divorced from its operatic context.
- Verdi reportedly kept the aria's manuscript locked away until the opera's premiere to prevent it from being leaked or performed prematurely, such was its potential as a hit.
- The aria's popularity contrasts sharply with the opera's dark tragic ending, wherein Rigoletto's plot to avenge his daughter results in unexpected betrayal and despair.
