# Tonality

## summary

Tonality is the organizational system underlying most Western music from roughly 1650 to 1900, in which all melodic and harmonic material gravitates toward a single pitch center (the *tonic* or home key). Built on the major and minor scales, tonality creates a hierarchy of musical relationships: tension and resolution, harmonic movement and return. It is the language of Bach, Mozart, Beethoven, and most music heard in concert halls today.

## you gotta know

- **Tonality** centers all musical motion on a single pitch (the tonic); the major and minor scales define the pitches that "belong" to a key, and chords are built from these scale degrees.
- The **major scale** (whole-whole-half-whole-whole-whole-half pattern) sounds bright and resolved; the **minor scale** is darker and more ambiguous, with three variants (natural, harmonic, melodic) used for different expressive effects.
- **Key signature** (the sharps or flats at the start of a staff) signals which tonal center and scale the music inhabits; a key signature of no sharps or flats indicates C major or A minor.
- **Harmonic function** — the roles chords play in tonal music: the tonic chord (I) is home, the subdominant (IV) moves away, the dominant (V) creates tension, pulling back to I; this tension-release cycle is the engine of tonal music.
- **Modulation** is the shift from one key to another within a piece; a temporary visit to a new tonal center; it provides structure and surprise.
- Tonality emerged during the Baroque era (1600–1750) as composers adopted the **equal temperament** tuning system, which made it practical to play in all keys on keyboard instruments.
- **Atonality** and the **twelve-tone technique**, pioneered by [[arnold-schoenberg]] in the early 20th century, rejected tonality's hierarchy, treating all twelve chromatic pitches as equal; this marked the end of the common-practice era.
