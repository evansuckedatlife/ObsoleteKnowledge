# Salome

## summary

Richard Strauss's *Salome* (1905) is a shocking, highly influential one-act German opera. The libretto is based on Hedwig Lachmann's German translation of Oscar Wilde's French play *Salomé*, which recounts the biblical story of the stepdaughter of King Herod Antipas. Renowned for its intense psychological realism and avant-garde dissonance, the opera depicts Salome's obsessive, morbid desire for the captive prophet Jokanaan (John the Baptist). When Jokanaan rejects her advances, Salome uses Herod's lustful obsession with her to demand the prophet's head on a silver platter in exchange for performing the seductive "Dance of the Seven Veils." The opera's graphic themes of necrophilia and incest caused severe censorship scandals at its premiere but solidified Strauss's reputation as a leader of modernism.

## you gotta know

- **Salome** is the teenage Judean princess who becomes obsessed with Jokanaan's white skin, black hair, and red lips, culminating in her demanding his execution.
- **Herod Antipas** is the tetrarch of Judea who is plagued by superstitious fears, is married to his brother's widow Herodias, and harbors a incestuous desire for his stepdaughter Salome.
- **Jokanaan** (John the Baptist) is the prophet imprisoned in a dry cistern who repeatedly calls out prophecies denouncing Herodias's sins and refuses to let Salome touch him.
- **Dance of the Seven Veils** is the famous instrumental number in which Salome dances suggestively for Herod, gradually removing seven veils until she stands naked or nearly naked.
- **Narraboth** is a Syrian captain of the guard infatuated with Salome; he commits suicide in front of her when he realizes she has fallen in love with Jokanaan.
- **The executioner** Naaman descends into the cistern and emerges with Jokanaan's severed head, which Salome passionately addresses and kisses on the lips.
- **Herod's command** "Kill that woman!" (*Man töte dieses Weib!*) ends the opera, as his guards crush Salome to death under their shields.
- **Musical style** is marked by a massive orchestra, chromaticism, and the famous dissonant "Salome chord" (a polytonal combination of A-major and F-sharp-major elements) played just before Jokanaan's head is brought out.
