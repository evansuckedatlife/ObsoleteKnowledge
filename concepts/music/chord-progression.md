# Chord Progression

## summary

A chord progression is a sequence of chords arranged to support melody and create harmonic motion in tonal music. It is the backbone of Western musical structure, organizing the movement from tension to resolution and providing the harmonic framework within which melody unfolds. Understanding chord progressions is essential to both composing and analyzing music from the Baroque era to pop and jazz.

## you gotta know

- A **chord** is a combination of three or more pitches sounded simultaneously; the most basic chords are the **triad** (three notes: root, third, fifth) built on each degree of a scale.
- **Chord progressions** are named by Roman numerals indicating their scale-degree position: I (tonic), ii, iii, IV (subdominant), V (dominant), vi, vii°; uppercase = major, lowercase = minor, ° = diminished.
- The **I–IV–V–I** progression is the most fundamental in Western music: it moves from home (I) to subdominant tension (IV), dominant tension (V), and back home; this cycle underlies countless classical, pop, and folk melodies.
- The **I–vi–IV–V** progression (or "vi–IV–I–V") is ubiquitous in popular music and doo-wop; examples include "All You Need Is Love" and countless pop songs.
- The **12-bar blues progression** (I–I–I–I–IV–IV–I–I–V–IV–I–V, with variations) is the harmonic template for blues and jazz; it allows infinite melodic and improvisational variation over a fixed harmonic foundation.
- **Harmonic function** — the role each chord plays: tonic (rest), subdominant (departure), dominant (tension toward resolution); understanding function makes progressions coherent and expressive.
- The **[[circle-of-fifths]]** is a visual map of harmonic relationships; chords a fifth apart are closely related; progressions that follow the circle create natural forward motion.
