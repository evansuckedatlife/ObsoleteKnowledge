# Key Signature

## summary

A *key signature* is a set of sharps or flats written at the beginning of a staff, immediately after the clef, that identifies the home key of a piece and avoids the need to notate accidentals for the diatonic pitches of that key. It is the first visual clue to a piece's tonality and fundamentally shapes how performers perceive melodic and harmonic motion.

## you gotta know

- The *major key signature* specifies how many sharps or flats define that major scale; for example, G major has one sharp (F#), and D major has two (F# and C#).
- The order of sharps (F–C–G–D–A–E–B) and flats (B–E–A–D–G–C–F) is standardized and must be memorized by musicians.
- A *relative minor* shares the same key signature as its major counterpart: A minor has no sharps/flats (same as C major).
- Key signatures eliminate accidentals, making notation cleaner and the scale structure visually clear; a piece in A major would be unreadable without its three sharps.
- The number of sharps or flats is mnemonic: "sharp keys" go up by fifths (C, G, D, A, E, B, F#, C#), and "flat keys" go up by fourths (C, F, Bb, Eb, Ab, Db, Gb, Cb).
