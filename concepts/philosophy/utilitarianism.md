# Utilitarianism

## summary

*Utilitarianism* is a consequentialist ethical theory holding that the morally right action is the one that maximises overall happiness or well-being for the greatest number of people. Founded by *Jeremy Bentham* and refined by *John Stuart Mill* in the 18th and 19th centuries, utilitarianism reduces ethics to a calculus of pleasure and pain, or more broadly, preference satisfaction and flourishing. Utilitarianism is strikingly egalitarian—each person's happiness counts equally—and radically consequentialist—intentions and duties matter only insofar as they produce good outcomes. Though controversial (it can justify harming innocents if aggregate happiness increases), utilitarianism has profoundly influenced moral philosophy, public policy, economics, and contemporary effective altruism.

## you gotta know

### Foundations and Development
- *Jeremy Bentham* (1748–1832) invented utilitarian ethics; argued morality rests on the principle of utility (maximising pleasure, minimising pain).
- *John Stuart Mill* (1806–1873) refined utilitarianism; distinguished higher and lower pleasures (mental pleasures superior to physical).
- *Henry Sidgwick* (1838–1900) systematised utilitarianism and exposed the "dualism of practical reason"—the tension between self-interest and altruism.

### Core Principles
- *Greatest happiness principle*: the right act maximises overall happiness/well-being; evaluate acts by consequences, not motives.
- Impartiality: each person's happiness counts equally; no privileging of oneself or one's tribe.
- *Act* vs. *rule* utilitarianism: act utilitarians maximise utility case-by-case; rule utilitarians follow rules (honesty, justice) that generally maximise utility.

### Criticisms and Variants
- *Demandingness*: utilitarianism seems to require enormous sacrifice (donate all surplus to aid organisations).
- *Integrity and special obligations*: hard to accommodate why you should prioritise your own projects and relationships.
- Modern variants: *preference utilitarianism*, *negative utilitarianism* (minimise suffering), *two-level utilitarianism* (*Hare*).

## context

Utilitarianism emerged during industrialisation and political reform, offering a principle that transcended tradition and prejudice: pursue the greatest happiness for the greatest number. It grounded radical-for-its-time positions (women's suffrage, animal welfare) in a single, secular principle. Utilitarianism became the language of policy analysis and economics (cost-benefit analysis, welfare economics). Its assumption that well-being is the ultimate value remains dominant in contemporary ethics, even among non-utilitarians. Effective altruism, AI alignment research, and global health prioritisation all invoke utilitarian reasoning. Yet utilitarianism's apparent permission to harm the innocent, sacrifice integrity, or impose collectivist demands has generated persistent ethical objections.
