## summary

*Scholasticism* is the medieval Christian intellectual tradition that sought to reconcile Greek philosophy—especially *Aristotle*—with theological doctrine using rigorous logical and dialectical methods. Flourishing in cathedral schools and universities from the 11th to 15th centuries, with luminaries like *Thomas Aquinas* and *Duns Scotus*, scholasticism applied *Aristotelian* logic to defend Christian faith against both external critique and internal logical tension. Rather than blind faith, scholastics argued that reason and revelation are harmonious tools for understanding God and creation. The method—the *scholastic method*—proceeds via *quaestiones* (structured argument and counter-argument) and became the backbone of medieval university education.

## you gotta know

### Foundations and Development
- Emerged in cathedral schools (~11th century) with figures like *Anselm* and *Abelard*; reached maturity in 13th-century universities.
- *Thomas Aquinas* (1225–1274) was the defining scholastic, synthesizing *Aristotle* with Christian theology in the *Summa Theologiae*.
- Relied on the recovery and Latin translation of *Aristotle's* works through Islamic and Jewish scholars, particularly after the Crusades.

### Method and Approach
- The *scholastic method*: pose a question (*quaestio*), present opposing authorities and arguments (*pro et contra*), then resolve via logical analysis.
- *Summa*: a comprehensive treatise organized in this disputational style; the *Summa Theologiae* is the paradigm.
- *Universals*: debated whether universals (e.g., "humanity") exist in reality (*realism*) or only in mind (*nominalism*).

### Key Doctrines and Figures
- *Thomas Aquinas*: synthesized *Aristotle* with theology; argued reason and faith are complementary; developed *natural theology*.
- *Duns Scotus*: emphasized will over intellect and the primacy of individual particulars; critiqued Aquinas on causation.
- *William of Ockham*: nominalist; argued against unnecessary multiplying of entities (*Ockham's razor*).

## context

Scholasticism emerged at a cultural hinge: the medieval West had lost and then recovered *Aristotle*, and Islamic civilization had preserved and advanced his legacy. Scholasticism was not dogmatism but a genuinely exploratory dialogue between reason and revelation. It created the university as an institution, formalized argument, and produced works of extraordinary intellectual refinement. Medieval scholars proved that logic could defend faith without abandoning either. Though Renaissance humanists and Enlightenment thinkers mocked scholastic subtlety, the method's influence persists in legal reasoning, theological disputation, and the PhD seminar. Modern analytic philosophy owes more to scholastic rigor than it often acknowledges.
