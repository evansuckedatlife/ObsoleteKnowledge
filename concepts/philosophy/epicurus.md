# Epicurus

## summary

**Epicurus** (341–270 BCE) was a Greek philosopher who founded an influential school teaching that pleasure is the highest good, but by "pleasure" meant the absence of pain and fear—not hedonistic excess. His ethical system, grounded in atomism and a radically naturalistic worldview, has been systematically misrepresented since antiquity.

## you gotta know

### Life and School
- Born on the island of Samos; spent most of his life in Athens.
- Founded the *Garden*, a philosophical community emphasizing simple living, friendship, and intellectual discussion.
- Unlike Plato's Academy, the Garden welcomed women and slaves as students.
- Wrote over 300 works; most are lost; main source is letters preserved by Diogenes Laërtius.

### Philosophy and Ethics
- *Hedonism*, but redefined: pleasure is not indulgence but the absence of pain (*aponia*) and mental disturbance (*ataraxia*).
- Advocated modest living: bread, water, and simple vegetables; luxury and excess create dependency and anxiety.
- Friendship is the highest good; it combines mutual benefit, loyalty, and freedom from fear.
- Distinguished necessary desires (food, shelter, basic security) from vain desires (wealth, fame, luxury).

### Physics and Cosmology
- Adopted atomism: the universe is atoms and void, governed by mechanical causation.
- Atoms are eternal, indivisible, and infinite; motion and collision are eternal.
- Atoms swerve randomly (the *clinamen*): a small deviation allowing human free will and moral responsibility.
- Matter is not divine; the gods exist but take no interest in human affairs. This removes divine judgment as a source of fear.

### Influence and Misinterpretation
- Called "Epicurean" in antiquity as a slur (meaning hedonist); Christians and Stoics attacked him relentlessly.
- Medieval and Renaissance thinkers equated Epicureanism with gluttony, a grossly unfair stereotype.
- Modern scholarship recognizes him as an ascetic philosopher whose ethics advocate modest pleasure grounded in physical and mental tranquility.

## context

Epicurus represents a distinct ethical tradition: the pursuit of tranquility and freedom rather than virtue, glory, or divine grace. His integration of atomism with ethics—the claim that a mechanistic universe permits happiness through modest living and friendship—anticipates modern secular ethics and utilitarianism. His insistence that women and slaves belong in a philosophical community was radical for his time. The persistent slander against his name is itself a historical lesson in how philosophical positions can be caricatured by hostile traditions.

## connections

- [[stoicism]] — Epicureanism's great rival; Stoics advocated virtue over pleasure, rationality over atomism.
- [[atomism]] — Epicurus adopted atomism from *Democritus* and integrated it into ethics.
- [[pleasure-and-pain]] — his central question: how to understand pleasure rightly.
- [[lucretius]] — Roman poet and Epicurean; his *On the Nature of Things* preserves Epicurean physics and ethics.
- [[virtue-ethics]] — Aristotle and the Stoics champion virtue; Epicurus offers a counter-tradition.
- [[ataraxia]] — Epicurean tranquility; shared goal with Stoics, different paths.
- [[free-will]] — the atomic swerve is Epicurus' answer to determinism.
