# Virtue Ethics

## summary

*Virtue ethics* is a branch of moral philosophy that asks "What kind of person should I be?" rather than "What should I do?" or "What consequences will result?" It holds that moral excellence (*virtue*) is the central concept, defined as a stable character trait cultivated through habituation and exemplified by virtuous agents. Unlike deontological ethics (which emphasizes duty and rules) and consequentialism (which emphasizes outcomes), virtue ethics evaluates actions as expressions of character and asks whether they flow from and reinforce virtuous dispositions. The tradition originates with *Aristotle*, revived in the 20th century by philosophers like *Alasdair MacIntyre*, and offers an alternative framework that emphasizes human flourishing, narrative, and the social contexts in which virtue is cultivated.

## you gotta know

- Virtue ethics takes its name from the Greek *arete* (excellence, virtue); *Aristotle* defined virtue as a *hexis* (stable disposition) to act well, acquired through practice and habituation—not knowledge alone.
- The paradigm of virtue ethics is the *virtuous person* or exemplar, whose character integrates practical wisdom (*phronesis*) with the virtues—courage, temperance, justice, generosity, and others. You learn virtue by imitating such exemplars.
- A virtue is the *mean* between extreme vices: courage is the mean between cowardice and recklessness; generosity between stinginess and profligacy. This is *not* statistical averaging but the optimal response contextually.
- Eudaimonia (*flourishing* or often mistranslated as "happiness") is the ultimate good; it consists in living virtuously and exercising virtue in accordance with reason. For humans, this involves fulfilling our distinctive function (*ergon*) as rational, social beings.
- Virtue ethics embeds agents in social and narrative contexts: we learn virtue from communities, traditions, and stories. A virtuous life is a coherent narrative, not an algorithm for right action.
- 20th-century revival figures like *Philippa Foot* and *Alasdair MacIntyre* recovered virtue ethics as an alternative to 20th-century *moral philosophy*'s obsession with rules and consequences; MacIntyre argued virtue only makes sense within a tradition and a narrative conception of human life.
- Virtue ethics can seem indeterminate ("What would a virtuous person do?") compared to rule-based ethics, because it requires *phronesis*—practical wisdom, judgment refined by experience. It privileges wisdom over rules.

## connections

- [[aristotle]] — founder of virtue ethics; his *Nicomachean Ethics* is the canonical text.
- [[virtue]] — the central concept; moral excellence as a stable character trait.
- [[eudaimonia]] — the ultimate good for Aristotle; living virtuously is the path to flourishing.
- [[phronesis]] — practical wisdom; the capacity to judge virtuously in particular circumstances.
- [[stoicism]] — an ancient virtue-ethics tradition emphasizing duty and conformity to reason.
- [[confucianism]] — an alternative ancient virtue-ethics tradition centered on relational virtues and exemplary conduct.
- [[alasdair-macintyre]] — 20th-century philosopher who revived virtue ethics as a critique of modern moral philosophy.
- [[moral-philosophy]] — virtue ethics is one major branch alongside consequentialism and deontological ethics.
- [[character]] — virtue ethics makes character, not action or outcome, fundamental to ethics.
- [[human-flourishing]] — eudaimonia is the aim; virtue ethics is teleological, oriented toward the good life.
