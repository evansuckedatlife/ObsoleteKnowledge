# Confucianism

## summary

*Confucianism* is a Chinese philosophical, ethical, and social tradition founded on the teachings of *Confucius* (551–479 BCE). Emphasizing virtue, ritual propriety (*li*), filial piety, loyalty, and the cultivation of a morally exemplary person (*junzi*), Confucianism shaped governance, family life, and education across East Asia for over 2,500 years. Unlike Western philosophy's focus on abstract reason, Confucianism grounds ethics in relationships—parent-child, ruler-subject, friend-friend—and sees moral perfection as achievable through ritual, self-discipline, and emulation of virtuous exemplars. It transcends philosophy as a worldview, influencing law, statecraft, and the daily life of millions.

## you gotta know

### Confucius and Core Principles
- *Confucius* (K'ung Fu-tzu, 551–479 BCE) was a Chinese sage and political advisor; his teachings were compiled by disciples into the *Analects* (*Lunyu*).
- Central virtue is *ren* (humaneness, benevolence), cultivated through the practice of *li* (ritual propriety, etiquette, and moral conduct).
- The *junzi* (gentleman, exemplary person) is not born but cultivated through self-discipline, learning, and emulation of virtue; he embodies moral perfection achievable in this life.
- Filial piety (*xiao*) is the root of all virtue; respect for parents and ancestors underpins all social and political order.

### Relationships and Social Order
- Five cardinal relationships (*wuxun*): ruler-subject, father-son, husband-wife, elder-younger brother, friend-friend; each involves duties and reciprocal obligations.
- The rectification of names (*zhengming*): words must correspond to reality; a ruler must truly rule, a father must truly father, or social order collapses.
- Virtue radiates outward: perfect the self, then the family, then the state, then the world—a nested hierarchy of moral cultivation.
- Government by virtue (*dezheng*) is superior to government by law; the exemplary ruler inspires his people through moral example.

### Development and Legacy
- *Mencius* (c. 372–289 BCE) developed Confucian theory, arguing that human nature is inherently good and that moral cultivation is a process of recovering lost goodness.
- *Xunzi* (c. 310–235 BCE) countered that human nature is evil and requires ritual, learning, and law to be corrected—a more pessimistic but rigorous account.
- After the Han Dynasty (206 BCE–220 CE), Confucianism was institutionalized as the state ideology and examined civil service; it remained the orthodoxy of East Asian statecraft until the 20th century.
- Neo-Confucianism (Song Dynasty onward) synthesized Confucian ethics with Buddhist and Daoist metaphysics, creating a comprehensive world-view rivaling Western scholasticism.

### Modern Interpretation
- Contemporary Confucianism grapples with gender hierarchy (the subordination of women and younger people to male elders) and authoritarianism (potential justification for autocratic rule).
- Some scholars emphasize Confucianism's ethical humanism and relational ontology as correctives to Western individualism and abstract universalism.
- East Asian capitalism and "developmental states" are sometimes attributed to Confucian virtues of discipline, long-term thinking, and social harmony.

## context

Confucianism is more than a school of philosophy; it is a comprehensive civilizational legacy that rival civilizations (Christianity, Islam) influenced at scale. For over two millennia, Confucian ideology underpinned the examination systems that selected officials in China, Korea, Vietnam, and Japan, making it perhaps the most consequential ethical system in human history by population reach and duration. Unlike Western virtue ethics, which emphasizes individual excellence, Confucianism is radically relational: virtue exists *in* the bonds between people, not in abstract principles. The tradition has survived emperors, conquest, communist revolution, and modernization—adapting its meaning but retaining its centroid of relational ethics, exemplary conduct, and the possibility of human moral perfectibility. Confucianism is undergoing a global revival as an alternative to Western individualism and secularism, and as a resource for rethinking global ethics in a pluralistic age.
