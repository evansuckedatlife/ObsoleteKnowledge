# Socrates

## summary

**Socrates** (469–399 BCE) was an Athenian philosopher who revolutionized Western thought by turning away from natural philosophy toward ethics and the examined life. He left no writings, but his method—eliciting truth through dialogue and exposing contradiction—shaped Plato and centuries of philosophy to follow. His trial and execution by Athens became philosophy's defining image of the martyr for truth.

## you gotta know

### Life and Method
- Known through dialogues written by his students, chiefly *Plato* and *Xenophon*.
- Wandered Athens engaging anyone (politicians, poets, craftsmen) in conversation, questioning their assumptions.
- The *Socratic method*: pretend ignorance, ask probing questions, expose contradictions, lead toward truth.
- Claimed to know nothing (`I know that I know nothing`), treating wisdom as recognition of one's own ignorance.

### Philosophy and Ethics
- Rejected natural philosophy in favor of ethical inquiry: how should one live?
- Believed virtue is knowledge—wrongdoing stems from ignorance, not malice.
- Argued virtue cannot be taught but is discovered through dialogue and self-examination.
- Held that the soul is immortal and moral integrity more valuable than life itself.

### Death and Legacy
- Charged with impiety and corrupting youth; tried before an Athenian court in 399 BCE.
- Refused to flee or recant; drank hemlock and died calmly, becoming philosophy's martyr-hero.
- Never wrote anything; all knowledge of him comes from Plato, Xenophon, Aristophanes, and Aristotle.

## context

Socrates represents the birth of moral philosophy in the Western tradition. His refusal to reduce truth to received opinion or conventional piety, and his willingness to die for intellectual integrity, made him the archetype of the philosopher as truth-seeker and ethical exemplar. The Socratic method remains central to education and philosophical practice. His influence flows through Plato into Aristotle, and forward into every school that values questions over dogma.

## connections

- [[plato]] — his most famous student; the Platonic dialogues are our primary source for Socrates.
- [[aristotle]] — student of Plato; inherited and refined Socratic inquiry into metaphysics and science.
- [[xenophon]] — military commander and writer; another student who documented Socratic teachings.
- [[athens]] — birthplace and deathplace; his trial reflects Athenian democratic politics.
- [[virtue-ethics]] — Socrates pioneered inquiry into virtue as the central ethical question.
- [[pre-socratic-philosophy]] — Socrates marked the shift from natural philosophy to ethics and epistemology.
- [[stoicism]] — later Stoics revered Socratic indifference to external pain and commitment to virtue.
