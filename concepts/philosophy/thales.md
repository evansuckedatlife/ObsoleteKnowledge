# Thales of Miletus

## summary

**Thales of Miletus** (c. 624–546 BCE) was an early Ionian Greek philosopher credited with founding Western natural philosophy by asking what the fundamental substance of the universe is. He proposed water as the primal element from which all things arise, and he introduced mathematics and astronomy to the Greeks—making him, by Aristotle's account, the first philosopher.

## you gotta know

### Life and Influence
- Born in Miletus, an Ionian Greek city in what is now Turkey; lived in an era of political upheaval.
- No writings survive; knowledge of him comes from Aristotle, Herodotus, and later sources.
- Aristotle credits him as the founder of natural philosophy (*physika*): inquiry into the nature of things.
- Traveled to Egypt and Babylon; reportedly introduced geometry and astronomy to Greece.

### Water as First Principle
- Proposed that water is the fundamental substance (*arche*) of all things.
- All life depends on moisture; seeds are moist; decay is drying out. Water seems primordial.
- Every living thing arises from water and returns to water; heat and cold are modifications of moist matter.
- This is natural philosophy, not myth: explanation via physical process, not divine will.

### Natural Philosophy and Early Science
- Explained phenomena mechanically: eclipses are explained by the moon blocking the sun, not divine intervention.
- Allegedly predicted a solar eclipse (possibly the eclipse of 585 BCE), though the historical claim is disputed.
- Introduced mathematics to Greece and applied geometry to practical problems (measuring heights, distances).
- Advocated clear thinking and observation, not revelation or poetry.

### Legacy
- Founded the Milesian school (*Anaximander*, *Anaximenes*), which sought a single primal substance.
- Shift from mythological to physical explanation defines the birth of Western science.
- Though his claim that water is fundamental is false, his method—seeking a unifying physical principle—became the foundation of physics.

## context

Thales represents the birth of rational inquiry into nature. By asking "What is it made of?" rather than "Why did the gods make it?", he opened the path to natural science and metaphysics. The Milesian school's search for a single primal substance prefigures atomic theory and the quest for a unified physics. Thales' error—that water is fundamental—matters less than his insight: that nature has an underlying rational structure knowable through thought and observation, not revelation.
