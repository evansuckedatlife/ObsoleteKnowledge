# Pragmatism

## summary

*Pragmatism* is an American philosophical tradition that judges the truth and meaning of a claim by its practical consequences and usefulness, rather than by its correspondence to abstract reality or logical coherence. Developed in the late 19th and early 20th centuries by *Charles Peirce*, *William James*, and *John Dewey*, pragmatism rejects the search for certainty and absolute truth, instead advocating inquiry as an ongoing process of refining practical understanding. For pragmatists, ideas are tools for solving problems; their value lies in whether they *work* in context, not in metaphysical purity. Pragmatism profoundly influenced American educational philosophy, psychology, and democratic thought, and has experienced sustained philosophical revival in recent decades.

## you gotta know

### Foundations
- *Charles Peirce* (1839–1914) founded pragmatism; defined meaning via observable practical consequences (*pragmatic maxim*).
- *William James* (1842–1910) popularised pragmatism; argued truth is what "works" and proves useful in life.
- *John Dewey* (1859–1952) applied pragmatism to education, democracy, and social reform; emphasised experience and experimentation.

### Core Doctrines
- *Pragmatic maxim* (Peirce): the meaning of a concept is exhausted by the practical consequences of its truth.
- Truth as utility: a belief is true if it is useful, coherent, and guides successful action in the world.
- Fallibilism: no belief is infallible or final; all knowledge is provisional and subject to revision via experience.

### Philosophical Implications
- Rejection of *a priori* certitude: emphasises experimentation and feedback from consequences.
- Instrumentalism: concepts and theories are instruments for achieving practical goals, not mirrors of reality.
- Democracy as method: Dewey saw democratic inquiry as the pragmatist model—plural perspectives, open debate, practical testing.

## context

Pragmatism emerged in late-19th-century America as a response to abstract European metaphysics and the rise of experimental science and industrial technology. Pragmatists argued that philosophy should not seek timeless truths but help solve concrete problems. *Dewey's* educational philosophy transformed pedagogy by emphasising learning through doing. Pragmatism declined mid-century but has resurged in epistemology, philosophy of science, political philosophy, and artificial intelligence. Contemporary thinkers recognise that pragmatism's insight—that truth is tied to successful action and that inquiry is context-dependent—applies to how humans and AI systems actually solve problems and learn.
