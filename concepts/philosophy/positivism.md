# Positivism

## summary

*Positivism* is a philosophical movement originating with *Auguste Comte* (19th century) holding that only empirically verifiable or logically necessary knowledge is meaningful, and that metaphysics and theology are cognitively empty. *Logical positivism* (early 20th century), associated with the Vienna Circle and thinkers like *Wittgenstein* and *Carnap*, sharpened this via the *verification principle*: a statement is meaningful if and only if it is either analytically true or empirically verifiable. Positivism aspired to place philosophy on the firm foundation of science, eliminating pseudo-problems and meaningless speculation. Though the movement declined after sustained philosophical critique (the verification principle proved self-refuting), positivism profoundly shaped analytic philosophy, philosophy of science, and the modern scientific worldview.

## you gotta know

### Foundations and Development
- *Auguste Comte* (1798–1857) founded positivism; argued history progresses through theological, metaphysical, and positive (scientific) stages.
- *Vienna Circle* (1920s–1930s) crystallised logical positivism; *Wittgenstein*, *Carnap*, *Schlick*, *Ayer* developed it.
- *Logical positivism*: sharpened positivism via formal logic; only analytic truths and empirically verifiable statements are meaningful.

### Core Doctrines
- *Verification principle*: a statement is cognitively meaningful iff it is (a) a tautology (logically necessary) or (b) empirically testable.
- *Metaphysical elimination*: claims about God, the soul, causation beyond constant conjunction, *a priori* knowledge—all are meaningless pseudo-problems.
- *Unity of science*: all genuine knowledge follows the scientific method; physics is the model; other sciences are special cases.

### Decline and Legacy
- The verification principle proved self-refuting: the principle itself is neither tautological nor empirically verifiable.
- *Quine's* critique of the analytic-synthetic distinction undermined logical positivism's foundation.
- *Popper* argued falsifiability, not verifiability, is the criterion of science; *Kuhn* showed science is paradigm-bound, not cumulative.

## context

Logical positivism emerged in 1920s Vienna as an attempt to place philosophy on science's secure foundation, rejecting centuries of metaphysical confusion. The movement was scientistic and politically progressive—it aimed to expose ideological pseudo-problems. World War II scattered the Vienna Circle; logical positivism declined by the 1960s as philosophers recognised the verification principle's fatal flaw and that science is not as logically transparent as positivists imagined. Yet positivism's legacy persists: the scientific worldview, skepticism about metaphysics, and the analytic tradition's emphasis on clarity and argument. Contemporary philosophy of science grapples with positivism's legacy—how to respect science without reducing meaning to empirical verifiability.
