## summary

*Cynicism* is an ancient Greek school of philosophy founded by *Antisthenes* and exemplified by *Diogenes of Sinope*, teaching that virtue is the highest good and achieved through the renunciation of worldly desires, social conventions, and physical comfort. Unlike *Stoicism*, which accepts social duty and reason-based participation in civic life, Cynics rejected society itself as corrupt and artificial, advocating instead a radical asceticism and provocative mockery of conventional values. The Cynic sage lives simply, often in public transgression of social norms, to expose the arbitrary and constraining nature of custom and to liberate the mind from false desires. Though the movement declined in antiquity, Cynicism reappeared in the Enlightenment and remains a strand in modern anti-establishment thought.

## you gotta know

- Founded by *Antisthenes* (~445–365 BCE), a student of Socrates; *Diogenes of Sinope* (~404–323 BCE) became the movement's most famous exemplar, living in a barrel and defying authority and decorum.
- Cynics taught that virtue is the only true good, but virtue is defined as *freedom from desire* and *independence*; comfort, wealth, reputation, and even family are impediments to freedom and virtue.
- The Cynic path requires *askesis* (training, discipline, ascetic practice)—not to achieve transcendence but to strip away the false needs society has imposed. A Cynic eats the cheapest food, wears rags, and scorns luxury.
- Cynics employed *parrhesia* (bold speech) and transgressive public acts to shock people into seeing through social illusions—hence *cynical* in the modern sense, skeptical of professed motives and conventional morality.
- Unlike Stoics, Cynics rejected civic participation, law, and justice as invented fictions. The Cynic wise person has no loyalty to the state or its laws—only to virtue and freedom.
- *Diogenes* is remembered for extreme acts: allegedly living in a tub, masturbating in the agora, instructing people to tread on his pride, and famously telling *Alexander the Great* to get out of his light.
- Cynic ideas influenced later Christian monasticism (asceticism, renunciation) and modern critiques of capitalism and consumerism.

## connections

- [[diogenes]] — the exemplary Cynic; his provocative life defines the movement.
- [[antisthenes]] — the founder; student of Socrates and teacher of Diogenes.
- [[virtue]] — the highest good for Cynics; virtue as freedom from desire.
- [[stoicism]] — related to Cynicism but accepts duty, reason, and social obligation; Stoics saw Cynics as virtue-seekers who mistakenly rejected society.
- [[simplicity]] — Cynic asceticism; living with minimal possessions and comfort.
- [[social-convention]] — Cynicism's target; the critique of arbitrary customs and imposed social roles.
- [[socrates]] — spiritual ancestor; Cynicism traces itself to Socratic virtue and his rejection of material goods.
- [[nihilism]] — modern cynicism (lowercase) shares the Cynic rejection of conventional values, though for different reasons.
