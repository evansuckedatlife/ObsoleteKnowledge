# Empiricism

## summary

*Empiricism* is the epistemological position that all knowledge derives ultimately from sensory experience, not innate ideas or pure reason. Emerging in 17th- and 18th-century Britain with *John Locke*, *David Hume*, and *John Stuart Mill*, empiricism insists that the mind begins as a blank slate (*tabula rasa*) and that complex ideas are built from simple impressions via association and reflection. Empiricists challenged rationalist confidence in deduction and the *a priori*, arguing instead that knowledge of the world comes through careful observation and inductive reasoning. Empiricism became the philosophical foundation for modern science and shaped contemporary debates about perception, induction, and the limits of reason.

## you gotta know

### Foundations
- *John Locke* (1632–1704) argued against innate ideas; all knowledge begins in sensation and reflection on experience.
- *David Hume* (1711–1776) radicalised empiricism, arguing that causation, induction, and even personal identity have no rational foundation but are habits of mind.
- *John Stuart Mill* (1806–1873) extended empiricism to logic and mathematics, arguing even mathematical truths derive ultimately from experiential patterns.

### Core Doctrines
- *Tabula rasa*: the mind is blank at birth; all ideas enter through the senses.
- Simple vs. complex ideas: simple impressions combine to form complex thoughts; reason is rearrangement, not creation.
- *Induction*: knowledge of the world proceeds via observation and generalization, not deductive axioms.

### Epistemological Consequences
- Skepticism about metaphysics: propositions not grounded in experience (God, the soul, causal necessity) are cognitively empty.
- Problem of induction: Hume showed that induction itself cannot be rationally justified—a persistent epistemological challenge.
- Nominalism: only particulars (this red apple) are real; universals (redness, appleness) are mental constructs.

## context

Empiricism crystallised as a reaction against rationalism's confidence in deduction and innate knowledge. It aligned philosophy with the emerging scientific method—Boyle, Newton, and later figures practiced empirical investigation. Hume's radical critique of induction and causation exposed deep problems in both empiricism and rationalism, spawning *Kant's* critical response. Empiricism became the dominant Anglo-American philosophical tradition and undergirds modern epistemology and philosophy of science. Its emphasis on observation and testability prefigured logical positivism and remains central to how science understands justification and truth.
