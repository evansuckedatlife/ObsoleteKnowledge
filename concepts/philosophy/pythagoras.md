## summary

**Pythagoras** (c. 570–495 BCE) was an Ionian Greek philosopher and mathematician who founded a mystical and scientific community in southern Italy, teaching that numbers are the fundamental principle of reality and that the cosmos is governed by mathematical harmony. His ideas on transmigration of souls, dietary restrictions, and the music of the spheres merged mathematics with religion.

## you gotta know

### Life and Movement
- Born on Samos, an Ionian island; little of his life is certain (later accounts are heavily mythologized).
- Migrated to Croton (southern Italy) and founded a quasi-religious community, the *Pythagorean society*, around 532 BCE.
- The society was secretive, communal, and ascetic; membership required initiation and vows of secrecy.
- Persecuted and apparently killed by Croton citizens; the community scattered but survived as a philosophical tradition.

### Mathematics and Cosmology
- Believed numbers and their ratios are the ultimate stuff of reality: "all things are numbers."
- Discovered that musical intervals correspond to simple numerical ratios (e.g. octave = 2:1).
- *Pythagorean theorem* (attributed to him; likely known earlier): in a right triangle, a² + b² = c².
- Posited ten pairs of opposite principles organizing reality: odd/even, limited/unlimited, one/many.
- Conceived of the cosmos as spheres in mathematical harmony—the *music of the spheres*.

### Philosophy and Mysticism
- Taught *metempsychosis* (transmigration of souls): the soul is immortal and reincarnates.
- Imposed dietary taboos (especially a ban on beans) and ritual purification as spiritual disciplines.
- Viewed philosophy as *purification* of the soul, liberation from the cycle of rebirths.
- Combined mathematics (science) with mysticism (religion) in a unified worldview.

### Influence
- Influenced Plato deeply (especially the theory of forms and the idea of a transcendent realm of numbers).
- The Pythagorean theorem bears his name and is the foundation of Euclidean geometry.
- Inspired mystical number symbolism and sacred geometry throughout Western esotericism.

## context

Pythagoras represents the merger of rationalism and mysticism—the idea that reality is fundamentally mathematical yet divine. His insight that the physical world obeys mathematical principles was prescient and became central to modern physics; yet his reliance on mystical purification and secret doctrine also made Pythagoreanism a template for esoteric traditions. The Pythagorean society was the first philosophical community organized as a way of life, not merely an intellectual school, and Plato's Academy imitated this model.
