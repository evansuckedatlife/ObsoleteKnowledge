# Existentialism

## summary

*Existentialism* is a 20th-century philosophical movement asserting that *existence precedes essence*—that we are radically free to create ourselves through choice and action, and that this freedom entails responsibility and anxiety. Associated with thinkers like *Jean-Paul Sartre*, *Albert Camus*, and *Martin Heidegger*, existentialism rejects the notion that human nature is fixed or predetermined by God, biology, or society. Instead, existentialists emphasise individual authenticity, the absurdity of a universe indifferent to human meaning, and the weight of *bad faith*—self-deception about our freedom and responsibility. Existentialism profoundly influenced post-war culture, literature, psychology, and contemporary debates about identity and freedom.

## you gotta know

### Foundations and Key Figures
- *Jean-Paul Sartre* (1905–1980) crystallised existentialism; argued consciousness is "nothingness" and we are "condemned to be free."
- *Martin Heidegger* (1889–1976) examined *Being* and authenticity; influenced existentialism though he rejected the label.
- *Albert Camus* (1913–1960) explored the *absurd*—the conflict between our need for meaning and the universe's silence.

### Core Doctrines
- *Existence precedes essence*: there is no human nature; we create ourselves through choices and actions.
- *Radical freedom*: we are fundamentally free in every situation; even refusing to choose is a choice.
- *Bad faith* (*mauvaise foi*): self-deception about our freedom; pretending we have no choice (via role, tradition, determinism).
- Authenticity: confronting our freedom and responsibility; living honestly according to our values rather than others' expectations.

### Themes and Concerns
- *Anguish* and *absurdity*: the vertiginous weight of freedom and the universe's indifference to our projects.
- *Authenticity* vs. *bad faith*: the existential imperative to act freely and own one's choices.
- *Facticity*: we inherit a situation (body, history, society) we did not choose, yet remain free within it.

## context

Existentialism emerged in post-war Europe confronting totalitarianism, genocide, and the collapse of grand ideologies. It offered a philosophy that placed the individual's freedom and responsibility at the centre, rejecting both determinism and authority. *Sartre's* existentialism became politically engaged, grappling with Marxism and liberation. *Camus* countered that absurdity, not ideology, is the human condition—we must rebel against meaninglessness. Existentialism profoundly influenced literature (*Sartre*, *Beckett*, *Ionesco*), psychotherapy (logotherapy, existential psychology), and debates about identity, authenticity, and freedom. Though academic existentialism declined, its themes—authenticity, freedom, the burden of choice—remain central to how we understand ourselves.

## connections

- [[jean-paul-sartre]] — the defining existentialist; developed the philosophy of freedom and *bad faith*.
- [[albert-camus]] — explored existentialism's confrontation with the absurd; argued for revolt and solidarity.
- [[martin-heidegger]] — influenced existentialism via analysis of Being and authenticity.
- [[freedom]] — the central existentialist value; radical, vertiginous, and inescapable.
- [[authenticity]] — living according to one's values rather than in *bad faith*.
- [[phenomenology]] — the philosophical method existentialists employed to analyse consciousness and existence.
- [[absurdism]] — closely related; the view that life's absurdity cannot be escaped or rationalised away.
