## summary

**Parmenides** (c. 515–445 BCE) was an Eleatic Greek philosopher who argued that reality is a single, unchanging, eternal, indivisible whole—"Being"—and that change, motion, and plurality are illusions. His radical monism challenged pre-Socratic pluralists and forced all later philosophy to reckon with the problem of permanence versus change.

## you gotta know

### Life and School
- Born in Elea, a Greek colony in southern Italy; founded the Eleatic school of philosophy.
- His only surviving work is a philosophical poem, written in verse.
- Socrates supposedly met the elderly Parmenides in Athens; this meeting is dramatized in Plato's *Parmenides*.
- Influenced a line of Eleatic philosophers, notably his student *Zeno of Elea*.

### The Argument for Being
- Reality is *One*: a single, undifferentiated whole, necessarily eternal and unchanging.
- "What is, is; what is not, is not"—Being cannot come from non-being, nor pass into it.
- Being cannot move, change, divide, or perish, because any such event would require non-being (which does not exist).
- Plurality, motion, and time are illusions generated by the senses; reason alone grasps true reality.

### Critique of Change
- Generation and destruction are impossible; what is cannot arise from nothing.
- Motion requires void (empty space), but void is non-being; hence motion cannot be.
- Change requires the thing to be partly what it was and partly what it will be—contradicting the unity of Being.
- Therefore, the phenomenal world of change and flux (Heraclitus' vision) is mere appearance.

### Logical Legacy
- Parmenides pioneered the method of rational proof: argue from first principles (Being cannot not-be) to conclusions that overturn experience.
- This deductive method shaped philosophy; Plato and Aristotle grappled with his arguments.
- His challenge forced subsequent thinkers to explain multiplicity, change, and motion—problems central to Western metaphysics.

## context

Parmenides stands at the birth of logic and metaphysics. By arguing that reason and sensory perception give opposite accounts of reality, he crystallized a fundamental Western philosophical tension: the appearance of change versus the logic of immutable Being. Though nearly all later philosophy rejects his monism, it does so by refining his arguments, not ignoring them. Parmenides made "Why is there something rather than nothing?" and "How can change be real?" the deepest questions philosophy must answer.

## connections

- [[heraclitus]] — his opposite: Heraclitus champions flux; Parmenides champions being.
- [[zeno-of-elea]] — his student, who defended Parmenides with logical paradoxes (Zeno's paradoxes).
- [[being-and-nothingness]] — the core problem: is Being one or many, changing or static?
- [[pre-socratic-philosophy]] — Parmenides is a watershed figure, challenging earlier pluralist physicists.
- [[plato]] — deeply engaged with Parmenides; the *Parmenides* dialogue is a critique of Parmenidean forms.
- [[aristotle]] — refined the problem of change and permanence in his doctrine of actuality and potentiality.
- [[metaphysics]] — Parmenides' questions about being and non-being define the field.
