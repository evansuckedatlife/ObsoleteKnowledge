# Stoicism

## summary

*Stoicism* is a Hellenistic and Roman philosophy teaching that virtue is the highest good and the path to a fulfilling life. Founded in Athens by *Zeno of Citium* around 300 BCE, Stoicism holds that external events—wealth, health, reputation—are fundamentally indifferent; what matters is conforming one's will and judgment to reason and universal nature. By accepting what cannot be controlled and focusing effort on virtue, duty, and rational choice, the Stoic achieves *ataraxia* (mental tranquility) and *apatheia* (freedom from destructive emotion). The philosophy dominated Roman intellectual life and profoundly influenced Christianity, modern cognitive therapy, and secular ethics.

## you gotta know

### Foundations
- Founded by *Zeno of Citium* (~300 BCE) and developed by thinkers like *Cleanthes*, *Chrysippus*, *Epictetus*, and *Marcus Aurelius*.
- Central claim: virtue—living according to reason and nature—is the *only* true good; all else (health, wealth, fame) is merely "preferred indifferent."
- The universe operates via *logos* (divine rational principle); human reason is a fragment of this cosmic reason.

### Ethics and Practice
- *Dichotomy of control*: distinguish firmly between what lies in your control (judgment, choice, desire) and what does not (body, property, reputation).
- *Apatheia* and *ataraxia*: freedom from destructive passion and mental disturbance through rational acceptance and duty.
- Duty (*kathkon*) to oneself, family, society, and the cosmos—virtue expresses itself through action aligned with reason and nature.

### Influence and Legacy
- Dominated Roman elite philosophy; *Meditations* by the emperor *Marcus Aurelius* is the canonical surviving Stoic text.
- *Epictetus*, a slave, taught that inner freedom transcends external circumstance—a radical claim that influenced Christian and modern thought.
- Core insight—that suffering arises from irrational judgment rather than events themselves—anticipates modern Cognitive Behavioral Therapy.

## context

Stoicism emerged as a response to the loss of Greek city-state autonomy after Alexander's conquests; it offered individuals a path to freedom and meaning independent of political circumstance. Its insistence that virtue alone suffices for happiness, and that reason is universal and accessible to all humans, made it profoundly egalitarian for its era—a slave could be wise as an emperor. The school shaped Roman law (with its emphasis on natural justice), Christian virtue ethics (especially asceticism and duty), and modern secular psychology (CBT borrows the Stoic insight that irrational *impressions* corrupt judgment). Stoicism endures because it addresses a perennial problem: how to maintain dignity and purpose when circumstances lie beyond your power.

## connections

- [[zeno-of-citium]] — founder of the school; developed the core ethical system.
- [[marcus-aurelius]] — Roman emperor and Stoic practitioner whose *Meditations* is the canonical surviving text.
- [[epictetus]] — former slave who taught that inner freedom transcends external condition; influenced Christian asceticism.
- [[virtue]] — the central organizing concept; the only true good in Stoic ethics.
- [[reason]] — humanity's divine nature; the faculty by which we align with the cosmic logos.
- [[cynicism]] — a related ancient school emphasizing virtue but rejecting social duty and reason-based acceptance.
- [[epicureanism]] — often misunderstood as hedonist excess, but shares Stoicism's goal of tranquility (ataraxia).
