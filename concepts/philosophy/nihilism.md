# Nihilism

## summary

*Nihilism* is the philosophical view that life is without objective meaning, purpose, or value; that morality is baseless; and that truth or knowledge are impossible or illusory. A *nihilist* denies the existence of intrinsic human values, transcendent purposes, and binding moral truths. Nihilism emerged as a diagnosis in 19th-century thought—a cultural sickness following the collapse of religious and metaphysical certainties—most famously articulated by *Friedrich Nietzsche*, who saw it as both a threat to civilization and a necessary prelude to new values. The term is also used (loosely) to describe any radical skepticism about values or the meaningfulness of human endeavor. Nihilism stands in tension with philosophy's entire tradition, which presumes meaning and value are either discovered or created; it remains a powerful contemporary position, especially among those who question grand narratives and universal truths.

## you gotta know

- Nihilism denies *three* core philosophical commitments: objective truth or knowledge (epistemological nihilism), intrinsic value or meaning (axiological nihilism), and binding moral norms (moral nihilism).
- *Nietzsche* diagnosed nihilism as the historical consequence of Christianity and metaphysics: the traditional sources of meaning have been undermined, leaving a void. Nihilism is both a danger (it can produce despair and collapse of cultural values) and an opportunity (to create new values beyond good and evil).
- Moral nihilism holds that no moral facts exist; there are no objective truths about right and wrong. Morality is projection, convention, or subjective preference—a radical departure from most ethical traditions which presume moral realism.
- Existentialist response: *Sartre* and others accepted that the world is meaningless but argued humans are *condemned to be free*—we must create meaning through authentic choices and projects, even in an absurd universe.
- *Albert Camus* developed *absurdism*: the absurd is the conflict between the human need for meaning and the universe's silence. The absurd cannot be overcome by nihilism or faith; instead, one should imagine Sisyphus happy—accepting the struggle without hope.
- Nihilism in cultural life: the rejection of all values and traditions can manifest as cynicism, despair, or revolutionary destruction. 19th- and 20th-century nihilist movements sometimes championed violence as a negation of corrupt societies.
- Theoretical nihilism is distinct from *practical* nihilism: one can philosophically affirm nihilism while living as if meaning and value exist. The question of whether nihilism can be lived authentically remains contested.

## connections

- [[friedrich-nietzsche]] — the philosopher most associated with diagnosing and responding to nihilism; his philosophy is both a critique of nihilism and a critique of traditional values.
- [[meaning]] — nihilism denies the existence of objective meaning; the quest for meaning drives much philosophy in response.
- [[morality]] — moral nihilism denies the reality of moral facts; contrasts with moral realism and other normative positions.
- [[existentialism]] — existentialists accept a nihilistic premise (no transcendent meaning) but reject nihilistic conclusions (create meaning through authentic choice).
- [[absurdism]] — Camus' position on nihilism: accept the absurd without escape or despair.
- [[will-to-power]] — Nietzsche's response to nihilism; the doctrine that life and value are grounded in creative force, not reason or God.
- [[relativism]] — related skepticism about objective truth; nihilism extends this to values and meaning.
- [[postmodernism]] — contemporary philosophy influenced by nihilist suspicion of grand narratives and essences.
- [[value]] — nihilism's counterpart; what grounds value if nihilism is false?
