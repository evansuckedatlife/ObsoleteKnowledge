## summary

**Plato** (428–348 BCE) was an Athenian philosopher and student of Socrates who founded the Academy and authored the dialogues that preserve Socrates' method while advancing his own metaphysics, epistemology, and ethics. His theory of eternal, immaterial forms shaped Western thought for two millennia, and his *Republic* remains the foundational text of political philosophy.

## you gotta know

### Life and Works
- Born into a wealthy Athenian family; became Socrates' student around age 20.
- Witnessed Socrates' trial and execution, an event that haunted him and drove much of his philosophy.
- Founded the *Academy* in Athens (c. 387 BCE), an institution for philosophical inquiry and mathematics; lasted nearly 900 years.
- Authored ~30 surviving dialogues, almost all featuring Socrates as the main speaker.

### Theory of Forms
- Posited a realm of eternal, immutable, abstract "forms" or "ideas" transcending the physical world.
- Physical objects are imperfect copies or "shadows" of the forms (e.g. physical chairs participate in the form of Chair).
- Forms explain universals and permanence; the material world is flux and illusion.
- Only through reason and philosophical contemplation can we grasp forms; sensory experience is unreliable.

### Ethics and Politics
- In the *Republic*, sketched an ideal state where philosopher-kings (educated in mathematics and dialectic) rule.
- Virtue arises from knowledge and the harmony of the soul's three parts: reason, spirit, and appetite.
- Justice is the right ordering of these parts; the just state mirrors the just soul.
- Rejected democracy (which he witnessed fail in Athens); advocated rule by the wise and educated.

### Method and Influence
- The Platonic dialogue: Socrates engages an interlocutor, exposes their confusion, and points toward truth through questioning.
- Influenced Aristotle (his student), medieval theology (Augustine, Aquinas), Renaissance philosophy, and modern metaphysics (Descartes, Kant).

## context

Plato stands at the crossroads of Western intellectual history. His theory of transcendent forms answered the pre-Socratic puzzle of permanence and change, and shaped every metaphysical tradition that appeals to abstract universals or eternal truths. The Academy was the first research university; his dialogues are literature and philosophy simultaneously, making Socrates immortal through dialogue. Platonism—the belief in a realm beyond the material—recurs in theology, mathematics, and logic across two thousand years.
