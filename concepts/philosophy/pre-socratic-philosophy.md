# Pre-Socratic Philosophy

## summary

Pre-Socratic philosophy encompasses the Greek natural philosophers active before or contemporary with *Socrates* (c. 470–399 BCE) who sought rational explanations for the nature of reality, change, and being. These thinkers—including the *Milesians*, the *Pythagoreans*, *Heraclitus*, and *Parmenides*—abandoned mythological accounts and pioneered Western logic, metaphysics, and natural science. Though their works survive only in fragments and later quotations, they established the foundational problems and methods that defined philosophy for the next 2,400 years.

## you gotta know

- The earliest Pre-Socratic thinkers, the *Milesians* (*Thales*, *Anaximander*, *Anaximenes*), sought a single underlying substance from which all reality arises—water, apeiron (the infinite), and air respectively.
- *Pythagoras* founded a religious and philosophical community emphasizing the mystical importance of number and geometry; Pythagorean harmonies influenced music theory and cosmology.
- *Heraclitus* taught that reality is in constant flux and that fire is the underlying principle; he emphasized the paradoxical unity of opposites and the logos governing change.
- *Parmenides* argued that reality must be eternal, unchanging, and indivisible; change and plurality are illusions—a thesis that challenged the evidence of the senses and provoked centuries of response.
- *Zeno of Elea* devised paradoxes (Achilles and the tortoise, the arrow) to defend Parmenidean monism by exposing contradictions in accepting plurality and motion.
- The *Atomists* (*Leucippus*, *Democritus*) resolved the Parmenides-Heraclitus tension by positing indivisible atoms moving in void, explaining change and multiplicity without violating Parmenidean logic.
- Pre-Socratic thinkers pioneered the demand for natural (non-mythological) causes and rational argument—establishing the epistemic norms that define Western philosophy.

## connections

- [[heraclitus]] — the flux philosopher; taught that fire and paradox govern reality.
- [[parmenides]] — challenged change itself; his monism provoked all subsequent metaphysics.
- [[pythagoras]] — mathematician and mystic; numbers and harmony as cosmic principles.
- [[thales]] — the first Milesian; argued water is the primary substance.
- [[zeno-of-elea]] — the paradoxes defending Parmenidean monism against motion and plurality.
- [[socrates]] — the watershed figure; he redirected philosophy toward human ethics and knowledge.
- [[plato]] — his forms partly respond to Pre-Socratic problems about change and being.
- [[aristotle]] — his logic and natural philosophy supersede Pre-Socratic frameworks.
- [[metaphysics]] — Pre-Socratic thinkers pose the foundational questions of metaphysics.
- [[logic]] — Pre-Socratic argument and paradox pioneer rational inference.
