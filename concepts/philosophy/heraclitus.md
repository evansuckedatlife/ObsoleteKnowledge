# Heraclitus

## summary

**Heraclitus** (c. 535–475 BCE) was an Ionian Greek philosopher known for the doctrine of universal flux—that all things are in constant change—and the unifying *logos* (rational principle) that governs reality. His aphoristic, paradoxical style and emphasis on tension and opposition made him a defining voice in pre-Socratic philosophy and a foil for Parmenides.

## you gotta know

### Life and Method
- Born in Ephesus, a wealthy Ionian city; lived a reclusive life of contemplation.
- Wrote a single work, known as the *logos*, now lost; what survives are fragments quoted by later philosophers.
- Expressed his ideas in cryptic, aphoristic statements and riddles, earning the nickname "the Obscure."
- Deliberately obscure style was meant to exclude the vulgar and reward careful thought.

### Flux and Change
- *"Everything flows"* (`panta rhei`); the world is in constant flux and motion.
- *"You cannot step into the same river twice"* — change is so radical that neither the river nor you are the same.
- Fire is the primal element; it symbolizes change, transformation, and the dynamic nature of reality.
- Opposition and tension are not static but dynamic: things are held in unity through perpetual opposition.

### The Logos
- Reality is governed by a rational principle, the *logos*—an order and rationality that pervades all things.
- Humans are separated from this logos by ignorance and sleep; philosophy awakens us to its truth.
- The logos is divine and immanent, not transcendent.
- Although all things change, the logos (the principle of change) is eternal and unchanging.

### Contrast with Parmenides
- Parmenides later argued for unchanging Being; Heraclitus prioritized becoming.
- This opposition—flux vs. permanence—defined a central tension in Western metaphysics.

## context

Heraclitus anticipated modern process philosophy and quantum mechanics with his vision of a dynamic, fluid universe. His integration of logic (logos) with change (flux) resolved an apparent contradiction: permanence is not the absence of change but the constancy of the principle that governs change. Hegel revived Heraclitus enthusiastically, seeing dialectical opposition as Heraclitean flux. His style—aphoristic, paradoxical, symbolic—made him the poet-philosopher, influencing how philosophers use language to provoke insight.
