# Rationalism

## summary

*Rationalism* is the epistemological view that reason—particularly deductive logic and *a priori* thought—is the primary source of knowledge about reality, and that certain truths (especially mathematical and metaphysical) are knowable independent of experience. Championed by 17th-century thinkers *Descartes*, *Leibniz*, and *Spinoza*, rationalism holds that the mind possesses innate ideas or principles, and that systematic deduction from these foundations can yield certain knowledge. Where empiricists trusted observation, rationalists trusted the power of reason alone to penetrate reality's structure. Rationalism declined after *Hume's* empirical critique but was partially rehabilitated by *Kant*; modern logic and mathematics vindicate the rationalist conviction that abstract reasoning can yield objective truth.

## you gotta know

### Foundations and Key Figures
- *René Descartes* (1596–1650) grounded knowledge in reason (*Cogito, ergo sum*) and deduction from clear and distinct ideas.
- *Gottfried Leibniz* (1646–1716) argued for innate *dispositions* and developed monadology via pure reason.
- *Baruch Spinoza* (1632–1677) built a geometric ethics and metaphysics from rationalist axioms; influenced modern logic.

### Core Doctrines
- *A priori knowledge*: truths knowable by reason alone, independent of sensory experience (mathematics, logic, some metaphysics).
- Innate ideas: the mind possesses fundamental principles or ideas (God, infinity, geometry) not derived from experience.
- *Deductive method*: systematically derive conclusions from axioms via logical necessity, as in geometry.

### Rationalist Metaphysics
- *Substance* and its attributes: reality consists of fundamental substances (mind, matter, God) with essential properties.
- *Necessity*: the rationalist universe is ordered by logical necessity, not contingency or divine will alone.
- Mind-body problem: how immaterial mind and physical body interact—a puzzle *Descartes* struggled with (*pineal gland*).

## context

Rationalism emerged as a response to medieval scholasticism's over-reliance on authority and as a celebration of the new mathematical science—geometry and algebra seemed to reveal reality's deep structure. Rationalists believed reason could rival (or surpass) observation in penetrating truth. *Hume's* empirical critique exposed foundational problems: where do innate ideas come from? How does deduction yield knowledge of contingent facts? Yet rationalism was not defeated—modern logic, mathematics, and analytic philosophy vindicate the rationalist insight that abstract reasoning can yield objective, universal truth. Contemporary rationalism about logic, mathematics, modality, and morality remains vigorous.
