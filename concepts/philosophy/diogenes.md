# Diogenes of Sinope

## summary

**Diogenes of Sinope** (c. 412–323 BCE) was a Greek philosopher who founded Cynicism, a school advocating radical asceticism, defiance of social convention, and virtue as the only good. Living as a vagrant in a barrel and confronting authority without shame, Diogenes made his life itself a philosophical statement against hypocrisy and the illusory values of civilization.

## you gotta know

### Life and Method
- Born in Sinope, on the Black Sea; exiled and came to Athens around age 30.
- Became a student of *Antisthenes*, the founder of Cynicism, and radicalized the school.
- Lived in extreme poverty, wearing tattered clothes, sleeping in a barrel or the street.
- Used shock and crude behavior (defecating publicly, urinating on people, lewdness) to provoke reflection and undermine social propriety.

### Philosophy and Virtue
- Virtue is the only good; all else—wealth, health, reputation, pleasure, pity—is indifferent or harmful illusion.
- Lived according to nature, not convention; rejected cultural values as false and enslaving.
- Freedom (autarkeia) is self-sufficiency: needing only what nature provides, unbound by desire or fear.
- The life of philosophy is living poorly and fighting against convention, as a kind of spiritual exercise.

### Famous Anecdotes
- When *Alexander the Great* visited Athens and asked what he could do for Diogenes, he replied: "Get out of the way; you're blocking the sun."
- Called himself a "cosmopolitan" (kosmopolites)—no nation or tribe, only humanity—an early vision of universal ethics.
- Taught by example: his bare life was the message, more powerful than discourse.

### Cynicism as Philosophy
- Cynics believed civilization corrupts virtue; return to natural simplicity and radical independence.
- Shamelessness as freedom: refusing to hide bodily functions or obey arbitrary rules.
- Cynicism influenced Stoicism (both taught virtue as the only good, but Stoics were more ascetic, less crude).

## context

Diogenes represents philosophy as lived practice, not mere doctrine. His deliberate poverty and provocative behavior were not madness but method: to awaken others from the dream of false values. Cynicism, though often dismissed as misanthropy, embodies a serious ethical vision: that virtue requires stripping away the false desires civilization instills. Later Stoics adopted much of Cynicism's ethics—virtue as the only good—while rejecting its shamelessness. Diogenes' fame rests partly on anecdotes, but his core insight endures: that freedom and authenticity may demand radical non-conformity.
