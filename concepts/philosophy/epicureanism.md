## summary

*Epicureanism* is the ancient philosophical school founded by *Epicurus* (~307 BCE) teaching that the goal of life is pleasure, understood not as hedonistic excess but as freedom from pain (*aponia*) and mental disturbance (*ataraxia*). Contrary to later caricature, *Epicurus* advocated modest living, simple food, and prudent friendship, arguing that many desires are unnecessary and that their pursuit produces suffering. For Epicureanism, intellectual and philosophical pleasures are superior to bodily ones; a life of contemplation with trusted friends in a garden is the ideal. Epicureanism shares with Stoicism the goal of tranquility but achieves it through prudent pleasure rather than duty; the school remained influential throughout the Roman era and was revived in the early modern period.

## you gotta know

### Foundations
- *Epicurus* (341–270 BCE) founded the school in his garden in Athens; taught that pleasure is the highest good.
- Misrepresented by detractors as advocating excess; actually taught that most desires are harmful and unnecessary.
- The Epicurean ideal: modest living, philosophical conversation, simple food (bread, water, occasional cheese), and freedom from fear and pain.

### Epicurean Ethics and Practice
- *Aponia* and *ataraxia*: absence of bodily pain and mental disturbance are the true pleasures.
- Categorisation of desires: natural and necessary (food, water, shelter), natural but unnecessary (luxury foods), and vain (wealth, fame).
- Prudence in pursuing pleasure: avoid desires that produce greater suffering; the examined life minimises net pain.

### Pleasures and Philosophy
- Intellectual pleasures (philosophy, friendship, memory) are superior to bodily ones and less dependent on external circumstance.
- Philosophy is practical wisdom, not abstract theory; its goal is to liberate from fear (of gods, death, pain).
- *Tetrapharmakos* (the four-part cure): don't fear God, don't fear death, pain is easily endured, the good is easy to acquire.

## context

Epicureanism was a countercultural philosophy in a hierarchical world obsessed with honor and power. By arguing that pleasure—rightly understood—is the good, and that virtue (prudence, moderation, friendship) serves pleasure, Epicureanism offered liberation from status anxiety and political ambition. The Epicurean community lived outside the civic hierarchy; women and slaves could participate in philosophical conversation as equals. Though Church fathers denounced Epicureanism as libertine, philosophers like *Gassendi* and *Hume* rehabilitated it, recognising that Epicurus taught disciplined pleasure, not excess. The modern confusion of epicureanism with gluttony reflects centuries of misreading; the authentic school is austere.
