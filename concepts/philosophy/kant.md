## summary

**Immanuel Kant** (1724–1804) was a Prussian philosopher and the central figure of the *Enlightenment*, whose revolutionary epistemology, metaphysics, and ethics profoundly shaped Western thought. Kant argued that the human mind does not passively receive sense impressions but actively structures experience through categories of space, time, and causality—a "Copernican revolution" in philosophy. His *Critique of Pure Reason* (1781/1787) drew a sharp line between phenomena (things as they appear to us) and noumena (things-in-themselves), limiting knowledge to the phenomenal realm and thereby defending both science and morality against skepticism and dogmatism. In ethics, he rejected consequentialist reasoning and proposed the *categorical imperative*: act only according to maxims you would will as universal laws. Kant's moral philosophy is deontological—grounded in duty and respect for rational agents—and stands as one of the most systematic and influential ethical frameworks in history.

### Life and Career
- Born and lived his entire life in Königsberg, Prussia; a man of habit and routine, yet intellectually revolutionary.
- Studied natural philosophy and metaphysics; initially influenced by Leibniz and Wolff, then awakened from "dogmatic slumber" by reading *David Hume*.
- Spent decades developing his critical philosophy in relative obscurity; published the *Critique of Pure Reason* at age 57 and consolidated his system in the 1780s–1790s.
- His works include the three *Critiques* (*Critique of Pure Reason*, *Critique of Practical Reason*, *Critique of Judgment*), the *Prolegomena*, the *Groundwork*, and essays on politics, religion, anthropology, and the perpetual peace.

### The Critical Project: Epistemology and Metaphysics
- The *Copernican revolution*: Kant inverted the relation between mind and world. Instead of assuming our minds conform to objects, he proposed that objects conform to our minds—we structure experience through *a priori* categories (space, time, causality, substance, etc.).
- *A priori* knowledge (like mathematics and natural science) is possible because these categories are universal features of human cognition, not derived from experience but constitutive of it.
- Phenomena and noumena: we have knowledge only of things as they appear to us (phenomena), structured by our cognitive forms; things-in-themselves (noumena) lie beyond knowledge. This distinction secures science against skepticism while denying metaphysical knowledge of God, the soul, or the infinite.
- Analytic and synthetic judgments: Kant distinguished judgments that are analytic (the predicate is contained in the subject, e.g., "bachelors are unmarried") from synthetic (the predicate adds new information, e.g., "the sky is blue"). His innovation was *synthetic a priori* knowledge—like geometry and physics—which is not tautological but grounded in the structure of cognition.

### Moral Philosophy: The Categorical Imperative
- Ethics is grounded in *pure practical reason*, not in consequences or happiness (*eudaimonia*). An action is moral if and only if it flows from duty and conforms to the moral law.
- The *categorical imperative* is the supreme moral principle: "Act only according to that maxim whereby you can, at the same time, will that it should become a universal law."
- A maxim is the principle or reason for one's action. Moral maxims pass the universalizability test; immoral ones contradict themselves when universalized (e.g., "make false promises when convenient" cannot be universalized because promises would become impossible).
- Second formulation: act so as to treat humanity, in your own person and in that of others, always as an end in itself, never merely as means. This grounds the dignity of rational agents and forbids exploitation.
- *Respect for the moral law* is the sole proper motive for moral action; actions done from inclination (even generous ones) lack moral worth unless done from duty.

### Transcendental Idealism and the Two Standpoints
- Kant argues we must adopt two standpoints: from the phenomenal standpoint, we are determined by natural laws (causality); from the noumenal standpoint, we are free rational agents capable of following moral law.
- This reconciles scientific determinism with moral freedom: nature is fully causal, but we, as rational beings, transcend nature through reason and moral autonomy.
- Autonomy (*Autonomie*) is the capacity to legislate moral law for oneself; it is the ground of dignity and the highest value in Kantian ethics.

### Aesthetics and Teleology
- The *Critique of Judgment* extends Kant's system to aesthetic experience and purposiveness in nature. Aesthetic judgments are subjective but claim universal validity; beauty involves a harmony between imagination and understanding.
- Purposiveness (*Zweckmässigkeit*) appears in nature (organisms, ecosystems) without requiring a teleological metaphysics; we view nature *as if* designed without asserting a transcendent designer.

## context

Kant is the watershed in modern European philosophy. Before Kant, rationalist and empiricist schools battled over the foundations of knowledge; Kant synthesized both by showing that reason and experience are inseparable partners, each constrained by the form of human cognition. His critical philosophy established the framework—subjects actively constitute their world; knowledge is limited to phenomena; reason must be justified within its proper bounds—that every major subsequent philosophy (German Idealism, Pragmatism, Phenomenology, Logical Positivism) had to reckon with. In ethics, Kant's deontological framework stands as the primary modern alternative to consequentialism and utilitarianism; his insistence on duty, universality, and the dignity of rational agents shaped liberal democratic theory and remains the basis of much contemporary moral philosophy. His influence on political philosophy—the social contract, rights, the rule of law—is incalculable. Kant also pioneered the project of meta-philosophy: asking not just about the world but about the preconditions and limits of human knowledge itself. His central insight—that our minds are not passive mirrors of reality but active structures shaping experience—remains foundational to epistemology, psychology, neuroscience, and artificial intelligence research. Kant's prose is notoriously difficult, and he has spawned two centuries of interpretation; nevertheless, his influence permeates modern thought wherever questions of knowledge, morality, and human freedom are taken seriously.
