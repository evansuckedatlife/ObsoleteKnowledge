# René Descartes

## summary

**René Descartes** (1596–1650) was a French mathematician, natural philosopher, and metaphysician who established modern philosophy's foundational concerns: the nature of mind, the existence of God, and the possibility of knowledge. Descartes pioneered the *method of doubt*—systematically questioning every belief until reaching an indubitable foundation—and famously concluded "*Cogito, ergo sum*" (I think, therefore I am), making the thinking subject the ground of all knowledge. His *substance dualism* (the doctrine that mind and body are two distinct substances) established the mind-body problem that has haunted Western philosophy for 350 years. Descartes also made fundamental contributions to mathematics (Cartesian coordinates), natural science (laws of motion, refraction), and metaphysics (the existence of God, the nature of material reality). His systematic method and commitment to rational inquiry secured his status as the founder of *Rationalism* and a watershed figure of the Scientific Revolution.

## you gotta know

- Descartes was trained in Jesuit scholasticism but rejected Aristotelian physics; he sought a new philosophical method grounded in mathematical certainty and rational doubt.
- The *method of doubt*: systematically doubt everything that can possibly be doubted—sensory experience (illusions, dreams), logic (perhaps a deceiver clouds even reason), even one's own existence. The goal is to find an indubitable foundation for knowledge.
- "*Cogito, ergo sum*" ("I think, therefore I am"): even if he is deceived about everything, the fact of his thinking proves he exists. The thinking subject cannot be doubted away; it is the Archimedean point of certainty.
- *Substance dualism*: mind (res cogitans, thinking substance) and body (res extensa, extended substance) are two distinct kinds of substance; the mind is immaterial and not subject to physical laws, while the body is pure extension.
- The *mind-body interaction problem*: if mind and body are separate substances, how do they causally interact? Descartes struggled with this; he proposed the pineal gland as the seat of interaction, but the problem remains unresolved in his framework.
- Descartes proved God's existence through the *trademark argument*: the idea of infinity (God) in a finite mind (us) must have been caused by an actual infinite being; a finite mind could not generate this idea. God's existence guarantees that reason is reliable.
- In natural philosophy, Descartes rejected substantial forms and final causes (Aristotelian concepts) and proposed *mechanism*: the physical world operates via efficient causes and the contact of bodies in motion, like a machine.
- His *Discourse on Method* (1637) and *Meditations on First Philosophy* (1641) are the canonical texts; they are written in accessible prose and presented as a personal intellectual journey, not a system of doctrines.
