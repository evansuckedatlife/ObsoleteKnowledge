## summary

*Logic* is the systematic study of the principles of valid reasoning and inference—the laws that govern the transition from premises to conclusions. It is the foundation of philosophy, mathematics, and science, distinguishing sound arguments from fallacies. *Formal logic* (also called symbolic or mathematical logic) treats reasoning as a formal system using symbols and rules; *informal logic* examines reasoning in natural language. Logic asks: What makes an argument valid? What conditions must be met for a conclusion to follow necessarily from premises? The earliest formal system, *Aristotle's* logic of the *syllogism*, dominated Western thought for nearly 2,000 years; modern symbolic logic (19th century onward) vastly expanded its scope, enabling the formalization of mathematics and computer science.

## you gotta know

- *Aristotle* invented formal logic and the *syllogism*, the canonical form of deductive argument: "All men are mortal; Socrates is a man; therefore, Socrates is mortal." A syllogism has a major premise, minor premise, and conclusion.
- *Deductive reasoning* (or deduction) draws conclusions that necessarily follow from premises; if the premises are true and the argument is valid, the conclusion must be true. Deduction guarantees certainty.
- *Inductive reasoning* (or induction) generalizes from particular cases to universal conclusions; it is ampliative (the conclusion goes beyond the premises) and does not guarantee certainty, but makes probable conclusions. Scientific inference is largely inductive.
- The *law of non-contradiction*: nothing can both be and not be in the same respect at the same time. This is the foundational logical law; rejection of it seems to make reasoning impossible.
- A *fallacy* is an invalid argument that may appear valid but violates the rules of logic. Examples include *ad hominem* (attacking the speaker rather than the argument), *begging the question* (assuming the conclusion in the premises), and *false dilemma* (presenting only two options when more exist).
- *Formal logic* uses symbols (propositional variables, quantifiers, logical connectives) to represent arguments in a precise notation, allowing mechanical checking of validity.
- In the 19th–20th centuries, *Boole*, *Frege*, *Russell*, and others developed symbolic logic, enabling the formalization of mathematics and revealing the deep structure of logical inference. This work led to modern computability theory and formal languages in computer science.
- *Truth tables* and *natural deduction* are methods of checking validity; *Gödel's incompleteness theorems* revealed that no formal system capturing arithmetic can be both complete and consistent—a revolutionary limit on formal logic.

## connections

- [[aristotle]] — the inventor of formal logic and the syllogism; logic was his "organon" (tool) for all inquiry.
- [[syllogism]] — the classical form of deductive argument, the centerpiece of Aristotelian logic.
- [[deductive-reasoning]] — logic as the study of necessary inference; the ideal of reasoning.
- [[inductive-reasoning]] — the alternative pattern of inference; generalizing from cases to principles.
- [[law-of-non-contradiction]] — the foundational principle of logic; the law that makes reasoning possible.
- [[fallacy]] — logic's counterpart; the study of invalid arguments and errors in reasoning.
- [[philosophy]] — logic is the foundation of philosophical method; all rigorous philosophy depends on logical principles.
- [[mathematics]] — modern logic is inseparable from mathematics; formal systems enable both.
- [[bertrand-russell]] — 20th-century logician who pioneered symbolic logic and revealed paradoxes in naive set theory.
- [[gödel-incompleteness]] — the revolutionary limit on formal logic; no consistent formal system is complete.
