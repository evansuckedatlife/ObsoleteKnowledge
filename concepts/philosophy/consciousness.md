# Consciousness

## summary

*Consciousness* is subjective, first-person experience—the felt quality of seeing red, tasting chocolate, or feeling pain. It is the hard problem of philosophy: explaining how physical matter (the brain) generates subjective experience, why it feels like something to have experiences, and how the immaterial mind relates to the material body. *Phenomenal consciousness* (what experiences are like) is distinguished from *access consciousness* (having information available for reasoning and action). Though consciousness has been a perennial philosophical problem, it became acute in modern philosophy after *Descartes* made the thinking subject foundational and established the *mind-body problem*. Contemporary consciousness studies span philosophy, neuroscience, psychology, and artificial intelligence, but the core puzzle remains: how to bridge the explanatory gap between objective physical description and subjective experience.

## you gotta know

- *Phenomenal consciousness* is the subjective quality of experience, often called *qualia*—the redness of red, the painfulness of pain. These are first-person, private, and difficult to convey to others or analyze objectively.
- The *hard problem of consciousness* (formulated by *David Chalmers*): explaining *why* physical processes produce subjective experience at all. Why isn't the processing of visual stimuli purely unconscious, like the filtering of unwanted noise?
- The *explanatory gap*: there seems to be a logical gap between objective, physical descriptions of the brain and subjective experience. Knowing all the neural correlates of pain does not seem to explain what pain feels like.
- *Physicalism* (or materialism) holds that consciousness is ultimately physical—either identical to brain states or supervenant on them. But how physics can generate the felt quality of experience remains obscure.
- *Dualism* (mind-body dualism) proposes that consciousness is non-physical, a separate substance causally interacting with the body. This avoids the hard problem but raises the *interaction problem*: how can a non-physical mind affect a physical body?
- *Panpsychism* is the view that consciousness (or proto-consciousness) is a fundamental feature of the physical world, present in all matter at some level. This avoids the hard problem but faces charges of implausibility and vagueness.
- *Functionalism* defines mental states by their functional roles—their causes, effects, and relations to other mental states—rather than their intrinsic character. This fits consciousness into a physicalist ontology but may ignore the qualitative, subjective dimension.
- The *hard problem* should be distinguished from the *easy problems* of consciousness—explaining behavioral and cognitive capacities (attention, memory, discrimination). These are scientifically tractable; consciousness is not.
