# Skepticism

## summary

*Skepticism* in philosophy is the position that knowledge—or at least certainty—cannot be reliably attained, and that rational suspension of judgment (*epoché*) is the wisest response to dogmatic claims. Emerging from *Pyrrho of Elis* in the 4th century BCE, skepticism does not claim "nothing can be known" dogmatically, but rather argues that for every plausible argument, an equally plausible counter-argument exists. Rather than paralysis, ancient skeptics sought *ataraxia* (tranquility) by abandoning the fruitless quest for absolute certainty and accepting practical guidance from custom and appearance. Skepticism shaped medieval and modern epistemology, from *Montaigne* through *Hume* to contemporary discussions of justification and belief.

## you gotta know

### Ancient Skepticism
- *Pyrrho of Elis* (~365–275 BCE) taught that suspension of judgment is the path to tranquility; he traveled with Alexander and incorporated non-Western philosophies.
- *Carneades* (~214–129 BCE), head of the Academy, developed systematic skeptical arguments against Stoic epistemology and theory of perception.
- Skeptics did not claim "nothing is knowable" (a dogmatic assertion) but argued that certainty is unattainable and dogmatism self-defeating.

### Core Doctrines
- *Equipollence* (*isostheneia*): for any convincing argument, an equally convincing counter-argument can be posed.
- *Epoché* (suspension of judgment): refusal to assent to dogmatic claims; a practical stance, not paralysis.
- *Ataraxia* as the goal: mental tranquility achieved by abandoning the futile pursuit of absolute certainty.

### Modern Skeptical Thought
- *Montaigne* (16th century) revived Pyrrho and used skepticism to critique dogmatism and pride in human reason.
- *Hume* (18th century) argued that causation, induction, and the self are empirically unwarranted, anticipating modern epistemology.
- Contemporary skepticism about knowledge, justification, and the external world remains a central epistemological tradition.

## context

Skepticism addresses a fundamental tension: we crave certainty and systematic knowledge, yet every systematic claim faces objections. Rather than a counsel of despair, ancient skepticism was therapeutic—it promised liberation from anxiety about ever-failing certainty. The tradition recurs whenever confidence in a paradigm (scholasticism, empiricism, rationalism) collides with unresolved objections. Philosophers like Hume used skeptical arguments not to deny knowledge but to clarify what we can and cannot reasonably expect our rational faculties to deliver. In modern epistemology, skeptical challenges (the problem of induction, the external world, foundationalism) remain the standard test of any theory of justification.
