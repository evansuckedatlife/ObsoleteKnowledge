# Negev Desert

## summary

The *Negev* is a triangular arid desert region comprising roughly 4,700 square miles of southern Israel, extending southward from Beer Sheba to the Red Sea port of Eilat. Though harsh, it has supported Bedouin pastoralists and ancient settlements for millennia, and is central to modern Israeli history, economy, and national identity.

## you gotta know

- Derives its name from the Hebrew word *'negev'*, meaning "south"; the Negev has long been Israel's southern frontier.
- Receives between 1 and 8 inches of rainfall annually, depending on elevation; the northern Negev is marginally less arid than the southern portions.
- Home to the *Dead Sea*, the world's lowest land elevation at approximately 1,410 feet below sea level, and to salt deposits of significant economic value.
- Bedouin communities have herded sheep and camels in the Negev for centuries; their traditional settlement patterns and land tenure have been complicated by Israeli state consolidation.
- The *Ramon Crater* (*Makhtesh Ramon*), one of the world's largest erosion craters, offers striking geological formations and is a major tourist destination.
- Ancient Nabataean cities like *Avdat* and *Mampsis* flourished in the Negev as trading hubs on the spice routes connecting Arabia to the Mediterranean.
- Modern Israeli development has included irrigation agriculture, kibbutzim, moshavim, and industrial cities; the Negev is now slated for major population expansion.
