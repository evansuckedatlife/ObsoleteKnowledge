# The Matterhorn

## summary

*The Matterhorn* is an iconic Alpine peak at 14,692 feet on the Switzerland-Italy border, famous for its distinctive pyramidal form and treacherous rock. The first ascent in 1865 by Edward Whymper and his party ended in tragedy—four climbers fell to their deaths on descent—launching the mountain into cultural legend as a symbol of mountaineering ambition and peril.

## you gotta know

- Height of 14,692 feet (4,478 meters); located in the Pennine Alps near Zermatt, Switzerland.
- The pyramid shape and dramatic exposed rock make it visually striking and instantly recognizable; glaciers are minimal at this elevation.
- First ascent on July 14, 1865, by Edward Whymper's party of seven; four died on the descent, including climber Robert Hadow and guide Michel Croz—an event that shocked Victorian Europe.
- The accident galvanized debate about the ethics of mountaineering and the role of guides; it remains the most famous climbing tragedy of the era.
- Climbs typically take 1–2 days; the "normal" route from Zermatt is non-technical scrambling but exposed and requires surefootedness and acclimatization.
- Loose rock and poor weather claim 12–15 climbers per year; the mountain is notoriously dangerous despite its modest elevation.
- Over 3,000 climbers attempt the peak annually; the small season window (June-September) concentrates traffic and hazard.
