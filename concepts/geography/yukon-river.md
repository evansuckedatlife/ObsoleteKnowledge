# Yukon River

## summary

The *Yukon River* flows approximately 1,980 miles through western Canada and Alaska from the Whitehorse region of the Yukon Territory northwestward to the Bering Sea, making it one of North America's longest rivers. It drains a vast subarctic and arctic region and has been central to indigenous life, the Klondike Gold Rush, and modern Alaskan geography.

## you gotta know

- Originates near Whitehorse in the Yukon Territory of Canada, flowing northwestward across the territory before entering Alaska.
- Passes through *Dawson City*, the site of the *Klondike Gold Rush* of the 1890s, which brought thousands of prospectors seeking gold along its banks and tributaries.
- Named from a native Athabaskan word *Youcon* or *Ykon*, meaning "great river."
- Freezes completely in winter and becomes the primary transportation corridor for indigenous peoples and explorers during the brief summer season.
- Flows through wilderness largely untouched by development for most of its course; the landscape ranges from boreal forest to tundra.
- Discharges into the Bering Sea near the Alaska-Russia maritime boundary; historically important for salmon fisheries and indigenous subsistence.
- The river and its valleys contain unique subarctic ecosystems with wildlife including moose, grizzly bears, and migratory caribou herds.
