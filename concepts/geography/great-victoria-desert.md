# Great Victoria Desert

## summary

The *Great Victoria Desert* is Australia's largest desert, sprawling roughly 134,000 square miles across the border of Western Australia and South Australia. Named by the 19th-century explorer *Ernest Giles* in honor of *Queen Victoria*, it is a vast plateau of red sand, spinifex grassland, and rocky outcrops inhabited by Aboriginal peoples and remarkable desert-adapted wildlife.

## you gotta know

- The largest desert in Australia and the fifth-largest in the world; spans nearly 2,000 miles east to west and up to 400 miles north to south.
- Characterized by a mix of sand dunes, stony plains, and scattered shrubland; the red soil comes from iron oxide in the ancient Precambrian rocks beneath.
- Summer temperatures exceed 45°C (113°F); rainfall is highly variable and unpredictable, averaging 6–8 inches annually, with some years passing without significant precipitation.
- Aboriginal peoples—including the Martu, Pitjantjatjara, and Ngaanyatjarra—have inhabited and managed the desert for at least 40,000 years via complex knowledge systems.
- Named by the explorer *Ernest Giles* in 1875 during his transcontinental journey; European contact profoundly disrupted traditional Aboriginal lifeways.
- Wildlife includes kangaroos, emus, bearded dragons, and venomous snakes; the ecosystem relies on deep underground aquifers and sporadic rainfall events.
- The desert contains sacred Aboriginal sites central to songlines, dreaming stories, and spiritual geography; land rights remain contested between Aboriginal groups and the Australian state.
