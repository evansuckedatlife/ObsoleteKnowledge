# Saint Petersburg

## summary

Saint Petersburg is the second-largest city in Russia, located on the Gulf of Finland at the mouth of the Neva River. Founded in 1703 by *Peter the Great* as a deliberate attempt to create a "window to the West," it served as the capital of the Russian Empire for over 200 years. The city became the center of Russian culture, art, and intellectual life; it witnessed the Russian Revolution of 1917 and remains a repository of imperial palaces, museums, and artistic masterworks. Despite renaming to Leningrad during the Soviet period, it remains an icon of imperial ambition and cultural refinement.

## you gotta know

- *Peter the Great* founded St. Petersburg in 1703 as Russia's new capital, deliberately moving power from Moscow to establish closer ties with Western Europe and assert Russian modernization.
- The city was built on swampy, inhospitable terrain at enormous human cost; thousands of serfs died in its construction, but it became one of Europe's most beautiful and planned cities.
- The *Winter Palace*, the imperial residence of the Russian tsars, was the center of political power and is now home to the *Hermitage Museum*, one of the world's greatest art collections.
- St. Petersburg became the intellectual and cultural heart of the Russian Empire, producing literary giants like *Fyodor Dostoevsky* and *Alexander Pushkin* and musical masters like *Pyotr Ilyich Tchaikovsky*.
- The city was renamed *Petrograd* during World War I (due to anti-German sentiment), and was the epicenter of the *Russian Revolution* of 1917; the *Winter Palace* was stormed by revolutionaries in October 1917.
- During the Soviet period, the city was renamed *Leningrad* (1924–1991) and endured a brutal 900-day siege during World War II; the population was devastated by starvation and bombardment.
- St. Petersburg's neoclassical architecture, designed by Italian and Russian architects like Bartolomeo Rastrelli, exemplifies 18th-century imperial grandeur and remains largely preserved.
