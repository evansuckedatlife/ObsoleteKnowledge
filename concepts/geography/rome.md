# Rome

## summary

Rome is the capital of Italy and one of the oldest and most influential cities in human history. Founded in 753 BCE, it became the seat of the Roman Republic and then the Roman Empire, which at its height ruled over one-third of the world's population. Rome's political institutions, legal systems, architectural innovations, and cultural legacy shaped Western civilization profoundly. It remains the spiritual and administrative center of the Catholic Church and is dotted with monuments from millennia of history.

## you gotta know

- Rome was traditionally founded on April 21, 753 BCE; it grew from a settlement of Latin peoples on the Tiber River to dominate the Italian peninsula and eventually the Mediterranean world.
- The *Roman Republic* (c. 509–27 BCE) established representative government, law codes, and the Senate, institutions that influenced all later democracies and constitutional systems.
- The *Roman Empire*, established by *Augustus Caesar* in 27 BCE, expanded to rule from Britain to Mesopotamia; it lasted until 476 CE in the west, becoming the template for empire and governance.
- The *Colosseum*, built 70–80 CE under the Flavian emperors, is the largest surviving Roman structure and hosted gladiatorial combat and public spectacles; it exemplifies Roman engineering and social spectacle.
- Rome's legal traditions—codified in the *Digest*, *Pandects*, and *Justinian Code*—form the foundation of civil law systems used in most of the world (except common-law countries like England and the US).
- *Christianity* became the official religion of the Roman Empire under *Constantine* in 313 CE; Rome remains the seat of the *Catholic Church* and the *Vatican City*, the world's smallest sovereign state.
- Roman architectural innovations—the arch, vault, concrete, and aqueduct—enabled monumental construction and remained the template for Western architecture for centuries.
