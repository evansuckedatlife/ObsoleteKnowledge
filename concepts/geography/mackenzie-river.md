# Mackenzie River

## summary

The *Mackenzie River* flows approximately 1,025 miles northwestward from *Great Slave Lake* to the Arctic Ocean, serving as Canada's largest river by discharge and a crucial feature of the subarctic landscape. It is one of North America's great arctic waterways and has historically been important for fur trading and indigenous navigation.

## you gotta know

- Originates at the outflow of *Great Slave Lake* in the Northwest Territories, draining a vast expanse of Canada's northern interior.
- Named after Scottish-Canadian fur trader *Alexander Mackenzie*, who explored the river in 1789 searching for a route to the Pacific.
- Flows northward through a landscape of tundra, muskeg (spongy terrain), and boreal forest, eventually widening into a massive delta before emptying into the Arctic Ocean near Inuvik.
- Discharges more water into the Arctic Ocean than any other North American river, with enormous seasonal flooding during the brief subarctic summer.
- Freezes over completely in winter, making it impassable for most of the year; spring breakup can be spectacular and destructive.
- Historically the lifeblood of the fur trade in northwestern Canada, with Hudson's Bay Company posts established along its course.
- Home to diverse arctic and subarctic fauna, including muskrats, beavers, and migratory fish species; critical habitat for Dene and Métis peoples.
