# Ancient Greece

## summary

*Ancient Greece* refers to the civilizations of the Greek-speaking peoples from roughly the eighth century BCE until the conquest by Rome in the second century BCE. Centered on the *Aegean Sea* and Mediterranean, ancient Greece was not a unified nation but a collection of independent city-states (*poleis*) dominated by *Athens* and *Sparta*. Ancient Greece created the foundations of Western philosophy, democracy, drama, science, mathematics, architecture, and athletics, establishing cultural forms and intellectual traditions that continue to shape Western civilization.

## you gotta know

- Ancient Greek civilization emerged from the collapse of the Bronze Age *Mycenaean* civilization around 1100 BCE, followed by the *Dark Ages* (1100–800 BCE) before the *Archaic Period* (800–480 BCE) saw the rise of independent city-states.
- The *Aegean Sea* was central to Greek life: its islands and coast facilitated trade, communication, and colonization; Greeks established settlements throughout the Mediterranean and Black Sea regions, spreading Greek language and culture.
- *Athens* and *Sparta* emerged as the dominant city-states: Athens developed *democracy*, the first large-scale system of popular self-governance; Sparta created a militaristic oligarchic state based on a warrior aristocracy.
- The *Peloponnesian Wars* (495–479 BCE) saw Greek city-states unite against Persian invasion, with *Athens* defeating the Persian navy at *Marathon* (490 BCE) and *Salamis* (480 BCE), establishing Athens as a major power.
- The *Classical Period* (480–323 BCE) witnessed Athens' golden age under *Pericles*: the construction of the *Parthenon*, the flowering of drama (*Aeschylus*, *Sophocles*, *Euripides*, *Aristophanes*), and philosophy (*Socrates*, *Plato*, *Aristotle*).
- Greek philosophy established traditions of rational inquiry: the *Stoics*, *Epicureans*, *Cynics*, and *Skeptics* debated fundamental questions of knowledge, ethics, and the good life, creating the intellectual foundations for Western thought.
- Greek democracy in Athens created the world's first system of citizenship and popular participation in governance, though it excluded women, slaves (who comprised 30% of the population), and non-native residents.
- The *Olympic Games*, held every four years at Olympia, were a pan-Hellenic festival celebrating athletic excellence and divine favor, featuring competition in wrestling, boxing, running, discus, javelin, and other sports.
- *Alexander the Great* (356–323 BCE), the Macedonian conqueror, conquered the Persian Empire and extended Greek civilization across Asia, ushering in the *Hellenistic Era*, which blended Greek and Oriental culture.
- Greek drama—tragedy and comedy—was performed at festivals (*City Dionysia*, *Lenaea*) and explored universal human themes of fate, justice, ambition, and the divine; dramatic works like *Oedipus Rex*, *The Odyssey*, and *Medea* remain canonical.

## connections

- [[greece]] — the modern nation occupying the same Mediterranean territory as ancient Greece.
- [[athens]] — the city-state that developed democracy and dominated the Classical period, home to Socrates, Plato, and Aristotle.
- [[sparta]] — the militaristic rival city-state emphasizing warrior culture and rigid social discipline.
- [[alexander-the-great]] — the Macedonian conqueror who spread Greek civilization across three continents.
- [[socrates]] — the philosopher whose questioning method defined Western intellectual tradition.
- [[plato]] — the student of Socrates who founded the Academy and developed idealist philosophy.
- [[aristotle]] — the philosopher who tutored Alexander and created comprehensive systems of logic, metaphysics, and natural science.
- [[the-trojan-war]] — the legendary conflict central to Greek mythology and epic literature.
- [[the-iliad]] — Homer's epic poem recounting the Trojan War, foundational to Greek literature.
- [[the-odyssey]] — Homer's epic of Odysseus' return home, exemplifying Greek narrative artistry.
- [[mediterranean-sea]] — the sea enabling Greek trade, colonization, and cultural exchange.
