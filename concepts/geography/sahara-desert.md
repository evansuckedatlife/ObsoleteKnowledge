# Sahara Desert

## summary

The *Sahara* is the world's largest hot desert, spanning roughly 3.6 million square miles across North Africa from the Atlantic Ocean to the Red Sea. Its harsh climate and vast expanses historically shaped migration patterns, trade routes, and the development of African civilizations, from ancient kingdoms to modern cities built around oases.

## you gotta know

- Stretches across eleven North African countries, including Egypt, Libya, Algeria, Morocco, Tunisia, and Sudan.
- Temperatures regularly exceed 50°C (122°F) during summer; nights can drop near freezing due to the lack of atmospheric moisture.
- The *Sahel* region to its south marks its semi-arid boundary, a critical zone for drought, desertification, and climatic variation.
- Contains the *Rub' al-Khali* (Empty Quarter), one of its largest continuous sand expanses, and the *Erg Chech*, another massive sand sea.
- Supports oasis settlements—towns like *Timbuktu* and *Gao*—which became centers of medieval Islamic scholarship and trans-Saharan commerce.
- The desert is expanding southward at roughly 48 kilometers per decade, a process called desertification, driven by overgrazing and climate change.
- Home to salt mining in places like *Taoudenni*, Mali, which was traded across medieval Africa as a luxury commodity.

## connections

- [[nile-river]] — the lifeline of Egypt on the Sahara's eastern edge.
- [[african-empires]] — Mali, Songhai, and Kanem-Bornu controlled trans-Saharan trade routes.
- [[mansa-musa]] — ruled the Mali Empire and crossed the Sahara on pilgrimage to Mecca.
- [[roman-empire]] — controlled the Sahara's northern coast; the desert was its southern boundary.
- [[islam-in-africa]] — spread southward via merchants and traders crossing the Sahara.
