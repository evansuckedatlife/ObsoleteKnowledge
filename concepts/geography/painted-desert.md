# Painted Desert

## summary

The *Painted Desert* is a semi-arid badlands region in northeastern Arizona, stretching roughly 100 miles east to west across the Colorado Plateau. Named for the vivid bands of color in its exposed rock layers—blues, purples, reds, and yellows—it is renowned among geologists for its paleontological deposits and among artists for its dramatic, otherworldly landscape.

## you gotta know

- The colorful striations come from iron oxide, manganese oxides, and other mineral compounds in sedimentary layers; the colors shift throughout the day with changing light.
- Adjacent to the *Petrified Forest National Park*, which contains one of the world's largest deposits of Triassic-era petrified wood (roughly 225 million years old).
- Archaeological sites throughout the region contain evidence of ancestral Pueblo and Navajo habitation; the Navajo Nation borders and partially encompasses parts of it.
- The landscape was shaped by erosion of the Colorado Plateau and lacks significant vegetation; annual rainfall is typically 4–10 inches, making it a semi-arid rather than true desert.
- Contains important Triassic fossil beds with early dinosaur and synapsid remains; paleontologists have recovered skeletons of early phytosaurs and primitive mammals.
- The name "Painted Desert" was popularized in the early 20th century by railroad companies and tourism literature highlighting its scenic beauty.
- Navajo artisans have long used pigments from the Painted Desert's rocks in traditional weaving and art.
