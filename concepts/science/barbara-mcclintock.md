# Barbara McClintock

## summary

American geneticist who discovered transposable elements (jumping genes) in maize—segments of DNA that move within the genome and regulate neighbouring genes. Her work revealed that the genome was dynamic and controllable, overturning the view of genes as static, unchanging units. She conducted her entire career at Cold Spring Harbor Laboratory and won the Nobel Prize in 1983, the year before her death.

## you gotta know

- Discovered *transposable elements* (1940s–1950s): DNA sequences that could move from one location to another on chromosomes, causing visible mutations in corn kernels.
- Observed *variegated corn kernels* with spontaneous colour patterns; genetic analysis traced the instability to mobile genetic elements she called "Ac" and "Ds" (Activator/Dissociation).
- Realized that transposable elements could *regulate gene expression* — turning genes on and off — a mechanism of genetic control unknown before her work.
- Her theories were initially met with scepticism; the molecular revolution of the 1960s–1970s vindicated her: transposons were found in bacteria, fungi, and humans.
- Worked in isolation at Cold Spring Harbor for decades, conducting meticulous genetic experiments with maize over generations.
- Recognised that genetic variation and evolution depended not just on random mutation but on the genome's ability to reorganise itself.

## connections

- [[thomas-hunt-morgan]] — predecessor who established corn and *Drosophila* as model organisms; McClintock used corn to reveal deeper mechanisms.
- [[james-watson-and-francis-crick]] — contemporaries who decoded DNA structure; transposable elements were later explained as physical DNA sequences.
- [[alexander-fleming]] — peer in experimental biology; both revealed hidden mechanisms through careful observation and rigorous experimental design.
