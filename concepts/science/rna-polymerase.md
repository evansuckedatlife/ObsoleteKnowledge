## summary

RNA polymerase catalyzes *transcription*—the synthesis of RNA from a DNA template strand in the 5' to 3' direction. Unlike DNA polymerase, it requires no primer, initiates synthesis de novo, and uses ribonucleotides (NTPs) instead of deoxyribonucleotides. The enzyme recognizes promoter sequences to start transcription and continues until termination signals halt synthesis.

## you gotta know

- Catalyzes: formation of phosphodiester bonds between ribonucleotides (NTPs)
- Reads template: DNA 3' → 5' direction, synthesizing RNA 5' → 3'
- No primer needed: initiates transcription directly; major difference from DNA polymerase
- Prokaryotic forms: single RNA polymerase catalyzes all transcription
- Eukaryotic forms: three main polymerases—Pol I (rRNA), Pol II (mRNA), Pol III (tRNA); each recognizes different promoter elements
- Promoters: binding sites where RNA polymerase recognizes and unwinds DNA; -10 and -35 boxes in *E. coli*, TATA box in eukaryotes
- Termination: intrinsic (rho-independent) hairpin structures or rho protein-dependent (prokaryotes); polyadenylation signals in eukaryotes

## context

Transcription is the first step of gene expression—converting genetic instructions into functional RNA molecules. RNA polymerase is the molecular reader that makes this conversion possible. Unlike replication, which copies the entire genome with nearly perfect fidelity, transcription is selective and regulated; cells turn genes on and off through RNA polymerase binding and activity. The enzyme's ability to initiate without a primer reflects that transcription has clear start signals (promoters) imposed by evolution, while replication's primer requirement evolved as a checkpoint mechanism.
