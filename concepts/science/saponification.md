## summary

Saponification is an organic chemical reaction that hydrolyzes an ester under basic conditions to yield an alcohol and the salt of a carboxylic acid. It is best known historically and commercially as the process used to manufacture soap by reacting animal fats or vegetable oils (triglycerides) with a strong aqueous base.

## you gotta know

- *Proceeds via an irreversible nucleophilic acyl substitution*, where hydroxide ions attack the ester carbonyl, forming a tetrahedral intermediate that collapses to expel an alkoxide ion, which instantly deprotonates the resulting carboxylic acid.
- *Driven to completion thermodynamically* by the final deprotonation step, which forms a resonance-stabilized carboxylate anion that is completely unreactive toward nucleophiles.
- *Hydrolyzes triglycerides* (triesters of glycerol and fatty acids) to yield glycerol and three molecules of soap (fatty acid carboxylate salts).
- *Characterized by the saponification value*, an analytical index defined as the mass of potassium hydroxide in milligrams required to saponify one gram of fat, which is inversely proportional to the average molecular weight of the fatty acids.
- *Determines soap texture based on the base used*, where sodium hydroxide (NaOH) produces hard bar soaps, and potassium hydroxide (KOH) produces soft or liquid soaps.

## connections

- [[ester]] — esters are the chemical substrates cleaved by basic hydrolysis in this reaction.
- [[sodium]] — sodium hydroxide is the standard lye reagent used to produce hard soaps.
- [[potassium]] — potassium hydroxide is the standard reagent used to produce soft, liquid, or shaving soaps.
