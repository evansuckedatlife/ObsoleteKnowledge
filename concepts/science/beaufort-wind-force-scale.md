# Beaufort wind force scale

## summary

The **Beaufort wind force scale** ranks wind strength from 0 (calm) to 12+ (hurricane) based on observable effects on land and sea. Developed in 1805 by Irish-born Royal Navy officer *Francis Beaufort*, it provided a standardized descriptive framework long before reliable anemometers existed. Wind speeds are now formally linked to the scale's categories.

## you gotta know

- Named after *Admiral Sir Francis Beaufort*, who developed the scale to standardize wind observations across maritime operations.
- Scale ranges from *Force 0* (calm, < 1 km/h) to *Force 12* (hurricane, ≥ 118 km/h), with descriptive observations (smoke rises vertically at Force 0; trees uprooted at Force 12).
- Each force level corresponds to observable sea state and land effects: leaves rustle (Force 2), twigs snap (Force 6), chimneys blown down (Force 8), widespread damage (Force 11).
- Wind speed roughly follows a power law relationship: each increase of one Beaufort force does not represent a fixed speed increase but a nonlinear progression.
- The scale is *ordinal* (ordered categories), not precisely logarithmic, though some approximations exist to map Beaufort to wind speed.
- Historically crucial for sailors and meteorologists who lacked instruments; observations like "small waves begin to break" or "spray affects visibility" translated into systematic wind classification.
- Extended beyond Force 12 in modern meteorology; the Saffir–Simpson scale is more precise for tropical cyclones.
