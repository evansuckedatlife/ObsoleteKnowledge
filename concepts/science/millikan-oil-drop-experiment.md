# Millikan Oil-Drop Experiment

## summary

Between 1909 and 1916, *Robert Millikan* sprayed tiny oil droplets into an *electric field* and measured their fall velocity to determine the *charge of the electron*. By balancing *gravity* against electric force, he demonstrated that electric *charge* comes in discrete units (*quantised*) and proved the electron's mass and charge to stunning precision.

## you gotta know

- *Millikan* suspended oil droplets in air using an upward *electric field*, adjusting voltage until the electrostatic force balanced the droplet's weight, halting its fall.
- By measuring the droplet's mass (from its density and size, calculated from a separate terminal-velocity measurement) and the field strength, he computed the electric charge on each droplet.
- He observed that charge always appeared in integer multiples of a base unit: the charge on a *single electron* (`e ≈ 1.602 × 10^−19` coulombs).
- The experiment vindicated the *quantisation* of charge — a radical idea that was not universally accepted before his measurements.
- His precision was extraordinary for the era; his values remained the accepted standard for decades.
- The experiment elegantly reduced a submicroscopic property (electron charge) to macroscopic observations (droplet motion, field voltage).

## connections

- [[electron]] — the fundamental particle whose charge he quantified.
- [[charge]] — proven to be discrete, not continuous.
- [[quantisation]] — the principle that some quantities come only in fixed units.
- [[electric-field]] — the tool used to suspend the droplets.
- [[photoelectric-effect]] — another quantum phenomenon *Einstein* and *Millikan* both studied.
