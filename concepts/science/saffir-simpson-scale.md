# Saffir–Simpson scale

## summary

The **Saffir–Simpson scale** classifies tropical cyclones (hurricanes) into five categories (1–5) based on sustained wind speed and potential damage. Developed in the 1970s by civil engineer *Herbert Saffir* and meteorologist *Robert Simpson*, it provides a standardized framework for communicating hurricane intensity and expected impact to the public. Category 5 represents the most intense and destructive storms.

## you gotta know

- Named after *Herbert Saffir* (civil engineer) and *Robert Simpson* (meteorologist) who jointly developed the scale for the National Hurricane Center.
- *Category 1*: winds 119–153 km/h; minimal damage. *Category 2*: 154–177 km/h; moderate damage. *Category 3*: 178–208 km/h; severe damage. *Category 4*: 209–251 km/h; catastrophic damage. *Category 5*: ≥ 252 km/h; catastrophic damage and uninhabitable conditions for weeks.
- The scale is based *solely* on wind speed; it does not directly account for rainfall, storm surge, or freshwater flooding, which can be equally or more destructive than wind.
- More precise and useful than the Beaufort scale for tropical cyclones because it focuses on a specific storm type and provides quantitative wind thresholds.
- A Category 3 or higher hurricane is considered a *major hurricane*.
- The scale has been revised slightly over time; damage descriptions and thresholds have been refined to reflect observed impacts.
- Widely used by meteorologists, emergency management, and media to communicate hurricane risk and prepare evacuations.
