# Dicots

## summary

**Dicots** are *flowering plants* with two *cotyledons* (seed leaves), comprising roughly 70% of angiosperm diversity. The group includes most broadleaf trees, shrubs, and herbaceous flowering plants — from oaks and roses to beans and daisies — and dominates temperate and tropical forests.

## you gotta know

- Two *cotyledons* in the embryo; monocots have one, affecting seedling development and root/shoot structure.
- Leaves typically have *reticulate venation* (branching vein network forming a web-like pattern) rather than monocot *parallel venation*.
- Vascular bundles arranged in a *ring* around the stem (clear cortex and pith regions); this architecture supports strong secondary growth (wood formation).
- Deep *taproot* system (one central root with branches) in many dicots, though some develop fibrous roots; enables deep water access in established plants.
- Flower parts typically in *multiples of four or five* (e.g. 5 petals, 5 sepals, many stamens) rather than threes.
- Include most broadleaf forest trees (oaks, maples, beeches), most wildflowers, legumes (beans, peas, clovers), and composites (daisies, sunflowers).

## connections

- [[monocots]] — the complementary major angiosperm division; dicots and monocots together comprise nearly all flowering plants.
- [[angiosperms]] — dicots are one of two major subclasses of angiosperms.
- [[cotyledon]] — the two seed leaves that define the dicot clade.
- [[seed]] — dicot seeds contain two cotyledons.
- [[xylem]] — dicots develop secondary xylem (wood) via a *vascular cambium*.
