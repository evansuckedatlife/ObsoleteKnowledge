## summary

**Flow cytometry** is an analytical method that counts and sorts individual cells or particles based on their size, granularity, and fluorescence. Cells are stained with fluorescent-labeled antibodies, drawn in a single-file stream through a laser beam, and detected in real time, enabling rapid profiling of cell populations and isolation of subsets.

## you gotta know

- *Single-cell analysis* — cells are suspended in fluid and forced through a narrow channel (sheath fluid) so they pass one at a time through a laser.
- *Scatter properties* — *forward scatter* (light bending straight ahead) correlates with cell size; *side scatter* (light diffracted at 90°) reflects internal complexity (granularity, DNA content).
- *Fluorescent labels* — cells are stained with fluorescent antibodies (e.g., anti-CD4 for T cells); each antibody-dye combination excites at a different wavelength, allowing simultaneous detection of multiple markers (multicolor panels).
- *Real-time detection* — as each cell passes the laser, emitted light is collected by detectors and converted to digital signals; tens of thousands of cells are analyzed per second.
- *Cell sorting* — selected populations (e.g., CD4+ T cells) can be electronically sorted into separate collection tubes for downstream culture or analysis.
- Used in immunology (T-cell counts in HIV), cancer diagnostics (leukemia classification), vaccine development, and basic cell biology.

## connections

- [[cell]] — the unit of analysis; each event in the cytometer is one cell.
- [[fluorescence]] — the detection principle; fluorescent dyes emit light when excited.
- [[antibody]] — staining reagents that bind cell-surface or intracellular antigens.
- [[immunology]] — often used to profile immune cell populations and their activation states.
