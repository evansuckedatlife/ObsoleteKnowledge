# Quartz

## summary

**Quartz** is silicon dioxide (SiO₂), the second most abundant mineral in Earth's crust. It forms in igneous, sedimentary, and metamorphic rocks, and is valued for its hardness, clarity, and piezoelectric properties. Its varieties range from transparent crystals to coloured gemstones like amethyst and rose quartz.

## you gotta know

- *Silicon dioxide* — the chemical formula is SiO₂, making it a *silicate mineral*.
- Rated 7 on the *Mohs hardness scale*, harder than most common minerals but softer than corundum and diamond.
- Forms beautiful *hexagonal crystals* when given space to grow; cloudy varieties result from impurities and rapid cooling.
- Piezoelectric — generates electrical charge when compressed, used in watches, electronics, and oscillators.
- *Transparent varieties* (rock crystal, amethyst, citrine, rose quartz) are prized gemstones; opaque types form *sandstone* when cemented together.
- Melts at ~1710°C; used to make glass, ceramics, and semiconductors.
- Acid-resistant and chemically stable, which is why quartz persists in sedimentary rocks after other minerals weather away.

## connections

- [[granite]] — quartz is a primary constituent of granite alongside feldspar and mica.
- [[sandstone]] — formed from cemented quartz grains, often derived from the weathering of quartz-rich rocks.
- [[silicate]] — quartz is the simplest silicate mineral, lacking aluminium in its structure.
- [[mohs-scale]] — quartz is the reference point (hardness 7) for mineral hardness testing.
- [[feldspar]] — companion mineral in granitic rocks, though feldspar is softer and more prone to weathering.
