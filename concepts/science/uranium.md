# Uranium

## summary

Uranium is a heavy, silvery-white, weakly radioactive actinide metal that is the heaviest naturally occurring element in significant abundance on Earth. Because of its atomic instability, all isotopes of uranium are radioactive and decay over time. It is a critical fuel source for nuclear power plants and the primary fissile material used in the first nuclear weapons due to the properties of its isotope uranium-235.

## you gotta know

- *Discovered by Martin Heinrich Klaproth* in 1789 in the mineral pitchblende, who named the element after the recently discovered planet *Uranus*.
- *Revealed the phenomenon of radioactivity* in 1896, when Henri Becquerel discovered that uranium crystals emitted invisible rays that could expose photographic plates.
- *Mined chiefly as uraninite or pitchblende* and refined into a concentrated oxide powder known as *yellowcake* (composed mainly of triuranium octoxide, U3O8).
- *Separated into isotopes via uranium hexafluoride* (UF6), a volatile compound that is fed into gas centrifuges or gaseous diffusion barriers to enrich the fissile uranium-235 content.
- *Undergoes induced nuclear fission* when struck by thermal neutrons, a chain reaction that releases immense energy and is the operating principle of nuclear reactors.

## connections

- [[helium]] — the radioactive alpha decay of uranium in the crust is the source of terrestrial helium gas.
- [[gold]] — uranium mineralizations frequently occur alongside gold in ancient conglomerates, allowing joint mining operations.
- [[plutonium]] — neutron capture by uranium-238 in breeder reactors produces uranium-239, which decays into neptunium-239 and then plutonium-239.
