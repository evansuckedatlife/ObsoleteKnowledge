# Limestone

## summary

**Limestone** is a sedimentary rock composed primarily of calcium carbonate (calcite), typically formed from the skeletal material and shells of marine organisms. It is abundant in shallow marine deposits and forms cliffs, caves, and karst landscapes. Limestone is economically important as a construction material, aggregate, and source of lime (calcium oxide) for cement and steel production.

## you gotta know

- *Sedimentary rock* formed in shallow marine environments from the accumulation of shell fragments, skeletal debris, and inorganic carbonate precipitation.
- Composed almost entirely of *calcite* (CaCO₃); often contains fossil shells, corals, and foraminifera that are visible to the naked eye.
- Fizzes vigorously when exposed to dilute *hydrochloric acid* — a definitive field test that confirms the presence of carbonate minerals.
- Light coloured — typically white, cream, grey, or tan — due to its pure carbonate composition and lack of iron.
- Soluble in slightly acidic water, leading to *karst topography*: sinkholes, caves, underground streams, and dramatic cliff formations.
- When buried and heated, limestone metamorphoses to *marble*, which is harder and denser due to recrystallisation of calcite.
- Major industrial uses: crushed for aggregate, burned to produce lime (CaO) for cement and steel-making, and used as dimension stone.

## connections

- [[calcite]] — the primary mineral component of limestone; identifies it as a carbonate rock.
- [[marble]] — metamorphic rock formed from limestone buried and heated; harder and more compact than its parent limestone.
- [[sedimentary-rock]] — limestone is the quintessential biochemical sedimentary rock, formed from organism activity.
- [[metamorphic-rock]] — limestone becomes marble when subjected to burial heat and pressure.
- [[fossil]] — limestone preserves fossils abundantly because shells and skeletal material accumulate on the seafloor.
