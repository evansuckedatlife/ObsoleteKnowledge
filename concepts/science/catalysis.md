# Catalysis

## summary

*Catalysis* is the acceleration of a chemical reaction by a substance—a *catalyst*—that participates in the reaction but is not consumed by it. Catalysts lower the *activation energy* required for a reaction to proceed, allowing it to reach equilibrium faster without shifting the position of that equilibrium. Catalysis is central to chemistry, biology, and industrial processes.

## you gotta know

- A *catalyst* provides an alternative reaction pathway with lower *activation energy*, enabling reactants to form products more rapidly.
- Catalysts are **regenerated** at the end of the reaction—they are not consumed stoichiometrically and can be reused indefinitely (or until deactivated).
- The catalyst does **not** change the *Gibbs free energy* (ΔG) or equilibrium position of the reaction; it only accelerates approach to equilibrium.
- *Enzymes* are biological catalysts—proteins (usually) that accelerate metabolic reactions with extraordinary specificity and efficiency.
- The *active site* of an enzyme is the region where substrate molecules bind and catalysis occurs; it typically uses *non-covalent interactions* (H-bonds, van der Waals) to stabilise the *transition state*.
- *Homogeneous catalysis*: catalyst is dissolved in the same phase as reactants (e.g., acid-catalysed esterification in solution).
- *Heterogeneous catalysis*: catalyst exists in a different phase—commonly a solid catalyst for gas or liquid reactants (e.g., platinum in catalytic converters).
- *Competitive inhibition* of enzymes occurs when an inhibitor competes with substrate for the active site; *non-competitive inhibition* binds elsewhere and impairs catalysis regardless of substrate concentration.
