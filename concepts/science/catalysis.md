# Catalysis

## summary

*Catalysis* is the acceleration of a chemical reaction by a substance—a *catalyst*—that is not permanently consumed. Catalysts work by lowering the *activation energy*, the energy barrier that must be surmounted for reactants to transform into products, allowing reactions to reach equilibrium far more rapidly. Catalysis is universal: it governs industrial chemistry (ammonia synthesis, petroleum refining), all biological processes (enzyme-driven metabolism), and environmental phenomena (ozone depletion). Understanding catalysis is essential across chemistry, biochemistry, materials science, and environmental science.

## you gotta know

### Mechanism and Principles
- A *catalyst* provides an alternative reaction pathway with lower *activation energy* (*Ea*), enabling reactants to transform into products more rapidly without changing the inherent thermodynamics.
- Catalysts are **regenerated** unchanged at the end of the reaction—they are not consumed stoichiometrically—and can be reused indefinitely (until poisoned or deactivated).
- The catalyst does **not** change the *Gibbs free energy* (ΔG) or equilibrium constant (*K*) of the reaction; it only accelerates approach to equilibrium and does not favour products or reactants thermodynamically.
- The *transition state* is the highest-energy configuration along the reaction pathway; catalysts stabilize this state via direct binding, reducing the energy difference between reactants and transition state.

### Types and Examples
- *Homogeneous catalysis*: catalyst is dissolved in the same phase as reactants (e.g., acid-catalysed esterification in aqueous solution, sulfuric acid in organic synthesis).
- *Heterogeneous catalysis*: catalyst exists in a different phase—commonly a solid catalyst on which gas or liquid reactants adsorb and react (e.g., platinum in automotive catalytic converters, iron in the Haber-Bosch ammonia synthesis).
- *Enzymatic catalysis*: biological catalysts (enzymes, usually proteins) accelerate metabolic reactions with extraordinary specificity; enzymes achieve rate accelerations of 10^6 to 10^17 times.

### Enzyme Action and Inhibition
- The *active site* of an enzyme is the region where substrate molecules bind and catalysis occurs; it uses *non-covalent interactions* (hydrogen bonds, van der Waals forces, electrostatic) to stabilize the transition state and lower *Ea*.
- *Substrate specificity*: enzymes recognize and bind only specific substrates, allowing cells to control metabolism with precision.
- *Competitive inhibition*: an inhibitor competes with substrate for the active site; it can be overcome by increasing substrate concentration.
- *Non-competitive inhibition*: an inhibitor binds elsewhere (an allosteric site) and impairs catalysis regardless of substrate concentration, often used for metabolic regulation.

## context

Catalysis is one of the pillars of modern civilization. The *Haber-Bosch process* (ammonia synthesis using an iron catalyst) feeds billions by enabling synthetic fertilizer; catalytic cracking of petroleum refined the oil industry; platinum and palladium catalysts enable much of fine organic chemistry; and enzymes make life possible. Industrially, catalysis reduces energy consumption, increases selectivity, and enables "green" chemistry. In environmental science, catalysts explain ozone destruction (chlorine radical catalysis) and air pollution mitigation. In biochemistry, enzymes are the primary mechanism by which cells control reaction rates and direct metabolism. Catalysis appears constantly in chemistry, biochemistry, and materials-science problems, often in the context of industrial applications, biological regulation, or reaction mechanisms.
