# Mohs scale

## summary

The **Mohs scale** ranks minerals by hardness from 1 (softest) to 10 (hardest), based on the ability of one mineral to scratch another. Developed in 1812 by German mineralogist *Friedrich Mohs*, it is an ordinal (not quantitative) ranking: it orders minerals but does not measure hardness in absolute units. Talc (1) is softest; diamond (10) is hardest.

## you gotta know

- Named after *Friedrich Mohs*, an Austrian mineralogist who introduced the scale as a practical tool for field identification.
- Hardness is defined operationally: a mineral with Mohs hardness N will scratch minerals rated N−1 or lower and be scratched by minerals rated N+1 or higher.
- The reference set includes ten index minerals: talc (1), gypsum (2), calcite (3), fluorite (4), apatite (5), orthoclase feldspar (6), quartz (7), topaz (8), corundum (9), diamond (10).
- The scale is *ordinal*; intervals are *not* evenly spaced. The jump from corundum (9) to diamond (10) represents a far greater absolute hardness difference than the jump from talc (1) to gypsum (2).
- Hard minerals (quartz and above) are used in abrasives and cutting tools; the scale helps miners and gemologists identify minerals in the field.
- Fingernail (~2.5), copper coin (~3.5), and steel knife (~5.5) provide practical reference points.
- The scale is limited; it does not measure brittleness, toughness, or resistance to abrasion at atomic or quantitative scales.
