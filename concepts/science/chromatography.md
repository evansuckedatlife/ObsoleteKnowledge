# Chromatography

## summary

Chromatography is a versatile laboratory technique used to separate, purify, and analyze the individual components of complex chemical mixtures. The separation is achieved by distributing the mixture between two phases: a stationary phase, which remains fixed in place, and a mobile phase, which flows through or over it. As the mobile phase carries the mixture along, different components interact differently with the stationary phase based on size, charge, adsorption, or solubility, causing them to travel at different velocities and separate. It is widely employed in pharmaceuticals, forensics, proteomics, and environmental monitoring.

## you gotta know

### basic principles & parameters

- *Characterized by the retention factor* (Rf value) in planar chromatography, which is calculated as the ratio of the distance traveled by a specific analyte to the distance traveled by the solvent front.
- *Relies on partition coefficients*, which describe the thermodynamic equilibrium distribution of an analyte between the stationary and mobile phases.
- *Modeled by the van Deemter equation*, which relates the height equivalent to a theoretical plate (HETP) to the mobile phase linear velocity, accounting for eddy diffusion, longitudinal diffusion, and mass transfer resistance.

### common modes & setups

- *Includes thin-layer chromatography* (TLC), a rapid analytical technique that uses a stationary phase of silica gel or alumina coated on a glass or plastic plate and a liquid mobile phase.
- *Utilizes column chromatography* for preparative separation, where a liquid mobile phase washes a mixture down a column packed with a solid adsorbent stationary phase.
- *Employs gas chromatography* (GC) for volatile compounds, passing a carrier gas mobile phase (like helium) through a long capillary column coated with a liquid or polymer stationary phase.
- *Applies high-performance liquid chromatography* (HPLC), which uses high pressure to pump a liquid solvent containing the sample through a column packed with microparticles.

### specialized separations

- *Includes size-exclusion chromatography* (gel filtration), which separates macromolecules by size, allowing larger molecules to elute first because they are excluded from the pores of the stationary phase.
- *Features ion-exchange chromatography*, which separates polar or charged molecules based on their affinity to a charged stationary phase resin.
- *Employs affinity chromatography*, which purifies proteins by exploiting highly specific biological interactions, such as binding between antibodies and antigens.

## connections

- [[distillation]] — a peer separation technique based on vapor pressure and boiling point differences rather than phase partitioning.
- [[filtration]] — a simple mechanical separation technique that chromatography columns often require as a pre-treatment step.
- [[spectroscopy]] — chromatography is frequently coupled with analytical techniques, such as gas chromatography-mass spectrometry (GC-MS).
- [[carbon]] — stationary phases in reversed-phase chromatography often consist of carbon chains (such as C18 silica columns).
- [[amino-acids]] — chromatography was historically advanced by separating amino acids on paper and column beds.
- [[helium]] — helium is commonly used as the inert carrier gas in gas chromatography.

## context

Chromatography is the most powerful and ubiquitous separation method in chemical laboratories. Its development revolutionized biochemistry and analytical chemistry by enabling the purification of complex biomolecules. In quiz bowl, chromatography is heavily clued through the van Deemter equation (and its A, B, and C terms), the retention factor (Rf), stationary and mobile phases, and its various forms like HPLC, TLC, GC, and affinity chromatography.
