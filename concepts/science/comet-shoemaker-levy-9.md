# Comet Shoemaker-Levy 9

## summary

**Comet Shoemaker-Levy 9** is the first and only comet known to have impacted a planet in recorded history. Discovered in 1993 already fragmented into a "string of pearls" of 20+ nuclei, it was captured into Jupiter's orbit and systematically struck the planet over six days in July 1994. The impacts produced explosion fireballs, scars visible for months, and seismic shocks, making it a watershed event in planetary science.

## you gotta know

- *Discovered* on 24 March 1993 by Carolyn Shoemaker, Eugene Shoemaker, and David Levy (collectively the namesake); already fragmented into a train of fragments.
- *Gravitationally shattered* by Jupiter's tidal forces (the *Roche limit*) during an earlier close approach; the 1992 encounter disrupted the nucleus into ~20 large pieces.
- *Trapped in Jupiter's orbit* after the 1992 pass; calculations showed it was doomed to collide with Jupiter at perihelion two years later.
- *Impacted July 16–22, 1994:* a train of fragments slammed into Jupiter's upper atmosphere in rapid succession, each explosion visible from Earth with modest telescopes, some fireballs exceeding 10,000 km across.
- *Dark scars persisted* for weeks; the largest impacts created rings and clouds of heated gas, disrupting Jupiter's atmospheric belts and provoking seismic oscillations detected worldwide.
- *Energies enormous:* the largest impacts released ~600 megatons TNT equivalent, far exceeding the Tunguska event that flattened Siberia in 1908.

## connections

- [[jupiter]] — Shoemaker-Levy 9 provided the only direct observation of planetary bombardment; Jupiter's immense gravity was its captor and executioner.
- [[tidal-disruption]] — the comet's fragmentation exemplifies how differential gravity across an extended body can tear it apart.
- [[comet]] — Shoemaker-Levy 9 redefined understanding of comet hazards and planetary impact processes.
