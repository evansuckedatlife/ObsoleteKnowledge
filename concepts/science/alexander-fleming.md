# Alexander Fleming

## summary

Scottish bacteriologist who discovered penicillin in 1928 through the chance observation of a contaminating mould that inhibited bacterial growth. His finding launched the antibiotic era and saved countless lives from bacterial infection. He shared the 1945 Nobel Prize with Howard Florey and Ernst Boris Chain, who developed penicillin into a clinically viable drug.

## you gotta know

- Discovered *penicillin* (1928) when a mould colony (*Penicillium notatum*) contaminating a bacterial culture plate lysed surrounding *Staphylococcus* colonies — serendipity met with an observant mind.
- Worked at St. Mary's Hospital in London for most of his career, where he also discovered the enzyme *lysozyme* in tears and saliva.
- Recognized penicillin as a *selective* antimicrobial: it killed bacteria without immediately poisoning mammalian cells — the "magic bullet" Ehrlich had envisioned.
- Struggled to isolate and stabilise penicillin; only with Florey and Chain's wartime effort was it purified and manufactured at scale.
- His discovery prompted the systematic search for antibiotics from microorganisms — streptomycin, tetracycline, and countless others followed.
- Remained humble about his role, calling penicillin a "lucky find" — but preparation and a keen eye made him the fortunate recipient.

## connections

- [[paul-ehrlich]] — Fleming's penicillin embodied Ehrlich's chemotherapy vision: a specific agent killing pathogens with minimal host toxicity.
- [[james-watson-and-francis-crick]] — successors who decoded the molecular mechanism of bacterial resistance to antibiotics.
- [[karl-landsteiner]] — peer in immunology; understood how penicillin interacted with immune response.
- [[thomas-hunt-morgan]] — contemporary; both used model systems to unlock mechanisms of disease and therapy.
