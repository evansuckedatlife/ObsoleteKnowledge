# Bryophytes

## summary

**Bryophytes** are the simplest land plants — mosses, liverworts, and hornworts — lacking *vascular tissue* (xylem and phloem). They reproduce primarily by *spores* and remain small and moisture-dependent, thriving in damp, shaded habitats where they often form the green carpet of forest floors and wet rocks.

## you gotta know

- No *vascular bundles*; water and nutrients diffuse directly through cells rather than being transported through dedicated tissue.
- The *gametophyte* (spore-bearing haploid form) is the dominant, visible generation — unlike ferns and seed plants where the *sporophyte* dominates.
- Reproduce via *spores* released from a *sporangium* (spore case), not seeds; require free water or very high moisture for *flagellate sperm* to swim to the egg.
- Tiny size (typically <1 cm tall) reflects their inability to transport water upward against gravity; most stay in low-water niches.
- Lack true roots — instead have *rhizoids* (hair-like structures) that anchor but do not absorb water efficiently.
- Three main groups: *mosses* (most familiar, with a leafy stem), *liverworts* (often flat and ribbon-like), and *hornworts* (sporophyte resembles a horn).

## connections

- [[pterophytes]] — the next step up: ferns have vascular tissue and a dominant sporophyte, but still reproduce by spores.
- [[angiosperms]] — flowering plants skip the free-spore stage entirely, using seeds instead of spores.
- [[non-vascular-plants]] — bryophytes are the canonical non-vascular group.
- [[xylem]] — bryophytes lack this water-transport tissue.
- [[photosynthesis]] — bryophytes photosynthesize despite their simplicity, using chlorophyll like all green plants.
