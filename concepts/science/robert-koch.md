# Robert Koch

## summary

German physician and microbiologist who identified the bacterium *Mycobacterium tuberculosis* as the causative agent of tuberculosis and established the foundational principles for linking microorganisms to disease. His "*Koch's postulates*" (the experimental framework proving pathogenic causation) remain central to microbiology. He shared the 1905 Nobel Prize with Paul Ehrlich for their work on immunity and chemotherapy.

## you gotta know

- Isolated *Mycobacterium tuberculosis* in 1882 — the first bacterium definitively proven to cause a specific disease, validating the germ theory.
- Developed *Koch's postulates*: the logical steps (isolate the pathogen, culture it, infect an animal, re-isolate the pathogen) required to prove causation in infectious disease.
- Pioneered staining techniques (notably the *Ziehl-Neelsen stain*) that made tubercle bacilli visible under the microscope.
- Directed the Berlin Institute for Infectious Diseases; trained a generation of bacteriologists who shaped modern microbiology.
- Investigated cholera and other epidemics, advancing both laboratory methods and epidemiological field investigation.
- Believed disease eradication was possible through public health measures; his optimism drove tuberculosis sanatorium reform across Europe.

## connections

- [[paul-ehrlich]] — shared the 1905 Nobel Prize; both foundational to the theory that specific microorganisms cause specific diseases.
- [[ronald-ross]] — extended Koch's principles to parasitic and vector-borne diseases.
- [[alexander-fleming]] — built on bacteriology's foundations to discover penicillin.
