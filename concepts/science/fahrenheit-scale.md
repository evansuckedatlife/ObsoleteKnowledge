# Fahrenheit scale

## summary

The **Fahrenheit scale** is a temperature scale developed by German physicist *Daniel Gabriel Fahrenheit* in the early 18th century, setting the freezing point of water at 32 °F and its boiling point at 212 °F at sea-level atmospheric pressure. Though largely superseded by Celsius in scientific contexts, it remains the everyday standard in the United States and a few former British territories.

## you gotta know

- Fahrenheit originally defined the lowest temperature he could reliably produce in his laboratory (using a salt-ice mixture) as 0 °F and normal human body temperature as 96 °F; the scale was later adjusted so water freezes at exactly 32 °F.
- One Fahrenheit degree is smaller than one Celsius degree: 1 °F = 5/9 °C (or about 0.556 °C), so the same temperature difference spans a larger numerical range on the Fahrenheit scale.
- Conversion formula: °F = (°C × 9/5) + 32, or °C = (°F − 32) × 5/9.
- The boiling point of water is 212 °F, creating an 180-degree interval between water's freezing and boiling points (versus 100 degrees on Celsius).
- Negative temperatures are possible on the Fahrenheit scale; −40 °F is the temperature at which Fahrenheit and Celsius scales coincide numerically.
- Named after its inventor, physicist *Daniel Gabriel Fahrenheit*, who also pioneered the mercury thermometer.
- The scale is *linear*, not logarithmic, enabling straightforward arithmetic.
