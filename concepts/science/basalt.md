# Basalt

## summary

**Basalt** is a fine-grained, dark *mafic* igneous rock formed from the rapid cooling of low-silica lava flows. It is the most abundant igneous rock on Earth, making up most of the oceanic crust and the floors of mid-ocean ridges. Basalts are rich in iron and magnesium, giving them their characteristically dark colour and high density.

## you gotta know

- *Mafic* igneous rock — "mafic" refers to high magnesium (ma) and iron (fe) content, and low silica (~50% SiO₂).
- Forms from *lava flows* that cool rapidly at the surface, producing a *fine-grained* or *glassy* texture; cannot resolve mineral grains with the naked eye.
- Primary minerals are *plagioclase feldspar* (calcium-rich) and *pyroxene* (iron-magnesium silicate); olivine is common in some basalts.
- Dark grey to black in colour due to high iron and magnesium content; denser (~3.0 g/cm³) than granitic rocks (~2.7 g/cm³).
- Comprises most of the *oceanic crust*, formed at mid-ocean ridges where mantle rock melts and erupts onto the seafloor.
- Can weather into fertile soil because iron and magnesium are plant nutrients; volcanic regions with basaltic soils are often agriculturally rich.
- Used as construction aggregate, crushed for road base, and historically as whetstones and tool-making material.

## connections

- [[granite]] — granite is felsic (light, silica-rich); basalt is mafic (dark, silica-poor); together they represent opposite ends of the igneous rock spectrum.
- [[feldspar]] — plagioclase feldspar is the dominant mineral in basalt, though the calcium-rich variety differs from orthoclase in granite.
- [[igneous-rock]] — basalt is the quintessential extrusive (lava) igneous rock; granite is the intrusive equivalent.
- [[metamorphic-rock]] — basalt can be metamorphosed to *greenschist* or *amphibolite* under burial and heat.
- [[olivine]] — olivine is a primary mineral in olivine-rich basalts and is more dense than pyroxene.
