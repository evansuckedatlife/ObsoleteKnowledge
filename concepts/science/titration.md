# Titration

## summary

Titration is a quantitative chemical analysis method used to determine the unknown concentration of an identified analyte. A reagent of known concentration and volume, called the titrant, is added from a buret to a known volume of the analyte solution until the chemical reaction reaches its equivalence point, which is often detected using a color-changing chemical indicator or a pH meter.

## you gotta know

- *Signals reaction completion at the equivalence point*, which is visually indicated by the endpoint of a chemical indicator, such as phenolphthalein (which turns from colorless to pink in basic conditions).
- *Displays a sharp vertical inflection on a titration curve*, which plots pH (for acid-base titrations) against the volume of added titrant, with the midpoint of the vertical section representing the equivalence point.
- *Allows calculation of pH in the buffer region* (prior to the equivalence point of a weak acid titration) using the Henderson-Hasselbalch equation.
- *Includes Karl Fischer titration*, a specialized redox method that uses coulometric or volumetric techniques to selectively determine trace amounts of water in a sample.
- *Employs complexometric methods* using chelating agents like EDTA (ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid) to determine the concentration of divalent metal ions in solution.

## connections

- [[water]] — Karl Fischer titration is the gold-standard method for determining trace moisture in organic solvents and products.
- [[sodium]] — sodium hydroxide is a common strong base titrant used to analyze weak and strong acids.
- [[potassium]] — potassium hydrogen phthalate (KHP) is a widely used primary standard for calibrating base titrants.
