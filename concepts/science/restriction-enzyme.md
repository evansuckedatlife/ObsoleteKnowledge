# Restriction enzyme

## summary

Restriction enzymes are bacterial nucleases that recognize and cleave DNA at specific palindromic sequences, typically 4–8 bp long. They originated as bacterial immune defenses and have become indispensable molecular tools for cloning, sequencing, and genetic analysis.

## you gotta know

- Catalyzes: sequence-specific DNA cleavage, producing defined fragments
- Recognition: binds palindromic double-stranded DNA sequences (read identically forward and reverse on complementary strands)
- Cleavage products: blunt ends (direct cuts) or sticky/cohesive ends (staggered cuts leaving overhangs)
- Specificity: exceptional—some recognize 6 bp sequences occurring statistically ~every 4,000 bp in random DNA; 8-cutters rare in genome
- Bacterial origin: part of restriction-modification (R-M) systems; methylase protects host DNA while restriction nuclease destroys foreign DNA
- Common examples: EcoRI (recognizes GAATTC), BamHI (GGATCC), PstI (CTGCAG)—hundreds of distinct enzymes catalogued
- Fragment cloning: sticky-end compatibility enables insertion of one digest product into another, forming recombinant DNA
- Isoschizomers: different enzymes recognizing the same sequence; neoschizomers recognize similar but slightly different sequences

## context

Restriction enzymes were essential to modern molecular biology's emergence in the 1970s. Their discovery transformed genetic engineering from a theoretical possibility into practical reality; scientists could now break DNA at known locations and reassemble it in new configurations. The tools enabled DNA fingerprinting for forensics, the first restriction maps of genomes, and the construction of the first recombinant DNA molecules. Though sequencing and direct gene synthesis have superseded them for some applications, restriction enzymes remain fundamental in molecular diagnostics, cloning, and teaching biochemistry's practical foundations.
