# Diels-Alder Reaction

## summary

The Diels-Alder reaction is a landmark organic chemical reaction that proceeds via a single-step, concerted mechanism to form a six-membered ring. Classified as a [4+2] cycloaddition, it occurs between a conjugated diene containing four pi electrons and a substituted alkene or alkyne called a dienophile containing two pi electrons. Discovered by Otto Diels and Kurt Alder, who shared the 1950 Nobel Prize in Chemistry, the reaction is exceptionally valuable in synthetic chemistry because it establishes two new carbon-carbon sigma bonds and up to four new stereocenters simultaneously. It is a thermally allowed pericyclic reaction operating under orbital symmetry control.

## you gotta know

### reaction components & stereochemistry

- *Requires a conjugated diene* that must adopt or be locked in an s-cis conformation to react, as dienes locked in s-trans conformations are unreactive and cyclic dienes (like cyclopentadiene) react extremely rapidly.
- *Requires a dienophile*, which is typically activated by electron-withdrawing groups (such as carbonyls, nitriles, or nitro groups) that lower its LUMO energy.
- *Follows the Alder endo rule*, which describes a stereochemical preference where the dienophile's substituents point toward the newly formed double bond in the transition state due to secondary orbital interactions.
- *Is stereospecific*, meaning the relative stereochemistry of both the diene (cis/trans) and the dienophile is strictly preserved in the resulting cyclohexene product.

### orbital symmetry & thermodynamics

- *Classified as a pericyclic [4+2] cycloaddition*, proceeding through a aromatic-like cyclic, six-membered transition state without forming carbocation or radical intermediates.
- *Governed by the Woodward-Hoffmann rules*, which dictate that the reaction is thermally allowed and photochemically forbidden because the HOMO of the diene and the LUMO of the dienophile have matching phase symmetries.
- *Reversible at high temperatures*, undergoing a retro-Diels-Alder reaction that cracks cyclohexenes back into dienes and dienophiles, a technique commonly used to generate monomeric cyclopentadiene from dicyclopentadiene.

## context

The Diels-Alder reaction is the classic example of a concerted cycloaddition, demonstrating the power of orbital symmetry control. It remains a cornerstone of natural product synthesis, enabling the construction of complex polycyclic systems in a single step. In quiz bowl, the reaction is clued through its pericyclic concerted mechanism, the s-cis diene requirement, electron-withdrawing groups on the dienophile, the endo rule (endo selectivities), retro-Diels-Alder cracking, and its description as a [4+2] cycloaddition.
