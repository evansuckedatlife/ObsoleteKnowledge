## summary

*Planck's constant* (symbolized *h*) is the fundamental constant of quantum mechanics, equal to approximately 6.626 × 10<sup>−34</sup> joule-seconds. It quantifies the relationship between a photon's energy and its frequency via the relation *E* = *hν*, and appears throughout quantum mechanics as the scale of quantization. The constant is named after Max Planck, who introduced it in 1900 to explain blackbody radiation, launching the quantum revolution. Planck's constant is so small in everyday units that quantum effects are invisible at macroscopic scales; the reduced Planck constant ℏ = *h*/(2π) is equally fundamental in wave mechanics and angular momentum.

## you gotta know

- *E* = *hν*: a photon's energy is proportional to its frequency. This simple relation explains the photoelectric effect and shows that light energy is quantized in packets.
- ℏ = *h*/(2π) (h-bar): the reduced Planck constant, appearing in the *Schrödinger equation*, the *uncertainty principle*, and angular momentum quantization.
- The *uncertainty principle*: Δ*x* · Δ*p* ≥ ℏ/2. The product of uncertainties in position and momentum is bounded below by Planck's constant—a cornerstone of quantum indeterminacy.
- *Planck units*: Planck's constant (combined with the speed of light and Newton's gravitational constant) defines natural scales for quantum gravity: the *Planck length* (~10<sup>−35</sup> m), *Planck time* (~10<sup>−43</sup> s), and *Planck mass* (~10<sup>−8</sup> kg). At these scales, quantum gravity becomes strong and classical spacetime breaks down.
- *Action* in classical mechanics is energy × time; quantized action comes in multiples of ℏ. Feynman's path integral formulation of quantum mechanics weights each history by exp(*iS*/ℏ), where *S* is action.
- Blackbody radiation: Planck derived the energy spectrum of thermal radiation by assuming quantized energy levels separated by *hν*, solving the "ultraviolet catastrophe" that classical physics predicted (infinite energy for hot objects).
