# Quantum Mechanics

## summary

*Quantum mechanics* is the fundamental branch of physics governing phenomena at the atomic and subatomic scales. It describes how particles—electrons, photons, quarks, and others—behave in ways that defy classical intuition: they exist in *superposition* (multiple states simultaneously), exhibit *wave-particle duality*, and their properties are constrained by fundamental constants like *Planck's constant*. Quantum mechanics is the foundation of modern physics, chemistry, materials science, and technology; it explains atomic structure, chemical bonding, the behavior of semiconductors, and the operation of lasers and nuclear reactions.

## you gotta know

### Historical Development
- *Classical mechanics* (Newton, Lagrange) failed to explain observations at small scales: the stability of atoms, the discrete colors of light emitted by hot atoms, and the behavior of light.
- The **quantum revolution** began with *Max Planck's* hypothesis (1900) that energy is emitted in discrete packets—*quanta*—proportional to frequency: E = hf (where h is Planck's constant).
- *Albert Einstein's* explanation of the *photoelectric effect* (1905) treated light as particles (*photons*) with quantized energy, earning him the Nobel Prize.
- *Niels Bohr* (1913) proposed quantized electron orbits in atoms, explaining hydrogen's spectral lines (but the model later proved incomplete).

### Quantum Principles
- **Wave-particle duality**: particles exhibit properties of both waves (interference, diffraction) and particles (discrete energy, momentum). The boundary between them blurs at quantum scales.
- **Superposition**: a quantum system exists in a linear combination of possible states until measured; measurement *collapses* it into a single eigenstate.
- **Quantization**: energy, angular momentum, and other properties come in discrete bundles, not continuous ranges.
- **Uncertainty principle** (*Heisenberg*, 1927): Δx·Δp ≥ ℏ/2 — you cannot simultaneously know a particle's position and momentum with arbitrary precision. A fundamental limit, not a limitation of measurement.

### Key Equations and Frameworks
- *Schrödinger's equation* (1926) describes how a quantum system's wavefunction evolves in time; solving it yields the allowed energy levels and probabilities.
- *Dirac's equation* (1928) unified quantum mechanics with special relativity; predicted antimatter and explained the electron's intrinsic spin.
- *The Standard Model* (built from quantum field theory) describes all known particles and interactions except gravity.

### Quantum Tunneling and Phenomena
- **Quantum tunneling**: particles can pass through energy barriers that would be insurmountable classically—essential in nuclear fusion, alpha decay, and tunnel diodes.
- **Entanglement**: two or more particles become correlated such that measuring one instantly affects the other, even at a distance (Einstein famously called it "spooky action at a distance").
- **Decoherence**: quantum superposition decays into classical behavior when a system interacts with its environment—explains why macroscopic objects don't superpose.

## context

Quantum mechanics is perhaps the most successful scientific theory ever: it makes predictions (often to many decimal places) that match experimental results with extraordinary precision. Yet it remains philosophically unsettling—the measurement problem, the nature of superposition, the role of observers—still provoke debate. Experimentally, quantum mechanics underlies technology: semiconductors (transistors, diodes), lasers, nuclear energy, X-ray diffraction, NMR spectroscopy, and the emerging field of quantum computing. Its marriage with relativity (quantum field theory) extended its reach to particle physics and cosmology. Quantum mechanics is not optional for understanding the modern world; it is the fundamental law of nature at all scales.
