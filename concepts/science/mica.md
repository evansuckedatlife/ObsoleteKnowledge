# Mica

## summary

**Mica** is a group of silicate minerals characterised by perfect basal cleavage, producing thin, flexible sheets. The two most common varieties are *muscovite* (potassium-aluminium mica) and *biotite* (iron-magnesium mica). Micas are common in granites, schists, and other metamorphic rocks, and their sheet structure gives them unique optical and thermal properties.

## you gotta know

- *Silicate minerals* with a layered crystal structure — atoms are arranged in sheets bonded by weak forces perpendicular to the layers.
- Perfect *basal cleavage* — splits easily into thin, transparent or translucent sheets along one direction, a hallmark feature for field identification.
- Rated 2.5 to 3 on the *Mohs hardness scale* — relatively soft, but hardness is anisotropic (varies with direction of scratch).
- *Muscovite* is colourless to pale (potassium-aluminium mica); *biotite* is dark brown to black (iron-magnesium mica); both are common in granites.
- Thermally insulating and electrically insulating in the basal direction; historically used in electrical insulation and as a window pane substitute.
- Found in granites alongside feldspar and quartz; also a major component of *schist* and *gneiss* (metamorphic rocks).
- Weathers slowly because the sheet structure resists chemical attack; mica flakes persist in sediments longer than feldspar or other minerals.

## connections

- [[granite]] — mica is a primary constituent of granite alongside feldspar and quartz.
- [[feldspar]] — companion mineral in granites and metamorphic rocks.
- [[metamorphic-rock]] — mica is abundant in schist, gneiss, and other high-grade metamorphic rocks.
- [[silicate]] — micas are silicate minerals with a distinctive layered structure.
- [[mohs-scale]] — mica (hardness 2.5–3) is at the soft end of the hardness scale, easily scratched by feldspar and quartz.
