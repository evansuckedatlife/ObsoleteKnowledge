# pH scale

## summary

The **pH scale** measures the acidity or basicity of a solution on a logarithmic scale from 0 to 14, where 7 is neutral. Values below 7 indicate *acidity*; values above 7 indicate *alkalinity* (basicity). The scale is defined mathematically as the negative logarithm of hydrogen ion concentration [H⁺], compressing a vast range of concentrations into a handy numerical framework.

## you gotta know

- Stands for "*power of hydrogen*," derived from pH = −log₁₀[H⁺], where [H⁺] is the molar concentration of hydrogen ions.
- The scale is *logarithmic*: each unit represents a tenfold change in acidity or basicity. A pH of 3 is ten times more acidic than pH 4.
- pH 7 (at 25 °C) is *neutral*, where [H⁺] = [OH⁻] = 10⁻⁷ mol/L. Pure water is neutral.
- *Acidic* solutions have pH < 7; common examples: lemon juice (pH ~2), vinegar (pH ~3), stomach acid (pH ~1.5).
- *Basic* or *alkaline* solutions have pH > 7; common examples: baking soda solution (pH ~8.5), ammonia solution (pH ~11), sodium hydroxide (pH ~14).
- Introduced by *Søren Peder Lauritz Sørensen*, a Danish biochemist, in 1909.
- Used across chemistry, biology, medicine, environmental science, and industry to monitor and control solution chemistry.
