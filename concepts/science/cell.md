# Cell

## summary

The *cell* is the smallest unit of life capable of independent existence and reproduction. All living organisms are composed of one or more cells, which arise only from pre-existing cells. Cells are bounded by a *cell membrane* that controls what enters and exits; they contain DNA as their genetic material and machinery for energy production, protein synthesis, and growth. Two major types exist: *prokaryotic* cells (bacteria and archaea), which lack a nucleus, and *eukaryotic* cells (animals, plants, fungi), which compartmentalize functions into specialized organelles.

## you gotta know

### Universal Features
- All cells are bounded by a *cell membrane* (plasma membrane): a lipid bilayer with embedded and peripheral proteins that controls transport and signaling.
- All cells contain *cytoplasm*: a gel-like matrix containing water, salts, proteins, and organelles.
- All cells contain *DNA* as their hereditary material; DNA is replicated and passed to daughter cells during cell division.
- All cells carry out *metabolism*: chemical reactions that extract energy from nutrients and build cellular components.
- All cells synthesize *proteins* via ribosomes, using the genetic code to translate DNA instructions.

### Prokaryotic Cells (Bacteria and Archaea)
- Small (~1–10 micrometers), no nucleus; DNA is a single circular chromosome in the *nucleoid* region, not enclosed by a membrane.
- Lack membrane-bound organelles; instead, metabolic functions occur in the cytoplasm or attached to the cell membrane.
- Fast reproduction: can divide every 20 minutes under optimal conditions via binary fission (asexual reproduction).
- Surrounded by a rigid *cell wall* (in bacteria, made of peptidoglycan) that provides structural support.

### Eukaryotic Cells (Animals, Plants, Fungi)
- Larger (~10–100 micrometers), with a true nucleus enclosed by a nuclear envelope; DNA is organized into linear chromosomes.
- Contain *membrane-bound organelles*, each with specialized functions: *mitochondria* (energy production), *endoplasmic reticulum* (protein and lipid synthesis), *Golgi apparatus* (modification and packaging), *lysosomes* (digestion), *chloroplasts* (photosynthesis, in plants).
- Slower reproduction: typically divide once per 24 hours; sexual reproduction allows genetic recombination.
- Cytoskeleton: networks of protein filaments that provide structure, enable movement, and facilitate transport.

### The Cell Cycle
- Cells grow and divide in a coordinated process: *G1 phase* (gap, growth), *S phase* (DNA synthesis), *G2 phase* (gap, preparation), and *M phase* (mitosis, division).
- During S phase, DNA replication is semiconservative; checkpoint controls ensure replication is complete and accurate before division proceeds.
- Cell cycle is regulated by *checkpoint* proteins (including p53, the "guardian of the genome") that detect errors and halt the cycle or trigger cell death if damage is irreparable.

### Cell Division
- *Mitosis* produces two genetically identical daughter cells; essential for growth, repair, and asexual reproduction.
- *Meiosis* produces four haploid gametes (sperm, egg cells) with half the chromosome number; essential for sexual reproduction and genetic diversity.
- *Binary fission* (in prokaryotes) is rapid and asexual.

### Cell Specialization and Multicellularity
- In multicellular organisms, different cell types (nerve cells, muscle cells, immune cells) express different sets of genes, resulting in specialized functions and structures.
- *Differentiation* is the process by which a generic cell becomes specialized; it is largely irreversible and driven by transcription factors and epigenetic modifications.
- Specialized cells lose the ability to divide (e.g., neurons) or divide infrequently (e.g., muscle cells).

## context

The cell is the fundamental unit of life. Leeuwenhoek's early microscope observations and Schleiden and Schwann's *cell theory* (all organisms are made of cells; cells come only from pre-existing cells) established that the cell is not merely a container but the essence of life. The discovery of the nucleus, mitochondria, and other organelles revealed the cell's internal complexity. Modern cell biology uses molecular techniques to understand how DNA is read, how proteins are made and folded, how energy is harvested, and how cells communicate and divide. Cell division gone awry — uncontrolled proliferation — underlies cancer; understanding the cell cycle and its checkpoints is crucial to cancer biology and treatment. The variety of cell types in a multicellular organism, all from the same genetic blueprint, illustrates how differential gene expression creates complexity. Cells in culture are powerful tools for research; they model disease, test drugs, and are the foundation of biotechnology and regenerative medicine.
