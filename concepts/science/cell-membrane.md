## summary

The *cell membrane* (also called the *plasma membrane*) is the semipermeable boundary surrounding every cell that separates the internal environment from the external. It is a dynamic structure composed primarily of a *lipid bilayer* studded with proteins, which collectively regulate what enters and exits the cell, mediate cell-cell communication, and provide structural integrity. The cell membrane is crucial to cell survival: without it, the cell cannot maintain its internal chemistry or respond to its environment.

## you gotta know

### The Lipid Bilayer
- The foundation is a *lipid bilayer*: two layers of *phospholipids* arranged with their hydrophobic tails facing inward and hydrophilic heads facing outward.
- Phospholipids are amphipathic: one end is polar (head) and one is nonpolar (two fatty acid tails); this structure causes them to spontaneously form bilayers in aqueous solution.
- *Cholesterol* molecules (in animal cells) nestle between phospholipids, reducing membrane fluidity at high temperatures and preventing excessive fluidity at low temperatures.
- The membrane is *fluid*, not rigid — a principle described by the *Fluid Mosaic Model* (Singer & Nicolson, 1972): lipids and proteins move laterally but do not flip between layers.

### Membrane Proteins
- *Integral proteins* span the entire membrane, forming channels or transporters; many are *glycoproteins* (have attached carbohydrate chains).
- *Peripheral proteins* are attached to the inner or outer surface, often anchoring the cytoskeleton or serving signaling functions.
- Proteins vastly outnumber lipids by mass (~50:50 ratio) but are fewer in number; a typical cell membrane has thousands of different protein types.
- *Channel proteins* form pores for ions and small molecules; *carrier proteins* bind and transport specific substances; *receptor proteins* detect signals.

### Transport Mechanisms
- *Passive transport*: diffusion of small nonpolar molecules (O₂, CO₂) and osmosis of water across the membrane without energy.
- *Facilitated diffusion*: transport proteins channel polar molecules or ions down their concentration gradient (e.g., glucose via GLUT1).
- *Active transport*: proteins use ATP energy to pump ions or molecules against their concentration gradient (e.g., Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase pumps sodium out and potassium in).
- *Endocytosis* and *exocytosis*: the membrane invaginates to engulf large particles or vesicles, or fuses with the membrane to expel contents.

### Cell Recognition and Signaling
- *Glycoproteins* and *glycolipids* on the cell surface carry carbohydrate "identity tags" that other cells recognize; crucial for immune recognition, development, and organ transplantation (blood types).
- *Receptors* embedded in the membrane bind signaling molecules (hormones, growth factors, neurotransmitters) and transmit signals into the cell via intracellular cascades.
- Gap junctions (in animals) form channels between adjacent cells, allowing direct exchange of small molecules and ions.
- Tight junctions seal adjacent cells, controlling paracellular transport (e.g., in the intestinal epithelium and blood-brain barrier).

### Structure and Organization
- The membrane is ~ 7–10 nanometers thick; electron microscopy revealed the "unit membrane" structure: a lipid bilayer with protein layers on either side.
- Lipids and proteins are not uniformly distributed; some regions (*lipid rafts*) are enriched in cholesterol and certain lipids and serve as signaling platforms.
- The cytoplasm-facing side of the membrane is typically negatively charged, influencing local ion concentrations and electrostatic interactions.

## context

The cell membrane is one of life's critical innovations. Its semipermeable nature allows cells to maintain different internal and external conditions — different ion concentrations, pH, and solute concentrations — essential for energy production, signaling, and metabolism. The discovery of lipid bilayers was gradual: early observations of cell shape and osmotic behavior hinted at a boundary layer; lipid chemistry revealed the amphipathic nature of phospholipids; and electron microscopy visualized the membrane's structure. The Fluid Mosaic Model unified observations of membrane fluidity and dynamics and explained how proteins function within an fluid lipid environment. Understanding membrane transport is crucial to medicine: many drugs target membrane transporters or receptors (e.g., statins block cholesterol transporters; beta-blockers bind adrenergic receptors). Ion channels, pumps, and aquaporins are among the most important protein families. Membrane dysfunction underlies many diseases: cystic fibrosis involves a faulty chloride channel; cholera toxin causes diarrhea by forcing ion transport; and many cancers involve aberrant cell-surface signaling.
