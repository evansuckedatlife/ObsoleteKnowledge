# Calcite

## summary

**Calcite** is calcium carbonate (CaCO₃), a ubiquitous mineral found in sedimentary, metamorphic, and igneous rocks. It forms the primary constituent of limestone and marble, and is crucial to coral reefs and mollusc shells. Its softness, solubility in acid, and tendency to form crystals make it highly recognisable in the field.

## you gotta know

- *Calcium carbonate* — the chemical formula CaCO₃, making it a *carbonate mineral*, not a silicate.
- Rated 3 on the *Mohs hardness scale* — soft enough to scratch with a knife, much softer than quartz or feldspar.
- Fizzes when exposed to dilute *hydrochloric acid*, a key field test for identification.
- Forms beautiful *rhombohedral crystals* (three-sided diamond shapes); transparent varieties are used as optical elements (Iceland spar).
- The primary mineral in *limestone* (sedimentary) and *marble* (metamorphic), which are transformed by burial and heat.
- Biogenic origin — much calcite precipitates from seawater via organisms (corals, foraminifera, molluscs) rather than inorganic crystallisation.
- Soluble in acidic water, so limestone landscapes develop karst topography (caves, sinkholes, underground rivers).

## connections

- [[limestone]] — calcite-rich sedimentary rock formed from shell fragments and skeletal material in ancient seas.
- [[marble]] — metamorphosed limestone, where calcite crystals grow larger and recrystallise under heat and pressure.
- [[mohs-scale]] — calcite (hardness 3) is the reference mineral for the lower end of hardness testing.
- [[metamorphic-rock]] — marble is calcite-based metamorphic rock formed from limestone protoliths.
- [[quartz]] — quartz is far more abundant and harder; calcite is one of the few minerals that breaks it down chemically over time.
