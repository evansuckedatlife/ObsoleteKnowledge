# ATP

## summary

*ATP (adenosine triphosphate)* is the primary energy currency of cells. It is a small molecule consisting of adenosine (a nucleobase plus a five-carbon sugar) bonded to three phosphate groups. The high-energy bonds between the phosphate groups store chemical energy; when ATP releases a phosphate group (becoming ADP or AMP), that energy is released and used to drive cellular processes — muscle contraction, active transport, protein synthesis, DNA replication, and countless other reactions. The continuous synthesis and breakdown of ATP couples energy-releasing reactions (catabolic) to energy-requiring reactions (anabolic).

## you gotta know

### Structure and Energy
- ATP consists of *adenosine* (the nucleobase adenine plus ribose sugar) bonded to a chain of three *phosphate groups* (hence "tri-phosphate").
- The bonds between phosphate groups (especially the terminal bond between the second and third phosphates) are *high-energy bonds*: their hydrolysis releases ~30.5 kJ/mol under standard conditions (actual cellular conditions make this ~50 kJ/mol).
- Hydrolysis of ATP to ADP + Pi (inorganic phosphate) is highly exergonic (releases energy); this energy is captured by enzymes and used to drive otherwise unfavorable reactions.

### ATP Synthesis
- *Oxidative phosphorylation* in mitochondria: the electron transport chain pumps protons into the intermembrane space, creating a proton gradient; ATP synthase harnesses this gradient to phosphorylate ADP + Pi → ATP.
- A single NADH oxidized through the electron transport chain yields ~2.5 ATP; a single FADH₂ yields ~1.5 ATP.
- Complete oxidation of one glucose yields ~30–32 ATP (under optimal conditions); this is far more efficient than anaerobic fermentation (~2 ATP per glucose).
- *Photophosphorylation* in chloroplasts: light energy is used directly to pump protons and drive ATP synthesis via the same ATP synthase mechanism.
- *Substrate-level phosphorylation*: in glycolysis and the citric acid cycle, ATP is produced directly when a phosphate group is transferred from a high-energy intermediate to ADP.

### ATP Consumption
- ATP is hydrolyzed (converted to ADP or AMP) to provide energy for:
  - *Active transport*: pumps (like Na⁺/K⁺-ATPase) use ATP to move ions against their concentration gradient.
  - *Protein synthesis*: aminoacyl-tRNA synthetases use ATP to charge tRNAs; the ribosome uses ATP (or GTP) to move along mRNA.
  - *Muscle contraction*: myosin heads use ATP to perform the power stroke, pulling actin filaments.
  - *DNA replication and repair*: DNA polymerases and helicases use ATP/dNTPs.
  - *Biosynthesis*: building new molecules (proteins, lipids, nucleic acids) requires ATP.
- A typical human uses their body weight in ATP each day, yet contains only ~0.2 mol of ATP at any moment — ATP must be recycled rapidly.

### The ATP/ADP Cycle
- ATP ↔ ADP + Pi is the central reaction of bioenergetics; the ratio of ATP to ADP/AMP reflects cellular energy status.
- *AMP-activated protein kinase* (AMPK) senses low energy (high AMP/ATP ratio) and activates catabolic pathways while inhibiting anabolic pathways, balancing cellular energy.
- When ATP levels are high, feedback inhibition dampens glycolysis, the citric acid cycle, and other catabolic pathways, preventing overproduction of ATP.

### Nucleotide Derivation
- ATP, like other nucleotides, is built from the nucleobase adenine, the sugar ribose, and the phosphate groups; its synthesis occurs via de novo and salvage pathways.
- ATP is also the source of nucleotides for RNA synthesis: the *RNA polymerase* uses ATP (and GTP, CTP, UTP) as substrates.
- ATP synthesis and breakdown are tightly coupled to maintain cellular energy homeostasis.

## context

ATP is often called the "energy currency" of life, and the metaphor is apt: just as money enables exchange of goods and services in an economy, ATP enables the coupling of energy-releasing reactions (oxidation of fuel) to energy-requiring processes (work). The discovery of ATP and its role in energy metabolism was revolutionary: it explained how cells extract useful work from chemical reactions and unified biochemistry around a single molecule. The structure of ATP synthase — a rotary molecular motor driven by a proton gradient — is among the most elegant and exquisite discoveries in molecular biology. Cells can't store large amounts of ATP (it would be energetically wasteful); instead, they maintain a steady-state level and continuously synthesize ATP as it is consumed. The rate of ATP synthesis is staggering: the average human synthesizes roughly their body weight in ATP daily. Understanding ATP is central to biochemistry, physiology, medicine, and biotechnology: drugs target enzymes that produce or consume ATP; exercise physiology is built on ATP availability; and metabolism itself is fundamentally about harnessing energy in the form of ATP.
