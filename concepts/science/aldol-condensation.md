## summary

The aldol condensation is a classic carbon-carbon bond-forming reaction in which an enol or enolate ion reacts with a carbonyl compound (an aldehyde or ketone) to form a beta-hydroxy carbonyl species, which often undergoes subsequent dehydration to yield a conjugated enone. It is a fundamental tool in organic chemistry used to build complex carbon frameworks.

## you gotta know

- *Requires acid or base catalysis* to deprotonate the alpha-carbon of a carbonyl compound, generating a nucleophilic enolate ion or enol intermediate.
- *Produces a beta-hydroxy aldehyde or beta-hydroxy ketone* in the initial addition phase, named "aldol" as a portmanteau of *aldehyde* and *alcohol*.
- *Undergoes subsequent dehydration* (loss of water) upon heating to yield an alpha,beta-unsaturated carbonyl compound, driven by the thermodynamic stability of the resulting conjugated pi system.
- *Utilizes crossed aldol reactions* between two different carbonyl compounds, which are most efficient when only one reactant has acidic alpha-hydrogens (such as benzaldehyde or formaldehyde) to prevent multiple self-condensation products.
- *Proceeds intramolecularly* to construct cyclic systems, forming stable five- or six-membered rings, which is the final step of the Robinson annulation sequence.

## connections

- [[aldehyde]] — aldehydes serve as common substrates for both enolate generation and nucleophilic attack in the reaction.
- [[ketone]] — ketones undergo aldol reactions, though their equilibrium is generally less thermodynamically favorable than that of aldehydes due to steric and electronic factors.
- [[water]] — the elimination of water is the defining step that converts the aldol addition product into the final condensation product.
