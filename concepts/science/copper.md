# Copper

## summary

Copper is a soft, malleable, and ductile transition metal with exceptionally high thermal and electrical conductivity. Known for its distinctive reddish-orange metallic luster, it was one of the first metals ever smelted by humans and, when alloyed with tin, inaugurated the Bronze Age. Today, it remains a critical material in electrical wiring, plumbing, and various industrial technologies.

## you gotta know

- *Possesses an anomalous electron configuration* of [Ar] 3d10 4s1, which gains stability by having a completely filled d-subshell and a half-filled s-subshell.
- *Corrodes in moist air to form a green patina* of basic copper carbonate, a protective layer that prevents further weathering (notably coloring the Statue of Liberty).
- *Alloys with other metals* to form bronze (when combined with tin) and brass (when combined with zinc).
- *Serves as the oxygen-carrier in hemocyanin*, a copper-based protein that turns blue when oxygenated, used by many mollusks and arthropods instead of iron-based hemoglobin.
- *Acts as a catalyst in Click Chemistry*, specifically facilitating the copper-catalyzed azide-alkyne cycloaddition (CuAAC) reaction to link molecular building blocks.

## connections

- [[gold]] — belongs to Group 11 (the coinage metals) along with copper and silver.
- [[iron]] — historically replaced by iron for weaponry and tools, marking the transition from the Bronze Age.
- [[zinc]] — combines with copper in varying ratios to produce brass.
