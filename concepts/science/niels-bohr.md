## summary

A Danish physicist, **Niels Bohr** made foundational contributions to understanding atomic structure and quantum mechanics. He developed the Bohr model of the atom, which proposed that electrons travel in discrete orbits and jump between energy levels by emitting or absorbing light. As the founder and director of the Institute for Theoretical Physics in Copenhagen, he became the primary spokesman for the orthodox "Copenhagen interpretation" of quantum mechanics, receiving the 1922 Nobel Prize in Physics.

## you gotta know

### atomic structure & quantum theory

- *Developed the Bohr model of the atom* in 1913, which modified Ernest Rutherford's model by postulating that electrons orbit the nucleus in quantized, stable shells and emit radiation only when jumping between levels.
- *Formulated the complementarity principle*, which states that physical systems exhibit mutually exclusive wave-like and particle-like properties depending on how they are measured.
- *Led the Copenhagen interpretation* of quantum mechanics alongside Werner Heisenberg, advocating for a probabilistic view of physical reality where a system lacks definite properties until it is observed.
- *Proposed the correspondence principle*, asserting that the rules of quantum mechanics must match the predictions of classical physics when describing systems with high quantum numbers.
- *Debated Albert Einstein* at the Solvay Conferences regarding the completeness of quantum mechanics, successfully defending the probabilistic framework against Einstein's thought experiments.

### nuclear physics & wartime work

- *Formulated the liquid drop model* of the atomic nucleus in 1936, which treated the nucleus as a drop of incompressible fluid and provided the theoretical basis for explaining nuclear fission.
- *Proposed the compound nucleus hypothesis*, which described nuclear reactions as occurring in two distinct stages: the capture of a projectile and the subsequent decay of the compound state.
- *Escaped German-occupied Denmark* in 1943, fleeing to Sweden in a fishing boat, and eventually traveled to the United States to work on the Manhattan Project under the pseudonym Nicholas Baker.

## connections

- [[albert-einstein]] — his chief intellectual rival with whom he debated the philosophical foundations of quantum mechanics.
- [[werner-heisenberg]] — his student and close collaborator who developed the uncertainty principle under Bohr's guidance.
- [[erwin-schrodinger]] — whose wave equation Bohr incorporated into the Copenhagen interpretation.
- [[max-planck]] — whose quantum constant Bohr incorporated into his quantized atomic model.
- [[manhattan-project]] — the nuclear weapons research project Bohr contributed to during World War II.
- [[ernest-rutherford]] — Bohr's mentor whose planetary model of the atom Bohr corrected using quantum principles.

## context

Niels Bohr was the principal architect and philosopher of the early quantum revolution. His institute in Copenhagen served as the global capital of theoretical physics, where he mentored Heisenberg, Pauli, and Dirac. In quiz bowl, Bohr is frequently tested on his atomic model (specifically the Rydberg constant and Bohr radius), his complementarity principle, his debates with Einstein, and the Bohr-Sommerfeld quantization rules.
