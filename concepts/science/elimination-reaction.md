# Elimination Reaction

## summary

An elimination reaction is a class of organic reactions in which two substituents are removed from a molecule in either a one- or two-step mechanism. The primary result of this process is the formation of a new pi bond, which converts saturated compounds into unsaturated ones, such as alkenes or alkynes.

## you gotta know

- *Proceeds via the E2 pathway*, a concerted bimolecular mechanism that requires a specific anti-periplanar transition state geometry between the departing leaving group and the beta-proton.
- *Proceeds via the E1 pathway*, a stepwise unimolecular mechanism involving a carbocation intermediate, which is favored by polar protic solvents and weak bases.
- *Obeys Zaitsev's rule* under standard conditions, which predicts that elimination will selectively yield the more substituted and thermodynamically stable alkene.
- *Yields Hofmann products* (less substituted alkenes) when using a sterically hindered, bulky base (like potassium tert-butoxide) that cannot easily access internal protons.
- *Favored by elevated temperatures*, which increase the entropy term ($\Delta S$) of the free energy equation, biasing reaction mixtures toward elimination rather than substitution.

## connections

- [[nucleophilic-substitution]] — competes directly with elimination for alkyl halide substrates, depending on base strength and temperature.
- [[organic-halide]] — alkyl halides are classic substrates for elimination, acting as sources of halogen leaving groups.
- [[potassium]] — potassium tert-butoxide is a classic sterically hindered base used to drive E2 eliminations toward the less-substituted Hofmann alkene.
