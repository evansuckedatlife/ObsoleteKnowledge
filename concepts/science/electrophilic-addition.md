# Electrophilic Addition

## summary

Electrophilic addition is an organic reaction where a pi bond in an unsaturated compound (such as an alkene or alkyne) is broken to react with an electron-deficient species (an electrophile), resulting in the formation of two new sigma bonds. This reaction class is the primary method for functionalizing carbon-carbon double and triple bonds.

## you gotta know

- *Obeys Markovnikov's rule* for hydrohalogenation, which dictates that the hydrogen atom adds to the carbon with the greater number of hydrogens, a regioselectivity dictated by the formation of the more stable carbocation intermediate.
- *Proceeds via a cyclic halonium ion* during halogenation (addition of Cl2 or Br2), which blocks one face of the molecule to force an anti-addition stereochemical outcome.
- *Includes hydroboration-oxidation*, a two-step reaction that bypasses Markovnikov regioselectivity to yield the anti-Markovnikov alcohol via a concerted syn-addition of boron and hydrogen.
- *Subject to carbocation rearrangements* (such as hydride or alkyl shifts) in reactions like acid-catalyzed hydration, which occur to form a more stable carbocation.
- *Avoids rearrangement via oxymercuration-demercuration*, which utilizes a cyclic mercurinium ion intermediate to cleanly yield the Markovnikov hydration product.

## connections

- [[organic-halide]] — addition of hydrogen halides (HCl, HBr, or HI) to alkenes is a standard method for producing alkyl halides.
- [[alcohol]] — acid-catalyzed hydration or hydroboration-oxidation of alkenes yields alcohols.
- [[mercury]] — mercury(II) acetate is the electrophilic reagent used to form the cyclic intermediate in oxymercuration.
