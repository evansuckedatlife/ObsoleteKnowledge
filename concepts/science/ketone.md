# Ketone

## summary

A ketone is an organic compound characterized by a carbonyl functional group (C=O) bonded to two carbon atoms, represented by the general formula R-CO-R'. Unlike aldehydes, the carbonyl carbon of a ketone is situated within a carbon skeleton rather than at a terminal position, rendering them more resistant to oxidation.

## you gotta know

- *Synthesized by the oxidation of secondary alcohols* using reagents like chromic acid, PCC, or via the Swern oxidation.
- *Fails to react with Tollens' or Fehling's reagents*, a property that distinguishes them from aldehydes due to the absence of a hydrogen atom directly bonded to the carbonyl carbon.
- *Detected using the iodoform test* if they are methyl ketones (containing a -COCH3 group), which react with iodine and base to form a yellow precipitate of triiodomethane (iodoform).
- *Reacts with nucleophiles* (such as Grignard reagents) in nucleophilic additions to form tetrahedral intermediates, which yield tertiary alcohols upon protonation.
- *Includes acetone* (propanone), the simplest and most common ketone, which is a volatile, flammable liquid widely used as an industrial and laboratory solvent.

## connections

- [[alcohol]] — secondary alcohols are oxidized to ketones, and ketones can be reduced back to secondary alcohols using sodium borohydride.
- [[aldehyde]] — ketones and aldehydes are constitutional isomers when they share the same molecular formula (e.g., acetone and propanal).
- [[carbon]] — the carbonyl carbon is sp2 hybridized, creating a planar geometry that allows nucleophiles to attack from either face.
