# Electrolysis

## summary

Electrolysis is a technique that uses a direct electric current (DC) to drive an otherwise non-spontaneous chemical reaction. It is widely used in laboratory and industrial settings to decompose chemical compounds, isolate highly reactive elements, refine metals, and coat surfaces via electroplating.

## you gotta know

- *Governed by Faraday's laws of electrolysis*, which state that the mass of a substance produced at an electrode is directly proportional to the total electric charge passed through the electrolyte.
- *Takes place in an electrolytic cell*, where a power source drives electrons from the anode (the site of oxidation, which attracts anions) to the cathode (the site of reduction, which attracts cations).
- *Produces aluminum industrially via the Hall-Héroult process*, which electrolyzes aluminum oxide dissolved in molten cryolite using consumable carbon anodes.
- *Extracts metallic sodium via the Downs cell*, which performs electrolysis on molten sodium chloride.
- *Drives the chloralkali process*, an economically vital industrial process that electrolyzes aqueous sodium chloride brine to yield chlorine gas, hydrogen gas, and sodium hydroxide.

## connections

- [[sodium]] — Humphry Davy first isolated elemental sodium in 1807 by electrolyzing molten sodium hydroxide.
- [[copper]] — purified industrially using electrorefining, where impure copper anodes dissolve and deposit as pure copper at the cathode.
- [[hydrogen]] — electrolysis of water splits liquid water into gaseous hydrogen at the cathode and oxygen at the anode.
