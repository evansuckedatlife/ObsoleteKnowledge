# Photon

## summary

A *photon* is the fundamental quantum of electromagnetic radiation—the smallest indivisible unit of light and all forms of electromagnetic energy. It is a massless boson that mediates the electromagnetic force within the Standard Model. Photons carry energy proportional to their frequency via Planck's relation *E* = *h*ν, where *h* is Planck's constant and ν is frequency. They travel at the speed of light in vacuum and embody the wave-particle duality central to quantum mechanics: they behave as waves (exhibiting diffraction and interference) and as particles (carrying discrete energy and momentum).

## you gotta know

- A photon has zero rest mass but carries energy *E* = *hν* and momentum *p* = *E*/*c* = *hν*/*c*, where *c* is the speed of light.
- Photons are spin-1 particles (massless vector bosons); the two spin states correspond to the two polarizations of light (vertical and horizontal, or left and right circular).
- In the quantum field picture, photons are excitations of the electromagnetic field. The number of photons in a region of space can fluctuate; even "empty" vacuum fluctuates with virtual photon pairs.
- Photons do not decay; once emitted, a photon can travel indefinitely (or until absorbed by matter). An electron absorbing a photon gains its energy; an electron dropping to a lower energy state emits a photon.
- The photoelectric effect (Einstein, 1905) demonstrated photons are quantized: a single photon must have enough energy to eject an electron from a metal surface, no matter how intense the light—disproving the classical wave picture.
- High-energy photons (X-rays, gamma rays) can create particle-antiparticle pairs in the presence of a nucleus; low-energy photons (radio, microwave) are absorbed and re-emitted as thermal radiation.
