# Celsius scale

## summary

The **Celsius scale** is a practical temperature scale where 0 °C is the freezing point of water and 100 °C is its boiling point at standard atmospheric pressure. Developed in the 18th century by Swedish astronomer *Anders Celsius*, it divides the water-ice to water-steam range into 100 equal intervals. It remains the most widely used scale in everyday life and science globally.

## you gotta know

- Originally called the *centigrade scale* because it has 100 degrees between the two water reference points.
- Renamed to *Celsius* in 1948 to honor its inventor and avoid confusion with other centigrade systems.
- The scale is offset from the Kelvin scale by exactly 273.15 units (0 °C = 273.15 K).
- Negative temperatures are possible on the Celsius scale, representing conditions colder than water's freezing point.
- Intervals are the same size as Kelvin intervals, making Celsius practical for everyday and laboratory use while Kelvin handles physics requiring absolute temperature.
- Water's freezing point (0 °C) and boiling point (100 °C) are defined at standard sea-level atmospheric pressure (101.325 kPa).
- The scale is *linear*, not logarithmic, allowing direct comparison and arithmetic across temperature differences.
