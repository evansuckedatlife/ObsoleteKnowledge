# Fujita–Pearson scale

## summary

The **Enhanced Fujita scale** (EF scale) rates tornado intensity from EF0 to EF5 based on wind speed estimates derived from structural damage rather than direct wind measurements. Developed by *Ted Fujita* and updated by *Don Pearson*, the scale provides a standardized framework for assessing tornado damage severity and estimating peak wind speeds in the field.

## you gotta know

- Named after *Theodore Fujita*, a pioneering tornado researcher, and *Don Pearson*, who enhanced the scale to improve accuracy and account for building variations.
- The original *F scale* (1971) was replaced by the *Enhanced Fujita scale* (EF scale) in 2007 to better reflect building performance and modern construction standards.
- Ratings range from *EF0* (65–85 mph winds; minor damage) to *EF5* (≥ 200 mph winds; catastrophic damage and total devastation of strong structures).
- Crucially, wind speeds are *inferred from damage*, not measured directly. Damage surveys of trees, buildings, and structures are analyzed to estimate winds.
- The scale is *ordinal*; damage categories correspond to observable destruction: branches stripped (EF0), roof surfaces peeled (EF2), frame houses blown away (EF4), entire communities obliterated (EF5).
- EF ratings are subjective and depend on the quality of damage surveys and building construction variability, so there is inherent uncertainty.
- Used by meteorologists, engineers, and emergency managers to understand tornado impacts and improve building codes and hazard preparation.
