# Cooling System

## summary

A **cooling system** (or thermal management system) removes excess heat generated by electronic components and dissipates it into the environment. In computers, cooling prevents thermal throttling (performance loss), hardware damage, and premature component failure. Modern systems combine passive methods (heatsinks exploiting conduction and convection) and active methods (fans and liquid cooling loops) to maintain safe operating temperatures.

## you gotta know

- **Heat sources:** the CPU is the primary heat generator (proportional to power consumption and frequency); GPUs, power supplies, and voltage regulators also dissipate significant heat.
- **Thermal design power (TDP):** the maximum heat a component is designed to dissipate; specifies the cooling capacity required (e.g., a 150W CPU requires a cooler rated for ≥150W).
- **Passive cooling:** heatsinks (metal fins) conduct heat away from the component (e.g., CPU die) and radiate/convect it into ambient air; no active power required but limited by surface area.
- **Active cooling:** fans force air over heatsinks (air cooling) or circulate liquid through tubing (liquid cooling), vastly improving heat transfer rate and enabling higher sustained power.
- **Thermal interface material (TIM):** a paste or pad between the CPU and heatsink fills microscopic gaps, improving conduction; poor TIM application causes thermal hot-spots.
- **Thermal resistance:** measured in °C/W, it quantifies temperature rise per watt dissipated; lower is better (R_th = ΔT / Power). Path: chip → TIM → heatsink → air involves multiple resistances in series.
- **Liquid cooling loops:** circulate coolant (water or specialty fluid) through CPU/GPU blocks, then through a radiator; more efficient than air but requires maintenance and carries leak risk.

## connections

- [[thermodynamics]] — cooling exploits heat flow down temperature gradients (second law).
- [[power-supply]] — thermal management of the PSU itself is critical; efficiency loss (excess input power) becomes heat.
- [[cpu]] — the hottest component in most systems; cooler design directly limits frequency/performance.
- [[gpu]] — high-end graphics accelerators are extremely power-dense and thermally demanding.
- [[conduction]] — heatsink fins conduct heat away from the component; TIM enables conduction across interfaces.
- [[convection]] — air movement over heatsink fins transfers heat to the environment; fans enhance convection.
- [[radiation-heat]] — radiators in liquid cooling systems dissipate heat via convection to ambient air.
