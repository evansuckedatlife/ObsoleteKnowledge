# Friedel-Crafts Reaction

## summary

The Friedel-Crafts reaction is a set of organic reactions developed by Charles Friedel and James Crafts in 1877 to attach substituents to an aromatic ring. Classified as an electrophilic aromatic substitution, the reaction is divided into alkylation (which attaches an alkyl group) and acylation (which attaches an acyl group), providing a primary method for functionalizing benzene derivatives.

## you gotta know

- *Requires a strong Lewis acid catalyst* (most commonly aluminum chloride, AlCl3) to react with an alkyl or acyl halide to generate a highly reactive carbocation or acylium ion electrophile.
- *Alkylation is prone to carbocation rearrangements*, where the intermediate carbocation shifts to a more stable form (e.g., primary to secondary) before electrophilic attack, leading to isomer mixtures.
- *Alkylation is prone to over-alkylation*, as the newly introduced alkyl group acts as an electron-donating activator that makes the ring more reactive to subsequent substitutions.
- *Acylation avoids rearrangement and over-alkylation*, because the intermediate acylium ion is resonance-stabilized and does not rearrange, and the product ketone is electron-withdrawing, deactivating the ring to further reaction.
- *Inhibited by strongly deactivated rings*, failing to occur on aromatic systems containing strong electron-withdrawing groups, such as nitrobenzene or sulfonic acid derivatives.

## connections

- [[organic-halide]] — alkyl and acyl halides are the electrophilic reactants that are activated by the Lewis acid catalyst.
- [[ketone]] — acylation produces aromatic ketones, which can be subsequently reduced to alkyl chains using the Clemmensen or Wolff-Kishner reductions to bypass alkylation rearrangement problems.
- [[carbon]] — the reaction forms a new carbon-carbon bond between the aromatic ring and the alkyl or acyl group.
