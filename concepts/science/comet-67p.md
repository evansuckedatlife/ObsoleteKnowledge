## summary

**Comet 67P/Churyumov-Gerasimenko** is a small periodic comet notable for being the first comet to be orbited and studied at close range by a spacecraft. The European Space Agency's *Rosetta* mission, which intercepted 67P in 2014 and followed it for over two years, revealed an irregularly shaped *contact-binary* nucleus, vigorous outgassing jets, and rich diversity of volatile ices and organic chemistry. It exemplifies the complexity of cometary nuclei and the feasibility of close planetary reconnaissance.

## you gotta know

- *Discovered* in 1969 by Soviet astronomers Svetlana Gerasimenko and Kliment Churyumov while photographing comet Churyumov-Gerasimenko's expected location; the "67P" designation reflects its numbering in the periodic-comet catalogue.
- *Contact-binary nucleus:* two lobes connected at a narrow neck, sometimes called a "rubber duck" shape; roughly 4.3 km × 2.5 km × 2 km overall.
- *Rosetta spacecraft* (launched 2004) rendezvoused with 67P in August 2014 and executed the first-ever spacecraft landing on a comet nucleus (Philae lander) on 12 November 2014.
- *Vigorous outgassing:* jets of water vapor and dust erupted from localized vents, particularly as the nucleus approached perihelion; activity intensified dramatically near closest approach to the Sun.
- *Organic-rich surface:* spectroscopy detected a dark carbonaceous crust composed of complex organics and silicate minerals; water-ice outcrops appeared at depth, suggesting interior heterogeneity.
- *Low density* of ~400 kg/m³, indicating the nucleus is highly porous and likely a gravitational aggregate — loosely held together by weak self-gravity.

## connections

- [[kuiper-belt]] — 67P is a *short-period comet* likely captured from the Kuiper Belt and nudged into its current orbit by Jupiter's gravity.
- [[rosetta-mission]] — the defining observational achievement, transforming understanding of cometary nuclei from theoretical models to in-situ data.
- [[comet]] — 67P exemplifies how modern space missions demystify cometary nature and chemistry.
