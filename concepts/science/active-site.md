# Active Site

## summary

The *active site* of an enzyme is the specific three-dimensional region where substrate molecules bind and catalysis occurs. Formed by a precise arrangement of amino acids, the active site uses non-covalent interactions to stabilise substrates and facilitate their conversion to products, typically lowering activation energy and conferring the enzyme's characteristic specificity and efficiency.

## you gotta know

- The active site is a *pocket* or cleft within the enzyme's *tertiary structure*, formed by amino acids from distant regions of the protein that fold together.
- *Substrate specificity* arises from the shape, charge distribution, and hydrogen-bonding pattern of the active site—the *lock-and-key* model describes this fit, though the more accurate *induced-fit* model allows the enzyme to change shape upon substrate binding.
- *Non-covalent interactions* (hydrogen bonds, van der Waals forces, electrostatic attractions, hydrophobic effects) hold substrate to the active site; these are weak individually but collectively provide both affinity and reversibility.
- The active site stabilises the *transition state* of the reaction more effectively than the ground state, lowering the energy barrier for catalysis.
- *Allosteric regulation*: some enzymes have regulatory sites distant from the active site that, when bound by effector molecules, alter the active site's shape or affinity—enabling feedback control of metabolic pathways.
- *Cofactors* and *coenzymes* (metal ions or small organic molecules like NAD⁺ or ATP) often participate directly in the active site's catalytic mechanism.
- The active site is typically composed of only ~5–10% of the enzyme's amino acids, despite the whole protein being necessary for its proper fold and function.

## connections

- [[catalysis]] — the active site is where catalysis takes place.
- [[enzyme]] — the protein that houses and deploys the active site.
- [[protein]] — the three-dimensional polymer of amino acids that forms the active site.
- [[substrate]] — the molecule that binds to the active site and undergoes catalysis.
- [[photosynthesis]] — Rubisco's active site catalyses CO₂ fixation, the first committed step of the Calvin cycle.
- [[rubisco]] — a massive enzyme with a large, imperfect active site that occasionally oxygenates instead of carboxylating RuBP.
