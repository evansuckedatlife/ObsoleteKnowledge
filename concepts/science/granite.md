# Granite

## summary

**Granite** is a coarse-grained, light-coloured *felsic* igneous rock composed primarily of quartz, feldspar, and mica. It forms deep underground from slow-cooling magma and is the most abundant intrusive igneous rock. Granites are highly prized as building stone and countertops because of their durability, aesthetic appeal, and resistance to weathering.

## you gotta know

- *Felsic* igneous rock — "felsic" refers to high feldspar (fels) and silica (sil) content; ~70% SiO₂, much higher than basalt.
- Forms from *slow cooling* of magma deep underground, allowing large mineral crystals to grow; mineral grains are visible to the naked eye (coarse-grained texture).
- Primary minerals are *quartz*, *feldspar* (both orthoclase and plagioclase), and *mica* (muscovite and/or biotite) in roughly equal proportions.
- Light coloured — typically pink, white, or grey — due to high content of light minerals (quartz and potassium feldspar) and low iron/magnesium.
- Less dense (~2.7 g/cm³) than basalt, and more chemically resistant to weathering because quartz is so stable.
- Comprises most of the continental crust; exposed in mountain cores, shield regions, and eroded mountain belts.
- Prized as dimension stone for building facades, countertops, monuments, and decorative tile; also crushed for aggregate.

## connections

- [[basalt]] — basalt is mafic (dark, low-silica); granite is felsic (light, high-silica); they represent opposite ends of igneous diversity.
- [[quartz]] — the dominant mineral in granite; its hardness and stability allow granite to resist weathering.
- [[feldspar]] — orthoclase feldspar is a defining mineral of granite, alongside plagioclase.
- [[mica]] — mica gives granite its characteristic sparkle and is a key identifying mineral alongside quartz and feldspar.
- [[igneous-rock]] — granite is the prototypical intrusive igneous rock, cooled slowly beneath the surface.
