## summary

The endoplasmic reticulum (ER) is a vast, interconnected membrane network that synthesizes and transports proteins and lipids throughout the cell. The rough ER (studded with ribosomes) specializes in producing secreted and membrane proteins, while the smooth ER (ribosome-free) synthesizes lipids and metabolizes drugs. The ER serves as a distribution hub, directing newly made molecules to their correct cellular destinations.

## you gotta know

- **Rough ER**: ribosomes attached to outer membrane; synthesizes proteins for secretion, membrane insertion, or organellar targeting
- **Smooth ER**: lacks ribosomes; site of lipid synthesis, drug metabolism, and calcium storage
- **Signal sequences**: amino acid signals direct proteins destined for secretion to rough ER during translation
- **Transitional ER**: regions where vesicles bud off toward the Golgi apparatus
- **Interconnected network**: continuous with outer nuclear envelope; forms extensive tubular and cisternal system
- **Co-translational translocation**: proteins enter ER through translocon channel *while* being synthesized
- **Lipid bilayer synthesis**: ER produces phospholipids and cholesterol for cellular membranes

## context

The ER is the entry point for the secretory pathway, a sophisticated cellular trafficking system that ensures proteins reach correct compartments. Its enormous surface area (amplified by extensive folding) makes it highly efficient at protein and lipid synthesis. In liver and muscle cells, smooth ER is particularly abundant due to high demands for lipid synthesis and drug metabolism. The ER also functions as a calcium reservoir, releasing Ca2+ to trigger muscular contraction and intracellular signaling cascades.

- [[ribosome]] — bound ribosomes attached to rough ER membrane
