## summary

*Thomas Young's* landmark 1801 experiment demonstrated that light behaves as a *wave*, not merely as particles, by passing it through two parallel slits and observing interference patterns on a screen. When measured at the quantum scale, however, light behaves differently — a later paradox that would become central to quantum mechanics' strange nature.

## you gotta know

- *Thomas Young* split a beam of light through two adjacent slits, producing alternating bright and dark bands (*interference fringes*) on a distant screen — the hallmark of *wave* behaviour.
- The pattern only appears when both slits are open; closing one erases the interference, replacing it with simple diffraction.
- The fringes reveal that light from each slit *interferes* with light from the other — constructive interference (bright) where waves align, destructive (dark) where they cancel.
- In the *quantum* variant, single photons or electrons seem to go through both slits simultaneously, yet "know" when they're observed — the *measurement problem*.
- The experiment's elegance lies in proving *wave* properties (superposition, diffraction) from a simple apparatus; its paradox lies in the measurement: observe which slit the particle uses, and the interference vanishes.
- Young's result displaced centuries of *corpuscular* (particle) theories of light championed by *Newton*.
