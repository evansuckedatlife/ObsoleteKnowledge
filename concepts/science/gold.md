# Gold

## summary

Gold is a dense, soft, malleable, and highly ductile transition metal with a distinctive bright, warm yellow color. It is one of the least reactive chemical elements, resistant to most acids and atmospheric gases, which allows it to persist in its native, uncombined state in nature. Because of its rarity, ease of working, and resistance to corrosion, it has served as a primary standard of currency, jewelry, and art throughout human history.

## you gotta know

- *Dissolves in aqua regia*, a corrosive mixture of concentrated nitric acid and hydrochloric acid, which converts the gold into soluble tetrachloroauric acid.
- *Extracted using gold cyanidation* (the Elsner reaction), which uses aqueous cyanide solution in the presence of oxygen to leach gold out of low-grade ores.
- *Exhibits significant relativistic effects*, which contract the 6s electron orbital and lower its energy, explaining both its yellow color (due to absorption of blue light) and its chemical inertness.
- *Serves as the target in Rutherford's gold foil experiment* in 1909, which demonstrated the existence of a dense atomic nucleus by scattering alpha particles.
- *Forms amalgams easily with mercury*, a property historically exploited in gold panning and mining to consolidate fine gold particles.

## connections

- [[copper]] — a Group 11 congener that shares high conductivity and malleability.
- [[mercury]] — dissolves gold to form amalgams, which can be heated to distill away the mercury and recover pure gold.
- [[uranium]] — often found associated with gold in quartz-pebble conglomerate deposits, such as those in the Witwatersrand basin in South Africa.
