# Io

## summary

**Io** is the innermost of Jupiter's four large *Galilean moons* and the most volcanically active body in the entire solar system. Discovered by Galileo Galilei in 1610, Io's extreme volcanism—hundreds of active volcanoes, lava lakes, sulfur geysers reaching heights of 500 km—is driven by intense tidal heating: Jupiter's massive gravity repeatedly flexes and squeezes Io's interior, generating enormous internal friction. Its constantly reshaped, colorful surface (yellows, oranges, reds from sulfur and sulfur dioxide) makes Io a natural laboratory for understanding volcanism, planetary interiors, and the physics of tidal dissipation.

## you gotta know

### Volcanic Activity and Tidal Heating
- *Most volcanically active body in the solar system*: hundreds of active volcanoes; eruption plumes detected at altitudes exceeding 500 km, the highest known in the solar system.
- *Tidal heating mechanism*: Jupiter's immense gravity (and resonance with neighboring moons Ganymede and Europa) alternately compresses and stretches Io's interior at orbital frequencies, generating frictional heat that far exceeds heat from radioactive decay alone.
- *Lava composition*: Io's lavas are primarily sulfur and silicate; lava temperatures exceed 1,500 K, among the hottest known in the solar system.
- *Lack of a permanent atmosphere*: Io has only a transient atmosphere of sulfur dioxide (SO₂); in polar regions, SO₂ condenses as frost and sublimates seasonally.

### Physical Characteristics
- *Diameter 3,643 km*; fourth-largest moon overall; slightly larger than Earth's Moon and similar in density (~3.5 g/cm³).
- *Orbital period 1.77 days*; in orbital resonance with Europa (1:2) and Ganymede (1:4), the 2:1:4 resonance drives the tidal heating.
- *Surface topography*: covered in sulfur compounds and lava flows; colors range from yellow (sulfur) and orange-red (sulfur oxides) to black (fresh lava); highest peaks (Boösaule Montes, etc.) reach ~9 km elevation.

### Discovery and Observation
- *Discovered by Galileo Galilei in 1610*, one of the four Galilean moons; historically significant as evidence for the heliocentric model.
- *Observed closely by Voyager spacecraft* (1979) and subsequently by Galileo orbiter (1995–2003), revealing active volcanism in real time.
- *Io-Jupiter plasma torus*: atoms (primarily sulfur and oxygen ions) stripped from Io's volcanoes and atmosphere form a toroidal cloud of charged particles around Jupiter at Io's orbital distance.

## context

Io is a masterclass in the power of tidal forces and a stark contrast to the other Galilean moons. While Europa and Ganymede harbor subsurface oceans and are geologically varied, Io is a world of fire and sulfur, its interior heating running away as a consequence of orbital resonance and tidal dissipation. This makes Io crucial to understanding how planetary bodies evolve: tidal heating can sustain liquid water (Europa, Enceladus), drive extreme volcanism (Io), or cool a moon entirely (Callisto). Io's active volcanism is also a window into planetary interiors—the materials, temperatures, and compositions of lava reveal the moon's internal structure. More broadly, Io exemplifies the diversity of solar-system bodies and the geophysical processes that shape them.
