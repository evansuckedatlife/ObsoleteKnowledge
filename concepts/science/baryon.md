# Baryon

## summary

**Baryons** are *hadrons* composed of three *quarks* held together by *gluon* exchange via the strong force. The most familiar baryons are the *proton* and *neutron*, which form atomic nuclei. Baryons carry integer or half-integer *baryon number*, obey *Fermi-Dirac statistics*, and include exotic particles like the Lambda and Sigma hyperons.

## you gotta know

- Composed of three valence *quarks* (bound by *gluons*)
- Baryon number +1; antiparticles have baryon number −1
- Include *nucleons* (proton, neutron) and *hyperons* (Lambda, Sigma, Xi, Omega)
- Carry *spin-3/2* or *spin-1/2* depending on internal quark configuration
- Subject to *baryon number conservation* in weak and strong interactions
- Lightest and most stable are the proton; neutron decays with 880-second half-life
- Exotic baryons (pentaquarks) theoretically predicted and recently observed

## context

Baryons make up the visible universe—protons and neutrons form the nuclei of all atoms. The existence of baryons beyond the nucleon was a major discovery of mid-20th century particle physics and motivated the quark model. Baryon asymmetry (more matter than antimatter) remains a deep cosmological puzzle, yet baryon number is strictly conserved at high energies.

## connections

- [[hadron]] — baryons are a subclass of hadrons
- [[quark]] — baryons are bound states of three quarks
- [[proton]] — the lightest, stable baryon; nucleus of hydrogen
- [[neutron]] — beta-decays into proton; essential for nuclear stability
- [[gluon]] — mediator of quark binding in baryons
