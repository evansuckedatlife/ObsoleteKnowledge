## summary

**Mesons** are *hadrons* composed of a *quark*-*antiquark* pair bound by *gluon* exchange. They carry integer *spin* and integer charge, obey *Bose-Einstein statistics*, and decay via the weak force. The *pion* and *kaon* are the lightest and most common mesons; together with baryons, they comprise the *hadron* family.

## you gotta know

- Quark-antiquark composite: one *quark* + one *antiquark*
- Baryon number zero; possess no baryon number conservation barrier to decay
- Integer spin (0, 1, 2, …) — *bosonic* despite being made of fermions
- Examples: *pion* (lightest, mediator of nuclear force), *kaon* (contains strange quark), *rho* (vector meson)
- Most mesons decay within picoseconds via *weak force* (flavor-changing)
- Carry opposite electric charges for quark-antiquark pairs
- Pseudoscalar and vector mesons dominate low-energy hadron phenomenology

## context

Mesons were the first composite particles predicted and observed, historically motivating the quark model itself. The *pion*, discovered in cosmic rays, explained the range of the nuclear force through *Yukawa's meson exchange theory*. Meson oscillations (kaon mixing, B meson oscillations) provide unique windows into CP violation and rare decays, essential for understanding matter-antimatter asymmetry.

## connections

- [[hadron]] — mesons are one of two hadron families
- [[quark]] — composed of quark-antiquark bound states
- [[baryon]] — the other hadron class; contain three quarks
- [[gluon]] — binds quarks within mesons
- [[pion]] — the lightest, most abundant meson
- [[antimatter]] — mesons naturally contain particle-antiparticle pairs
- [[boson]] — mesons are composite bosons
