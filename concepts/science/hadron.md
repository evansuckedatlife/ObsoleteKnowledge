# Hadron

## summary

**Hadrons** are composite particles made of *quarks* bound by *gluon* exchange through the *strong nuclear force*. The two primary hadron classes are *baryons* (three quarks) and *mesons* (quark-antiquark pairs). All hadrons are color-neutral and confined—individual quarks cannot escape. The proton and neutron are the most stable and abundant hadrons.

## you gotta know

- Composite structures of *quarks* and *gluons* ("*partons*")
- Color confinement: color-neutral combinations only—reds cancel with antired, etc.
- Two main categories: *baryons* (three quarks, baryon number +1) and *mesons* (one quark-antiquark pair, baryon number 0)
- Interact via all four fundamental forces except that the strong force dominates their structure
- Most hadrons are unstable and decay within picoseconds or less
- Hadron spectrum: hundreds of resonances discovered; patterns explained by quark model
- Internal structure probed via deep inelastic scattering reveals parton distributions

## context

Hadrons are the only *quarks* observed in nature—single quarks cannot be isolated. The *proton*, composed of two up quarks and one down quark, is the universe's most abundant baryon. Hadron phenomenology drives modern particle physics: rare hadron decays constrain physics beyond the Standard Model, and the quark-gluon plasma—a hadron-dissolving state—existed microseconds after the Big Bang.

## connections

- [[quark]] — hadrons are bound states of quarks
- [[gluon]] — mediates the strong force binding hadrons
- [[baryon]] — hadrons composed of three quarks
- [[meson]] — hadrons composed of quark-antiquark pairs
- [[proton]] — the stable baryon nucleus of hydrogen
- [[neutron]] — the stable baryon; decays when free
- [[quantum-chromodynamics]] — the theory governing hadron structure
