# decibel scale

## summary

The **decibel** (dB) is a logarithmic unit measuring the ratio of a physical quantity (most commonly sound intensity or power) relative to a reference standard. A 10 dB increase represents a tenfold increase in power; a 20 dB increase represents a hundredfold increase in intensity (depending on the reference). Its logarithmic nature compresses the vast human-perceptible range into a manageable numerical scale.

## you gotta know

- Named after *Alexander Graham Bell*; the *bel* is the unit, and a *decibel* is one-tenth of a bel.
- The decibel is *logarithmic*: dB = 10 log₁₀(measured value / reference value). This compression reflects how human perception of loudness and power scales logarithmically, not linearly.
- Reference standards vary: for sound intensity in air, the reference is typically 10⁻¹² W/m² (the threshold of human hearing). For sound pressure, it is 20 μPa. Context determines which applies.
- Normal conversation is roughly 60 dB; a jet engine is 140 dB; the threshold of pain is around 130 dB.
- A 3 dB increase represents roughly a doubling of perceived loudness energy; a 10 dB increase is roughly a tenfold increase in power.
- Used in acoustics, electronics, communications, and many engineering disciplines to express power, intensity, and attenuation ratios.
- Negative dB values are meaningful (e.g., −6 dB is one-quarter the reference power).
