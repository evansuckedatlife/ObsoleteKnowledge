# Pepsin

## summary

Pepsin is a serine protease secreted in the stomach that catalyzes the hydrolysis of *peptide bonds* in proteins, initiating protein digestion. It functions in the highly acidic stomach environment (pH 1.5–2.5) and is notably specific for hydrophobic residues flanking the bond.

## you gotta know

- Catalyzes: hydrolysis of peptide bonds, especially those adjacent to aromatic or branched-chain amino acids (Phe, Trp, Tyr, Leu)
- Substrate: proteins and polypeptides; begins digestion before other proteases in the small intestine
- Secretion: produced as inactive zymogen (pepsinogen) in stomach chief cells; activated by low pH and autocleavage
- pH optimum: 1.5–2.5; native enzyme denatures and irreversibly inactivates at neutral pH
- Products: large peptide fragments (10–30+ amino acids); not complete amino acid liberation (accomplished later by pancreatic proteases)
- Mechanism: aspartic protease active site contains two catalytic aspartate residues that facilitate nucleophilic attack on the peptide carbonyl
- Clinical relevance: peptic ulcers result from pepsin-induced tissue damage when stomach protection is compromised

## context

Pepsin represents an elegant adaptation to gastric digestion: it functions optimally in an environment (pH 1.5–2.5) where most proteins are denatured and most enzymes are destroyed. The stomach's extreme acidity itself serves protective functions—killing pathogens and providing an antiseptic barrier. Pepsin's specificity for hydrophobic positions reflects the prevalence of hydrophobic residues in protein interiors; by preferentially cleaving bonds exposing these residues, pepsin opens up the protein structure, accelerating subsequent digestion by intestinal proteases. The zymogen activation system (pepsinogen → pepsin) adds a regulatory layer: pepsinogen cannot autodigest the stomach lining, but active pepsin is instantly self-activating once a sufficient concentration is produced.
