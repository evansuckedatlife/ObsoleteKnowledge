# Gymnosperms

## summary

**Gymnosperms** are *seed-bearing plants* whose seeds develop *naked* — exposed directly to the air rather than enclosed in a fruit. The group includes conifers (pines, firs, spruce), cycads, ginkgos, and gnetophytes. They dominated terrestrial ecosystems for over 200 million years before flowering plants rose to prominence.

## you gotta know

- Seeds lack a protective fruit layer; instead, they sit exposed on the surface of a *cone* scale or equivalent structure, distinguishing them from *angiosperm* seeds enclosed in an ovary.
- All gymnosperms possess *vascular tissue* and are *woody*, with a durable secondary growth pattern (tree rings in conifers).
- Reproduce via *pollination* — wind-blown *pollen* delivers *sperm* to the egg, eliminating dependence on free water entirely; the *gametophyte* is microscopic and housed within the seed.
- Four major groups: *Coniferophyta* (pines, firs, spruces—most diverse), *Cycadophyta* (palm-like, ancient), *Ginkgophyta* (one surviving species, the ginkgo), and *Gnetophyta* (rare, varied forms).
- Dominated the Mesozoic Era (age of dinosaurs); conifers remain ecologically dominant in cool or harsh climates (boreal forests, high mountains).
- Many conifers produce *resin* and *secondary metabolites* that deter herbivores and provide commercial products (turpentine, pitch).

## connections

- [[pterophytes]] — gymnosperms evolved from fern-like ancestors by acquiring seeds; spores are now enclosed inside seeds rather than broadcast to the air.
- [[angiosperms]] — flowering plants are the modern descendants of early gymnosperms, adding flowers and fruits.
- [[seed]] — the defining innovation of gymnosperms and angiosperms.
- [[monocots]] — a major subdivision of angiosperms.
- [[dicots]] — the complementary major subdivision of angiosperms.
- [[vascular-plants]] — gymnosperms are firmly within the vascular clade.
