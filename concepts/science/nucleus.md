# Nucleus

## summary

The nucleus is the membrane-bound organelle that contains and protects the cell's genetic material (DNA), serving as the command center for cellular operations. It controls gene expression and coordinates the synthesis of proteins needed for cell survival and function. Present in all eukaryotic cells, the nucleus is typically the largest organelle and is essential for cell division and heredity.

## you gotta know

- **Nuclear envelope**: double-membrane structure with *nuclear pores* that control selective passage of molecules
- **Chromatin**: DNA wrapped around histone proteins; condenses into *chromosomes* during cell division
- **Nucleolus**: dense region within the nucleus where *ribosomal RNA (rRNA)* is synthesized
- **Gene expression**: nucleus regulates transcription (DNA → RNA) and indirectly controls translation in the cytoplasm
- **Nuclear localization signals**: amino acid sequences that direct proteins back into the nucleus
- **Euchromatin vs. heterochromatin**: loosely packed (active) vs. tightly packed (inactive) DNA regions
- **Continuous exchange**: nucleus maintains dynamic molecular traffic with cytoplasm through nuclear pores

## context

The nucleus arose in early eukaryotes and fundamentally distinguishes them from prokaryotes. By compartmentalizing DNA, eukaryotes achieved greater control over gene regulation and allowed for more complex cellular specialization. The nucleus acts as a processing center: DNA is replicated and transcribed here, mRNA is processed and exported, and responses to environmental signals are coordinated. This separation of transcription from translation—unique to eukaryotes—permits sophisticated regulation of protein production.
