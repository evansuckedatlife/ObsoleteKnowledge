# Superposition

## summary

Superposition is the quantum principle that a system can exist simultaneously in multiple states or configurations, each with a definite probability of being observed. The *superposed state* is not a mixture of definite states but a coherent combination described by a single [[wavefunction]]. Upon measurement, the superposition "collapses" to one definite outcome.

## you gotta know

- A quantum system is in a *linear combination* of basis states: |ψ⟩ = c₁|state₁⟩ + c₂|state₂⟩ + …
- The coefficients c are *complex amplitudes*; their squared magnitudes give probabilities
- Before measurement, both/all superposed states coexist; the system has no definite value for the observable
- Superposition applies to all quantum properties: position, momentum, spin, energy
- [[wave-particle-duality]] makes superposition intuitive: waves interfere because they're in multiple states simultaneously
- Quantum [[interference]] patterns arise from superposed paths or states
- *Entangled* systems exhibit correlated superpositions across multiple particles

## context

Superposition is counterintuitive because classical objects seem to occupy one state. But quantum objects are described by waves, which can occupy multiple locations (or multiple momenta, or multiple spin orientations) at once. The [[schrodinger-equation]] describes how superposed states evolve smoothly in time. Measurement breaks this evolution, forcing the system to choose one outcome according to the probabilities embedded in the [[wavefunction]]. This is not ignorance—experiments confirm the superposition is genuinely real until measured.

## connections

- [[wavefunction]] — the mathematical encoding of a superposed state
- [[measurement]] — the act that collapses superposition to a single outcome
- [[quantum-entanglement]] — superpositions can be *non-separable* across multiple particles
- [[quantum-spin]] — a spin-1/2 system is naturally a superposition of spin-up and spin-down
- [[uncertainty-principle]] — arises because momentum and position superpositions are complementary
- [[interference]] — superposed paths interfere in the double-slit experiment
