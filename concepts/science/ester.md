# Ester

## summary

An ester is a class of organic compounds derived from an acid (usually a carboxylic acid) and an alcohol, characterized by the general formula R-CO-OR'. Esters are polar but cannot form hydrogen bonds with themselves, leading to lower boiling points than their parent carboxylic acids. They are famous for their pleasant, fruity odors and are widely found in fruits, fats, oils, and synthetic flavorings.

## you gotta know

- *Synthesized via Fischer esterification*, a reversible acid-catalyzed condensation reaction between a carboxylic acid and an alcohol, driven to completion by removing water.
- *Undergoes basic hydrolysis* (commonly called saponification), which reacts the ester with a strong base to produce an alcohol and a carboxylate salt, the basis for soap making.
- *Participates in transesterification*, where the alkoxy group of an ester is exchanged with that of an alcohol, which is the primary reaction used to produce biodiesel from vegetable oils.
- *Undergoes Claisen condensation*, a carbon-carbon bond-forming reaction in which two esters react in the presence of a strong base to form a beta-keto ester.
- *Includes lactones*, which are cyclic esters formed by the intramolecular condensation of a hydroxyl group and a carboxyl group within a single molecule.

## connections

- [[carboxylic-acid]] — carboxylic acids are the structural precursors to esters, which can be regenerated by acid or base hydrolysis.
- [[alcohol]] — alcohols act as the nucleophiles that attack carboxylic acids or their derivatives to form esters.
- [[sodium]] — sodium hydroxide is the standard base used to hydrolyze fatty acid esters in saponification.
