# Spectroscopy

## summary

Spectroscopy is the study of the interaction between electromagnetic radiation and matter. By measuring the absorption, emission, or scattering of light across different wavelengths, chemists can identify the structure, composition, and physical properties of chemical compounds. It encompasses a wide range of methods, from simple colorimetry to complex multi-dimensional resonance techniques.

## you gotta know

- *Governed by the Beer-Lambert law* for liquid solutions, which states that absorbance is directly proportional to the concentration of the analyte, the path length of the sample cell, and the molar attenuation coefficient.
- *Uses nuclear magnetic resonance* (NMR) spectroscopy to map the carbon-hydrogen framework of organic compounds, exploiting the spin states of nuclei in a strong magnetic field.
- *Employs infrared* (IR) spectroscopy to identify functional groups, measuring the absorption of infrared light as it excites specific molecular bonds to stretch, bend, or vibrate.
- *Utilizes ultraviolet-visible* (UV-Vis) spectroscopy to observe electronic transitions of valence electrons, commonly used to study conjugated carbon systems and transition metal complexes.
- *Couples with chromatography* in hyphenated techniques like Gas Chromatography-Mass Spectrometry (GC-MS), although mass spectrometry itself measures mass-to-charge ratios of ionized fragments rather than light absorption.

## connections

- [[helium]] — first discovered in 1868 through solar emission spectroscopy during a solar eclipse before it was ever isolated on Earth.
- [[sodium]] — emission spectroscopy of sodium vapor produces the characteristic yellow doublet D-lines.
- [[carbon]] — carbon-13 NMR spectroscopy is a fundamental tool for determining the skeletal structures of organic carbon molecules.
