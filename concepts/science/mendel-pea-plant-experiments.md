# Mendel's Pea-Plant Experiments

## summary

*Gregor Mendel*, an Augustinian friar and botanist, conducted systematic breeding experiments on pea plants between 1856 and 1863, uncovering the fundamental laws of *heredity*. By crossing plants with contrasting traits and counting offspring across generations, he established that *inheritance* follows predictable patterns — the birth of modern *genetics*.

## you gotta know

- *Mendel* chose the garden pea (*Pisum sativum*) for its ease of breeding, many visible traits (seed color, plant height, pod shape), and ability to self-pollinate or be cross-pollinated under controlled conditions.
- He tracked single traits (monohybrid crosses: tall vs. short, green vs. yellow seeds) through two generations and found the offspring ratios matched 3:1 (*dominant* to *recessive*), not a blend.
- His *law of segregation* states that each parent contributes one of two *alleles* (trait variants) to each offspring; paired alleles separate during reproduction.
- His *law of independent assortment* (from dihybrid crosses, tracking two traits) showed that different traits segregate *independently* — a founding principle now explained by chromosomal mechanics.
- The experiments were quantitative and statistical; *Mendel* counted thousands of plants and computed ratios, pioneering the use of mathematics in biology.
- His work went unrecognized during his lifetime and was rediscovered independently by three biologists in 1900, launching modern *genetics*.
