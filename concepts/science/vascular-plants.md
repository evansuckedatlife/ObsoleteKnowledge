# Vascular plants

## summary

**Vascular plants** are plants that possess specialized conducting tissues — *xylem* and *phloem* — enabling them to transport water, minerals, and organic compounds efficiently. This innovation allows vascular plants to grow tall, colonize diverse terrestrial habitats, and dominate ecosystems; the group includes ferns, gymnosperms, and angiosperms, and comprises the vast majority of living plant species.

## you gotta know

- Possess two key *vascular tissues*: *xylem* (water and mineral transport) and *phloem* (organic compound transport).
- The evolution of xylem and phloem allowed plants to grow tall, with roots in soil and shoots reaching upward toward sunlight, unbounded by diffusion limits.
- Include three major groups: *Pterophytes* (ferns and allies; reproduce by spores), *Gymnosperms* (conifers, cycads; seeds naked), and *Angiosperms* (flowering plants; seeds in fruits).
- Have true roots (modified for absorption) and true stems (modified for support and conduction), as opposed to *non-vascular plants* with rhizoids and no true roots.
- Include both *herbaceous* plants (soft, short-lived stems) and *woody* plants (hard, long-lived stems with secondary growth).
- Evolving vascular tissue also enabled the development of *lignin*, a polymer that reinforces cell walls and provides mechanical strength.

## connections

- [[non-vascular-plants]] — bryophytes lack vascular tissue and remain small and moisture-dependent.
- [[xylem]] — the water and mineral conducting tissue that defines vascular plants.
- [[phloem]] — the organic compound conducting tissue that complements xylem.
- [[pterophytes]] — the earliest vascular plants to evolve and colonize land.
- [[gymnosperms]] — vascular plants that produce naked seeds.
- [[angiosperms]] — vascular plants that produce flowers and fruits.
