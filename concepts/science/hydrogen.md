# Hydrogen

## summary

*Hydrogen* (atomic number 1, symbol *H*) is the lightest and most abundant chemical element in the universe, comprising roughly 75% of all ordinary matter by mass. At room temperature on Earth, hydrogen exists as a diatomic gas (*H₂*) and is the fundamental building block of water, organic molecules, and fossil fuels; it is central to chemistry, astrophysics, and emerging clean-energy technologies.

## you gotta know

- Hydrogen has one proton and one electron; its *isotopes*—deuterium (one neutron) and tritium (two neutrons)—are the only ones with special names.
- Hydrogen forms single covalent bonds with nearly all elements; its behaviour ranges from hydridic (donation-like, in metal hydrides) to cationic (losing the electron).
- *Hydrogen bonding* between hydrogen and electronegative atoms (O, N, F) creates weak but directional interactions essential to water's structure, protein folding, and DNA base-pairing.
- In *photosynthesis*, hydrogen ions (protons) are liberated from water during photolysis and accumulate in the thylakoid lumen, generating an electrochemical gradient that powers ATP synthesis.
- Hydrogen combustion yields water and vast energy (ΔH ≈ −286 kJ/mol for H₂ + ½O₂ → H₂O); it is the fuel of choice for space rockets and a candidate for carbon-free energy.
- Hydrogen is the primary constituent of the Sun and stars, where it undergoes *nuclear fusion* under extreme pressure and temperature to produce helium and the energy that powers stellar luminosity.
- Industrial *Haber-Bosch synthesis* (N₂ + 3H₂ → 2NH₃) fixes atmospheric nitrogen using hydrogen, enabling large-scale fertiliser production and modern agriculture.
