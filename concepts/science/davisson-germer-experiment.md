# Davisson–Germer Experiment

## summary

In 1927, *Clinton Davisson* and *Lester Germer* fired a beam of electrons at a nickel crystal and observed a diffraction pattern characteristic of *waves*, not particles. This landmark result provided experimental proof of *Louis de Broglie's* prediction that matter possesses wave-like properties — the converse of light's particle nature.

## you gotta know

- *Davisson* and *Germer* initially intended to study how electrons scatter off crystal surfaces; an accidental heating episode changed the nickel's crystalline structure and revealed an unexpected result.
- Electrons scattered at certain angles with maxima and minima — the distinctive *diffraction* pattern of waves passing through a *crystal lattice* with spacing comparable to their wavelength.
- Using *Bragg's law* (for X-ray diffraction), they calculated the *de Broglie wavelength* from the diffraction pattern and found it matched *de Broglie's* prediction: λ = h / p (Planck's constant divided by momentum).
- The wavelike behaviour required electron energies of only 50–150 volts — low enough to make the experiment practical, yet the wavelengths (~10^−10 m) were comparable to atomic spacings.
- The result vindicated *de Broglie's* 1924 hypothesis that all matter carries a *wave* character, with wavelength inversely proportional to momentum.
- *George Thomson* (son of *J.J. Thomson*, who discovered the electron as a particle) simultaneously confirmed electron diffraction independently, solidifying the effect's reality.
