# Western Blotting

## summary

**Western blotting** is a method for detecting, identifying, and quantifying specific proteins in a sample by separating them by molecular weight (via SDS-PAGE), transferring them to a membrane, and probing with antibodies. It measures protein levels, post-translational modifications, and confirms gene expression at the protein level.

## you gotta know

- *SDS-PAGE separation* — proteins are denatured, coated with negatively charged SDS detergent (which masks their native charge), and run on polyacrylamide gel where they separate by size alone.
- *Transfer to membrane* — denatured proteins are electroblotted (electric field) onto a nitrocellulose or PVDF membrane, where they adhere and remain accessible.
- *Antibody detection* — a primary antibody (specific to the protein of interest) binds the target; a secondary antibody (raised against the primary, coupled to an enzyme or fluorophore) enables visualization.
- *Chemiluminescent detection* — enzyme-linked secondaries (e.g., HRP) catalyze a reaction that emits light, captured by film or digital camera; intensity correlates with protein abundance.
- *Band position* = molecular weight — the membrane is marked with a protein ladder so target bands are sized.
- Used in clinical diagnostics (HIV, prion disease), cancer research (oncoproteins), and validating genetic or pharmacological manipulations.
