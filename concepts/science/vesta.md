# Vesta

## summary

**Vesta** is the second-most-massive body in the asteroid belt and a prototype of the *differentiated asteroid* — a small planetary body with an iron core, rocky mantle, and basaltic crust. Named for the Roman goddess of the hearth, it is one of the few asteroids visible to the naked eye under dark skies. The *Dawn* spacecraft's study of Vesta revealed it to be geologically complex, bearing the scars of a massive ancient impact and evidence of volcanic activity dating to the early solar system.

## you gotta know

- *Discovered* on 29 March 1807 by Heinrich Olbers; named for Vesta, goddess of the hearth and home, one of the few naked-eye asteroids.
- *Second-largest asteroid:* diameter ~525 km; only Ceres is more massive in the belt, but Vesta is far denser and internally differentiated (core–mantle–crust structure).
- *Basaltic surface:* composed of *howardite* rock (basalt) and other igneous materials; spectral reflectance matches *eucrite* meteorites found on Earth, establishing Vesta as a parent body for meteorite families.
- *Rheasilvia basin:* a massive south-pole impact crater ~500 km across, excavated ~1 billion years ago; the collision ejected thousands of fragments into heliocentric orbits (the *Vesta family*).
- *Internal differentiation:* seismic data and density models indicate a metallic core, indicating Vesta melted and separated into layers early in solar-system history (within ~5 million years of formation).
- *Bright and ancient:* its high albedo (reflectivity) makes it visible without optical aid; ancient heating (from short-lived isotopes like Al-26) powered the volcanism and differentiation.

## connections

- [[ceres]] — together, Vesta and Ceres dominate the asteroid belt's mass; they represent opposite extremes (differentiated rock vs. icy dwarf).
- [[asteroid-belt]] — Vesta is the signature example of a main-belt asteroid and the source of common meteorite types in museum collections.
- [[basaltic]] — Vesta's crust is the canonical example of basaltic asteroid composition.
- [[giant-impact]] — the Rheasilvia event exemplifies bombardment in the early solar system and its role in planetary formation.
