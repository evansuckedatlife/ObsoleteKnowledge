# Cotyledon

## summary

A *cotyledon* is the embryonic leaf (or seed leaf) within a seed that serves as the primary nutrient-absorbing and, after germination, nutrient-synthesising organ of the seedling. Flowering plants are classified as monocots (one cotyledon) or dicots (two cotyledons), and cotyledons play a crucial role in the seedling's establishment before true leaves develop.

## you gotta know

- *Monocots* have one cotyledon; *dicots* have two; cotyledon number is a fundamental taxonomic dividing line among angiosperms.
- In *dicots*, cotyledons often absorb and store the endosperm during seed development, becoming fleshy and nutrient-rich (like the halves of a bean or peanut); in *monocots*, the single cotyledon (called a *scutellum*) remains thin and absorbs nutrients from the endosperm during germination.
- Upon germination, the cotyledon(s) emerge above the soil; they are often morphologically distinct from true leaves and may serve double duty as both photosynthetic tissue and nutrient translocators to the developing shoot.
- Cotyledons are typically simpler in structure than true leaves, lacking the complexity of venation and specialised tissues that develop later.
- *Epigeal germination* exposes cotyledons above ground (bean, sunflower); *hypogeal germination* keeps cotyledons underground and relies on their reserves to feed the emerging shoot (pea, corn).
- The cotyledon(s) and the primary root (radical) are the first structures to emerge during germination.
