# Photoelectric Effect

## summary

The *photoelectric effect* is the emission of electrons from a material when light shines on it. Discovered in the 1880s by Heinrich Hertz, it initially seemed to support the classical wave theory of light—more intense light should eject more electrons. But Albert Einstein's 1905 explanation showed the phenomenon actually reveals the quantum nature of light: electrons are ejected only if the light frequency exceeds a threshold, independent of intensity. Einstein's insight that light consists of discrete energy quanta (photons) with energy *E* = *hν* won him the 1921 Nobel Prize and became foundational to quantum mechanics.

## you gotta know

- The *stopping potential* (or stopping voltage): when light ejects electrons from a metal, a retarding electric field will stop even the fastest electrons. The kinetic energy of ejected electrons depends on light frequency, not intensity.
- *Einstein's photoelectric equation*: *KE*<sub>max</sub> = *hν* − *W*, where *KE*<sub>max</sub> is the maximum kinetic energy of ejected electrons, *h* is Planck's constant, ν is the light frequency, and *W* is the *work function* (minimum energy to remove an electron from the material).
- *Threshold frequency*: below a critical frequency ν<sub>0</sub> (determined by work function *W* = *hν*<sub>0</sub>), no electrons are ejected, no matter how bright the light. Above it, emission is immediate (within picoseconds).
- Intensity affects the *number* of ejected electrons, not their kinetic energy: doubling light intensity doubles the electron count but leaves maximum electron speed unchanged.
- Different metals have different work functions: alkali metals (low work function) emit electrons in visible light, while most metals require ultraviolet. Platinum has the highest work function among metals.
- Modern applications: photomultiplier tubes amplify faint light signals; image sensors in cameras exploit the reverse process (generating current from incident photons); solar cells rely on the photoelectric effect to convert sunlight into electrical current.
