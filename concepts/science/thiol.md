# Thiol

## summary

A thiol (historically referred to as a mercaptan) is an organosulfur compound containing a sulfhydryl functional group (-SH) bonded to a carbon atom. Structurally analogous to alcohols with sulfur replacing oxygen, thiols are notorious for their extremely potent and offensive odors, which resemble rotten eggs, garlic, onions, or skunk spray.

## you gotta know

- *Characterized by the sulfhydryl group* (-SH), where the low electronegativity of sulfur results in weak hydrogen bonding, giving thiols lower boiling points and lower water solubility than analogous alcohols.
- *Forms covalent disulfide bonds* (disulfide bridges) upon mild oxidation, a linkage (R-S-S-R) that plays a critical role in stabilizing the folded tertiary structures of proteins.
- *Added to natural gas as an odorant* for safety, using volatile thiols like tert-butyl mercaptan to give odorless natural gas a recognizable, pungent smell to alert users to leaks.
- *Acts as a powerful nucleophile*, readily displacing leaving groups in substitution reactions and binding with high affinity to heavy metal cations.
- *Derived the name mercaptan* from the Latin *mercurium captans* ("mercury capturing") due to the rapid, stable complexes they form with mercury and other heavy metals.

## connections

- [[alcohol]] — thiols are the sulfur analogues of alcohols, but are significantly more acidic due to the larger size of the sulfur atom and weaker S-H bond.
- [[mercury]] — mercaptans bind mercury ions strongly, and dimercaprol (British Anti-Lewisite) is a dithiol drug used to treat mercury and lead poisoning.
- [[amino-acids]] — the amino acid cysteine contains a thiol group, which is responsible for forming disulfide bonds that stabilize protein folds.
