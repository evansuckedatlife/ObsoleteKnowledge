## summary

Mitochondria are the membrane-bound organelles responsible for cellular respiration, converting nutrients (glucose, fatty acids) into usable energy in the form of ATP. Present in nearly all eukaryotic cells, mitochondria contain their own DNA and ribosomes, reflecting their evolutionary origin as free-living bacteria. They are often called the "powerhouse of the cell" due to their essential role in energy production.

## you gotta know

- **Double-membrane structure**: outer membrane (permeable) and inner membrane (highly folded into *cristae*) that create compartments
- **Cristae**: folds of inner membrane that maximize surface area for electron transport chain
- **Matrix**: innermost compartment where Krebs cycle occurs
- **Aerobic respiration**: uses oxygen to extract maximum energy from glucose (approximately 30–32 ATP per glucose)
- **Own genetic material**: mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA) encodes some respiratory proteins; maternal inheritance in most organisms
- **Own ribosomes**: 70S ribosomes similar to prokaryotic ribosomes support mitochondrial protein synthesis
- **Endosymbiotic origin**: evolved from engulfed alpha-proteobacteria billions of years ago

## context

The mitochondrial endosymbiotic event fundamentally transformed eukaryotic life by enabling efficient aerobic respiration. This partnership allowed eukaryotes to extract far more energy from nutrients than their prokaryotic ancestors, fueling the evolution of larger, more metabolically active cells. Mitochondrial dysfunction underlies numerous human diseases—diabetes, Parkinson's, and cardiac disease—highlighting their clinical significance. The existence of mitochondrial DNA also provides evolutionary evidence of their bacterial ancestry.
