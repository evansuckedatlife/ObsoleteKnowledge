# Miller–Urey Experiment

## summary

In 1952, *Stanley Miller* and *Harold Urey* simulated the *early Earth's atmosphere* and ocean in a closed flask, sparked it with electrical discharges (mimicking lightning), and detected *amino acids* — the building blocks of *proteins* — forming spontaneously. The result suggested that the chemistry of life could have emerged from simple inorganic precursors, supporting *abiogenesis*.

## you gotta know

- *Miller* and *Urey* filled a glass apparatus with methane, ammonia, water vapour, and hydrogen — hypothesised components of Earth's early atmosphere — and subjected the mixture to electric sparks and heating.
- After a week, the solution inside the flask had turned brown; analysis revealed dozens of *amino acids* — organic compounds essential to biology — had assembled from the inorganic gas mixture.
- They identified glycine, alanine, and other amino acids without any biological catalyst, proving that *organic compounds* could form from non-living chemistry under plausible prebiotic conditions.
- Later re-analysis of preserved samples with modern mass spectrometry revealed even more amino acids than *Miller* and *Urey* had detected, amplifying the finding.
- The experiment became iconic evidence that the *origin of life* need not invoke divine creation; chemical evolution could bootstrap complexity.
- Modern views revise the composition of Earth's early atmosphere (more CO₂, less ammonia) but show that variant conditions still yield *amino acids*, supporting the result's spirit.
