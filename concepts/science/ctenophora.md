# Ctenophora

## summary

A phylum of marine invertebrates commonly known as comb jellies, **Ctenophora** are characterized by rows of cilia that they use for swimming. They are the largest animals to move using cilia and are famous for their brilliant bioluminescence. Unlike jellyfish, they capture prey using sticky cells rather than stinging cells.

## you gotta know

- *Propel themselves using eight rows of fused cilia* (called comb rows or ctenes), making them the largest organisms to use cilia for locomotion.
- *Capture prey using sticky cells called colloblasts*, which secrete a glue-like substance to snare prey rather than stinging them like Cnidarian nematocysts.
- *Exhibit widespread bioluminescence*, producing green or blue light within specialized internal canals using photoproteins.
- *Possess an apical organ* at their aboral pole (opposite the mouth), containing a calcium carbonate statolith that acts as a gravity sensor for balance.
- *Digest food through a complete gut* containing anal pores, representing a key evolutionary distinction from the blind-gut system of Cnidarians.

## connections

- [[cnidaria]] — with which comb jellies are often confused, though ctenophores lack stinging nematocysts and possess a complete digestive tract.
- [[porifera]] — an older, tissue-less phylum with which ctenophores share basic marine ancestry.
