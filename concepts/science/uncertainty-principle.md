# Uncertainty principle

## summary

The *Heisenberg uncertainty principle* states that certain pairs of physical observables—position and momentum, energy and time—cannot simultaneously have arbitrarily small uncertainties. Mathematically, Δx·Δp ≥ ℏ/2, where ℏ = h/2π and Δ denotes standard deviation. This is not a limitation of measurement apparatus but a fundamental constraint on the nature of quantum reality.

## you gotta know

- Position-momentum uncertainty: Δx·Δp ≥ ℏ/2 ≈ 5.3 × 10⁻³⁵ J·s
- Energy-time uncertainty: ΔE·Δt ≥ ℏ/2
- *Complementary observables* (position-momentum, energy-time) obey uncertainty relations
- The [[commutator]] of two operators determines their uncertainty relation: [Â, B̂] ≠ 0 implies uncertainty
- If a state has definite position (small Δx), its momentum is completely uncertain (large Δp), and vice versa
- Uncertainty is not due to measurement disturbance alone; it reflects the structure of [[wavefunction]]
- Ground states and stable configurations always obey uncertainty: zero-point energy E > 0 for confined particles

## context

[[werner-heisenberg]] derived this principle from the commutation relations of quantum operators, showing it is inevitable in any theory where position and momentum are represented by non-commuting observables. The principle explains why electrons don't collapse into nuclei: confining an electron to a tiny nuclear volume gives it enormous kinetic energy via momentum uncertainty, making such a state unstable. It also sets the scale for quantum processes: at macroscopic scales (Δx ~ cm), Δp ~ 10⁻³⁴ m/s is negligible, so quantum effects vanish.
