# Catalase

## summary

Catalase is an antioxidant enzyme found in nearly all aerobic organisms that catalyzes the rapid decomposition of hydrogen peroxide (H₂O₂) into water and oxygen. It protects cells from oxidative damage by eliminating a toxic byproduct of aerobic metabolism with remarkable speed—one of the fastest enzymes known.

## you gotta know

- Catalyzes: 2 H₂O₂ → 2 H₂O + O₂
- Substrate: hydrogen peroxide, a toxic free radical generated in peroxisomes and mitochondria
- Turnover rate: ~1 million molecules per second per enzyme molecule—among the highest of any enzyme
- Cofactor: iron-containing heme group (iron protoporphyrin IX) at the active site
- Location: primarily in peroxisomes (organelles named for H₂O₂ production); also in mitochondria and cytoplasm
- Structure: tetrameric protein; four subunits cooperatively bind and process substrate
- Function: prevents H₂O₂ accumulation, which otherwise damages proteins, lipids, and DNA via hydroxyl radical formation

## context

Aerobic respiration inevitably generates hydrogen peroxide as electrons leak from the electron transport chain. While peroxide is chemically reactive—useful as a signaling molecule at low concentrations—high levels are poisonous. Catalase evolved as cells' primary H₂O₂ scavenger, enabling the metabolic advantages of aerobic life without catastrophic oxidative stress. Its extraordinary catalytic rate reflects the cell's urgent need to eliminate peroxide before it causes damage; catalase operates near the theoretical limit of enzyme efficiency. The enzyme's presence is essentially ubiquitous in aerobic life, underscoring its critical importance.
