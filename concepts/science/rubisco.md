## summary

RuBisCO (ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate carboxylase/oxygenase) is the most abundant enzyme on Earth and catalyzes the first committed step of the *Calvin cycle*, fixing CO₂ into organic molecules. It is a bifunctional enzyme with both carboxylase and oxygenase activity, the latter initiating *photorespiration*, a competing pathway that reduces photosynthetic efficiency.

## you gotta know

- Catalyzes: carboxylation of ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate (RuBP) with CO₂, producing two molecules of 3-phosphoglycerate (3-PG)
- Location: stroma of chloroplasts
- Abundance: comprises ~40–50% of soluble protein in chloroplasts; the most prevalent protein in most plant leaves
- Substrate: RuBP (5-carbon sugar) and CO₂; also competes for O₂ (oxygenase activity)
- Product: 3-PG enters glycolysis and gluconeogenesis pathways; feeds the Calvin cycle to produce glucose
- Photorespiration: RuBisCO oxygenase activity consumes O₂ and generates 2-phosphoglycolate, a metabolic dead-end that wastes ATP and NADPH
- Kinetics: relatively slow enzyme (~1 CO₂/second per enzyme molecule); huge amounts needed for sufficient flux
- Regulation: activated by light-dependent mechanisms; RuBP accumulation in darkness inactivates the enzyme

## context

RuBisCO is the gateway to the biosphere—nearly all carbon fixation for life on Earth passes through this single enzyme. Its abundance reflects not catalytic perfection but biological necessity; despite being "inefficient" by enzyme standards, cells must compensate with enormous quantities. The enzyme's oxygenase activity, which diverts fixed carbon away from productive pathways, represents an evolutionary compromise: RuBisCO cannot discriminate well between CO₂ and O₂ at the catalytic site. This limitation persists because improving specificity would require redesign that natural selection has not favored; evolving RuBisCO is deeply constrained. Recent synthetic biology efforts aim to engineer faster or more specific variants to boost crop yields.
