# Antibody

## summary

An *antibody* (also *immunoglobulin* or *Ig*) is a Y-shaped protein produced by B cells in response to infection or vaccination that binds specifically to foreign antigens and neutralises or marks them for destruction. Antibodies are central to humoral immunity and are among the most abundant proteins in mammalian blood.

## you gotta know

- Antibodies are *glycoproteins* composed of four *polypeptide chains*: two *heavy chains* and two *light chains*, cross-linked by *disulphide bonds*.
- The Y-shaped structure features two identical *antigen-binding sites* (the variable regions at the tip of each arm) and a *constant region* (the stem) that determines the antibody's class and effector function.
- Each antibody binds a *specific antigen* with high affinity, determined by the shape and chemical complementarity of its variable regions.
- Five major *isotypes* exist: *IgG* (most abundant in blood, crosses the placenta), *IgM* (first responder to new antigens), *IgA* (secreted in mucosal fluids), *IgE* (involved in allergies and parasitic infection), and *IgD* (regulatory role on B cell surfaces).
- Antibodies *neutralise* toxins, *opsonise* pathogens for phagocytosis, *agglutinate* pathogens into clumps, and *activate complement*—a cascade of proteins that punctures microbial membranes.
- *Affinity maturation* occurs when B cells are repeatedly exposed to an antigen; *somatic hypermutation* and *selection* generate higher-affinity variants over weeks to months.
- *Monoclonal antibodies* (all identical, derived from a single B cell) are now produced recombinantly for therapeutic use in cancer, autoimmune disease, and infectious disease.
