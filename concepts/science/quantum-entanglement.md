# Quantum entanglement

## summary

Quantum entanglement occurs when two or more particles share a [[wavefunction]] such that their quantum states are correlated in a way that cannot be described by treating each particle independently. Measuring one entangled particle instantaneously determines the outcome of measuring its partner, even if they are far apart. The correlation is not due to hidden variables set at creation; experiments violate [[bell-inequality]] constraints, confirming non-local correlations.

## you gotta know

- An entangled two-particle state cannot be written as a product |ψ₁⟩ ⊗ |ψ₂⟩; it is *non-separable*
- Measuring an observable on particle 1 affects the [[superposition]] available for particle 2
- The singlet state |ψ⟩ = (|↑↓⟩ − |↓↑⟩)/√2 is maximally entangled; opposite spins always measured
- Entanglement persists over arbitrary distances with no speed-of-light violation (no FTL communication)
- [[bell-inequality]] violations prove that local hidden-variable theories cannot explain quantum correlations
- Entanglement is a resource: quantum computing exploits it for speedup; quantum cryptography uses it for security
- Decoherence (interaction with environment) destroys entanglement

## context

Entanglement troubled [[albert-einstein]] and colleagues, who sought hidden variables to restore locality and determinism. Yet Bell's theorem (1964) and experiments (Aspect et al., 1980s onward) confirmed that entanglement is a genuine feature of quantum reality. The correlations are stronger than any classical system can produce, yet causality is preserved because measurement outcomes are random—no usable information travels faster than light. Entanglement is now harnessed in quantum computing, quantum key distribution, and quantum teleportation, making it a cornerstone of quantum information science.
