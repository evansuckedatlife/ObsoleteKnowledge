# Pauling scale

## summary

The **Pauling scale** quantifies the *electronegativity* of chemical elements—their tendency to attract electrons in a chemical bond. Developed by *Linus Pauling* in 1932, the scale ranges from approximately 0.7 (least electronegative) to 4.0 (most electronegative, fluorine). Electronegativity differences between bonded atoms predict bond polarity and are fundamental to understanding chemistry.

## you gotta know

- Named after *Linus Pauling*, American chemist and Nobel laureate who introduced the concept to explain molecular bonding and reactivity patterns.
- *Fluorine* has the highest electronegativity (4.0); *cesium* and *francium* have the lowest (~0.7). Electronegativity generally increases from left to right across a period and from bottom to top within a group in the periodic table.
- The scale is *dimensionless* and conventionally defined relative to hydrogen (2.20 on the Pauling scale).
- Electronegativity differences determine bond character: if the difference is < 0.4, the bond is essentially *nonpolar*; differences of 0.4–2.0 indicate *polar covalent* bonds; differences > 2.0 suggest *ionic* character.
- A nonpolar bond (e.g., C–C or H–H) occurs between atoms of similar electronegativity. A polar bond (e.g., C–O) occurs when electronegativities differ significantly.
- The scale predicts molecular geometry, solubility, reactivity, and acid-base properties—crucial for predicting chemistry.
- Later electronegativity scales (e.g., Allred-Rochow) were developed, but the Pauling scale remains the most widely taught and referenced.
