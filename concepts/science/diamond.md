# Diamond

## summary

**Diamond** is a metastable allotrope of carbon, crystallised under extreme pressure and temperature deep within the Earth's mantle. It is the hardest naturally occurring mineral and the most prized gemstone, valued for its transparency, refractive index, and durability. Diamonds are brought to the surface via rare kimberlite and lamproite pipes during explosive volcanic eruptions.

## you gotta know

- Pure *carbon* (C) arranged in an *isometric crystal lattice*, each atom bonded to four others in an extremely strong three-dimensional network.
- Rated 10 on the *Mohs hardness scale* — the hardest natural mineral, abrasive enough to cut all others; no natural substance can scratch it except another diamond.
- Forms only at pressures exceeding 5 GPa and temperatures above 1200°C, conditions found in the *mantle* 150+ km below the surface.
- *Refractive index* is exceptionally high (~2.42), giving diamonds their characteristic brilliance and "fire" (spectral dispersion).
- Metastable at Earth's surface — would revert to graphite given enough time, but the activation energy is so high that diamonds persist indefinitely.
- Brought to the surface via *kimberlite pipes*, narrow vertical magma channels that tap the upper mantle and erupt with violent force.
- Industrial diamonds are used as abrasives, cutting tools, and heat sinks; gem-grade diamonds command high prices based on the "Four Cs" (carat, colour, clarity, cut).

## connections

- [[carbon]] — diamond is an allotrope of carbon, as is graphite, with vastly different properties due to crystal structure.
- [[corundum]] — diamond and corundum compete as the hardest common minerals; diamond wins, but corundum is far more abundant.
- [[mohs-scale]] — diamond defines the top end (hardness 10) of the Mohs hardness scale.
- [[igneous-rock]] — kimberlite is an ultramafic igneous rock that transports diamonds from the mantle.
- [[gemstone]] — diamonds are the most valued gemstones by economic value, though rarity varies by colour and clarity.
