## summary

Centrioles are cylindrical, membrane-free structures composed of nine triplets of microtubules arranged in a 9-fold symmetric pattern. Typically present in pairs (forming a *centrosome* or *MTOC—microtubule organizing center*), centrioles anchor and nucleate the formation of microtubules that organize the cytoskeleton and spindle fibers during cell division. They are found in most animal cells but absent in plant cells and many other organisms.

## you gotta know

- **Nine-fold symmetry**: nine triplet microtubules (each triplet = 3 fused microtubules) arranged in a circle
- **Centrosome (MTOC)**: pair of centrioles surrounded by pericentriolar material; nucleates microtubule growth
- **Basal bodies**: centrioles modified into basal bodies serve as anchors for cilia and flagella
- **Microtubule nucleation**: gamma-tubulin ring complexes in pericentriolar material nucleate microtubule polymerization
- **Cell division**: centrosome duplicates in S phase; duplicated centrosomes move to opposite poles during mitosis
- **Inheritance**: centriole duplication occurs via procentriole formation; template-based assembly ensures transmission
- **Absence in plants**: plant cells organize microtubules without centrioles using alternative MTOC structures

## context

Centrioles represent specialized microtubule organizing centers that help animals organize their cytoskeletons and coordinate cell division with high fidelity. Their elegant symmetry—the 9+0 pattern, distinct from the 9+2 of cilia—suggests evolutionary divergence from a common ancestor. Centrioles duplicate once per cell cycle and segregate to daughter cells, ensuring each receives an MTOC. Their mechanism of template-guided duplication remains incompletely understood and is an active area of research. Mutations or amplification of centrosomes contribute to chromosomal instability in cancer cells.
