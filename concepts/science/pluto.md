# Pluto

## summary

**Pluto** is a dwarf planet and the prototype of a new class of icy solar-system bodies orbiting beyond Neptune. Discovered in 1930 as the ninth planet, it was reclassified in 2006 when the International Astronomical Union redefined "planet" to require clearing its orbital zone of debris — a criterion Pluto fails. Its five known moons and geologically active surface make it a rich target for planetary science.

## you gotta know

- *Discovered by Clyde Tombaugh* at Lowell Observatory on 18 February 1930; the search was motivated by perturbations in Neptune's orbit that turned out to be spurious.
- *Reclassified from planet to dwarf planet* in 2006 by the IAU, sparking cultural outrage and memes about planetary demotion that persist today.
- *Five moons:* **Charon** (so massive it forms a binary system), plus Styx, Nix, Kerberos, and Hydra, discovered between 1978 and 2012.
- *Diameter ~2,377 km* — smaller than Earth's Moon; composed of rock and water ice with a thin nitrogen/methane atmosphere.
- *Heart-shaped region* of light-colored nitrogen ice visible in New Horizons imagery (2015), suggesting cryovolcanism and ongoing geological activity despite its distance.
- *Orbital eccentricity* so high that it passes inside Neptune's orbit for 20 years at a time; its last perihelion was 1989.
- *Three times farther from the Sun* than Neptune; one orbit takes 248 Earth years.

## connections

- [[kuiper-belt]] — Pluto is the prototype of *plutinos*, icy bodies in a stable 2:3 resonance with Neptune's orbit.
- [[eris]] — a rival dwarf planet that is actually slightly more massive; Eris's discovery in 2005 triggered the IAU reclassification.
- [[dwarf-planet]] — the defining member of the dwarf-planet category.
- [[neptune]] — its orbital history is tied to Neptune's gravitational influence via the *Laplace resonance*.
- [[charon]] — Pluto's largest moon, so massive that the two orbit a common center of mass.
