# Molecular Cloning

## summary

**Molecular cloning** is the process of isolating a DNA fragment of interest, inserting it into a vector (typically a plasmid), and introducing the recombinant molecule into host cells for propagation or expression. It is the foundational technique for creating transgenic organisms, producing recombinant proteins, and constructing genetic libraries.

## you gotta know

- *Vector selection* — plasmids are small, circular DNA molecules (usually derived from bacteria) that replicate independently in cells; they carry an origin of replication, selectable markers (e.g., antibiotic resistance), and cloning sites.
- *Restriction digestion* — the target DNA and vector are cut with restriction enzymes at compatible sites, generating sticky ends (single-stranded overhangs) that can base-pair.
- *Ligation* — *DNA ligase* enzyme seals the phosphodiester backbone between the insert and vector, creating a circular recombinant plasmid.
- *Transformation* — competent cells (bacteria or yeast) are made permeable and exposed to the recombinant plasmid; a small percentage take it up and incorporate it into their chromosome or maintain it episomally.
- *Selection and screening* — transformed colonies are identified by selectable markers; the correct insert is confirmed by PCR, restriction digest, or sequencing.
- Classic method (since the 1970s); modern alternatives like Gibson assembly and TALEN/CRISPR offer seamless, scarless insertion.

## connections

- [[dna]] — the genetic material being cloned and manipulated.
- [[plasmid]] — the most common cloning vector; cheap, well-characterized, and easy to propagate.
- [[restriction-enzyme]] — cuts DNA at specific sites to generate compatible ends.
- [[dna-ligase]] — rejoins DNA strands after restriction cutting.
- [[pcr]] — often used to amplify the insert before cloning.
- [[transformation]] — the method for introducing the recombinant plasmid into cells.
