# Ordovician Period

## summary

The **Ordovician Period** (485–444 million years ago) saw an explosion of marine diversity—fishes emerged, corals and cephalopods flourished, and invertebrates colonised the seafloor. The period is named after the Ordovices, an ancient Welsh tribe. It ended abruptly with a mass extinction, possibly triggered by glaciation and ocean oxygen loss.

## you gotta know

- The *Ordovician Radiation* nearly tripled the number of marine families, diversifying the ocean into rich ecological zones.
- *Jawless fishes* (agnathans) appeared for the first time, pioneering vertebrate life in the oceans.
- *Cephalopods*—ancestors of modern squid and octopus—rose to prominence as top predators, with some reaching 10 metres long.
- *Brachiopods* and *trilobites* remained abundant; corals and sponges built the first reefs.
- The *Ordovician extinction* (~444 million years ago) was one of the Big Five mass extinctions, wiping out ~85% of marine species, likely due to *glaciation* and *ocean anoxia* (lack of oxygen).
- Land remained barren; all animal life was marine or in freshwater.
