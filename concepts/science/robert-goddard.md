# Robert Goddard

## summary

American physicist and engineer (1882–1945) who pioneered *liquid-fueled rocket propulsion* and laid the theoretical and practical foundations of modern rocketry. Though largely dismissed by the American scientific establishment during his lifetime, Goddard's patents and designs became the blueprint for postwar rocket development.

## you gotta know

- Launched the first *liquid-fueled rocket* (March 1926, Auburn, Massachusetts) — a modest 10-foot vehicle that rose 41 feet, but proved the concept viable when nearly all experts thought it impossible.
- Patented the *multi-stage rocket* concept (1914) and the idea of using a *nozzle to accelerate exhaust* — foundational to all modern rocket engines.
- Developed *gyroscopic stabilization* systems and *vanes in the exhaust stream* to steer rockets, solving the control problem that had stymied earlier attempts.
- His 1919 paper *A Method of Reaching Extreme Altitudes* was ridiculed by the press as unscientific; a New York Times editorial even doubted he understood Newton's third law (they retracted it in 1969, after Apollo 11).
- Built high-altitude experimental rockets in Roswell, New Mexico (1930–1941), reaching altitudes above 7,000 feet and speeds exceeding the speed of sound.
- The U.S. Army Signal Corps and later NASA based early ballistic missile and space programs on Goddard's patents; he held 214 rocket-related patents.

## connections

- [[konstantin-tsiolkovsky]] — Russian theorist working in parallel; Goddard proved Tsiolkovsky's equations could work in practice.
- [[wernher-von-braun]] — German engineer who studied Goddard's work and applied his principles to the V-2.
- [[v-2-rocket]] — the Nazi weapon that vindicated Goddard's vision when the U.S. finally invested heavily in rocketry.
