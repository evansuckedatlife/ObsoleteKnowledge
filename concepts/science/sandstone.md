## summary

**Sandstone** is a clastic sedimentary rock composed of sand-sized mineral grains (0.0625–2 mm) cemented together, most commonly quartz. It forms from the erosion and deposition of earlier rocks in fluvial, coastal, and desert environments. Sandstone is widespread, economically important as a building stone and reservoir rock for oil and water, and often preserves cross-bedding and ripple marks that record ancient environments.

## you gotta know

- *Clastic sedimentary rock* — formed from fragments (grains) of pre-existing rocks cemented together; contrast with chemical rocks like limestone.
- Composed predominantly of *quartz* grains, as quartz is the hardest and most chemically stable mineral and survives weathering while others break down.
- Sand-sized grains (0.0625–2 mm) distinguishing it from finer-grained mudstone/shale and coarser-grained conglomerate.
- Cemented together by silica (quartz), calcite, or iron-rich minerals; cementation type determines hardness and weathering resistance.
- Often red, tan, or buff-coloured; red coloration is due to iron oxide (hematite) coating the grains, common in desert sandstones.
- Contains accessory minerals like feldspar, mica, and heavy minerals (magnetite, garnet); these help identify sediment source and history.
- Major industrial uses: construction aggregate, dimension stone for buildings, and *reservoir rock* for oil and natural gas storage.

## connections

- [[quartz]] — quartz sand grains form the bulk of sandstone; quartz's stability and hardness are why it survives weathering.
- [[feldspar]] — feldspar grains may constitute 10–25% of sandstone; their presence indicates immature (recent) sediment.
- [[sedimentary-rock]] — sandstone is the prototypical clastic sedimentary rock, recording fluvial and coastal depositional environments.
- [[weathering]] — sandstone results from the weathering of granites and other rocks, with quartz being the resistant survivor.
- [[metamorphic-rock]] — under burial and heat, sandstone can metamorphose to *quartzite*, a much harder rock with interlocking quartz grains.
