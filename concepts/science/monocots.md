# Monocots

## summary

**Monocots** are *flowering plants* with a single *cotyledon* (seed leaf), comprising roughly 30% of angiosperm diversity. The group includes grasses, sedges, lilies, orchids, palms, and bananas — collectively some of the most economically important plants on Earth (wheat, rice, corn, sugar cane).

## you gotta know

- Single *cotyledon* in the embryo; dicots have two, which affects early seedling development and root/shoot architecture.
- Leaves typically have *parallel venation* (veins running side-by-side) rather than the *reticulate venation* (branching net) of dicots.
- Vascular bundles in the stem are *scattered* (arranged throughout the cross-section) rather than arranged in a ring; no clear distinction between cortex and pith.
- Fibrous root system (many thin roots from the base) rather than a *taproot*; exceptions include some monocot trees (palms).
- Flower parts typically in *multiples of three* (e.g. 3 sepals, 3 petals, 6 stamens) rather than multiples of four or five.
- Include grasses (Poaceae, the most economically vital plant family), orchids (most diverse angiosperm family), lilies, irises, and palms.

## connections

- [[dicots]] — the complementary major angiosperm division; monocots and dicots together comprise nearly all flowering plants.
- [[angiosperms]] — monocots are one of two major subclasses of angiosperms.
- [[cotyledon]] — the seed leaf that defines the monocot-dicot boundary.
- [[seed]] — monocot seeds contain a single cotyledon.
- [[pollination]] — monocots employ diverse pollination strategies (wind in grasses, insects in orchids).
