# Lysosome

## summary

Lysosomes are membrane-bound organelles filled with digestive enzymes (hydrolases) that break down cellular waste, damaged organelles, and ingested pathogens. Found primarily in animal cells, lysosomes function as the cell's "garbage disposal," maintaining cellular homeostasis by recycling materials and eliminating debris. They fuse with endocytic vesicles and autophagic vacuoles to digest their contents and return nutrients to the cell.

## you gotta know

- **Hydrolytic enzymes**: over 50 acid hydrolases that function optimally at pH 4.5–5.0
- **Acidic interior**: maintained by proton pumps in the lysosomal membrane
- **Autophagy pathway**: lysosomes fuse with autophagosomes to digest cellular components (organelles, proteins)
- **Phagocytosis**: lysosomes digest pathogens and large particles engulfed by the cell
- **Lysosomal biogenesis**: enzymes synthesized in rough ER, modified in Golgi, shipped to lysosomes via mannose-6-phosphate targeting
- **Membrane integrity**: lysosomal rupture releases enzymes and triggers apoptosis (programmed cell death)
- **Cellular recycling**: digestion products (amino acids, fatty acids, glucose) are returned to cytoplasm for reuse

## context

Lysosomes evolved as eukaryotic cells developed the capacity to regulate intracellular digestion separately from extracellular breakdown. Their acidic, enzyme-rich compartment enables degradation of virtually any biological macromolecule—a capability essential for cell survival, defense, and controlled remodeling. Lysosomal storage diseases (e.g., Gaucher disease, lysosomal acid lipase deficiency) result from enzyme defects, causing toxic accumulation of undigested material. Lysosomes also participate in cell death pathways, linking them to cancer, neurodegeneration, and aging.
