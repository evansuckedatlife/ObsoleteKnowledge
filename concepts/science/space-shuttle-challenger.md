# Space Shuttle Challenger

## summary

The Space Shuttle *Challenger* was destroyed 73 seconds after launch on January 28, 1986, killing all seven crew members aboard *STS-51-L*. The disaster was caused by an *O-ring failure* in the right solid rocket booster, allowing hot gases to erode structural supports and ultimately cause the external tank attachment to fail. The tragedy led to a two-year program shutdown and fundamental changes to shuttle safety protocols. A second shuttle, *Columbia*, was later lost on re-entry as *STS-107* in 2003.

## you gotta know

- *Challenger* exploded on *January 28, 1986*, 73 seconds after launch during mission *STS-51-L*, killing the *seven-member crew*.
- The accident was caused by an *O-ring failure* in the right *solid rocket booster*, which allowed hot gases to bypass a seal and erode critical structural supports.
- *Cold weather* at launch (36 degrees Fahrenheit) made the O-rings brittle; engineers had warned against launching in such cold, but the decision was made to proceed.
- The crew included *mission commander Dick Scobee*, *pilot Michael Smith*, and five specialists, including *Christa McAuliffe*, a teacher selected for the *Teacher in Space* program.
- The *Rogers Commission* investigated the disaster and found systemic failures in *NASA's* organizational culture, particularly the pressure to maintain launch schedules despite safety concerns.
- The *Space Shuttle* program was grounded for *32 months* for a comprehensive redesign and safety review.
- *Space Shuttle Columbia* was later lost on *February 1, 2003*, during re-entry as *STS-107*, when *foam insulation* struck the wing during launch, creating a fatal hole.
