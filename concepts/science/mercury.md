# Mercury

## summary

Mercury is a heavy, silvery transition metal that is the only metal that remains liquid at standard temperature and pressure. Known historically as quicksilver, it is highly toxic, bioaccumulating through aquatic food webs as methylmercury to cause neurological damage (Minamata disease). Due to its unique liquid state, high density, and electrical conductivity, it has been widely used in thermometers, barometers, electrical switches, and fluorescent lamps.

## you gotta know

- *Extracted chiefly from cinnabar*, a bright-red mineral of mercury(II) sulfide (HgS) that is roasted to release metallic mercury vapor.
- *Forms liquid or solid alloys called amalgams* with most metals like gold, silver, and sodium, but does not amalgamate with iron, allowing liquid mercury to be stored in iron flasks.
- *Served as the fluid for the first barometer*, invented by Evangelista Torricelli in 1643, leading to the pressure unit millimeters of mercury (mmHg) or Torr.
- *Led to the discovery of superconductivity* in 1911, when Heike Kamerlingh Onnes cooled mercury to 4.2 Kelvin and observed that its electrical resistance dropped to zero.
- *Forms the calomel electrode*, a reference electrode in electrochemistry composed of mercury and mercury(I) chloride (Hg2Cl2, calomel) in a potassium chloride solution.

## connections

- [[gold]] — readily dissolves in mercury to form amalgams, which are historically used in gold extraction.
- [[oxygen]] — heating mercuric oxide was the reaction Joseph Priestley used to discover oxygen gas.
- [[sodium]] — combines with mercury to form sodium amalgam, a widely used reducing agent.
