# Filtration

## summary

Filtration is a physical or mechanical laboratory operation used to separate solid particles from a fluid (liquid or gas) by passing the mixture through a porous medium. The solid residue is retained on the filter medium, while the purified fluid, known as the filtrate, passes through. It is a fundamental technique for isolation, purification, and waste treatment.

## you gotta know

- *Uses gravity filtration* for simple separations, where a mixture is poured through a fluted filter paper in a funnel, relying solely on gravity to separate the phases.
- *Employs vacuum filtration* (suction filtration) for rapid isolation of recrystallized solids, utilizing a Büchner or Hirsch funnel, a vacuum flask, and a water aspirator or pump.
- *Requires fritted glass funnels* (sintered glass) when handling highly reactive or corrosive solvents that would dissolve or react with standard paper filters.
- *Applies cold filtration* during recrystallization to remove insoluble impurities from a hot solution before crystallization of the target compound begins.
- *Utilizes membrane filters* with tiny, calibrated pore sizes (such as 0.22 micrometers) to sterilize heat-sensitive liquid media by physically trapping bacteria and other microbes.

## connections

- [[centrifugation]] — a peer separation technique that uses rapid spinning instead of a porous barrier to separate solids from liquids.
- [[water]] — municipal water treatment plants rely on sand and carbon filtration to remove particulate matter and impurities.
- [[recrystallization]] — filtration is used both to remove hot insoluble impurities and to isolate the final purified crystals.
