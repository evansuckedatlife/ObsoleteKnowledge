# Hermann Oberth

## summary

Austro-Hungarian physicist (1894–1989) who published the pioneering treatise *Die Rakete zu den Planetenräumen* (The Rocket into Planetary Space, 1923), synthesizing theoretical rocketry and astronautics. Though less famous than contemporaries, Oberth provided the intellectual bridge between Tsiolkovsky's equations and engineering practice, and mentored Wernher von Braun.

## you gotta know

- Published *Die Rakete zu den Planetenräumen* (1923), arguing rigorously that *liquid-fueled multi-stage rockets* could reach the moon and beyond — a feat deemed impossible by the scientific mainstream.
- Worked independently of Tsiolkovsky and Goddard; his book became the manifesto of German rocketry societies and drew young engineers (including von Braun) into spaceflight.
- Conceived of *rocket-powered spaceflight to Mars*, *space mirrors to focus solar energy on Earth*, and other megalomaniac schemes that were purely theoretical but scientifically reasoned.
- Struggled to secure funding and institutional support in Germany and later Romania; despite his brilliance, he remained a marginal figure in engineering circles until late in life.
- Emigrated to the United States after World War II (like von Braun) and continued publishing on space propulsion and extraterrestrial colonization into his 90s.
- His work was cited by von Braun and the German rocket societies as a key inspiration; Soviet engineers also read him and credited his influence.

## connections

- [[wernher-von-braun]] — student and protégé who built on Oberth's theoretical framework to create the V-2.
- [[konstantin-tsiolkovsky]] — Russian pioneer working in parallel on similar principles; Oberth's book synthesized and popularized these ideas.
- [[v-2-rocket]] — the V-2 program drew its aspirational vision from Oberth's writings on spaceflight.
