# Pipetting

## summary

Pipetting is a fundamental laboratory technique used to measure and transfer a precise volume of liquid. It utilizes a device called a pipette (or pipet) and is widely used across chemistry, biology, molecular biology, and clinical medicine to handle liquid reagents, prepare dilutions, and run assays.

## you gotta know

- *Uses volumetric pipettes* (also called bulb pipettes) to transfer a single, highly accurate volume of liquid, characterized by a large central bulb and a single etched calibration line.
- *Employs graduated pipettes* (such as Mohr or serological pipettes) to measure variable volumes, where serological pipettes are designed to be "blown out" to deliver their full volume.
- *Utilizes micropipettes* (piston-driven air displacement pipettes) for microliter-scale volumes (e.g., 1 to 1000 microliters), which require disposable plastic tips to prevent sample cross-contamination.
- *Includes Pasteur pipettes* (droppers), which are uncalibrated glass or plastic pipettes used for simple transfers where quantitative accuracy is not required.
- *Requires key technical steps* for accuracy, including pre-wetting the pipette tip, holding the pipette vertically during aspiration, and dispensing at a 45-degree angle with the tip touching the receiving vessel's wall.

## connections

- [[titration]] — a volumetric pipette is used to deliver the exact volume of analyte into the titration flask.
- [[water]] — micropipettes are calibrated gravimetrically by measuring the mass of purified water they dispense, using the temperature-dependent density of water.
- [[glass]] — classic volumetric and Pasteur pipettes are made of borosilicate glass, which is chemically inert and thermal-shock resistant.
