# Photosynthesis

## summary

*Photosynthesis* is the fundamental biochemical process by which plants, algae, and some bacteria convert light energy into chemical energy stored in glucose. Occurring primarily in leaf chloroplasts, it consumes carbon dioxide and water to produce carbohydrates and oxygen. Photosynthesis is the foundation of nearly all food chains and the primary source of atmospheric oxygen, making it essential to life on Earth.

## you gotta know

### Light-Dependent Reactions

- Occur in the *thylakoid membrane* of chloroplasts; capture photons via chlorophyll and other pigments.
- Split *water molecules* (photolysis) to release molecular oxygen as a byproduct, electrons, and protons.
- Generate *ATP* and *NADPH*—high-energy molecules that power the next stage of photosynthesis.
- Organised into two photosystems, *Photosystem II* and *Photosystem I*, which work in series.

### Calvin Cycle (Light-Independent Reactions)

- Occurs in the *stroma*; uses ATP and NADPH from light reactions to fix carbon dioxide.
- Catalysed by *Rubisco* (RuBP carboxylase), the most abundant enzyme on Earth; it binds CO₂ to ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate.
- Produces *3-phosphoglycerate* (3-PG), which is reduced to *glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate* (G3P).
- Most G3P is recycled to regenerate RuBP; the remainder exits the cycle to build glucose.

### Efficiency and Limiting Factors

- Overall equation: 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂.
- *Light intensity*, *CO₂ concentration*, and *temperature* all limit the rate of photosynthesis.
- C₃ plants (most crops) are less efficient than C₄ and CAM plants in arid conditions due to *photorespiration*—Rubisco's tendency to oxygenate RuBP instead of carboxylating it.

### Evolution and Significance

- Emerged roughly 2.5 billion years ago with cyanobacteria; transformed Earth's atmosphere from anoxic to oxygen-rich.
- Responsible for nearly all biomass production; animals are heterotrophs dependent on photosynthetic energy capture.

## context

Photosynthesis is a cornerstone of ecology and evolution: it literally transformed the planet's chemistry and made complex life possible. In quiz bowl and broader culture, it appears as a symbol of natural productivity and sustainability, and it recurs whenever questions touch photosynthetic organisms (plants, algae, cyanobacteria), the Calvin cycle, light reactions, or biogeochemical cycling. Understanding its two-stage structure—light-dependent and light-independent—is key to discussing chloroplast biology, metabolism, and the deep history of life.

## connections

- [[chloroplast]] — the organelle where photosynthesis occurs.
- [[rubisco]] — the enzyme that catalyses carbon fixation in the Calvin cycle.
- [[catalase]] — antioxidant enzyme that protects photosynthetic cells from reactive oxygen species.
- [[water]] — the electron and hydrogen source, cleaved in light reactions.
- [[hydrogen]] — liberated by water photolysis; crucial to proton gradient across thylakoid membrane.
- [[active-site]] — Rubisco's active site binds RuBP and CO₂ in the critical first step of the Calvin cycle.
- [[seed]] — the offspring product of photosynthesis-powered plant growth.
- [[cotyledon]] — embryonic leaf that conducts photosynthesis in seedlings.
