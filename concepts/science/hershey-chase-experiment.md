# Hershey–Chase Experiment

## summary

In 1952, *Alfred Hershey* and *Martha Chase* used radioactive isotopes to label the *DNA* and *protein* components of bacteriophages (viruses that infect bacteria) separately and tracked which entered bacterial cells during infection. Their elegant result proved that *DNA*, not *protein*, is the hereditary material — a watershed moment for molecular biology.

## you gotta know

- *Hershey* and *Chase* grew bacteriophages in media containing either radioactive sulphur (`³⁵S`, which labels protein) or radioactive phosphorus (`³²P`, which labels DNA), creating two isotopically distinct virus populations.
- They infected bacterial cells with each labelled phage, allowed infection to proceed, then used a blender to separate the phage *coats* (protein shells) clinging to the outside from the genetic material that entered the cells.
- The `³²P` (DNA) was found inside the bacteria and passed to progeny phages; the `³⁵S` (protein) remained outside with the empty phage coats, discarded by the cells.
- The result unambiguously showed that *DNA* is the *genetic material* — the carrier of hereditary instructions — not protein, which had been favored by many biologists.
- The experiment's power lay in its simplicity and the use of isotopic *labels*, allowing tracking without disrupting the infection process.
- The finding unified molecular biology, setting the stage for *Watson* and *Crick's* *DNA* structure determination two years later.
