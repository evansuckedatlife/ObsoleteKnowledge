# Carbon

## summary

Carbon is a nonmetallic tetravalent element that serves as the chemical basis for all known organic life. Its ability to form stable covalent bonds with other carbon atoms and a wide variety of elements allows for the existence of an almost infinite number of organic compounds. In its pure form, carbon exhibits several distinct allotropes with vastly different physical properties, ranging from the extremely soft graphite to the exceptionally hard diamond. As a major component of fossil fuels and the greenhouse gas carbon dioxide, its cycle through Earth's atmosphere, oceans, and biosphere plays a central role in regulating global climate.

## you gotta know

### allotropes & physical properties

- *Exists as diamond*, a metastable tetrahedral crystalline allotrope where each carbon is sp3 hybridized, forming the hardest naturally occurring material with high thermal conductivity.
- *Exists as graphite*, a stable planar allotrope where sp2 hybridized sheets are held together by weak van der Waals forces, making it soft, slippery, and electrically conductive.
- *Forms fullerenes and nanotubes*, which are closed cage-like molecules (such as the geodesic sphere buckminsterfullerene, C60) or cylindrical structures with high tensile strength and unique electrical properties.
- *Includes amorphous carbon*, non-crystalline forms such as charcoal, coal, and carbon black that lack long-range order.

### organic chemistry & isotopes

- *Exhibits catenation*, the ability of carbon to form long chains and rings through stable carbon-carbon bonds, which is the foundational basis for organic chemistry.
- *Has four valence electrons*, enabling it to form four covalent bonds in a tetrahedral geometry (sp3), planar geometry (sp2), or linear geometry (sp).
- *Utilized in radiocarbon dating* via the isotope carbon-14, which has a half-life of approximately 5,730 years and is used to estimate the age of organic archaeological artifacts.
- *Serves as the mass standard* for atomic weight, where the carbon-12 isotope is defined as having an atomic mass of exactly 12 daltons (atomic mass units).

### astrophysics & geological cycles

- *Produced in stars via the triple-alpha process*, where three helium-4 nuclei (alpha particles) fuse to form a carbon-12 nucleus in red giant stars.
- *Drives the CNO cycle* (carbon-nitrogen-oxygen cycle), a catalytic stellar hydrogen-fusion process dominant in stars heavier than the Sun.

## connections

- [[helium]] — three helium-4 nuclei fuse to form carbon-12 in the triple-alpha process in red giants.
- [[oxygen]] — carbon and oxygen form carbon dioxide, key to photosynthesis and the global carbon cycle.
- [[nitrogen]] — carbon and nitrogen are part of the catalytic CNO cycle in massive stars.
- [[organic-reactions]] — chemical reactions involving carbon compounds form the core of organic synthesis.
- [[functional-groups]] — structural groups of atoms containing carbon that dictate organic molecule reactivity.
- [[diamond]] — a major sp3 allotrope of carbon used as a gemstone and industrial abrasive.

## context

Carbon is the undisputed cornerstone of organic chemistry and biology, earning its place as the defining element of life. Its immense versatility in bond formation underlies the complexity of macromolecules like DNA, proteins, and carbohydrates. In quiz bowl, carbon is frequently clued through its allotropes (diamond, graphite, graphene, and C60 buckyballs), catenation, the triple-alpha fusion process, carbon-14 dating, and the definition of the atomic mass unit.
