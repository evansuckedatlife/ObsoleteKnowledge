# Richter scale

## summary

The **Richter scale** measures the *local magnitude* of earthquakes, quantifying the energy released by seismic waves as recorded on a Wood-Anderson seismograph. Developed in 1935 by American seismologists *Charles Richter* and *Beno Gutenberg*, it was the first instrumental standard for earthquake magnitude and remains iconic, though modern seismology uses moment magnitude for larger quakes.

## you gotta know

- Named after *Charles Francis Richter*, a seismologist at Caltech who developed the scale using recordings from Southern California earthquakes.
- The scale is *logarithmic*: each unit increase represents roughly a tenfold increase in the amplitude of seismic waves. A magnitude 5 earthquake has waves 10 times larger than a magnitude 4 quake.
- Magnitudes typically range from 0 to 9+. A magnitude 1–2 quake is imperceptible; magnitude 3–4 is minor; magnitude 5+ causes damage; magnitude 8+ is devastating globally.
- The scale saturates (loses accuracy) for very large earthquakes (above magnitude ~7) because it measures local wave amplitude, which plateaus; the *moment magnitude scale* (Mw) is used instead for large events.
- Richter magnitude is also called *local magnitude* (ML) because it depends on the distance of the seismograph from the epicenter.
- Earthquake frequency follows a power law: roughly 15 magnitude 8+ quakes occur per century globally; magnitude 6+ quakes are about 100 times more frequent.
- The scale has been refined and updated, but "Richter scale" remains a household term.
