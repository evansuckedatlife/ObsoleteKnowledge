# Organic Halide

## summary

An organic halide (commonly called a haloalkane or alkyl halide) is an organic compound containing one or more halogen atoms (fluorine, chlorine, bromine, or iodine) covalently bonded to a carbon atom. Due to the high electronegativity of halogens, the carbon-halogen bond is polar, rendering the carbon atom electrophilic and making organic halides primary substrates for nucleophilic substitution and elimination reactions.

## you gotta know

- *Serves as the substrate for nucleophilic substitutions*, undergoing SN1 reactions (via a carbocation intermediate) or SN2 reactions (which proceed in a single step with inversion of stereochemistry).
- *Undergoes elimination reactions* (E1 or E2) in the presence of base to form alkenes, generally following Zaitsev's rule to yield the most substituted and stable alkene product.
- *Acts as the precursor to Grignard reagents*, prepared by reacting alkyl or aryl halides with magnesium metal in an anhydrous ether solvent.
- *Displays leaving group reactivity* that increases down the halogen group ($I^- > Br^- > Cl^- > F^-$), correlating with weaker carbon-halogen bond strengths and the stability of the halide anion.
- *Includes chlorofluorocarbons* (CFCs), volatile synthetic organic halides that were widely used as propellants and refrigerants until banned under the Montreal Protocol for depleting the ozone layer.

## connections

- [[chlorine]] — a common halogen substituent in organic halides like chloroform and polyvinyl chloride (PVC) plastic.
- [[magnesium]] — inserts into the carbon-halogen bond of organic halides to form nucleophilic Grignard reagents.
- [[ozone]] — chlorine radicals released from photolyzed CFCs catalytically destroy ozone in the stratosphere.
