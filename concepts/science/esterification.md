## summary

Esterification is a chemical reaction that synthesizes an ester by reacting an organic acid (typically a carboxylic acid) with an alcohol. The most common method, Fischer esterification, is an acid-catalyzed condensation reaction that operates under thermodynamic equilibrium, releasing water as a byproduct.

## you gotta know

- *Proceeds via a reversible nucleophilic acyl substitution*, where protonation of the carbonyl oxygen of a carboxylic acid by a strong acid catalyst activates the carbon for attack by an alcohol nucleophile.
- *Driven to completion by Le Chatelier's principle*, which requires the removal of water (often using a Dean-Stark apparatus) or the use of one of the starting materials (commonly the alcohol) as the solvent.
- *Demonstrated by isotopic labeling* to proceed with the cleavage of the acyl-oxygen bond rather than the alkyl-oxygen bond, proving that the oxygen atom in the product ester originates from the alcohol.
- *Catalyzed by strong mineral acids* (such as sulfuric acid) or organic acids (such as p-toluenesulfonic acid), which act to increase the electrophilicity of the carboxyl group.
- *Occurs intramolecularly* in hydroxycarboxylic acids to yield cyclic esters, which are known as lactones.

## connections

- [[ester]] — the product class of the esterification reaction.
- [[carboxylic-acid]] — the primary acidic precursor that reacts with the alcohol.
- [[alcohol]] — the nucleophile that attacks the carbonyl center of the activated carboxylic acid.
