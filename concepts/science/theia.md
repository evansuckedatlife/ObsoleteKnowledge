# Theia

## summary

**Theia** is a hypothesized Mars-sized protoplanet that is believed to have collided with the proto-Earth roughly 4.5 billion years ago in the *Giant Impact* event. This collision, rather than destroying both bodies, forged the Earth we know today: it triggered planetary differentiation, spun the proto-Earth into faster rotation, and ejected material that coalesced into the Moon. Though Theia is no longer a distinct body, it is fundamental to planetary science and lunar-origin theory.

## you gotta know

- *Named for the Titaness Theia*, Greek goddess of light and daughter of Uranus and Gaia — apt for the parent of the Moon, which she bore indirectly.
- *Hypothesized existence:* not a directly observed object, but a theoretical body inferred from isotopic and dynamical studies of Earth and Moon composition; evidence is circumstantial but compelling.
- *Mars-sized body:* estimated at 0.1 Earth masses (~1,500 km diameter); orbited in the inner solar system during the late stage of planetary accretion.
- *Giant Impact event:* Theia struck the proto-Earth at a glancing angle (~45°) roughly 4.5 billion years ago; the collision melted both bodies and ejected a disk of material into Earth orbit.
- *Lunar origin:* the ejected debris disk coalesced over weeks to months into the Moon; isotopic ratios of oxygen and tungsten in lunar rocks match Earth's signature, supporting a terrestrial origin for lunar material.
- *Planetary differentiation:* the impact's energy melted the proto-Earth's mantle and contributed to the planet's iron core separation; rotation rate jumped from ~5 hours to ~6 hours (modern day: 24 hours, slowed by tidal friction).
- *Controversial details:* the exact collision angle, the degree of Theia's differentiation before impact, and the fraction of Theia's material retained in Earth vs. ejected to form the Moon remain debated.
