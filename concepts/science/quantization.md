## summary

Quantization is the principle that physical observables—energy, angular momentum, charge—can only take on discrete, allowed values rather than a continuous spectrum. This discrete nature emerges when a system is confined or constrained, such as an electron orbiting an atom or electromagnetic radiation in a cavity. The *ground state* represents the lowest-energy configuration a system can occupy.

## you gotta know

- Energy levels in atoms are discrete; electrons occupy specific orbital shells, not arbitrary distances from the nucleus
- The *quantum number* labels each allowed state (n, ℓ, m, s for electrons)
- Photons carry quantized energy: E = hf, where h is [[planck-constant]] and f is frequency
- Angular momentum is quantized: L = ℏ√(ℓ(ℓ+1)), where ℏ = h/2π
- A confined particle (particle in a box) has quantized momentum and energy; wavelengths that fit in the box are preferred
- Blackbody radiation problem was solved by assuming energy quantization
- Vibrational modes in solids (phonons) are quantized oscillators

## context

Quantization emerged as the core insight distinguishing quantum from classical mechanics. In classical physics, a pendulum or orbiting body can have any energy; in quantum systems, only specific energies are permitted by the [[schrodinger-equation]]. The discrete nature appears because of boundary conditions—an electron wave in an atom must "fit" within the atomic volume, favoring certain wavelengths. This constraint generates the periodic table's structure and explains atomic stability: atoms don't collapse because lower energy states are already occupied ([[pauli-exclusion-principle]]).

- [[planck-constant]] — the fundamental constant that sets the scale of quantization
