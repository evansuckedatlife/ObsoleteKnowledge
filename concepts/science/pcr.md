# PCR (Polymerase Chain Reaction)

## summary

**PCR** is a laboratory method for exponentially amplifying specific DNA sequences by repeatedly cycling through temperatures that denature, anneal, and extend DNA strands. Invented by Kary Mullis in 1983, it became the cornerstone of modern molecular biology, enabling forensic analysis, gene discovery, viral detection, and clinical diagnostics.

## you gotta know

- *Exponential amplification* — each cycle doubles the target DNA; ~30 cycles produce a millionfold increase (2^30 ≈ 10^9).
- *Three-step cycle*: high temperature (~94°C) denatures the double helix; lower temperature (~50°C) allows *primers* (short DNA probes) to bind flanking the target region; moderate temperature (~72°C) lets *DNA polymerase* extend the primers along the template.
- *Thermostable polymerase* — the enzyme (*Taq* polymerase, from *Thermus aquaticus*) survives repeated heating, unlike natural polymerases.
- *Primers* define the boundaries — they are complementary oligonucleotides that specify exactly which region gets copied; wrong primers amplify the wrong fragment.
- *Real-time PCR* monitors amplification by fluorescence during each cycle (quantitative PCR or qPCR), measuring how much product accumulates without waiting until the end.
- Used in COVID-19 testing, pathogen detection, forensics, cloning, and sequencing library prep.
