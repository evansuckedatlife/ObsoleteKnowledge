# Galileo Galilei

## summary

*Galileo Galilei* (1564–1642) was an Italian astronomer, physicist, and mathematician whose observations and experiments laid crucial foundations for modern science. He improved the telescope and used it to make revolutionary celestial discoveries, particularly the moons of Jupiter, which provided observational proof for heliocentrism. His rigorous experimental method, mathematical analysis, and willingness to challenge established authority made him one of the most influential figures of the Scientific Revolution, though his conflict with the Church over heliocentrism resulted in his trial and house arrest.

## you gotta know

- Galileo was born in Pisa in 1564; he studied mathematics and physics, eventually teaching at the universities of Pisa and Padua.
- In 1609, he learned of the telescope's invention in the Netherlands; he improved its design and turned it toward the heavens, making observations impossible with the naked eye.
- In January 1610, Galileo observed four large moons orbiting Jupiter, which he called the "Medicean planets"; this discovery provided evidence that celestial bodies orbit something other than Earth, supporting heliocentrism.
- He observed the *phases of Venus*, another phenomenon incompatible with the geocentric model; Venus appearing to orbit the Sun directly contradicted the Ptolemaic system.
- Galileo's *Dialogue Concerning the Two Chief World Systems* (1632), written in Italian rather than Latin, presented arguments for heliocentrism in an accessible form; it was banned by the Church.
- In 1633, the *Inquisition* tried Galileo for heresy; he was sentenced to house arrest and forbidden from publishing, though he continued writing in secret.
- Beyond astronomy, Galileo studied motion and inertia, laying groundwork for Newton's laws of mechanics; he famously (though perhaps apocryphally) dropped objects from the Leaning Tower of Pisa to study acceleration.
