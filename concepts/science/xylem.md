# Xylem

## summary

**Xylem** is the *vascular tissue* in plants that transports water and dissolved minerals upward from the roots to the leaves and stems. It is composed of specialized dead cells — *tracheary elements* (vessel members and tracheids) — that form continuous tubes through which water can move via *capillary action* and *transpirational pull*.

## you gotta know

- Functions passively: no metabolic energy required; water rises through *xylem* via *capillary forces* and the *transpiration pull* created as water evaporates from leaf cells.
- Composed of dead, hollow cells with *lignin*-reinforced walls; the cell walls remain after the cell contents die, forming a rigid tube network.
- Two main types of *tracheary elements*: *tracheids* (narrow, tapered, found in ferns and gymnosperms) and *vessel members* (wider, with perforated end walls, found in angiosperms).
- Secondary *xylem* is formed by the *vascular cambium* in woody plants, and accumulates as *heartwood* and *sapwood*; annual rings reflect the annual growth of secondary xylem.
- Transports *sap* — water plus dissolved minerals (nitrates, phosphates, potassium) absorbed from soil.
- Provides structural support to the plant via the rigidity of lignified walls; wood is largely secondary xylem.

## connections

- [[phloem]] — the complementary vascular tissue; phloem transports sugars and other organic compounds downward, whereas xylem transports water and minerals upward.
- [[vascular-plants]] — xylem is the defining feature that separates vascular plants (ferns, gymnosperms, angiosperms) from non-vascular plants (bryophytes).
- [[pterophytes]] — the earliest plants to evolve xylem; it enabled them to grow tall and colonize drier habitats.
- [[gymnosperms]] — xylem is essential to all seed plants.
- [[lignin]] — the organic polymer that reinforces xylem cell walls and gives wood its strength.
