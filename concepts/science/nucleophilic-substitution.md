# Nucleophilic Substitution

## summary

Nucleophilic substitution is a fundamental class of organic reactions where an electron-rich nucleophile selectively bonds with or attacks an electron-deficient atom to displace a leaving group. This reaction class is central to functional group interconversions in organic synthesis, commonly occurring at saturated carbon centers.

## you gotta know

- *Proceeds via the SN2 pathway*, a concerted bimolecular mechanism featuring back-side attack of the nucleophile, which results in Walden inversion of stereochemical configuration at the carbon center.
- *Proceeds via the SN1 pathway*, a stepwise unimolecular mechanism where the leaving group departs first to form a planar carbocation intermediate, leading to a racemic mixture of products.
- *Influenced by solvent polarity*, where polar aprotic solvents (like DMSO and DMF) accelerate SN2 reactions by leaving nucleophiles unsolvated, while polar protic solvents (like water) promote SN1 by stabilizing carbocation intermediates.
- *Governed by steric hindrance*, which severely retards SN2 reactions on secondary and tertiary carbons, rendering tertiary alkyl halides virtually inert to SN2 pathways.
- *Correlates with leaving group stability*, where weak bases (such as iodide, bromide, and tosylate) act as excellent leaving groups by stably accommodating negative charge.

## connections

- [[organic-halide]] — alkyl halides are the primary substrates for testing and performing aliphatic nucleophilic substitutions.
- [[elimination-reaction]] — a major competing reaction pathway that occurs when a nucleophile also acts as a strong base.
- [[alcohol]] — hydroxide or water nucleophiles substitute halides to form alcohols, while alcohols can act as weak nucleophiles in solvolysis reactions.
