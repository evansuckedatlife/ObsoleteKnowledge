## summary

The **Carboniferous Period** (359–299 million years ago) was the age of vast swamp forests, whose dead vegetation compacted into the coal deposits that gave the period its name. Giant insects, early reptiles, and early synapsids roamed the land; the atmosphere held nearly twice today's oxygen, enabling enormous arthropods. The supercontinent Pangaea was assembling.

## you gotta know

- Enormous *coal swamp forests* dominated tropical continents, dominated by giant club mosses, ferns, and conifers—the source of modern coal beds.
- *Giant arthropods* thrived in the oxygen-rich air: dragonflies with wingspans of 70 centimetres, scorpions as large as dogs.
- *Tetrapods* diversified into early *amphibians* (still tied to water for breeding) and early *reptiles* (with amniotic eggs, enabling full land colonisation).
- The first *synapsids*—sail-backed creatures like *Dimetrodon*—appeared, representing the lineage leading to mammals.
- *Oxygen levels* reached ~35% of the atmosphere (today: ~21%), enabling the size and activity of terrestrial life.
- The *supercontinent Pangaea* was forming as continents collided; equatorial rainforests were drier in the interior.

## connections

- [[devonian-period]] — the period when tetrapods first emerged.
- [[permian-period]] — the period that followed, when synapsids dominated and reptiles diversified.
- [[coal-forests]] — the distinctive vegetation that created modern coal.
- [[amphibians]] — the early tetrapods still dependent on water.
- [[reptiles]] — the amniotes that freed tetrapods from water dependency.
- [[synapsids]] — the mammal-like reptiles that rose during the Carboniferous.
