# Southern Blotting

## summary

**Southern blotting** is a technique for detecting specific DNA sequences in a sample by fragmenting genomic DNA (usually with *restriction enzymes*), separating by gel electrophoresis, transferring to a membrane, and hybridizing with a labeled probe. Invented by Edwin Southern in 1975, it reveals gene copy number, detects mutations and deletions, and maps restriction sites.

## you gotta know

- *Restriction digest first* — genomic DNA is cut at specific recognition sequences, generating fragments of different lengths.
- *Gel separation* — fragments are run on an agarose gel and sorted by size; smaller fragments migrate farther.
- *Capillary blotting* — the gel is placed on absorbent paper with a membrane on top; capillary action draws DNA upward onto the membrane, where it binds irreversibly.
- *Labeled probe* — a radioactive or fluorescent DNA probe (complementary to the target sequence) hybridizes to its match on the membrane.
- *Band pattern* reveals genetic variation — additional bands indicate extra copies (duplication); missing bands suggest deletion; shifted bands indicate restriction site mutations.
- Used in forensics, paternity testing, detecting gene amplifications (cancer), and diagnosing inherited disorders.
