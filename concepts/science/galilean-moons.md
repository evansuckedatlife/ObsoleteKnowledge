# Galilean Moons

## summary

The *Galilean moons* are the four largest satellites of Jupiter—*Io*, *Europa*, *Ganymede*, and *Callisto*—discovered by Galileo Galilei in January 1610. Their observation was revolutionary: four celestial bodies orbiting a planet other than Earth provided direct observational proof that the Earth is not the center of all celestial motion, lending powerful support to the heliocentric model. Each moon is geologically and atmospherically distinct, from Io's active volcanism to Europa's potentially habitable subsurface ocean.

## you gotta know

- *Io*, the innermost, is volcanically hyperactive due to tidal heating from Jupiter's gravity and orbital interactions with the other moons; sulfur compounds give it a yellow-orange color.
- *Europa*, the second, has a smooth icy crust concealing a subsurface water ocean; cracks and ridges suggest geothermal activity beneath the ice.
- *Ganymede*, the largest moon in the solar system (bigger than the planet Mercury), is the only moon with a magnetic field; it has both icy and rocky terrain.
- *Callisto*, the outermost, is heavily cratered and ancient, with a thick ice shell; it shows little signs of geological activity.
- Galileo observed them with a simple telescope in 1610 and initially called them the "Medicean planets," later renamed in his honor.
- The four moons orbit Jupiter with periods ranging from ~1.8 to ~16.7 days, tracing neat geometric patterns used by early astronomers to check the accuracy of telescopes.
- Tidal heating—caused by gravitational interactions—powers volcanic activity on Io and likely maintains liquid water on Europa.
