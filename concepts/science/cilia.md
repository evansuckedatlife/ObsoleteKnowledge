# Cilia

## summary

Cilia and flagella are hair-like, motile projections that extend from the cell surface and generate movement through beating or rotation. *Cilia* are shorter and more numerous, functioning in locomotion or fluid transport; *flagella* are longer and typically present singly, rotating like propellers. Both structures contain a core of *microtubules* arranged in a characteristic 9+2 pattern (nine outer doublets plus two central singlets) and are anchored by *basal bodies* (modified centrioles).

## you gotta know

- **Microtubule arrangement**: 9+2 pattern of microtubule doublets around a central pair
- **Dynein arms**: protein projections on outer doublets that bind adjacent doublets and generate movement
- **Nexin links**: elastic connectors between adjacent doublets that constrain sliding
- **Basal body**: anchor structure at the cell base; derived from centrioles in eukaryotes
- **Beating mechanism**: coordinated dynein activity generates bending waves traveling along the cilium
- **Primary cilia**: single, immotile cilium present on most mammalian cells; functions in sensory signaling
- **Prokaryotic flagella**: structurally distinct; protein filament rotates via motor protein, not microtubular sliding

## context

Cilia and flagella are ancient cell structures, with prokaryotic versions predating the evolution of eukaryotic 9+2 cilia by billions of years. Eukaryotic cilia represent remarkable feats of engineering—their coordinated beating can propel single-celled organisms or move fluids across tissue surfaces. In the respiratory tract, cilia beat in synchronized waves to clear mucus and pathogens. Primary cilia on most mammalian cells function as cellular "antennae," detecting chemical and mechanical signals critical for development and homeostasis. Ciliary dysfunction causes primary ciliary dyskinesia (Kartagener's syndrome), resulting in chronic infections and infertility.
