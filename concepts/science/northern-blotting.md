## summary

**Northern blotting** is a molecular technique for detecting and quantifying specific RNA molecules in a sample by separating total or mRNA by gel electrophoresis, transferring the RNA to a membrane, and hybridizing it with a labeled probe. Named by play on *Southern blotting* (which detects DNA), it measures gene expression and RNA splicing patterns.

## you gotta know

- *Gel separation first* — RNA is extracted from cells or tissue, run on a denaturing gel (usually formamide or glyoxal), and separated by size.
- *Transfer to membrane* — separated RNA is blotted (capillary or vacuum transfer) onto a nylon or nitrocellulose membrane, where it binds and is immobilized.
- *Labeled probe* — a complementary RNA or DNA probe (radioactively or fluorescently labeled) hybridizes to its target RNA on the membrane.
- *Washing and detection* — excess unbound probe is washed away; autoradiography or chemiluminescence reveals the position and intensity of the bound probe.
- *Band intensity* reflects mRNA abundance — brighter bands indicate more transcript.
- Useful for measuring mRNA levels, confirming gene knockout or knockdown, and studying tissue-specific or developmental gene expression.

## connections

- [[rna]] — the molecule being detected; often mRNA from a specific tissue or condition.
- [[southern-blotting]] — the DNA equivalent; northern was named by analogy.
- [[western-blotting]] — detects protein product; often paired with Northern to study gene expression at both RNA and protein levels.
- [[gel-electrophoresis]] — the first step; RNA must be separated by size before blotting.
