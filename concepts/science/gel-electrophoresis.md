# Gel Electrophoresis

## summary

**Gel electrophoresis** is a separation technique that forces charged molecules (DNA, RNA, or proteins) through a porous gel matrix under an electric field, sorting them by size and charge. Agarose and polyacrylamide gels are the most common matrices, allowing researchers to purify, identify, and analyze biomolecules.

## you gotta know

- *Charge-driven migration* — negatively charged DNA and RNA move toward the positive electrode; proteins migrate depending on pH and their net charge.
- *Sieve effect* — the gel's pores act as a molecular filter; smaller molecules move faster through the pores, larger ones lag behind, creating separation by size.
- *Agarose gels* use polysaccharide extracted from seaweed; standard for DNA and RNA (1–2% concentration typical).
- *Polyacrylamide gels* (PAGE) are denser and resolve smaller molecules and proteins with greater precision; often run under denaturing conditions (SDS-PAGE) to linearize proteins and separate by molecular weight alone.
- *Visualization* — after running, molecules are stained (ethidium bromide fluoresces under UV; safer dyes like GelRed or SYBR exist) and imaged.
- *Band patterns* reveal identity, purity, and integrity; a single sharp band indicates clean DNA; smearing or multiple bands suggest degradation or contamination.

## connections

- [[dna]] — the most common substrate in gel electrophoresis labs.
- [[pcr]] — the upstream step; gel is used to confirm PCR product size.
- [[protein]] — proteins are routinely separated by SDS-PAGE.
- [[restriction-enzyme]] — digest patterns are visualized by running digested DNA on a gel.
- [[dna-sequencing]] — fragment libraries are checked on a gel before sequencing.
