# Phloem

## summary

**Phloem** is the *vascular tissue* that transports sugars, amino acids, and other organic compounds produced by *photosynthesis* throughout the plant. Unlike *xylem*, phloem cells remain alive and require metabolic energy to actively move nutrients from sources (typically leaves) to sinks (roots, growing tips, storage organs).

## you gotta know

- Transports *organic compounds* — chiefly sucrose (glucose + fructose) — from *photosynthetic* tissues (sources) to non-photosynthetic or storage tissues (sinks).
- Composed primarily of *sieve-tube members* (the conducting cells, which remain living) and companion cells (smaller, metabolically active cells that support the sieve-tube members).
- Uses *active transport*: sugar is actively loaded into the phloem at source tissues, creating an *osmotic gradient* that drives water inflow and bulk flow toward sinks (contrast with passive xylem transport).
- Bidirectional: sugars can flow both upward (from leaves to roots) and downward (from leaves to developing fruits or storage roots), depending on source and sink locations.
- Primary *phloem* is laid down during growth; secondary *phloem* (produced by the *vascular cambium* in woody plants) accumulates outside the wood as the bark.
- Easily damaged by pests (aphids, scale insects) that puncture it; also damaged by girdling (removing a bark strip), which severs phloem and starves roots.

## connections

- [[xylem]] — the complementary vascular tissue; xylem transports water and minerals upward passively, while phloem transports organic compounds actively.
- [[vascular-plants]] — phloem is found in all vascular plants; it evolved alongside xylem.
- [[photosynthesis]] — phloem distributes the sugars produced by photosynthesis to the rest of the plant.
- [[pterophytes]] — the earliest plants to evolve phloem alongside xylem.
- [[respiration]] — the sieve-tube member cells consume ATP during active transport of sugars through phloem.
