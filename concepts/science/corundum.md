# Corundum

## summary

**Corundum** is aluminium oxide (Al₂O₃), the second hardest mineral after diamond. It forms in metamorphic rocks and some igneous settings, particularly at high temperatures. Its transparent red variety is called *ruby*, while all other colours (including blue sapphire) are termed *sapphire*. Corundum's hardness makes it valuable both as a gemstone and as an industrial abrasive.

## you gotta know

- *Aluminium oxide* (Al₂O₃) — a compound mineral with no silicon, making it structurally distinct from silicates.
- Rated 9 on the *Mohs hardness scale* — second only to diamond; hard enough to cut or polish all other minerals except diamond.
- Forms beautiful *hexagonal crystals*; transparency and colour depend on trace elements and impurities.
- *Ruby* is red corundum, coloured by *chromium* impurities; *sapphire* is blue (iron and titanium), and other colours exist (yellow, pink, orange).
- Highly valued as gemstones; fine rubies can command higher prices per carat than diamonds due to rarity.
- Forms primarily in *metamorphic rocks* (marble, schist) where aluminium-rich protoliths are heated and compressed; also in some alumina-rich igneous rocks.
- Industrial abrasive — used in grinding wheels, sandpaper, and polishing compounds; synthetic corundum (alumina) is manufactured at scale for these purposes.

## connections

- [[diamond]] — diamond ranks 10 (hardest), corundum ranks 9; together they dominate the top of the Mohs hardness scale.
- [[quartz]] — quartz (hardness 7) is much softer than corundum and is easily scratched by it.
- [[mohs-scale]] — corundum (hardness 9) is a reference mineral for hardness testing in the upper range.
- [[metamorphic-rock]] — most natural corundum forms in metamorphic rocks like marble and corundum schist.
- [[gemstone]] — ruby and sapphire are among the most valued gemstones, often outpricing diamonds in fine specimens.
