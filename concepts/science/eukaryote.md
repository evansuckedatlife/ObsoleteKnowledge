# Eukaryote

## summary

*Eukaryotes* are organisms whose cells contain a *nucleus* and other membrane-bound *organelles* — compartments that segregate cellular functions. This group includes animals, plants, fungi, and protists. The presence of a nucleus, housing the cell's DNA, is the defining feature; it allows eukaryotes to compartmentalize transcription (in the nucleus) and translation (in the cytoplasm), enabling more complex gene regulation than prokaryotes. Eukaryotes range from single-celled protists to multicellular organisms with trillions of specialized cells.

## you gotta know

### Cell Structure and Organization
- The defining feature is a *nucleus*: a membrane-bound compartment containing linear chromosomes of DNA.
- *Organelles* are membrane-bound compartments, each with specialized functions: *mitochondria* (energy production), *endoplasmic reticulum* (protein and lipid synthesis), *Golgi apparatus* (modification and packaging), *lysosomes* (intracellular digestion), *chloroplasts* (photosynthesis, in plants and algae).
- A *cytoskeleton* (network of protein filaments) provides structural support, enables cell movement, and facilitates intracellular transport.
- Cells are typically larger (~10–100 micrometers) than prokaryotic cells and more internally complex.

### Gene Expression and Regulation
- Transcription occurs in the nucleus; the primary mRNA transcript is processed (5' cap, 3' poly-A tail, splicing to remove introns) before export to the cytoplasm.
- Translation occurs on ribosomes in the cytoplasm (free) or on the rough endoplasmic reticulum (for secreted or membrane proteins).
- This compartmentalization allows more sophisticated gene regulation: control of splicing, selective mRNA export, and temporal separation of transcription and translation.
- *Epigenetic* modifications (DNA methylation, histone acetylation) regulate which genes are accessible for transcription.

### Diversity of Eukaryotes
- *Protists*: mostly single-celled; highly diverse; include amoebas, ciliates, and parasites like malaria-causing *Plasmodium*.
- *Fungi*: mostly multicellular (mushrooms, molds, yeasts); obtain nutrients by absorbing organic matter (decomposers); cell walls contain chitin.
- *Plants*: multicellular, photosynthetic; cell walls contain cellulose; contain chloroplasts; immobile.
- *Animals*: multicellular, heterotrophic (consume other organisms); no cell walls; typically mobile; include insects, fish, amphibians, reptiles, birds, and mammals.

### Cell Division and Reproduction
- *Mitosis* produces genetically identical daughter cells; used for growth, repair, and asexual reproduction.
- *Meiosis* produces haploid gametes (sperm, eggs) with half the chromosomes; essential for sexual reproduction and genetic recombination.
- Cell cycle: *G1* (growth), *S* (DNA synthesis), *G2* (preparation), *M* (mitosis); controlled by checkpoints (p53, other proteins) that detect errors and prevent damaged cells from dividing.
- Multicellular eukaryotes achieve complexity through *differentiation*: cell-type specialization driven by differential gene expression.

### Metabolic Complexity
- Aerobic respiration: mitochondria break down glucose in the presence of oxygen, producing ATP with far greater efficiency (~30–32 ATP per glucose) than anaerobic fermentation.
- Photosynthesis (in plants and algae): chloroplasts use light energy to fix CO₂ into glucose, producing oxygen as a byproduct.
- These energy-intensive processes drove eukaryotic evolution: the ability to harvest energy at scale allowed larger organisms and complex life.

### Evolutionary Origin
- Eukaryotes evolved from prokaryotes; *endosymbiotic theory* proposes that mitochondria (and chloroplasts) originated as engulfed prokaryotes that became integrated into the cell.
- The acquisition of these organelles — and the ability to harness oxygen for energy — was a watershed moment in evolution, enabling the radiation of complex life.

## context

Eukaryotes represent a quantum leap in biological complexity. The compartmentalization enabled by organelles allows eukaryotic cells to grow much larger and to orchestrate more intricate chemical processes. The nucleus's separation of transcription from translation opened new possibilities for gene regulation, including alternative splicing (one gene, many proteins) and long-distance signaling via nuclear export. Eukaryotic complexity undercuts the notion of a simple "tree" of life: eukaryotes are mosaics of prokaryotic ancestry (mitochondria, chloroplasts, genes from diverse bacterial sources). The success of eukaryotes is evident: the largest and most complex multicellular organisms are eukaryotes (whales, redwoods, fungi). But eukaryotic cell division (mitosis, meiosis) is also more error-prone than prokaryotic replication, and the need for more complex regulatory systems increases the opportunity for errors. Cancer, in particular, is a eukaryotic disease: uncontrolled cell division enabled by loss of cell cycle checkpoints. Understanding eukaryotic cell structure, gene expression, and division is foundational to molecular biology, development, medicine, and biotechnology.

## connections

- [[nucleus]] — the membrane-bound organelle housing eukaryotic DNA; the defining feature of eukaryotes.
