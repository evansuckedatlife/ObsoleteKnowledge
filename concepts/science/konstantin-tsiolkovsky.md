# Konstantin Tsiolkovsky

## summary

Russian schoolteacher and visionary (1857–1935) who developed the mathematical *rocket equation* and conceived the entire architecture of space exploration. Though he never built a working rocket, Tsiolkovsky's theoretical work — published in obscure journals and largely unknown to the West during his lifetime — became the foundation of Soviet cosmonautics and is now recognized as the starting point of modern astronautics.

## you gotta know

- Derived the *Tsiolkovsky equation* (the *rocket equation*), which relates a rocket's *delta-v* (change in velocity) to its *mass ratio* and exhaust velocity — the fundamental law governing all rocket motion.
- Designed the *multi-stage rocket* concept and argued that only a *staged vehicle* could escape Earth's gravity and reach orbit.
- Proposed using *liquid propellants* (liquid hydrogen and liquid oxygen) for efficiency, decades before anyone built such engines.
- Conceived of *space stations*, *long-duration spaceflight*, and *artificial gravity* via rotation — science fiction concepts he grounded in physics.
- Worked in near-total isolation in Kaluga, a provincial Russian city, publishing in obscure Russian journals that few Western scientists read until after World War II.
- After the 1917 Russian Revolution, he became a celebrated figure in the Soviet state and collaborated with rocket societies; his legacy became the philosophical core of Soviet space ambition.

## connections

- [[robert-goddard]] — American contemporary solving the same problems independently; Goddard proved Tsiolkovsky's equations in practice.
- [[sergei-korolev]] — Soviet engineer who studied Tsiolkovsky's writings and led the USSR's space program on Tsiolkovskian principles.
- [[sputnik]] — Korolev's Sputnik was the first vindication of Tsiolkovsky's vision that mankind could reach orbit.
