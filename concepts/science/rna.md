# RNA

## summary

*RNA (ribonucleic acid)* is a nucleic acid polymer that carries genetic information and catalyzes reactions in cells. Unlike DNA, RNA is typically single-stranded and uses ribose sugar (containing an extra hydroxyl group) instead of deoxyribose. RNA serves multiple critical roles: as a messenger carrying DNA's instructions to the ribosome for protein synthesis, as a structural and catalytic component of the ribosome itself, and as a regulator of gene expression.

## you gotta know

### Structure and Composition
- A polymer of *ribonucleotides*: each consists of ribose sugar, a phosphate group, and a nitrogenous base.
- Uses the base *uracil* instead of thymine (which appears in DNA); this single difference has profound functional consequences.
- Typically *single-stranded*, though it can fold back on itself to form secondary structures (stems, loops) stabilized by base pairing with itself.
- The 2'-OH group on ribose makes RNA chemically less stable than DNA and more susceptible to hydrolysis, particularly in alkaline conditions.

### Types and Functions
- *mRNA (messenger RNA)*: transcribed from DNA, carries the genetic code in codons to the ribosome; translated into protein.
- *tRNA (transfer RNA)*: a cloverleaf-shaped molecule with an anticodon that pairs with mRNA codons and carries the corresponding amino acid to the ribosome during translation.
- *rRNA (ribosomal RNA)*: a major structural and catalytic component of the ribosome; catalyzes peptide bond formation.
- *snRNA, snoRNA, miRNA, siRNA*: regulatory RNAs that process other RNAs, silence genes, or fine-tune protein production.

### Synthesis and Degradation
- *RNA polymerase* synthesizes RNA by reading the DNA template strand in the 3' to 5' direction and building the RNA strand 5' to 3'.
- In eukaryotes, *transcription* occurs in the nucleus; the primary transcript (pre-mRNA) is processed: a 5' cap and 3' poly-A tail are added, and introns are removed via *splicing*.
- RNA is degraded much faster than DNA, typically lasting minutes to hours; this rapid turnover allows cells to quickly adjust protein production.

### Central Dogma
- The genetic code flows DNA → RNA → Protein: DNA is transcribed to mRNA, which is translated by the ribosome into protein.
- RNA occupies a central role in this process: it is both the information carrier (like DNA) and the executor (like proteins), making it unique.
- *Reverse transcriptase* (found in retroviruses and some organisms) can synthesize DNA from an RNA template, reversing part of the central dogma.

## context

RNA is at the intersection of information and chemistry. Its discovery that RNA can catalyze reactions (the discovery of *ribozymes* by Cech and Altman) revealed that RNA is not merely a messenger but also a molecular machine. This dual role has led to the RNA World hypothesis — the idea that early life may have relied entirely on RNA for both information storage and catalysis, before the evolution of DNA and proteins. In modern cells, RNA's versatility in regulation and its role in the fundamental process of protein synthesis make it indispensable; newer discoveries of regulatory RNAs (miRNAs, siRNAs) have revolutionized our understanding of gene control. RNA also features prominently in modern biotechnology: mRNA vaccines, RNA interference therapeutics, and CRISPR systems all harness RNA's programmability.
