# Centrifugation

## summary

Centrifugation is a laboratory technique that uses centrifugal force to separate particles of different densities from a liquid mixture. By spinning samples at high speeds, the denser components migrate outward to the bottom of the container, forming a compact pellet, while the less dense liquid layer (the supernatant) can be easily decanted or pipetted off.

## you gotta know

- *Governed by the Svedberg equation*, which relates the sedimentation velocity of a particle to its molecular mass, density, and frictional coefficient, defining the sedimentation coefficient (expressed in Svedberg units, S).
- *Employs ultracentrifugation*, a technique pioneered by Theodor Svedberg that spins samples at extremely high speeds (often exceeding 100,000 revolutions per minute) to separate proteins and other macromolecules.
- *Uses density gradient centrifugation* (isopycnic or rate-zonal) using cesium chloride or sucrose gradients to separate cellular components or macromolecules based on buoyant density.
- *Acts as the key method in the Meselson-Stahl experiment* in 1958, which used cesium chloride density gradients to prove the semi-conservative replication of DNA.
- *Separates whole blood* into plasma, a "buffy coat" of white blood cells and platelets, and a pellet of dense red blood cells for medical diagnostics.

## connections

- [[nitrogen]] — density gradient centrifugation was used to separate DNA labeled with heavy nitrogen-15 from light nitrogen-14 in the Meselson-Stahl experiment.
- [[uranium]] — industrial gas centrifuges spin gaseous uranium hexafluoride to separate fissile uranium-235 from uranium-238 based on mass.
- [[filtration]] — a peer separation technique that relies on size exclusion through a physical barrier rather than density differences under rotational force.
