# Meselson–Stahl Experiment

## summary

In 1958, *Matthew Meselson* and *Franklin Stahl* grew bacteria in a medium containing a heavy isotope of nitrogen, then transferred them to normal medium and tracked *DNA* replication using density-gradient centrifugation. Their results definitively proved that *DNA* replicates in a *semi-conservative* manner — each new *DNA* molecule contains one original strand and one newly synthesized strand.

## you gotta know

- *Meselson* and *Stahl* labelled *Escherichia coli* DNA with *heavy nitrogen* (`¹⁵N`), making it denser than normal (`¹⁴N`) DNA; they could then separate heavy and light *DNA* by density using ultracentrifugation.
- After one round of replication in `¹⁴N` medium, all *DNA* was intermediate in density (*hybrid*) — each molecule had one heavy strand (original) and one light strand (new).
- After a second replication, half the *DNA* was hybrid (original heavy strand paired with new light strand) and half was fully light (both strands newly synthesized).
- This pattern matched the *semi-conservative* model: each original strand serves as a *template*, and the two daughter *DNA* molecules each contain one original and one newly synthesized strand.
- The experiment ruled out competing models: *conservative* replication (original *DNA* stays intact; a fully new copy emerges) and *dispersive* replication (both strands are fragmented and mixed).
- The technique of *isotope labeling* and density separation became a gold standard for molecular biology, refined for decades.
