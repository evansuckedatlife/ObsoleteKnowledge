## summary

Recrystallization is a chemical purification technique used to dissolve an impure solid in a hot solvent and then cool the solution slowly. As the temperature drops, the desired compound precipitates out of the supersaturated solution as highly ordered, pure crystals, while the impurities remain dissolved in the surrounding solvent (known as the mother liquor).

## you gotta know

- *Relies on temperature-dependent solubility*, requiring a solvent in which the desired solute is highly soluble at elevated temperatures but poorly soluble at low temperatures, while impurities remain soluble at all temperatures.
- *Induces nucleation* in supersaturated solutions by adding a tiny seed crystal or scratching the inner glass wall of the flask with a glass rod to create micro-particulate glass nucleation sites.
- *Removes colored impurities* by adding activated charcoal (decolorizing carbon) to the hot solution, which adsorbs high-molecular-weight organic impurities prior to hot filtration.
- *Isolates the purified solid* using vacuum filtration to separate the crystal crop from the mother liquor, followed by a wash with ice-cold solvent.
- *Employs a Craig tube* for microscale recrystallizations, a specialized glassware piece that combines dissolution, crystallization, and centrifugation-assisted filtration.

## connections

- [[carbon]] — activated carbon is used to decolorize impure solutions during the process.
- [[filtration]] — requires a two-step filtration process: hot gravity filtration to remove insoluble impurities, and cold vacuum filtration to collect the final crystals.
- [[water]] — water is a common, non-toxic recrystallization solvent for polar organic compounds.
