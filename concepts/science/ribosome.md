## summary

Ribosomes are *non-membrane-bound* cellular machines that synthesize proteins by translating messenger RNA (mRNA) into chains of amino acids. Found in all cells (prokaryotic and eukaryotic), ribosomes consist of ribosomal RNA (rRNA) and ribosomal proteins assembled into large and small subunits. They are the site where genetic instructions are converted into functional proteins.

## you gotta know

- **Two-subunit structure**: large and small subunits (in bacteria: 70S; in eukaryotes: 80S)
- **mRNA binding**: ribosome reads codons on mRNA in sequence from 5' to 3' direction
- **Peptide bond formation**: catalyzes covalent bonds between amino acids during translation
- **tRNA recognition**: matches amino acids to mRNA codons via anticodon-codon pairing
- **Free vs. bound**: free ribosomes in cytoplasm make cytoplasmic proteins; bound ribosomes on rough ER make secreted/membrane proteins
- **Ribosomal RNA synthesis**: rRNA is made in the nucleolus and combined with ribosomal proteins in the cytoplasm
- **Translation speed**: prokaryotic ribosomes are smaller and faster; eukaryotic ribosomes are larger and permit more regulation

## context

Ribosomes are ancient cellular structures found in all domains of life, suggesting their origin predates the split between prokaryotes and eukaryotes. They are among the largest and most complex molecular machines, capable of correctly assembling hundreds of amino acids per minute. The ribosome's catalytic activity resides in the rRNA itself (it is a *ribozyme*), highlighting the central role of RNA in early cellular chemistry. Most antibiotics target bacterial ribosomes, exploiting structural differences from eukaryotic versions.
