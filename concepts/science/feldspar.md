# Feldspar

## summary

**Feldspar** is the most abundant mineral in Earth's crust, comprising about 60% of all igneous rocks. It is an *aluminosilicate* mineral that forms at high temperatures and constitutes the primary component of granitic and basaltic rocks. Feldspars weather readily, breaking down into clay minerals that form the foundation of soils.

## you gotta know

- The most common mineral in the crust, making up the bulk of granites and basalts.
- *Aluminosilicate* composition — contains silicon, aluminium, and oxygen, typically with potassium, sodium, or calcium.
- Rated 6 to 6.5 on the *Mohs scale*, softer than quartz but harder than calcite.
- Forms *blocky crystals* in igneous rocks; easily cleaves along planes due to its internal structure.
- Two main groups: *orthoclase* (potassium-rich, found in granites) and *plagioclase* (sodium/calcium-rich, found in basalts).
- Weathers more readily than quartz, releasing silica and breaking down into clay and feldspar sand over geological time.
- Used in ceramics, glass manufacture, and as a flux in metalworking.

## connections

- [[granite]] — feldspar is a primary mineral in granite, alongside quartz and mica.
- [[basalt]] — plagioclase feldspar is the dominant mineral in basaltic lava flows.
- [[quartz]] — a companion mineral in most igneous rocks, though quartz is harder and more chemically stable.
- [[mica]] — often found alongside feldspar in granites and metamorphic rocks.
- [[silicate]] — feldspars are silicate minerals with the addition of aluminium.
