# Ozonolysis

## summary

Ozonolysis is an organic chemical reaction that uses ozone (O3) to cleave the unsaturated carbon-carbon double or triple bonds of alkenes or alkynes. By breaking both the sigma and pi bonds, the reaction splits the carbon skeleton to produce carbonyl compounds (such as aldehydes, ketones, or carboxylic acids) depending on the starting material's structure and the reaction workup conditions.

## you gotta know

- *Proceeds via a molozonide intermediate*, an unstable 1,2,3-trioxolane formed by a [3+2] cycloaddition of ozone across the double bond, which quickly rearranges into a more stable 1,2,4-trioxolane (ozonide).
- *Utilizes a reductive workup* using dimethyl sulfide (DMS) or zinc dust in acid to cleave the ozonide intermediate, yielding aldehydes and ketones as the final products.
- *Utilizes an oxidative workup* using hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) to convert the ozonide, which oxidizes any potential aldehyde products into carboxylic acids while leaving ketones intact.
- *Served historically as a primary analytical tool* to determine the position of double bonds in unknown organic compounds by analyzing the resulting carbonyl fragments.
- *Cleaves alkynes* to yield carboxylic acids or acid anhydrides directly, without requiring an oxidative workup.

## connections

- [[oxygen]] — ozone, the triatomic allotrope of oxygen, serves as the highly reactive electrophilic cleaving agent.
- [[zinc]] — metallic zinc is a common reducing agent used to decompose the ozonide intermediate during workup.
- [[aldehyde]] — reductive ozonolysis of terminal or monosubstituted alkenes yields aldehydes.
