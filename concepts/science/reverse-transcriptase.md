# Reverse transcriptase

## summary

Reverse transcriptase is an unusual polymerase that synthesizes DNA from an RNA template—the reverse of normal transcription. It originated in retroviruses and is now exploited extensively in molecular biology research and biotechnology. The enzyme converts single-stranded RNA into complementary DNA (cDNA), which can be further amplified or integrated into genomes.

## you gotta know

- Catalyzes: synthesis of DNA 5' → 3' using RNA 3' → 5' as template
- Substrates: deoxyribonucleotide triphosphates (dNTPs); no primer required for initiation
- Origin: derived from retroviral enzymes; HIV reverse transcriptase is the most well-characterized
- Fidelity: lower error rate than RNA-dependent RNA polymerases but higher error rate than DNA polymerase
- Uses: cDNA synthesis for molecular cloning, gene expression analysis (RT-PCR), first-strand synthesis libraries
- RT-PCR: reverse transcription followed by PCR amplification; detects and quantifies RNA levels in samples
- Structural features: lacks 3' to 5' exonuclease activity (no proofreading); RNase H domain degrades RNA template after synthesis

## context

Reverse transcriptase breaks the central dogma's unidirectional flow—information normally travels DNA → RNA → protein, but this enzyme allows RNA to generate DNA. Retroviruses use reverse transcriptase to convert their RNA genomes into DNA for integration into host chromatin, ensuring their persistence and replication. The enzyme's discovery was revolutionary for molecular biology; it enabled researchers to capture expressed genes as cDNA libraries, bypassing introns and identifying what genes cells actually transcribe. Its therapeutic applications extend to producing insulin and other recombinant proteins.
