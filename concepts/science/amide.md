## summary

An amide is an organic functional group characterized by a carbonyl carbon atom bonded to a nitrogen atom, represented by the general formula R-CO-NR'R''. Amides are exceptionally stable compared to other carboxylic acid derivatives due to resonance that delocalizes the nitrogen lone pair, imparting partial double-bond character to the carbon-nitrogen bond and making them non-basic. They are essential to biology, forming the peptide bonds that link amino acids into proteins.

## you gotta know

- *Exhibits significant resonance stabilization*, where the nitrogen lone pair is delocalized into the carbonyl oxygen, restricting rotation around the C-N bond and maintaining a planar geometry.
- *Lacks basicity in aqueous solution* because the nitrogen lone pair is involved in resonance, preventing the amide from acting as a proton acceptor under normal conditions.
- *Constitutes the peptide bond in biochemistry*, which covalently connects the carboxyl group of one amino acid to the amine group of another to form polypeptide chains.
- *Undergoes the Hofmann rearrangement*, reacting with bromine and sodium hydroxide to degrade primary amides into primary amines with the loss of a carbon atom as carbon dioxide.
- *Includes lactams*, which are cyclic amides categorized by ring size, such as the four-membered *beta-lactams* that form the active core of penicillin antibiotics.

## connections

- [[amine]] — amines act as nucleophiles to attack activated carboxylic acids to synthesize amides.
- [[carboxylic-acid]] — amides are synthesized from carboxylic acid derivatives and can be hydrolyzed back to carboxylic acids under harsh acidic or basic conditions.
- [[nitrogen]] — the nitrogen atom in an amide is sp2 hybridized to allow conjugation of its lone pair with the carbonyl pi system.
