# Stern–Gerlach Experiment

## summary

In 1922, *Otto Stern* and *Walther Gerlach* passed a beam of silver atoms through a non-uniform *magnetic field* and observed that the beam split into exactly two paths — not a continuum. This result was the first direct evidence of *quantised* angular momentum and *spin*, a peculiar quantum property with no classical analogue.

## you gotta know

- *Stern* and *Gerlach* expected atoms with random *magnetic moments* to spread continuously along the direction of a *magnetic field gradient*, like a classical compass in a gradient.
- Instead, the beam split sharply into just *two* components: atoms aligned "up" or "down" relative to the field.
- The splitting reveals that the *magnetic moment* (and hence the *spin* or *angular momentum* of the atom) is *quantised*: only discrete orientations are allowed, never arbitrary angles.
- If you take one of the two beams and pass it through a *second* Stern–Gerlach apparatus oriented the same way, it passes straight through undiverted — the outcome is deterministic.
- *Rotated* a second apparatus by 90 degrees, however, and the single beam splits again into two equal parts — the spin orientation is not "hidden" but genuinely unmeasured until the apparatus forces a choice.
- The experiment was the doorway to understanding *spin* as an intrinsic quantum property (not a literal rotation), central to fermions, the *Pauli exclusion principle*, and all of chemistry.
