# Vacuole

## summary

Vacuoles are large, membrane-bound compartments that store water, nutrients, ions, pigments, and waste products in plant and fungal cells. The *central vacuole* of plant cells can occupy up to 90% of cell volume, maintaining turgor pressure that keeps plants rigid and providing an osmotic environment for cellular functions. Vacuoles also perform digestive and storage roles similar to lysosomes in animal cells, though plant vacuoles are far larger.

## you gotta know

- **Central vacuole**: large, single vacuole in plant cells; surrounded by *tonoplast* membrane
- **Tonoplast**: selective membrane regulating ion and molecule transport into vacuole
- **Turgor pressure**: osmotic influx of water into vacuole maintains cell rigidity and plant structure
- **Storage functions**: accumulates amino acids, sugars, ions, and proteins for later use
- **Pigment storage**: anthocyanins and other pigments stored in vacuoles give flowers and fruits color
- **Waste sequestration**: toxic secondary metabolites and oxidative byproducts isolated from cytoplasm
- **Smaller vacuoles in animals**: contract, osmoregulate, or digest intracellular material; rare and small

## context

The plant vacuole represents a distinctive cellular adaptation enabling large cells with maintained structural integrity. The turgor pressure generated by vacuolar volume and osmotic potential allows plants to support themselves without rigid skeletons, making vacuoles essential to plant architecture. Vacuolar expansion during growth accounts for rapid cell enlargement following cell division. In times of stress (drought, salt exposure), vacuolar osmoregulation helps plants survive adverse conditions. The vacuole also represents a major cost of plant cell maintenance—maintaining turgor demands continuous energy investment in ion pumps.
