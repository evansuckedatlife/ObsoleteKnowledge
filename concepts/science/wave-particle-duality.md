# Wave-particle duality

## summary

Wave-particle duality asserts that all matter and energy exhibit both *wave* and *particle* properties depending on how they are observed or measured. [[louis-de-broglie]] showed that particles carry an associated wavelength λ = h/p; conversely, waves like light behave as discrete quanta (photons) with momentum p = h/λ. Neither aspect is primary; both are necessary to describe quantum phenomena.

## you gotta know

- Particles have a *de Broglie wavelength*: λ = h/p, where h is [[planck-constant]] and p is momentum
- Electrons, protons, and atoms all exhibit diffraction and interference (wave behavior)
- Photons have particle-like properties: energy E = hf and momentum p = E/c
- In the [[double-slit-experiment]], electrons create interference patterns (wave) but detectors register individual events (particle)
- The wavelength becomes significant when it's comparable to apparatus size; macroscopic objects have wavelengths too small to observe
- Wave properties enable [[superposition]] and interference; particle properties enable localized detection
- Neither wave nor particle picture alone is sufficient; the *true* nature is quantum mechanical

## context

The resolution of 19th-century puzzles—blackbody radiation, [[photoelectric-effect]], atomic structure—required abandoning the idea that waves and particles are distinct categories. Instead, quantum objects are described by *wavefunctions* that carry both aspects. When the system is large or interactions probe fine details, particle aspects dominate. When the system is isolated and interactions are gentle, wave aspects (interference, diffraction) emerge. The [[uncertainty-principle]] connects the two: precise localization (particle) requires high momentum uncertainty (short wavelength), while low momentum uncertainty means position is spread (wavelike).
