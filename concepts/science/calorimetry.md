# Calorimetry

## summary

Calorimetry is a laboratory technique used to measure the amount of heat transferred to or from a substance during chemical reactions, physical changes, or phase transitions. It relies on a closed vessel called a calorimeter, which insulates the system from the environment, allowing researchers to calculate changes in internal energy or enthalpy by monitoring temperature changes.

## you gotta know

- *Uses bomb calorimeters* to measure the heat of combustion of organic compounds under constant volume conditions, typically inside a pressurized steel vessel surrounded by water.
- *Employs coffee-cup calorimeters* for constant-pressure calorimetry, which are simple, open-system containers (often made of polystyrene) used to measure enthalpy changes in aqueous reactions.
- *Applies differential scanning calorimetry* (DSC) to study phase transitions, measuring the difference in heat flow between a sample and a reference as they are heated at a controlled rate.
- *Utilizes isothermal titration calorimetry* (ITC) in biochemistry to determine the binding affinity, stoichiometry, and enthalpy changes of molecular interactions in solution.
- *Calculates heat transfer* using the specific heat capacity equation $q = mc\Delta T$, where $q$ is the heat exchanged, $m$ is the mass, $c$ is the specific heat capacity of the substance, and $\Delta T$ is the temperature change.

## connections

- [[oxygen]] — bomb calorimeters are filled with high-pressure oxygen gas to ensure the complete combustion of the sample.
- [[water]] — water is the standard surrounding fluid in calorimeters due to its high and well-characterized specific heat capacity.
- [[carbon]] — measuring the caloric content of carbon-based foods and fuels is one of the most common applications of calorimetry.
