# Amylase

## summary

Amylase is a carbohydrate-digesting enzyme that catalyzes the hydrolysis of *glycosidic bonds* in starch and related polysaccharides, breaking them into smaller sugars. It is secreted in saliva and pancreatic juice, initiating carbohydrate digestion in the mouth and small intestine.

## you gotta know

- Catalyzes: hydrolysis of α-1,4-glycosidic bonds in starch, producing maltose and glucose
- Substrates: starch (plant storage polysaccharide), glycogen, and related α-linked polymers
- Types: α-amylase (cleaves internal bonds, random cleavage) and β-amylase (cleaves from ends, producing maltose)
- Location: salivary glands (salivary amylase) and pancreas (pancreatic amylase); secreted into saliva and small intestine
- Products: initially produces dextrins, then maltose and glucose; further hydrolysis occurs via brush-border enzymes
- Cofactor: requires Ca²⁺ for activity; metal ion stabilizes substrate and aids catalysis
- Kinetics: moderately fast enzyme; starch digestion begins immediately in mouth (though residence time is short)

## context

Amylase exemplifies dietary enzyme adaptation. Herbivores and omnivores rely on amylase to break down the enormous amounts of starch in plant foods, releasing glucose for energy. The enzyme's presence in saliva allows digestion to begin immediately upon chewing, and the process continues in the small intestine. Amylase activity is so critical that it appears in high concentrations in saliva and blood; elevated plasma amylase is a clinical marker of pancreatic disease. Different organisms have evolved varying amylase gene copy numbers reflecting their starch consumption; humans with historically high-starch diets show adaptations in amylase gene expression.
