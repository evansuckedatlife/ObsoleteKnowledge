## summary

An *enzyme* is a biological catalyst—a protein that accelerates chemical reactions by lowering the activation energy required for the reaction to proceed, without being consumed in the process. Enzymes are essential to life: they enable digestion, energy production, photosynthesis, DNA replication, and thousands of other metabolic processes to occur at rates compatible with living organisms. Each enzyme catalyzes a specific reaction or set of related reactions with remarkable precision.

## you gotta know

### Core Mechanism
- Enzymes are *proteins* with a three-dimensional *active site*—a pocket-shaped region with amino acids positioned to bind substrate molecules.
- The **lock-and-key model** explains specificity: substrate molecules fit into the active site like a key into a lock, forming an enzyme-substrate complex.
- Enzymes lower activation energy by stabilizing the transition state, allowing reactions to proceed faster at body temperature (37°C for humans) without denaturing the enzyme.
- An enzyme is not consumed; it catalyzes a reaction and is released unchanged, ready to catalyze again—a single enzyme molecule can process thousands of substrate molecules per second.

### Naming and Classification
- Enzyme names typically end in `-ase` (e.g., *amylase* breaks down starch; *lipase* breaks down lipids; *protease* breaks down proteins).
- The six major classes are: oxidoreductases (redox), transferases, hydrolases, lyases, isomerases, and ligases.

### Biological Roles
- *Digestion*: pepsin (stomach), amylase (saliva), lipase (pancreas) break down food macromolecules.
- *Energy production*: enzymes catalyze glycolysis, the citric acid cycle, and electron transport chain reactions.
- *DNA replication*: DNA polymerase, DNA ligase, helicase, and primase coordinate strand synthesis.
- *Photosynthesis*: rubisco (the most abundant protein on Earth) catalyzes carbon dioxide fixation.

### Factors Affecting Enzyme Activity
- **pH and temperature** affect enzyme shape and activity; extreme conditions denature enzymes irreversibly.
- **Substrate concentration** increases reaction rate until saturation is reached (Michaelis–Menten kinetics).
- **Enzyme inhibition**: competitive inhibitors compete with substrate for the active site; non-competitive inhibitors bind elsewhere and reduce enzyme efficiency.

## context

Enzymes are among the most sophisticated machines in nature. Their discovery—first recognized indirectly via fermentation in the late 1700s, then isolated and characterized chemically in the 1900s—was revolutionary. The realization that enzymes are proteins that act as catalysts unified biochemistry: it explained how living cells achieve the complex synthesis and breakdown of molecules without extreme temperatures and pressures. Industrial biotechnology relies entirely on engineered and natural enzymes: fermentation for alcohol and yogurt production, the synthesis of pharmaceuticals, DNA sequencing, and biofuel production. Understanding enzymes at the atomic level (via X-ray crystallography and cryo-EM) has enabled rational drug design—many medicines are enzyme inhibitors.
