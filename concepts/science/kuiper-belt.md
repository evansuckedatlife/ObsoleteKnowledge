# Kuiper Belt

## summary

The *Kuiper Belt* is a vast region of icy bodies orbiting the Sun beyond Neptune, stretching roughly 30 to 55 astronomical units (AU) from the Sun. Composed of dwarf planets, comets, and smaller icy objects, it is the source of short-period comets and home to **Pluto**, which was reclassified as a dwarf planet in 2006. The belt's discovery in the 1990s fundamentally revised our understanding of the outer solar system's structure and population.

## you gotta know

- The Kuiper Belt is named after Dutch-American astronomer *Gerard Kuiper*, who predicted its existence in 1951; it was not directly observed until 1992.
- It contains at least 100,000 objects larger than 100 kilometers in diameter, mostly composed of frozen methane, ammonia, and water ice.
- *Pluto*, long considered the ninth planet, was discovered in 1930 by Clyde Tombaugh; its 2006 reclassification as a dwarf planet sparked debate because it shares many traits with Kuiper Belt objects.
- Other notable dwarf planets in the Kuiper Belt include *Eris*, *Haumea*, and *Makemake*; *Eris* is actually more massive than Pluto.
- The belt is the primary source of *short-period comets*—those with orbital periods under 200 years—ejected from their stable orbits by gravitational perturbations.
- Objects in the Kuiper Belt orbit in nearly circular, low-inclination paths; this orderly structure contrasts with the spherical, high-inclination *Oort Cloud* beyond.
- The New Horizons spacecraft, launched in 2006, performed a historic flyby of Pluto in 2015, revealing a geologically diverse, complex world.
