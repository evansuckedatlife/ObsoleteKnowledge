# CRISPR/Cas9

## summary

**CRISPR/Cas9** is a genome-editing tool adapted from bacterial immune systems that uses a guide RNA to direct the *Cas9* nuclease to cut DNA at precise locations, enabling deletion, correction, or insertion of genetic sequences. Its programmability, simplicity, and efficiency have revolutionized genetic engineering and hold promise for treating genetic diseases.

## you gotta know

- *Bacterial origin* — CRISPR-Cas is a natural defense mechanism in bacteria and archaea against viral infection; the system stores fragments of invader DNA in repeating sequences.
- *Guide RNA* — a synthetic RNA (20–30 nucleotides) directs Cas9 to the target DNA sequence; changing the guide sequence reprograms the system to edit different genes without redesigning the nuclease.
- *Cas9 mechanism* — the nuclease cuts both strands of DNA at the target site; the cell's repair machinery either disables the gene (non-homologous end joining, NHEJ) or incorporates a new sequence if a template is provided (homology-directed repair, HDR).
- *Off-target effects* — the guide RNA can partially match unintended sites, causing unwanted edits; careful guide design and delivery minimizes this risk.
- *Multiplexing* — multiple guides can direct Cas9 to edit several sites simultaneously, enabling complex edits.
- Applications include correcting cystic fibrosis mutations, engineering disease-resistant crops, and developing cancer immunotherapies.
