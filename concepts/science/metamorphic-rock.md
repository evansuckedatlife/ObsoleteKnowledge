# Metamorphic Rock

## summary

*Metamorphic rocks* are rocks transformed by heat, pressure, and chemical processes deep within the Earth's crust, without melting. Formed at temperatures and pressures typically between 200 and 850 degrees Celsius and at depths often exceeding 5 kilometers, they constitute a major class of Earth's crustal material. The term *metamorphism* (meaning "change of form") describes the process; common examples include marble (metamorphosed limestone), slate (metamorphosed shale), and schist (metamorphosed mudstone or sedimentary rocks).

## you gotta know

- Metamorphic rocks form through *regional metamorphism* (large-scale transformation during mountain-building and plate collisions), *contact metamorphism* (localized heating by magma intrusion), or *dynamic metamorphism* (deformation during fault movement).
- Heat and pressure cause minerals in the original rock to recrystallize without the rock melting; new minerals form that are stable under the new conditions, often resulting in visible *foliation*—parallel layers or bands of minerals.
- *Foliated* metamorphic rocks display visible banding (e.g., slate, schist, gneiss) due to pressure-driven alignment of minerals; *non-foliated* rocks (e.g., marble, quartzite) lack this layering.
- *Marble*, formed by metamorphosis of limestone or dolomite, is prized for sculpture and architecture due to its softness and pure-white (if pure) or patterned coloring.
- *Slate*, a fine-grained metamorphic rock formed from shale, cleaves into thin, flat sheets used for roofing and writing surfaces.
- Index minerals (e.g., garnet, biotite, sillimanite) mark specific pressure and temperature ranges, allowing geologists to infer the metamorphic conditions a rock experienced.
- The rock cycle illustrates metamorphic rocks' place: sedimentary or igneous rocks subjected to deep burial and heat transform into metamorphic rocks; further heating may melt them into magma.
