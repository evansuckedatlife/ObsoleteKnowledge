# Kelvin scale

## summary

The **Kelvin scale** is the SI unit for measuring absolute temperature, starting at zero at the lowest possible temperature (absolute zero). Unlike Celsius and Fahrenheit, which use reference points tied to water, the Kelvin scale is rooted in thermodynamic principles and anchors to the complete absence of thermal energy. It is the standard temperature scale in physics and chemistry.

## you gotta know

- *Absolute zero*, defined as 0 K, is the theoretical temperature at which all molecular motion ceases—approximately −273.15 °C.
- Kelvin intervals are identical in size to Celsius degrees; the only difference is the offset (K = °C + 273.15).
- Named after physicist *William Thomson, 1st Baron Kelvin*, who developed the concept of absolute temperature in the 19th century.
- Water freezes at 273.15 K and boils at 373.15 K at standard atmospheric pressure.
- The scale is *not* logarithmic; it is a linear ratio scale, making it ideal for calculating ratios of thermal energy or heat transfer.
- Essential in physics because many gas laws and thermodynamic equations require temperature as an absolute (never negative) quantity.
- The triple point of water (273.16 K) was historically used as the defining reference, but has been superseded by the Boltzmann constant definition since 2019.
