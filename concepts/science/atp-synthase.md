## summary

ATP synthase is a molecular turbine that harnesses proton gradients across membranes to drive *ATP synthesis* from ADP and inorganic phosphate. It couples chemiosmotic potential energy—created by electron transport chains—into high-energy phosphate bonds, powering nearly all cellular work.

## you gotta know

- Catalyzes: ADP + Pi → ATP (and reverses to ADP + Pi under certain conditions)
- Mechanism: proton flow through the enzyme rotates a rotor subunit, driving conformational changes that close around ADP and Pi, facilitating phosphorylation
- Structure: F₀ (membrane-spanning proton channel) and F₁ (catalytic head); resembles a nano-scale electric motor with rotor and stator
- Location: inner mitochondrial membrane (eukaryotes), plasma membrane (prokaryotes), thylakoid membrane (chloroplasts)
- Proton gradient: driven by electron transport chain in mitochondria and by light-driven water splitting in chloroplasts
- Energy source: electrochemical gradient; ~3 protons per ATP in mammals; ~4 in plants/bacteria
- Rotation speed: rotor spins 100–300 times per second; among the fastest rotating nanomachines in nature

## context

ATP synthase is arguably the most important enzyme in energy metabolism—it is responsible for producing the ATP that powers life's cellular processes. Evolution converged on a rotary mechanism because it elegantly couples a concentration gradient to chemical bond formation. The enzyme's discovery, particularly the rotary mechanism (revealed by single-molecule experiments in the 1990s), was revolutionary; scientists could visualize the molecular machine spinning under a microscope. ATP synthase illustrates a profound principle: biological systems exploit gradients and conformational changes with exquisite efficiency.
