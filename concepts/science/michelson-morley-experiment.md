# Michelson–Morley Experiment

## summary

In 1887, *Albert Michelson* and *Edward Morley* used an *interferometer* to measure whether Earth's motion through space could be detected by changes in the *speed of light*. Their null result — no difference — became a cornerstone of *special relativity* and dealt a fatal blow to the *luminiferous ether* hypothesis.

## you gotta know

- The experiment split light into two perpendicular paths using a *half-silvered mirror*, reflecting each along orthogonal arms, then recombining them to observe *interference fringes*.
- If *ether* existed and Earth moved through it, light should travel faster in one direction than perpendicular to Earth's motion (like sound against wind).
- The *fringe shift* that should have appeared was not detected — an astounding null result suggesting the *speed of light* is *constant* regardless of Earth's motion.
- This impossibility (light speed invariant; Galilean addition of velocities broken) became the observational pillar of *Einstein's* special relativity.
- Morley later repeated the experiment multiple times, always with the same outcome.
- The result was so unexpected that physicists initially sought escape clauses; *Einstein* instead embraced it as fundamental.

## connections

- [[special-relativity]] — the theory that resolves the paradox.
- [[speed-of-light]] — proven constant in all inertial frames.
- [[ether]] — the discredited medium light supposedly needed.
- [[interferometer]] — the precision tool that made the measurement possible.
- [[lorentz-contraction]] — one failed escape route.
