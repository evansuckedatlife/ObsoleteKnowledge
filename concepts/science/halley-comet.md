# Halley's Comet

## summary

**Halley's Comet** is the most famous periodic comet in human history, visible from Earth roughly every 75 to 76 years. Named after astronomer **Edmund Halley**, who predicted its return in 1758 based on 17th-century observations, it has been recorded in historical chronicles and artworks for millennia. Its predictable orbit and spectacular tail make it the archetypal comet in Western culture.

## you gotta know

- *Orbital period:* 75–76 years; its passages have been documented in Chinese, Babylonian, and European records going back to at least 240 BCE.
- *Named after Edmund Halley*, who in 1705 used Newton's laws to calculate that comets observed in 1531, 1607, and 1682 were returns of the same object, predicted to reappear in 1758.
- *Nucleus composed* of rock and *dirty ice* — a low-density, fragile body roughly 15 km long; outgassing coma can extend to millions of kilometers.
- *Last perihelion* in 1986; next passage expected in 2061, making it the only comet most people will see twice in their lifetime.
- *Depicted in the Bayeux Tapestry* (1066) as an ill omen attending the Norman conquest; comets were thought to presage royal deaths and calamities.
- *Radiant source* for two meteor showers — the Eta Aquariids (May) and the Orionids (October) — caused by debris left along its orbit.

## connections

- [[edmund-halley]] — the astronomer who revolutionized comet science by applying celestial mechanics to predict its periodicity.
- [[comet]] — Halley is the prototype of the periodic comet and the most accessible example of cometary behavior.
- [[oort-cloud]] — while Halley likely originates from the *Kuiper Belt*, not the distant Oort Cloud, its orbit links to solar-system formation.
