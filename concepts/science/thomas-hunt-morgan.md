# Thomas Hunt Morgan

## summary

American biologist and embryologist who founded experimental genetics through his work with *Drosophila melanogaster* (fruit flies). His discovery of sex-linked inheritance and the linear arrangement of genes on chromosomes proved that genes had physical locations and obeyed Mendelian laws. He won the 1933 Nobel Prize for this work, transforming biology from descriptive to experimental.

## you gotta know

- Discovered *sex-linked inheritance* through the *white-eye mutation* in fruit flies: a trait inherited in a pattern tied to the X chromosome, proving genes resided on chromosomes.
- Established the fruit fly as the model organism for genetics — cheap, short-lived, thousands of mutants available, and amenable to precise breeding and counting.
- Developed *genetic mapping*: the concept that genes occupy linear positions on chromosomes, measurable by recombination frequency — a quantitative framework for genetics.
- Created the *Caltech School of Biology*; his "fly lab" became a world centre for genetics, attracting researchers from everywhere.
- Demonstrated that *mutations* were heritable, non-random changes in genes — not spontaneous reversion or environmental effects.
- Introduced the term "gene" as the hereditary unit; his *chromosome theory of inheritance* unified Mendelism and cytology.

## connections

- [[barbara-mcclintock]] — extended his genetic theories; discovered *transposable elements* and genetic regulation in corn.
- [[james-watson-and-francis-crick]] — successors who resolved the physical structure of genes (DNA) that Morgan's work mapped genetically.
- [[ronald-ross]] — contemporary naturalist; both used living organisms to decode underlying biological mechanisms.
