# Protein

## summary

*Proteins* are large polymers of amino acids, folded into precise three-dimensional shapes, that perform nearly every function in living cells. A protein's amino acid sequence, determined by DNA through mRNA, dictates its structure and function. Proteins serve as enzymes (catalyzing reactions), structural supports, hormones, antibodies, transporters, and regulators — making them the primary executors of genetic instructions and the most chemically diverse macromolecules in the cell.

## you gotta know

### Structure and Hierarchy
- Proteins are linear chains of *amino acids* linked by *peptide bonds* (a covalent bond between the carboxyl group of one amino acid and the amino group of the next).
- A protein's *primary structure* is its amino acid sequence; this sequence is determined by the mRNA codon sequence, which is transcribed from DNA.
- *Secondary structures* include alpha helices (coiled regions) and beta sheets (extended regions), stabilized by hydrogen bonding between backbone atoms.
- *Tertiary structure* is the overall 3D fold of the entire protein, determined by interactions between amino acid side chains: hydrogen bonds, ionic interactions, hydrophobic forces, disulfide bonds, and van der Waals forces.
- *Quaternary structure* occurs when multiple polypeptide chains assemble into a larger complex; e.g., hemoglobin consists of four subunits.

### Function and Specificity
- A protein's shape determines its function. The *active site* of an enzyme is a precisely folded pocket that binds substrate and catalyzes a reaction; the 3D structure ensures specificity and catalytic efficiency.
- Proteins are the primary *enzymes*, accelerating chemical reactions without being consumed. A single enzyme can catalyze thousands of reactions per second.
- *Antibodies* are proteins shaped to bind specific antigens; *hormones* are proteins that signal between cells; *transporters* move molecules across membranes.

### Synthesis and Translation
- Proteins are synthesized on the *ribosome* by the process of *translation*: the ribosome reads mRNA codons and recruits the corresponding tRNA molecules, each carrying an amino acid.
- Ribosomes catalyze peptide bond formation at a rate of ~20 amino acids per second in bacteria, ~2–8 amino acids per second in eukaryotes.
- After synthesis, proteins are modified (phosphorylated, glycosylated, cleaved) and folded — often with help from *chaperone proteins* — into their active form.

### Diversity and Evolution
- The 20 standard amino acids have diverse chemical properties (hydrophobic, hydrophilic, charged, polar), allowing proteins to adopt an enormous range of structures and functions.
- Proteins fold according to the *Anfinsen principle*: given the amino acid sequence and appropriate conditions, a protein will fold into a single, stable structure; the information for folding is in the sequence.
- Protein folding is thermodynamically favorable but kinetically slow in some cases, so cells use *chaperone proteins* and compartments (the endoplasmic reticulum) to facilitate proper folding.

## context

Proteins are the bridge between genotype (DNA sequence) and phenotype (observable traits). The central dogma — DNA encodes RNA, which is translated into protein — places proteins at the functional heart of every cell. Enzymes, the most catalytically efficient machines known, are proteins; the immune system's specificity relies on protein antibodies; development, metabolism, transport, and signal transduction all depend on proteins. Protein misfolding is implicated in neurodegenerative diseases (Alzheimer's, Parkinson's, prion diseases), making protein folding a frontier of medicine. Advances in protein engineering (directed evolution, CRISPR-based design) and structure prediction (AlphaFold) are opening possibilities for custom-designed proteins as drugs, therapeutics, and industrial catalysts. Understanding protein structure and function is foundational to molecular biology, biochemistry, and modern medicine.
