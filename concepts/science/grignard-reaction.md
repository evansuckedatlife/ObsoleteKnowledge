# Grignard Reaction

## summary

The Grignard reaction is a fundamental organometallic chemical reaction in which alkyl, allyl, vinyl, or aryl-magnesium halides (known as Grignard reagents) react as nucleophiles with carbon electrophiles, most commonly carbonyl compounds. This reaction is one of the most powerful methods for creating carbon-carbon bonds, converting aldehydes and ketones into secondary and tertiary alcohols, respectively. Discovered by Victor Grignard, who was awarded the 1912 Nobel Prize in Chemistry, it is characterized by a polar covalent carbon-magnesium bond that reverses the typical electrophilic nature of carbon. Because Grignard reagents are highly basic and nucleophilic, the reaction must be performed under strictly anhydrous conditions in ether solvents to prevent immediate protonation and decomposition.

## you gotta know

### reagent preparation & properties

- *Synthesized by reacting an organic halide* (such as bromobenzene or iodomethane) with magnesium metal in an anhydrous ether solvent, typically diethyl ether or tetrahydrofuran (THF).
- *Involves the insertion of magnesium* into the carbon-halogen bond, reversing the carbon's polarization (umpolung) to make it highly nucleophilic and basic.
- *Requires strictly anhydrous conditions* because Grignard reagents react violently with proton donors (including water, alcohols, and carboxylic acids) to form simple alkanes and magnesium salts.
- *Exhibits the Schlenk equilibrium*, a complex coordination dynamic in solution where the organomagnesium halide exists in equilibrium with dialkylmagnesium and magnesium halide species.

### nucleophilic addition & carbon-carbon bond formation

- *Reacts with formaldehyde* to yield primary alcohols after acid workup, extending the carbon chain by one carbon atom.
- *Reacts with other aldehydes* to synthesize secondary alcohols and with *ketones* to yield tertiary alcohols.
- *Reacts with esters or acid chlorides* in a two-equivalent addition, where the nucleophile attacks twice to produce a tertiary alcohol with two identical groups.
- *Reacts with carbon dioxide* (often dry ice) to produce carboxylic acids after acidic workup, a carboxylation process that adds a carboxyl group to the chain.
- *Reacts with epoxides* by attacking the less substituted carbon, opening the ring to yield a primary alcohol extended by two carbons.

## connections

- [[organic-halide]] — Grignard reagents are prepared directly from organic halides.
- [[alcohol]] — the primary product of Grignard additions to carbonyl compounds is an alcohol.

- [[aldehyde]] — aldehydes react with Grignard reagents to produce secondary alcohols.
- [[carboxylic-acid]] — Grignard reagents react with carbon dioxide to synthesize carboxylic acids; also, protonation by carboxylic acids immediately destroys the reagent.

## context

The Grignard reaction is the premier carbon-carbon bond-forming method in synthetic organic chemistry, immortalized by Victor Grignard's 1912 Nobel Prize. It exemplifies the concept of "umpolung" (polarity inversion), changing an electrophilic carbon in an alkyl halide to a nucleophilic one. In quiz bowl, the reaction is clued via its namesake reagents, the requirement of anhydrous ether (diethyl ether or THF), the Schlenk equilibrium, reactions with aldehydes/ketones/esters/CO2/epoxides, and its susceptibility to proton-donating impurities.
