## summary

*Quantum chromodynamics* (QCD) is the quantum field theory describing the *strong nuclear force*, the interaction that binds quarks together via exchange of massless particles called *gluons*. Like all modern particle physics, it is built on local gauge symmetry—in this case the *SU*(3) colour symmetry, which endows quarks with a property called *colour charge* (unrelated to visible colour, a whimsical naming choice). QCD is a cornerstone of the Standard Model and explains the structure of hadrons (protons, neutrons, and hundreds of other particles composed of quarks).

## you gotta know

- Quarks carry *colour charge*: red, green, or blue (and their corresponding anticolours for antiquarks). Gluons carry colour and anticolour, allowing them to interact with both quarks and each other.
- The strong force increases in strength as quarks move apart—a phenomenon called *asymptotic freedom*, the opposite of electromagnetism. At very high energies (short distances), quarks are nearly free; at large distances, the force becomes so strong quarks cannot be isolated—this is *confinement*.
- *Confinement* means quarks are always bound inside colour-neutral hadrons; a free quark has never been observed. A hadron with net colour charge would require infinite energy, making it impossible.
- QCD explains the abundance and properties of hadrons: a *meson* is a quark-antiquark pair; a *baryon* is three quarks (or three antiquarks). Heavier quarks (charm, bottom, top) lead to exotic hadrons with distinct decay signatures.
- *Running coupling*: in QCD, the effective strength of the force varies with energy scale. At low energies, interactions are strong and perturbative calculations fail; at high energies, the force weakens, allowing precise predictions via Feynman diagrams.
