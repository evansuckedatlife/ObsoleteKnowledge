# Non-vascular plants

## summary

**Non-vascular plants** are plants that lack *xylem* and *phloem* — the specialized conducting tissues that characterize vascular plants. The group comprises *bryophytes* (mosses, liverworts, and hornworts) and represents the most ancient lineage of land plants, predating vascular plants by tens of millions of years.

## you gotta know

- Lack *xylem* and *phloem*; water and nutrients diffuse directly through the plant body rather than being transported through specialized tubes.
- Small in stature (typically <10 cm tall) because they cannot support themselves against gravity without vascular tissue or conduct water upward to distant tissues.
- Lack true roots; instead use *rhizoids* (hair-like anchoring structures) that do not effectively absorb water. Water uptake is passive diffusion through the plant surface.
- Predominantly *bryophytes* — mosses (most familiar), liverworts (often flat and ribbon-like), and hornworts (sporophyte resembles a horn).
- Reproduce via *spores* released from a *sporangium*, not seeds; require *free water* for *flagellate sperm* to swim to the egg, limiting them to damp habitats.
- The *gametophyte* (haploid, spore-producing generation) is the dominant, visible form; the *sporophyte* is small and dependent on the gametophyte.
