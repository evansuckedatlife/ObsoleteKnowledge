## summary

An amine is a functional group and class of organic compounds containing a basic nitrogen atom with a lone pair, structurally derived from ammonia by replacing one or more hydrogen atoms with alkyl or aryl groups. Like ammonia, amines are weak bases and nucleophiles, playing a vital role in biochemistry as components of amino acids, neurotransmitters (like dopamine), and alkaloids.

## you gotta know

- *Classified as primary (1°), secondary (2°), tertiary (3°), or quaternary (4°)* depending on the number of carbon groups bonded directly to the nitrogen atom, with quaternary ammonium ions carrying a permanent positive charge.
- *Associated with intense, foul odors* resembling rotting fish or decaying flesh, as exemplified by the simple diamines putrescine and cadaverine.
- *Synthesized cleanly via the Gabriel synthesis*, which uses potassium phthalimide and an alkyl halide to produce primary amines while avoiding the over-alkylation common in direct amine alkylation.
- *Undergoes Hofmann elimination*, where an amine is treated with excess methyl iodide and silver oxide, followed by heating, to yield the least substituted alkene.
- *Produced via the Hofmann rearrangement*, which degrades a primary amide to an amine with one fewer carbon atom using bromine and a strong base.

## connections

- [[nitrogen]] — the lone pair of electrons on the nitrogen atom determines both the basicity and nucleophilicity of the amine.
- [[amide]] — condensation of an amine with a carboxylic acid or its derivatives yields an amide.
- [[amino-acids]] — organic molecules that serve as the monomers of proteins, characterized by having both an amine group and a carboxylic acid group.
