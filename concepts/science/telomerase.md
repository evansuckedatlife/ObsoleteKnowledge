# Telomerase

## summary

Telomerase is a specialized reverse transcriptase that extends chromosome ends (*telomeres*) by repeatedly adding tandem DNA repeats (TTAGGG in humans) using an internal RNA template. It solves the end-replication problem—the inability of DNA polymerase to fully replicate chromosome termini—and is essential for unlimited cell division in germ cells and some somatic stem cells.

## you gotta know

- Catalyzes: addition of 6 bp repeats (TTAGGG in vertebrates) to chromosome 3' ends
- Structure: ribonucleoprotein complex; protein component (TERT) plus RNA template (TERC/TR)
- Mechanism: *repeating addition processivity*—enzyme works like a molecular stamp, synthesizing repeats without moving along the chromosome
- Template: RNA component carries 3' CAAUCCCAAUC 5' sequence, serving as the template for reverse transcription
- Activity: high in germ cells, stem cells, and cancer cells; low/absent in most somatic cells
- Senescence: telomere shortening by ~50–200 bp per cell division acts as a replicative clock; cells enter senescence after ~50–70 divisions (Hayflick limit)
- Cancer connection: ~85–90% of cancers reactivate telomerase or use ALT (alternative lengthening of telomeres)

## context

The end-replication problem arises because DNA polymerase cannot fully replicate the 5' end of the lagging strand after primer removal, causing progressive shortening with each division. Telomerase evolved to reset this clock, allowing certain cell types to divide indefinitely. Most somatic cells have low or no telomerase activity, creating a built-in replicative limit that protects against unlimited growth—a tumor-suppressing mechanism. The discovery of telomerase in 1985 by Greider and Blackburn was groundbreaking; it revealed how cells could overcome replicative senescence and explained both normal aging and cancer immortalization.
