# Chloroplast

## summary

Chloroplasts are large, membrane-bound organelles found in plant and algal cells that convert light energy into chemical energy through photosynthesis. They contain the green pigment *chlorophyll* and stacked membrane structures called *thylakoids* where light reactions occur, plus a fluid-filled *stroma* where the Calvin cycle fixes carbon dioxide. Like mitochondria, chloroplasts contain their own DNA and ribosomes, evidence of their bacterial ancestry.

## you gotta know

- **Double-membrane envelope**: outer and inner membranes enclose the stroma
- **Thylakoid membrane system**: site of light-dependent reactions; organized into stacks called *grana*
- **Stroma**: inner compartment containing Calvin cycle enzymes and starch granules
- **Chlorophyll**: absorbs light energy; located in thylakoid membranes
- **Photosystems I and II**: protein complexes that split water, generate ATP, and produce NADPH
- **Own DNA and ribosomes**: chloroplast DNA encodes essential photosynthetic proteins; prokaryotic-like ribosomes
- **Endosymbiotic origin**: evolved from cyanobacteria engulfed by early eukaryotic cells

## context

Chloroplasts represent the primary engine of photosynthesis on Earth, responsible for converting solar energy into organic compounds that feed most life. Their evolution—via endosymbiosis of cyanobacteria—enabled plants and algae to thrive in diverse environments and eventually colonize terrestrial ecosystems. The light reactions in thylakoids generate the ATP and NADPH that power the Calvin cycle, a process that has remained fundamentally unchanged for billions of years. Chloroplast dysfunction affects crop yields and plays a role in plant stress responses to drought and temperature extremes.
