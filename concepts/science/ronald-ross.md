# Ronald Ross

## summary

British physician and parasitologist who identified the *Plasmodium* parasite as the causative agent of *malaria* and demonstrated its transmission via the *Anopheles* mosquito. His work in the 1890s proved the first example of disease transmission by an arthropod vector, opening the field of vector-borne epidemiology. He won the Nobel Prize in 1902.

## you gotta know

- Discovered the *Plasmodium* parasite in the mosquito's stomach (1897), establishing the mosquito-to-human transmission cycle for *malaria*.
- British Army surgeon who conducted much of his early work in India, observing disease patterns among troops.
- Proved mathematically that disease could be eliminated by reducing mosquito populations below a critical threshold — foundational to modern epidemiology.
- His work validated the germ theory and shifted public health from sanitation alone toward vector control as a disease prevention strategy.
- Coined the term "host" and "vector" in parasitology; established the discipline's modern vocabulary.
- Wrote extensively on poetry and the philosophy of disease; saw medicine as both science and art.

## connections

- [[ivan-pavlov]] — contemporary Nobel laureate; both worked on animal physiology and behaviour.
- [[robert-koch]] — pioneered bacteriology; Ross's parasitology extended the germ theory to multi-organism disease cycles.
- [[alexander-fleming]] — later antimicrobial work built on the foundation of pathogen identification Ross established.
