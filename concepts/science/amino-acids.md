## summary

*Amino acids* are organic molecules that serve as the building blocks of proteins. Each amino acid contains an amino group (−NH₂), a carboxyl group (−COOH), and a distinctive side chain (R group), all bonded to a central carbon atom. Twenty standard amino acids are incorporated into proteins during translation; their diverse chemical properties — ranging from hydrophobic to polar to charged — determine the structure and function of the resulting protein.

## you gotta know

### Basic Structure and Chemistry
- Every amino acid has the same backbone: an amino group (−NH₂), a carboxyl group (−COOH), and a hydrogen atom, all bonded to a central *alpha carbon*.
- The R group (side chain) attached to the alpha carbon is what distinguishes one amino acid from another and determines its chemical properties.
- Amino acids link via *peptide bonds*: the carboxyl group of one amino acid reacts with the amino group of the next, releasing a water molecule and forming a −CO−NH− bond.
- The resulting polymer is a *polypeptide* or *protein*; the order of amino acids (the *sequence*) is crucial and determines the protein's function.

### The Twenty Standard Amino Acids — Grouped by Property

**Hydrophobic (Nonpolar)**
- *Alanine, Valine, Leucine, Isoleucine*: small to medium, used to form hydrophobic cores in protein interiors.
- *Phenylalanine, Tryptophan, Proline*: aromatic or special; phenylalanine and tryptophan can absorb UV light; proline restricts the backbone conformation.
- *Methionine*: contains sulfur; also the start amino acid (as formyl-methionine in prokaryotes, methionine in eukaryotes).

**Polar Uncharged**
- *Serine, Threonine, Cysteine*: contain hydroxyl (−OH) or thiol (−SH) groups; involved in hydrogen bonding and often sites of post-translational modification.
- *Asparagine, Glutamine*: amide side chains; polar and involved in hydrogen bonding.
- *Glycine*: has only a hydrogen as its R group; the smallest amino acid, offering flexibility in tight turns and loops.

**Charged Positive**
- *Lysine, Arginine*: basic amino acids; positively charged at physiological pH; involved in salt bridges and DNA/RNA binding.
- *Histidine*: weakly basic; can be protonated or deprotonated near physiological pH; important in enzyme active sites and metal coordination.

**Charged Negative**
- *Aspartate, Glutamate*: acidic amino acids; negatively charged at physiological pH; involved in salt bridges and often found on protein surfaces.

### Significance and Discovery
- The *genetic code* specifies which amino acid should be incorporated at each position; sixty-four codons encode the twenty amino acids (degenerate code).
- *tRNA molecules* are charged with amino acids by enzymes (*aminoacyl-tRNA synthetases*); each tRNA carries a specific amino acid and recognizes the appropriate codon.
- *Chirality*: amino acids (except glycine) have a chiral center at the alpha carbon; proteins use only left-handed (*L*) amino acids, while bacteria and some other organisms use right-handed (*D*) amino acids in cell walls.

### Non-Standard Amino Acids
- About 20 additional amino acids occur in proteins through post-translational modification: *hydroxyproline* (found in collagen), *hydroxylysine*, *phosphoserine*, etc.
- Some organisms and engineered systems incorporate *selenocysteine* and *pyrrolysine* as the 21st and 22nd amino acids, expanding the genetic code.

## context

Amino acids are the alphabet of protein biochemistry. The discovery that proteins are built from a standard set of amino acids, and that the genetic code specifies which amino acid goes where, unified molecular biology in the 1960s. The diversity of the 20 amino acids — in size, charge, and hydrophobicity — allows cells to construct proteins with astonishing variety: rigid structural proteins, flexible fibrous proteins, globular enzymes with exquisite specificity. The fact that the genetic code is degenerate (multiple codons for the same amino acid) provides robustness against mutations; synonymous mutations often don't change the protein. However, nonsynonymous mutations (changing one amino acid to another) can have profound effects on protein folding and function, driving disease and evolution. Amino acids also have roles beyond protein synthesis: they serve as precursors for neurotransmitters, hormones, and other signaling molecules. Understanding amino acids is essential to biochemistry, molecular biology, and medicine.
