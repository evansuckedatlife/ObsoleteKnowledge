# Spin

## summary

Spin is an intrinsic form of *angular momentum* carried by elementary particles—electrons, photons, quarks—that has no classical analog. Spin is quantized: for electrons, the spin quantum number is s = 1/2, meaning the component along any axis is ±ℏ/2. Spin is not rotation of a physical object but a purely quantum property encoded in the [[wavefunction]].

## you gotta know

- Electrons are spin-1/2 particles; photons are spin-1; other particles have other spin values
- Measurement of spin along any axis yields one of two outcomes: "spin-up" (+ℏ/2) or "spin-down" (−ℏ/2) for electrons
- The spin operator S satisfies the angular momentum commutation relations: [Sₓ, Sᵧ] = iℏSᵤ
- A single electron spin state is a [[superposition]]: |spin⟩ = a|↑⟩ + b|↓⟩ (normalized)
- Stern-Gerlach experiments demonstrate discrete spin outcomes
- Spin couples to magnetic fields: energy μ·B, where μ is magnetic moment
- [[pauli-exclusion-principle]] forbids two electrons in the same orbital from having the same spin

## context

Spin emerged from relativistic quantum mechanics (Dirac's equation) and cannot be understood as literal rotation of an extended object—even a "point" electron would need to rotate faster than light. Instead, spin is a degree of freedom woven into the very structure of the electron's [[wavefunction]]. In multi-electron atoms, spin determines whether electrons pair up in orbitals (opposite spins) or spread across different orbitals. Spin-orbit coupling makes spin depend on orbital angular momentum, breaking degeneracies and creating fine structure in atomic spectra.
