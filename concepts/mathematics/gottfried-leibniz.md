# Gottfried Leibniz

## summary

**Gottfried Leibniz** (1646–1716) was a German polymath—mathematician, philosopher, physicist, logician, and diplomat—who independently developed calculus and created the notation (d/dx, ∫) still used today. Beyond calculus, he founded symbolic logic, explored the concept of *monads*, and worked across physics, theology, and metaphysics with equal brilliance. His superior notation and broader vision eventually overshadowed the calculus priority dispute with Newton, making him the intellectual ancestor of modern mathematical logic and notation.

## you gotta know

### Calculus
- Leibniz developed *calculus* independently of Newton, using *infinitesimals* and the quotient of differentials (dy/dx) as a true fraction.
- His notation—*d* for differential, ∫ for integral—is so intuitive that it displaced Newton's *fluxion* notation by the 18th century.
- He introduced the *chain rule* and understood *integration* as the inverse of *differentiation*, founding the fundamental theorem.

### Logic & Philosophy
- Pioneered *symbolic logic*, trying to create an *ars combinatoria*—a universal language for all knowledge using symbols and rules.
- The *principle of indiscernibles* states that if two things have identical properties, they are the same thing.
- His *principle of sufficient reason* asserts that every fact must have a reason or cause—an attempt to ground rational thought.

### Physics & Metaphysics
- Developed the concept of *monads*—indivisible, immaterial units of reality, anticipating aspects of quantum mechanics.
- Contributed to the science of *dynamics*—how force and motion relate—competing with Cartesian mechanics.

## context

Leibniz's legacy split into two streams. In mathematics, his superior notation and integration of calculus into a systematic discipline won out over Newton's approach; every physicist and engineer uses Leibniz's ∫ and d/dx notation. In philosophy, his metaphysical system (monadology, pre-established harmony) was less influential, superseded by Kant and later thinkers. Yet his dream of a symbolic logic—a universal language for reasoning—was realised 200 years later in the 20th century with formal logic and computer science. He stands as the last true Renaissance man: equally at home in mathematics, theology, diplomacy, and metaphysics, with contributions to each that shaped the future.
