# Calculus

## summary

**Calculus** is the mathematical study of continuous change—the twin disciplines of *differential* and *integral* calculus. Differential calculus concerns rates of change (derivatives), while integral calculus concerns accumulation and area under curves (integrals). Developed independently by Isaac Newton and Gottfried Leibniz in the late 17th century, calculus became the language of physics, engineering, and quantitative analysis. It enabled precise understanding of motion, forces, and growth, transforming natural philosophy into mathematical science.

## you gotta know

### Foundations & Core Concepts
- **Limits** underpin the rigorous definition of both derivatives and integrals—the value a function approaches as input approaches a point.
- **Derivatives** measure the instantaneous rate of change of a function; geometrically, they represent the slope of a tangent line to a curve.
- **Integrals** represent the accumulation of infinitesimal quantities; geometrically, they compute area under a curve and volume of solids.
- The **fundamental theorem of calculus** states that differentiation and integration are inverse operations, linking the two halves of calculus.

### Differential Calculus
- The **power rule**: the derivative of x^n is n·x^(n-1), simplifying polynomial differentiation.
- The **chain rule**: the derivative of a composite function f(g(x)) is f'(g(x))·g'(x)—essential for nested functions.
- The **product rule** and **quotient rule** extend derivative rules to products and ratios of functions.
- **Critical points** (where derivative = 0) reveal maxima, minima, and inflection points; crucial for optimization.

### Integral Calculus
- **Antiderivatives** are functions whose derivatives yield the original function; computing antiderivatives is integration.
- **Riemann sums** approximate area by summing rectangles; taking the limit as rectangles shrink yields the definite integral.
- **Integration by parts** is a technique analogous to the product rule, useful for integrating products of functions.
- **Substitution** (u-substitution) reverses the chain rule, simplifying integrals by change of variables.

### Advanced Topics
- **Multivariable calculus** extends derivatives and integrals to functions of multiple variables (partial derivatives, double/triple integrals).
- **Taylor series** represent functions as infinite power series around a point, enabling approximation and analysis of transcendental functions.
- **Differential equations** use derivatives to model rates of change in physical systems (population growth, heat flow, motion).

## context

Calculus emerged from two urgent problems: finding instantaneous rates of change (Newton's physics needed velocities and accelerations) and computing areas and volumes that earlier geometry could not handle. Newton's *method of fluxions* and Leibniz's differential notation arrived nearly simultaneously, but Leibniz's superior notation and elegant framework eventually dominated. By the 18th century, calculus became the primary tool for natural philosophy—the study of motion, gravity, light, heat, and every continuous phenomenon. The rigorous foundation (via limits and real analysis) came later, in the 19th century, when Cauchy, Weierstrass, and others formalised what Newton and Leibniz had intuited. Calculus remains the cornerstone of physics, engineering, statistics, economics, and data science; no quantitative field escapes it.
