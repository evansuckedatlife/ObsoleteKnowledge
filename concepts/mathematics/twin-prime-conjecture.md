## summary

The twin prime conjecture asserts that there are infinitely many pairs of primes that differ by exactly 2—such as (3, 5), (5, 7), (11, 13), and (29, 31). Despite its simplicity of statement, this conjecture remains one of the oldest and most tantalizing open problems in number theory, with no proof or disproof yet found.

## you gotta know

- Two primes p and p+2 form a "twin prime pair"; examples include (41, 43), (71, 73), (101, 103).
- The conjecture claims infinitely many such pairs exist, yet how many have actually been found is unclear for astronomically large ranges.
- Extensive computational searches have verified the conjecture for primes up to incredibly large magnitudes (trillions), but this is not a proof.
- The conjecture is related to questions about prime gaps—the distances between consecutive primes—and how often small gaps occur.
- Proving or disproving it would have implications for the distribution of primes and may require deep insights into analytic number theory.

## connections

- [[prime-number-theorem]] — concerns the overall density of primes; twin primes ask about frequent small gaps.
- [[goldbach-conjecture]] — another famous conjecture about additive properties of primes.
- [[infinitude-of-primes]] — asks whether infinitely many primes exist; this conjecture asks about dense pairs.
- [[riemann-hypothesis]] — any resolution of the twin prime conjecture may depend on advances toward the Riemann hypothesis.
