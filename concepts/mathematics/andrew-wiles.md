# Andrew Wiles

## summary

**Andrew Wiles** (born 1953) is a British mathematician best known for proving *Fermat's Last Theorem* in 1995, ending a 358-year quest. His proof, announced in 1993 and corrected in 1995, employed modern machinery: elliptic curves, modular forms, and the Taniyama-Shimura conjecture. Wiles' solution exemplifies how solving old problems often requires new conceptual frameworks, and how persistence—he pursued the theorem for decades—can lead to revolutionary insights spanning fields.

## you gotta know

### Fermat's Last Theorem
- Fermat claimed (in 1637) that no three positive integers satisfy a^n + b^n = c^n for any n > 2; the claim stood unproven for 358 years.
- Wiles proved it in 1993 (with a subtle error corrected in 1995) by showing it follows from the Taniyama-Shimura conjecture (now theorem).
- His proof uses *elliptic curves* and *modular forms*, connecting number theory to complex analysis in unexpected ways.

### Technical Contributions
- Developed deep results in *deformation theory* of Galois representations—the machinery needed to link elliptic curves and modular forms.
- His work has applications beyond Fermat: understanding *Diophantine equations*, arithmetic geometry, and the structure of numbers.

### Path & Perseverance
- He became obsessed with Fermat's theorem as a child after reading a popular book; he pursued it for over 30 years.
- His isolated work on the problem, away from the mainstream, proved fruitful—he avoided the conventional wisdom that the theorem was intractable.

## context

Wiles' proof of Fermat's Last Theorem is one of the 20th century's greatest mathematical achievements, yet it illustrates a broader truth: old, isolated problems often yield to new frameworks and cross-disciplinary insight. The Taniyama-Shimura conjecture (linking elliptic curves to modular forms) seemed abstract and distant from Fermat until Wiles saw the connection. His work energised arithmetic geometry and inspired a generation of mathematicians. The proof's announcement in 1993 and subsequent correction in 1995 also revealed the human side of mathematics: even great mathematicians make errors, and verification is essential. Wiles stands as a bridge between classical number theory (Fermat, Dirichlet, Kummer) and modern arithmetic geometry, showing how the old and new are woven together.
