# Spiral

## summary

A *spiral* is a curve that winds around a central point or axis, progressively getting closer to (or farther from) the center. Spirals appear ubiquitously in nature and mathematics, from nautilus shells and galaxy arms to the solutions of differential equations in physics.

## you gotta know

- The *Archimedean spiral* (the simplest type) has evenly-spaced turns; polar equation: `r = aθ`, where `a` is a constant.
- The *logarithmic spiral* expands at a constant exponential rate; polar equation: `r = ae^(bθ)`. It appears in biology, from ammonite shells to hawk flight paths.
- The *Fermat spiral* (parabolic spiral) has equation `r² = a²θ`; intermediate in behavior between Archimedean and logarithmic.
- A spiral is not closed: it passes through infinitely many points and cannot return to its starting location without crossing itself.
- The *Golden Spiral*—an approximation to the logarithmic spiral using Fibonacci rectangles—appears in art, design, and nature with surprising frequency.
- Spirals appear in *curl* dynamics: water draining from a bath, tornadoes, and rotating astrophysical objects.
- Archimedes himself studied the spiral bearing his name; it was used in ancient Greek technology for pumping water and grinding grain.

## connections

- [[archimedes]] — discovered and named the Archimedean spiral; studied its properties.
- [[cycloid]] — another rolling curve; contrasts with spirals in structure.
- [[catenary]] — another transcendental curve; different generating principle (gravity vs. angular growth).
- [[fibonacci-sequence]] — the golden spiral emerges from Fibonacci rectangles.
- [[lemniscate]] — another algebraic curve with elegant properties.
