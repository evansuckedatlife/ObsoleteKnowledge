# Carl Friedrich Gauss

## summary

**Carl Friedrich Gauss** (1777–1855) was a German mathematician and astronomer whose contributions span number theory, statistics, algebra, analysis, and geometry. Often called "the Prince of Mathematicians," Gauss proved the *Fundamental Theorem of Algebra*, developed the method of *least squares*, and made pioneering work in non-Euclidean geometry, magnetism, and astronomy. His extraordinary breadth and the elegance of his proofs set him apart even among giants; many of his most important results were published only after his death, hidden in notebooks.

## you gotta know

### Number Theory & Algebra
- The *Fundamental Theorem of Algebra* states every polynomial of degree n has exactly n roots (counting multiplicity, in the complex numbers).
- *Gaussian integers* (complex numbers a + bi where a and b are integers) have unique factorisation, extending prime factorisation into the complex plane.
- His *Disquisitiones Arithmeticae* (1801) is the foundational modern text on number theory, introducing modular arithmetic and the study of quadratic residues.

### Statistics & Astronomy
- The *normal distribution* (bell curve) is also called the *Gaussian distribution*; the method of *least squares* for fitting data became standard in science.
- He computed the orbit of the asteroid *Ceres* using incomplete observations, demonstrating the power of least squares in prediction.

### Geometry & Analysis
- Gauss developed the *Gaussian curvature* (intrinsic curvature of a surface), realising that space need not be Euclidean—a step toward non-Euclidean geometry.
- The *Divergence Theorem* (or *Gauss's Theorem*) relates the flux of a vector field through a closed surface to the divergence inside.

## context

Gauss was a colossus of 19th-century mathematics: his work in number theory reframed the discipline, his statistical methods became indispensable in all empirical science, and his geometric intuition presaged Einstein's curved spacetime. Yet he published conservatively, obsessed with perfection; many discoveries sat in his private notebooks for decades, their significance unrecognised until posthumously. This secrecy likely slowed some fields (e.g., non-Euclidean geometry might have developed faster if his private work had been public), but it also meant that whenever his work finally appeared, it was pristine and influential. His legacy is woven into the fabric of modern science: the bell curve in statistics, the normal equations in machine learning, the curvature tensor in relativity—all bear his mark.
