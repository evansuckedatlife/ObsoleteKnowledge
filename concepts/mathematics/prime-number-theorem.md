# Prime Number Theorem

## summary

The prime number theorem describes the asymptotic distribution of primes: the number of primes less than or equal to x approaches x divided by the natural logarithm of x as x becomes arbitrarily large. Proved independently by *Hadamard* and *de la Vallée-Poussin* in 1896, it governs how primes thin out among the integers.

## you gotta know

- Denoted π(x) ~ x / ln(x), meaning the density of primes near x is approximately 1 / ln(x).
- Answers the question "how many primes exist below a given number?"—roughly 1 in every ln(x) integers near x is prime.
- The proof requires complex analysis and properties of the *Riemann zeta function*, making it non-trivial despite its elegant statement.
- Primes become sparser at scale (fewer primes per unit interval) but never cease, consistent with their infinitude.
- Modern refinements using the *Riemann hypothesis* (still unproven) would yield tighter bounds on prime gaps and distribution.
