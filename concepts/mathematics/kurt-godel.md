# Kurt Gödel

## summary

**Kurt Gödel** (1906–1978) was an Austrian-American logician and mathematician whose *Incompleteness Theorems* (1931) revealed fundamental limits to formal systems and shattered the dream of a complete, consistent axiomatisation of all mathematics. Working in logic and set theory, he also proved the *consistency of the continuum hypothesis* with the axioms of set theory, and contributed to relativity theory (discovering rotating universes consistent with Einstein's equations). His theorems reshaped mathematics and philosophy, showing that provability has intrinsic bounds.

## you gotta know

### Incompleteness Theorems
- *Gödel's First Incompleteness Theorem*: any consistent formal system powerful enough to express arithmetic is *incomplete*—there exist true statements that cannot be proven within that system.
- *Gödel's Second Incompleteness Theorem*: a consistent system cannot prove its own consistency (a corollary of the first).
- These theorems showed that Hilbert's dream—a complete, consistent, decidable axiomatisation of mathematics—is impossible.

### Logic & Set Theory
- Proved the *completeness* of first-order logic (every logically valid sentence is provable)—a result both hopeful and constraining.
- Showed the *continuum hypothesis* (that no set exists larger than ℕ but smaller than ℝ) is *consistent* with ZFC set theory (but not provably true or false).
- Invented *Gödel numbering*—encoding logical formulas as numbers so arguments about proofs become arithmetic propositions.

### Contributions to Relativity
- Discovered the *Gödel metric*, a solution to Einstein's field equations describing a rotating universe (conceptually simple but with strange causal properties).

## context

Gödel's theorems hit the mathematical world like a thunderbolt. The 19th and early 20th centuries had seen a dream take shape: reduce all of mathematics to logic, find the axioms, and prove everything from them. Hilbert's *Entscheidungsproblem* (decision problem) asked whether a procedure exists to determine if any statement is provable. Gödel and later Church and Turing answered: no. Mathematics is fundamentally incomplete and undecidable. This was not a gap to be filled but an intrinsic feature of formal systems. Yet Gödel also proved the completeness of first-order logic, showing that within limits, mathematical logic is sound. His work reshaped the philosophy of mathematics, leading to deeper questions about the nature of proof, truth, and computation—questions that remain central to logic and theoretical computer science today.
