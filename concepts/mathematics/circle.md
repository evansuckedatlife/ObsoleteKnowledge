## summary

A *circle* is the set of all points in a plane equidistant from a fixed point called the center. It is the most fundamental and symmetric of all geometric curves, serving as a cornerstone of geometry, trigonometry, and calculus. From the earliest compass-and-straightedge constructions of ancient Greece to the development of the unit circle in modern analysis, the circle has shaped mathematical thought for millennia. Its constant ratio of circumference to diameter defines the mathematical constant $\pi$, linking it to infinite series, probability, and complex analysis.

## you gotta know

### Algebraic and Geometric Formulas

- *Equations*: Defined in Cartesian coordinates by the equation `(x - h)² + (y - k)² = r²`, where `(h, k)` is the center and `r` is the radius, or in polar coordinates by `r = c`.
- *Circumference and Area*: The perimeter (circumference) is $2\pi r$, and the area is $\pi r^2$; the area formula was first rigorously proved by *Archimedes* using the method of exhaustion.
- *Pi ($\pi$)*: The transcendental constant $\pi \approx 3.14159$ is defined as the ratio of any circle's circumference to its diameter.

### Conic Sections and Geometrical Theorems

- *Conic Section Case*: Appears as a degenerate or limiting case of an *ellipse* when the two foci coincide at the center, resulting in an eccentricity of exactly 0.
- *Invariance*: Cut by a plane parallel to the base of a right circular cone.
- *Thales's Theorem*: States that if three points $A$, $B$, and $C$ lie on a circle where the line $AC$ is a diameter, then the angle $\angle ABC$ is a right angle.
- *Ptolemy's Theorem*: For a cyclic quadrilateral (a quadrilateral inscribed in a circle), the product of the diagonals is equal to the sum of the products of the opposite sides.

### Tensors and Transcribed Curves

- *Apollonian Circles*: Defined as the set of points whose distances to two fixed points are in a constant ratio; for non-equal ratios, this locus forms a circle.
- *Rolling Curves*: Generating curve for the *cycloid* (rolled along a flat line) and the *cardioid* (rolled around a fixed circle of equal radius).

## context

The circle is the ultimate symbol of symmetry and infinity in mathematics, representing the transition from discrete polygons to continuous curves. Its properties underlay the ancient Greek search for "squaring the circle" (proven impossible by Ferdinand von Lindemann in 1882) and guided the astronomical models of Ptolemy and Copernicus, who relied on circular epicycles to explain planetary motions. In quiz bowl, the circle recurs in questions detailing its equations, Thales's theorem, Ptolemy's theorem, the history of calculating $\pi$, and its role as the generator of roulette curves like cycloids and cardioids.
