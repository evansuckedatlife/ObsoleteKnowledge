## summary

**Archimedes** (c. 287–212 BCE) was an ancient Greek mathematician, physicist, and engineer whose work in geometry, hydrostatics, and mechanics anticipated modern calculus by nearly 2,000 years. Working in Syracuse (Sicily), he calculated areas and volumes using infinitesimal methods (*method of exhaustion*), formulated the principle of buoyancy, and designed ingenious mechanical devices and weapons. His combination of rigorous proof, practical engineering, and mathematical daring made him perhaps the greatest mathematician of antiquity.

## you gotta know

### Geometry & Infinitesimals
- He calculated the *area of a parabolic segment* by exhaustion—approximating it with inscribed and circumscribed polygons, a precursor to integration.
- The *method of exhaustion* proved the *area of a circle* equals the product of its radius and half its circumference (A = πr²), effectively computing π.
- He approximated *π* to remarkable precision (between 3 1/7 and 3 10/71), a figure not significantly improved until the 17th century.

### Hydrostatics & Mechanics
- *Archimedes' Principle*: a body immersed in fluid experiences an upward buoyant force equal to the weight of fluid displaced—foundational to hydrostatics.
- He calculated the volume of a *sphere* inscribed in a cylinder and proved it is 2/3 the volume of the cylinder.
- The *Archimedean screw*—a helical device for raising water—remains in use today; he may also have invented the *lever* and *pulley* systems.

### Mechanics & Weapons
- His *On Floating Bodies* systematically studied stability and equilibrium of floating vessels.
- His war machines (catapults, mirrors for firing ships) defended Syracuse during its siege by Rome.
- He reportedly said, "Give me a place to stand and I will move the Earth"—illustrating the power of leverage.

## context

Archimedes bridged the ancient and modern worlds. His *method of exhaustion* was rediscovered in the 17th century and directly influenced Leibniz and Newton's development of calculus—his infinitesimal reasoning was remarkably close to theirs, just expressed geometrically rather than algebraically. His *Palimpsest* (a medieval copy of his writings, later erased and overwritten, then rediscovered in the 20th century) revealed methods he had kept private, proving he thought in ways far ahead of his era. He represents the apex of ancient mathematics: no Greek mathematician surpassed him, and even today his ingenuity astounds. His death at the hands of Roman soldiers during the siege of Syracuse symbolises the end of the Greek mathematical tradition.
