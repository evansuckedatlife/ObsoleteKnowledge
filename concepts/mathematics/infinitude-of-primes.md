# Infinitude of Primes

## summary

The infinitude of primes states that there is no largest prime number — the sequence of primes continues forever. *Euclid* proved this around 300 BCE using a proof by contradiction, establishing one of the earliest and most elegant results in number theory.

## you gotta know

- *Euclid's proof* assumes a finite list of all primes, multiplies them together, adds 1, and shows the result cannot be divisible by any prime on the list—proving either a new prime exists or a gap in the list.
- There is no "last prime"; for any prime p, another prime exists that is larger.
- The theorem has inspired multiple independent proofs over centuries, including arguments by *Fermat*, *Euler*, and modern variants using the divergence of the harmonic series over primes.
- The distribution of primes becomes sparser as numbers grow larger, yet their infinite supply guarantees they never cease.
- This foundational result anchors higher number theory and explains why prime-finding remains computationally relevant even for astronomically large numbers.
