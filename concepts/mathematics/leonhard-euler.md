# Leonhard Euler

## summary

**Leonhard Euler** (1707–1783) was a Swiss mathematician and physicist whose prolific output—roughly one paper per week for 56 years—shaped modern mathematics more broadly than perhaps any single figure. He formalised notation (e, i, f(x), Σ), founded graph theory, advanced analysis and complex numbers, and wrote across mechanics, astronomy, and philosophy. Despite going blind late in life, his productivity never ceased, dictating papers to an assistant until his death.

## you gotta know

### Analysis & Calculus
- Defined the *exponential function* e^x rigorously and recognised *e* (≈2.71828) as a fundamental constant, the base of natural logarithms.
- *Euler's formula*: e^(ix) = cos(x) + i·sin(x)—a breathtaking bridge between exponentials, trigonometry, and complex numbers.
- The *Euler-Lagrange equations* are the cornerstone of variational calculus and underpin the Lagrangian formulation of mechanics.

### Notation & Logic
- Introduced *f(x)* notation for functions (superseding earlier verbose notation).
- Popularised the summation symbol Σ and the symbol for imaginary unit *i* = √(-1).
- His use of *e* for the exponential base became universal in mathematics and science.

### Graph Theory & Combinatorics
- Solved the *Seven Bridges of Königsberg* problem by abstracting it as a graph—born modern graph theory.
- *Euler characteristic* (V - E + F = 2 for polyhedra) relates vertices, edges, and faces; generalises to topological spaces.
- Work in combinatorics and partitions.

### Complex Analysis & Number Theory
- Rigorously studied *complex numbers*, treating them as a field with algebraic properties.
- Made discoveries in *prime numbers* and infinite series.

## context

Euler was the mathematician's mathematician—his fluency with symbolic manipulation, his intuition, and his sheer work ethic set a standard never quite matched. He lived through the Age of Enlightenment and embodied its ideals: universal knowledge, the power of reason, and optimism about humanity's progress. His life was marked by personal tragedy (bankruptcy, loss of sight) and geopolitical upheaval (the Napoleonic era), yet his curiosity never dimmed. His papers—many unpublished at his death—kept scholars busy for decades afterward. Modern mathematics still inherits his notation, his conceptual frameworks, and his willingness to push algebra and calculus into new territories. For students and researchers, Euler is a model of fearless exploration.
