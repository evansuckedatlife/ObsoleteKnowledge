# Limacon

## summary

A *limacon* (or *limaçon*, French for "snail") is a rose-like curve defined in polar coordinates, known for its variety of shapes depending on a parameter. The cardioid is a special limiting case; more generally, limacons display either a single loop or an inward dimple, appearing frequently in rose-pattern problems and mechanical curves.

## you gotta know

- Polar equation: `r = a + b cos θ` (or sine for rotated versions), where `a` and `b` are constants.
- The curve's shape depends on the ratio of `b` to `a`: when `b = a`, it becomes a *cardioid*; when `b < a`, the curve is convex; when `b > a`, the curve has an inner loop.
- The name "limacon" comes from the resemblance to a snail's shell and spiral motion.
- The limacon was first studied by *Blaise Pascal*, though full recognition of its properties came later with systematic polar coordinate analysis.
- The curve is closed and smooth except (in the loop case) at the origin; it has high aesthetic appeal and symmetry properties.
- Special cases: when `b = a/2`, the curve is a *dimpled* limacon with a smooth inward curve. When `b = 2a`, the loop becomes prominent.
- Limacons appear in *antenna design*, *optics*, and various mechanical transmission curves.

## connections

- [[pascal]] — first studied the limacon; it bears his name in some contexts.
- [[cardioid]] — the special case of the limacon when the parameter ratio equals 1.
- [[epicycloid]] — rolling-circle curves related geometrically to limacons.
- [[polar-curves]] — limacons are a canonical family in polar coordinate geometry.
- [[spiral]] — both are transcendental and rising-motion curves.
