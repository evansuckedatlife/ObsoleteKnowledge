# William Rowan Hamilton

## summary

**William Rowan Hamilton** (1805–1865) was an Irish mathematician and physicist who revolutionised mechanics through his *Hamiltonian formulation* and invented *quaternions*, an algebra extending complex numbers to four dimensions. His reformulation of classical mechanics—phrased in terms of a *Hamiltonian function* rather than forces—became the framework for quantum mechanics and remains central to modern physics. His work bridges classical mechanics, algebra, and optics.

## you gotta know

### Hamiltonian Mechanics
- Reformulated Newton's laws using a single scalar function, the *Hamiltonian* H (total energy), replacing Newton's force-centric description.
- *Hamilton's equations*: time evolution of a system is determined by the Hamiltonian and symplectic geometry, not F = ma directly.
- This framework generalises seamlessly to quantum mechanics, where the Hamiltonian operator governs the Schrödinger equation.

### Quaternions
- Invented *quaternions* (a, b, c, d)—a four-dimensional algebra extending complex numbers to an even more exotic system.
- Quaternions are non-commutative: qp ≠ pq, violating a fundamental assumption of ordinary algebra.
- They encode *3D rotations* elegantly, modern 3D graphics and robotics use quaternions routinely.

### Mechanics & Physics
- Advanced the *calculus of variations* and variational principles in mechanics.
- Contributed to *optics* and wave theory, applying his mathematical methods to light propagation.

## context

Hamilton's Hamiltonian formulation seemed like a mathematical reformulation—a different way to say the same physics. Yet it revealed hidden structure: the concept of *phase space* (position and momentum together) and *symplectic geometry*. A century later, when quantum mechanics emerged, these ideas proved indispensable—the Hamiltonian became the central object of quantum theory. His quaternions, initially viewed as an algebraic curiosity (even he acknowledged their strangeness), found unimagined applications in computer graphics, robotics, and aerospace. Hamilton's life was marked by struggles with alcohol and personal turmoil, yet his intellectual output was extraordinary. He remains a model of the pure mathematician whose abstract creations later find unexpected, practical utility.
