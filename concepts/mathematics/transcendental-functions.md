# Transcendental Function

## summary

A *transcendental function* is a function that does not satisfy any polynomial equation whose coefficients are themselves polynomials. In other words, it "transcends" algebra and cannot be expressed as a finite sequence of algebraic operations (addition, multiplication, division, and root extraction). Common examples include exponential, logarithmic, trigonometric, and hyperbolic functions, which are vital for modeling physical processes like growth, decay, and oscillation.

## you gotta know

- Definition: A function $f(x)$ is transcendental if it is algebraically independent of the variable $x$, meaning there is no non-zero polynomial $P(x, y)$ such that $P(x, f(x)) = 0$ for all $x$.
- The class of algebraic functions (which includes polynomials, rational functions, and radicals) stands in contrast to transcendental functions.
- *Hermite-Lindemann Theorem*: States that if $a$ is a non-zero algebraic number, then $e^a$ is a transcendental number; this implies that the natural exponential function maps algebraic inputs to transcendental outputs (except at $x=0$).
- Transcendental functions are typically defined via infinite series, integrals, or differential equations; for instance, $\ln(x) = \int_1^x \frac{1}{t} dt$.
- The term "transcendental" was introduced by *René Descartes* in 1637 and later refined by *Gottfried Leibniz*.
