# Sine curve

## summary

A *sine curve* (or sinusoid) is the geometric wave shape traced by the sine function, $y = \sin(x)$. It is a smooth, continuous, periodic wave that oscillates symmetrically between its peaks and troughs. Sine curves are foundational in trigonometry and physics, representing simple harmonic motion, sound waves, alternating current (AC) electricity, and electromagnetic radiation.

## you gotta know

- Standard mathematical equation: $y = A \sin(B(x - C)) + D$, where $A$ is the *amplitude*, $2\pi/B$ is the *period*, $C$ is the *phase shift*, and $D$ is the *vertical shift*.
- The basic curve $y = \sin(x)$ has a period of $2\pi$, amplitude of 1, and passes through the origin $(0, 0)$ with a slope of 1.
- Symmetries: The sine curve is an *odd function*, meaning it is symmetric under a $180^\circ$ rotation about the origin, satisfying $\sin(-x) = -\sin(x)$.
- In physics, any system undergoing *simple harmonic motion* (like an idealized pendulum or a mass on a spring) traces out a sine curve over time.
- *Fourier's Theorem* states that any periodic wave can be decomposed into a sum of sine curves of different frequencies and amplitudes.
- The derivative of a sine curve is a shifted version of itself (a cosine curve): $\frac{d}{dx} \sin(x) = \cos(x)$.
