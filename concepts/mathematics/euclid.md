# Euclid

## summary

**Euclid** (c. 300 BCE) was an ancient Greek mathematician who compiled and systematised geometry into *The Elements*, the most influential mathematical text in history. Working in Alexandria during the Ptolemaic dynasty, he distilled geometric knowledge into a hierarchy of axioms, definitions, and propositions, establishing a deductive framework that remained the gold standard for rigorous proof for 2,000 years. His work shaped not only mathematics but philosophy, education, and the very meaning of logical certainty.

## you gotta know

### The Elements
- *The Elements* contains 13 books, covering geometry (plane and solid), number theory, and ratios; it systematises all Greek mathematics known at his time.
- Each book builds from *axioms* (self-evident truths), *definitions* (key terms), and *postulates* (geometric assumptions); theorems follow by deductive proof.
- The *five postulates* of Euclidean geometry include the controversial "parallel postulate"—through a point not on a line, exactly one parallel line can be drawn.

### Key Results
- The *Pythagorean theorem* is proven in Book I as Proposition 47 (the converse also appears).
- He proved there are *infinitely many primes* using a clever proof by contradiction (adding 1 to the product of all primes).
- His construction of *Platonic solids* (five regular polyhedra) appears in Book XIII.

### Geometric Methods
- *Compass and straightedge* construction: Euclid's geometry is entirely constructive—showing how to build figures with these tools, no coordinates.
- The *greatest common divisor* algorithm (the *Euclidean algorithm*) is essentially Euclid's method for reducing fractions.

## context

Euclid's *Elements* was the primary geometry textbook for 2,000 years and remains a model of rigorous exposition. The idea that mathematical truth flows from a small set of axioms via logical deduction became the template for all rigorous science and mathematics. Ironically, the "self-evident" parallel postulate haunted mathematicians for centuries—every attempt to prove it from the other four postulates failed, eventually leading 19th-century mathematicians to ask, "What if the parallel postulate is false?" The answer opened non-Euclidean geometry and revolutionised our understanding of space. For most of history, though, "geometry" meant Euclid, and his name was synonymous with mathematical proof itself.
