# Cycloid

## summary

A *cycloid* is the curve traced by a point on the rim of a circle as it rolls along a straight line without slipping. It is one of the most celebrated curves in calculus and mechanics, famous for its elegant mathematical properties and its role in solving classical problems of motion and optimization.

## you gotta know

- Parametric equations: `x = r(t - sin t)`, `y = r(1 - cos t)`, where `r` is the radius of the rolling circle and `t` is the angle of rotation.
- The cycloid has *cusps*—sharp points—at regular intervals where the rolling circle touches the baseline; between cusps it forms smooth arches.
- The *arc length* of one arch equals `8r`, exactly eight times the radius—a beautiful exact result discovered in the 17th century.
- The cycloid is the *brachistochrone curve*: the path of fastest descent—a ball rolling down a cycloid reaches the bottom faster than rolling down any other curve.
- The *tautochrone* property: a ball takes the same time to reach the bottom of a cycloid regardless of where it starts on the curve.
- The area under one arch equals `3πr²`, or exactly three times the area of the generating circle.
- *Galileo* conjectured about the cycloid; *Pascal*, *Newton*, and *Leibniz* all studied it intensely, making it a proving ground for early calculus.

## connections

- [[cardioid]] — another rolling-circle curve, but with a smaller circle rolling on a larger one.
- [[catenary]] — another famous curve studied in the era of cycloid discovery.
- [[pascal]] — conducted rigorous studies of the cycloid's properties.
- [[leibniz]] — engaged in priority disputes over cycloid properties; his calculus notation was refined in such debates.
- [[newton]] — independently discovered cycloid properties as part of developing calculus.
