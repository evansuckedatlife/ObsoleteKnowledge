# Logarithmic Function

## summary

A *logarithmic function* is the inverse of an exponential function, written as `f(x) = logₐ(x)`, and answers "what exponent do I raise *a* to in order to get *x*?" Logarithms compress scales, convert multiplicative processes into additive ones, and are essential in science (pH, decibels, earthquake magnitude) and computer science (time complexity).

## you gotta know

- General form: `f(x) = logₐ(x)`, read "log base *a* of *x*"; means "*a* to what power equals *x*?"—i.e., if `aʸ = x`, then `y = logₐ(x)`.
- The natural logarithm uses base *e*: `ln(x) = logₑ(x)`; the common (base-10) logarithm is `log₁₀(x)` or `log(x)`.
- Domain is (0, ∞); range is all real numbers; the function is continuous and differentiable on its domain.
- Key properties: `logₐ(xy) = logₐ(x) + logₐ(y)` (product); `logₐ(x/y) = logₐ(x) − logₐ(y)` (quotient); `logₐ(xⁿ) = n·logₐ(x)` (power).
- Derivative: `d/dx ln(x) = 1/x`; `d/dx logₐ(x) = 1/(x·ln(a))`.
- The graph has a vertical asymptote at *x* = 0; for *a* > 1 it increases unboundedly, but very slowly.
