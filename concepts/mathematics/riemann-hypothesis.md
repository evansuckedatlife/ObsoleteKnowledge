# Riemann Hypothesis

## summary

The Riemann hypothesis is one of the most famous unsolved problems in mathematics, conjecturing that all non-trivial zeros of the *Riemann zeta function* lie on the critical line where the real part equals 1/2. Proposed by *Bernhard Riemann* in 1859, it carries deep implications for understanding the distribution of prime numbers and is one of the *Millennium Prize Problems*.

## you gotta know

- The *Riemann zeta function* ζ(s) has "trivial zeros" at negative even integers and non-trivial zeros in the complex plane; the hypothesis asserts all non-trivial zeros have real part = 1/2.
- If true, the hypothesis would provide the tightest possible bounds on prime gaps and refine estimates from the *prime number theorem*.
- Millions of zeros have been computationally verified to satisfy the hypothesis, but a general proof remains elusive.
- The hypothesis is equivalent to many other deep statements in mathematics, making it a hub connecting number theory, analysis, combinatorics, and physics.
- Its resolution carries a \$1 million *Millennium Prize*; many believe a proof will require new mathematical frameworks.
