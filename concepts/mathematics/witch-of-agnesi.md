# Witch of Agnesi

## summary

The *Witch of Agnesi* is a smooth, bell-shaped curve with a centuries-long history of study, originating in *Maria Agnesi*'s 18th-century calculus text. Despite its whimsical name (from a mistranslation of the Italian "versiera"), it remains important in probability, physics, and signal processing as the graph of the Lorentzian function.

## you gotta know

- Named after *Maria Agnesi*, who described it in her *Instituzioni analitiche* (1748); the name "witch" comes from a misrendition of the Italian word "versiera."
- The curve was actually studied earlier by *Newton* and other mathematicians; Agnesi's main contribution was a systematic geometric construction.
- Cartesian equation: `(x² + a²)y = a³`, or equivalently `y = a³/(x² + a²)`, where `a` is a parameter.
- The curve has a bell shape, symmetric about the vertical axis, with maximum height `a/4` at the center.
- The curve approaches the horizontal asymptote `y = 0` as `x → ±∞`, but never touches it except at infinity.
- The area under the entire curve equals `πa²`, exactly π times the square of the parameter.
- In physics and engineering, it appears as the *Lorentzian* or *Cauchy* distribution—a probability density crucial to spectroscopy, resonance phenomena, and quantum mechanics.
