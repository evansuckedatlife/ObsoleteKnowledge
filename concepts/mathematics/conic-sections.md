# Conic Sections

## summary

*Conic sections* are the curves produced when a plane intersects a double cone at various angles. The four fundamental types—*circle*, *ellipse*, *parabola*, and *hyperbola*—form the backbone of analytic geometry and appear constantly in physics, astronomy, and engineering.

## you gotta know

- The four types are determined by the angle at which the cutting plane meets the cone's surface.
- A *circle* results when the plane is perpendicular to the cone's axis.
- An *ellipse* results when the plane is tilted but does not intersect the cone's base.
- A *parabola* results when the plane is parallel to one of the cone's generators (the slanting lines on its surface).
- A *hyperbola* results when the plane cuts through both nappes (the two parts) of the double cone.
- All conic sections can be expressed in the general form `Ax² + Bxy + Cy² + Dx + Ey + F = 0`; the discriminant `B² - 4AC` determines the type.
- The ancient Greeks, especially *Apollonius*, classified conics; the Renaissance recovered and extended their study.

## connections

- [[circle]] — the zero-eccentricity conic section.
- [[ellipse]] — eccentricity between 0 and 1.
- [[parabola]] — eccentricity exactly 1; the transition between ellipse and hyperbola.
- [[hyperbola]] — eccentricity greater than 1.
- [[euclid]] — foundational geometer; his *Elements* treated conics as geometric objects.
- [[apollonius]] — systematically classified all conic sections.
