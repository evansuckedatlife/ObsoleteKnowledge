# Pierre de Fermat

## summary

**Pierre de Fermat** (1607–1665) was a French lawyer and mathematician whose work in number theory, calculus, and probability was revolutionary, yet he published almost nothing in his lifetime. His famous conjecture—later called *Fermat's Last Theorem*—remained unproven for 358 years until Andrew Wiles solved it in 1995. Fermat's simultaneous work with Descartes on analytic geometry and his correspondence with Pascal on probability theory seeded entire disciplines.

## you gotta know

### Number Theory
- *Fermat's Last Theorem* states that no three positive integers a, b, c satisfy a^n + b^n = c^n for any integer n > 2 (true for n=2 as Pythagorean triples, but false for n>2).
- *Fermat's Little Theorem* asserts that if p is prime and a is not divisible by p, then a^(p-1) ≡ 1 (mod p)—foundational to modular arithmetic and cryptography.
- He proved that every *prime of the form 4k+1* can be uniquely expressed as the sum of two squares.

### Calculus & Geometry
- Independently developed *analytic geometry* alongside Descartes, using coordinates to represent curves algebraically.
- His *method of adequality* anticipated *calculus*—he treated infinitesimals as quasi-zero quantities to find tangent lines and extrema.
- Applied calculus informally to *maxima and minima* problems (optimisation) decades before Newton and Leibniz formalised the field.

### Probability
- Correspondence with Pascal on the *problem of points* founded modern probability theory—how to divide the stakes in an interrupted game fairly.
- His probabilistic reasoning laid groundwork for *expected value* and the foundations of statistics.

## context

Fermat was a gentleman mathematician who published little, leading to priority disputes (e.g., analytic geometry with Descartes) and the loss of many proofs. Yet his influence was enormous—his scribbled notes in margins of classical texts (rediscovered later) sparked centuries of research. *Fermat's Last Theorem*, annotated as "I have a truly marvellous proof of this, which this margin is too narrow to contain," became the most famous unsolved problem in mathematics, captivating mathematicians for 358 years. Its eventual proof by Wiles in 1995 required modern machinery (elliptic curves, the Taniyama-Shimura conjecture) that would have amazed Fermat. His legacy bridges the ancient number-theoretic tradition (Euclid, Diophantus) and the modern era of rigorous proof.
