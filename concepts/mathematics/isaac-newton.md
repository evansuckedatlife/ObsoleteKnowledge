# Isaac Newton

## summary

**Isaac Newton** (1642–1727) was an English mathematician, physicist, and astronomer whose *Principia Mathematica* (1687) founded modern mechanics and gravitation theory. He independently developed calculus (alongside Leibniz), formulated the laws of motion and universal gravitation, and advanced the study of light and colour through prisms. His synthesis of mathematics and experimental natural philosophy created the template for scientific method and dominated intellectual life for two centuries.

## you gotta know

### Mathematics
- Newton developed *calculus* (the *method of fluxions*) to solve problems in physics; Leibniz developed it independently with better notation.
- The *binomial theorem* allows expanding (a+b)^n as a power series without multiplying it out directly.
- His *inverse square law* relates gravitational force to the product of masses divided by distance squared.

### Physics & Gravity
- The three *laws of motion* (inertia, F=ma, action-reaction) are the foundation of classical mechanics.
- *Universal gravitation* unified terrestrial and celestial mechanics—the same force that drops an apple also orbits the Moon.
- He argued gravity acts at a distance, instantaneously, without a mechanical medium—a concept critics attacked as "action at a distance."

### Optics & Other Work
- Demonstrated that *white light* is composed of a spectrum of colours (visible via a prism) and that each colour has a fixed refractivity.
- Invented the *reflecting telescope* (a mirror-based design) to avoid chromatic aberration in lens telescopes.
- His work in *alchemy* and theology was extensive (unpublished in his lifetime) but often eclipsed by his scientific legacy.

## context

Newton stands at the hinge of the Scientific Revolution—he synthesised the work of Kepler, Galileo, and Descartes, and created a mathematical framework so powerful that it shaped physics until Einstein. His insistence that mathematical laws govern nature, and that observation/experiment must validate them, became the gold standard for science. The calculus he developed (or Leibniz's notation of it) became the language of all subsequent physics, engineering, and quantitative science. His reputation was so towering that it sometimes hindered progress—18th-century physicists hesitated to challenge Newtonian gravity even when problems hinted at cracks—yet his methods remained vindicated for 200+ years.
