# Franz Boas

## summary

*Franz Boas* (1858–1942) was a German-born American anthropologist who fundamentally reshaped the discipline by championing rigorous fieldwork, cultural relativism, and the rejection of racial hierarchies. He established anthropology as a scientific study grounded in empirical evidence rather than armchair speculation, and mentored an entire generation of American anthropologists who became the discipline's foundation.

## you gotta know

- Rejected the biological determinism and racial hierarchies prevalent in 19th-century anthropology, demonstrating through skull measurements that physical variation within racial groups exceeded variation between them.
- Pioneered systematic ethnographic fieldwork and participant observation, spending years with the *Kwakwaka'wakw* peoples of the Pacific Northwest, setting the standard for modern anthropology.
- Advanced *cultural relativism* — the principle that cultures must be understood on their own terms rather than judged by the observer's standards — transforming how anthropologists approach non-Western societies.
- Founded the American anthropology program at Columbia University (1896), mentoring *Margaret Mead*, *Ruth Benedict*, *Alfred Kroeber*, and other foundational figures.
- Challenged *Social Darwinism* and polygenesism with data, contributing to mid-20th-century anti-racist scholarship and the UNESCO Statement on Race (1950).
- His "four-field" approach unified physical anthropology, cultural anthropology, archaeology, and linguistics under one discipline — the model still used in American universities today.
