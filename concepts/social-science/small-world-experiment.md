# The Small-World Experiment

## summary

*Stanley Milgram* conducted experiments in 1967 in which he asked people to send letters to a distant stranger by passing them only through acquaintances they knew by first name. The letters reached their destination in an average of five to six hops, suggesting that vast social networks are connected by surprisingly short chains—the origin of the "six degrees of separation" concept.

## you gotta know

- Milgram gave letters to residents of Kansas and Nebraska addressed to a stockbroker in Boston; each person could only pass the letter to someone they knew personally.
- About 64 letters were sent; roughly one-third completed the chain, reaching the target in an average of 5.5 steps.
- The intermediate nodes were often "funnels"—a small number of well-connected people through whom many chains passed—usually determined by geography or profession.
- Milgram theorized that social networks contain many short paths between any two people, making the world "small" relative to its size.
- Participants showed systematic bias: they chose intermediaries closer in geography and social status to the target, suggesting people have mental maps of network structure.
- The experiment revealed that people rely on heuristic guessing ("pass it to someone in Boston," "someone in finance") rather than knowing actual network paths.
- The "six degrees of separation" phrase (not Milgram's own) became a cultural meme, influencing network science, the "Kevin Bacon game," and later social media.
