## summary

*African American Vernacular English* (AAVE) is a variety of English spoken by many African Americans, arising from the complex linguistic history of African languages, English, and creolization during slavery. It is a systematic dialect with distinctive phonology, grammar, and vocabulary, though it has been historically stigmatized despite its linguistic validity and has emerged as an important marker of cultural identity.

## you gotta know

- Distinctive use of invariant *be* to mark habitual action: "He be studyin'" means "He studies regularly," distinct from "He is studyin'."
- Deletion of auxiliary verbs: "You going?" instead of "Are you going?" and "I'ma go" instead of "I'm going to go."
- Consonant cluster reduction, dropping final consonants, so *test* becomes *tes* and *cold* becomes *col*.
- Use of *ain't* as a general negative covering past and present: "I ain't seen it."
- Phonological features including reduction of vowel distinctions before *r*, so *Carol*, *coral*, and *barrel* may merge.
- Vigorous debate over whether AAVE descends primarily from West African languages, English dialects, or creolization — linguists recognize it as a fully systematic rule-governed variety.

## connections

- [[southern-american-english]] — shares regional roots and some overlapping phonological features from the shared history of the American South.
- [[general-american]] — the prestige American standard by which AAVE has been unfairly measured and stigmatized despite its grammatical coherence.
- [[creole-languages]] — possibly ancestral to AAVE through earlier creolization during and after slavery.
