# Margaret Mead

## summary

*Margaret Mead* (1901–1978) was an American cultural anthropologist who brought ethnography into the public consciousness through popular writing and media work. Her fieldwork in Samoa and Bali on adolescence, gender roles, and child-rearing became influential in mid-20th-century debates about nature vs. nurture and challenged Western assumptions about universal stages of development.

## you gotta know

- Published *Coming of Age in Samoa* (1928), arguing that adolescent conflict is culturally constructed, not biologically inevitable — a claim that influenced parenting and education but later faced methodological criticism.
- Conducted extensive fieldwork in the *Manus* (Papua New Guinea) and *Balinese* cultures, returning to the same communities decades later to document cultural change.
- Advocated for *visual anthropology*, making ethnographic films and photographs central to her practice and public presentations.
- Wrote for *The Nation* and other mainstream media, positioning anthropology as essential to understanding human variation and addressing social problems.
- Studied gender roles across cultures, arguing that occupations and personality traits assigned to men or women vary drastically and are learned rather than innate.
- Married archaeologist *Gregory Bateson*; their collaboration bridged anthropology and systems theory.

## connections

- [[franz-boas]] — her mentor at Columbia; she carried his fieldwork rigor into the Pacific.
- [[ruth-benedict]] — colleague and friend; they shared interests in psychological anthropology.
- [[gregory-bateson]] — her third husband; collaborated on Balinese research and family systems theory.
- [[bali]] — site of extended fieldwork on ritual, child development, and trance states.
- [[samoa]] — her first major fieldwork site and subject of *Coming of Age*.
