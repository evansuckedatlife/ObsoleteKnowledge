# Behaviorism

## summary

*Behaviorism* is a school of psychology asserting that observable behavior, not internal mental states, is the proper subject of scientific study. Dominant throughout much of the 20th century, behaviorism proposed that learning occurs through stimulus-response associations and reinforcement, minimizing the role of innate factors or conscious thought.

## you gotta know

- Founded in its modern form by *John B. Watson*, who argued that psychology must be the science of behavior, not consciousness.
- Central mechanism: *stimulus-response* learning—a stimulus triggers a response; if rewarded, the association strengthens; if punished, it weakens.
- *Ivan Pavlov's* experiments with dogs showed that neutral stimuli (like a bell) can become conditioned stimuli, evoking reflexive responses after pairing with an unconditioned stimulus (food).
- *B.F. Skinner* advanced *operant conditioning*—organisms repeat behaviors that are reinforced and avoid those that are punished, building complex repertoires through shaping.
- Radical form: all behavior, including language and thought, is shaped by environmental contingencies; mental concepts like "intention" are epiphenomenal.
- *Noam Chomsky* famously critiqued behaviorism's inability to account for language's complexity and the creativity of speech.
- Influenced education, parenting, animal training, and clinical psychology (behavior modification), though cognitive approaches now dominate.

## connections

- [[b-f-skinner]] — chief architect of operant conditioning and radical behaviorism.
- [[john-b-watson]] — founder of behaviorism as a systematic psychology.
- [[ivan-pavlov]] — Russian physiologist whose classical conditioning experiments were foundational.
- [[cognitive-science]] — emerged partly as a reaction against strict behaviorism's denial of mental processes.
- [[noam-chomsky]] — his critique of behaviorist language learning theory helped trigger the cognitive revolution.
