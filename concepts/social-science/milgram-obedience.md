# The Milgram Obedience Experiment

## summary

*Stanley Milgram* conducted experiments in 1961–1962 in which ordinary participants administered apparently lethal electric shocks to a screaming victim on the orders of an experimenter in a white coat. The majority obeyed despite evident distress, revealing that normal people will commit harmful acts when instructed by an authority figure.

## you gotta know

- Participants were told they were testing whether punishment aided learning; they sat at a machine delivering shocks (fake) to a confederate in another room.
- The confederate made prearranged protests and eventually fell silent; the experimenter calmly ordered "You have no choice, you must continue."
- In the baseline condition, 65% of participants continued to the maximum 450-volt "shock"—labelled XXX and "Danger: Severe Shock."
- Participants obeyed not out of sadism but out of felt obligation to the authority figure and uncertainty about who bore responsibility.
- Obedience declined sharply when the experimenter left the room, when the participant had to touch the victim, or when peers rebelled.
- The experiments sparked enormous ethical outcry—participants experienced severe trauma, and the revelation challenged humanistic views of human nature.
- Milgram's work influenced ethics boards worldwide and became the textbook example of how ordinary people enable atrocity under authority.
