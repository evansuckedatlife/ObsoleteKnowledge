# Supply and Demand

## summary

*Supply and demand* is the foundational framework describing how prices are determined in a market economy. Supply is the quantity of goods producers are willing to offer at each price level; demand is the quantity consumers are willing to purchase. Markets find equilibrium where the supply curve and demand curve intersect—the price at which the quantity supplied equals the quantity demanded. This mechanism is the backbone of economic analysis and appears in nearly every market transaction.

## you gotta know

### The Mechanism
- *Demand curve*: slopes downward—as price rises, quantity demanded falls (and vice versa).
- *Supply curve*: slopes upward—as price rises, quantity supplied rises (and vice versa).
- *Equilibrium price*: the price at which quantity supplied equals quantity demanded; market forces push prices toward this level.
- *Shortage*: occurs when price is below equilibrium (demand exceeds supply), creating upward pressure on price.
- *Surplus*: occurs when price is above equilibrium (supply exceeds demand), creating downward pressure on price.

### Shifts and Determinants
- *Demand shifters*: consumer income, preferences, prices of related goods, population, expectations about future prices.
- *Supply shifters*: production costs, technology, number of producers, expectations about future prices.
- A shift in supply or demand curve (not a movement along the curve) can change equilibrium price and quantity dramatically.

### Market Behavior
- Perfectly competitive markets gravitate toward equilibrium as buyers and sellers respond to price signals.
- Black markets and price controls (price floors/ceilings) interfere with this mechanism, often creating unintended shortages or surpluses.

## context

Supply and demand is arguably the most powerful lens in economics because it explains price formation in any market—labor, housing, commodities, currencies, ideas. Understanding that prices are not arbitrary but the outcome of millions of independent decisions creates a framework for predicting what happens when tastes shift, technology improves, or regulations are imposed. It's also a framework prone to misapplication: not every market achieves equilibrium, not all goods have straightforward supply and demand curves (information goods, network effects), and real markets are rife with friction, incomplete information, and power imbalances. But the basic model is so useful that it appears in almost every economics course and nearly every quiz-bowl question about markets.
