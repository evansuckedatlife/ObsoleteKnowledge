# John Kenneth Galbraith

## summary

*John Kenneth Galbraith* was a 20th-century American economist and prolific public intellectual who challenged both laissez-faire economics and crude Keynesianism, arguing that large corporations and technocracies dominate modern capitalist economies rather than competitive markets. His books reached mass audiences and shaped liberal economic thinking from the 1950s onward.

## you gotta know

- Wrote *The Affluent Society* (1958), arguing that advertising and consumerism drive demand in rich nations, undermining the classical assumption of independent consumer preferences.
- Introduced the concept of the *technostructure*: the layer of managers, engineers, and experts who actually control large corporations, not shareholders.
- Argued that large firms exercise market power (oligopoly) and cannot be treated as price-takers; competition is an ideal of small firms, not modern reality.
- Criticized the pursuit of *aggregate growth* as a measure of success, highlighting "private affluence and public squalor"—wealthy individuals amid crumbling infrastructure and underfunded schools.
- Served as U.S. ambassador to India (1961–1963) under Kennedy and worked as an economic policy advisor.
- Lived to 97 and remained a prolific writer and television personality, translating economics into accessible language for lay readers.

## connections

- [[milton-friedman]] — ideological opponent; Galbraith rejected the free-market gospel that Friedman championed.
- [[john-maynard-keynes]] — influenced by Keynes's macroeconomic framework; extended it to corporate behavior and institutional analysis.
- [[thorstein-veblen]] — intellectual precursor; shared Veblen's skepticism about neoclassical assumptions and interest in the "leisure class" and conspicuous consumption.
- [[karl-marx]] — shared some skepticism about capitalist harmony, though Galbraith was no revolutionary.
