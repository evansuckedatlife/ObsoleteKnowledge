# Phillips Curve

## summary

The *Phillips Curve* is an empirical relationship named after economist *A.W. Phillips*, showing an inverse trade-off between the rate of unemployment and the rate of wage inflation (or price inflation). Widely adopted as a guide for monetary policy in the 1960s, it collapsed in the 1970s, reshaping economic theory and practice.

## you gotta know

- *A.W. Phillips* (1958) observed data from the UK (1861–1957) showing that lower unemployment correlated with higher wage inflation—the curve slopes downward.
- Suggested to policymakers that they could *choose* a point on the curve: accept higher inflation to achieve lower unemployment, or tolerate more unemployment to keep inflation down.
- Became a cornerstone of Keynesian macroeconomic management in the 1960s; many governments tried to exploit the trade-off.
- Collapsed in the 1970s during stagflation (simultaneous high inflation and high unemployment), which the original Phillips Curve implied could not happen.
- *Milton Friedman* and others argued that inflation expectations shift the curve; attempting to permanently lower unemployment below its "natural rate" only raises inflation.
- The curve's failure prompted a reckoning in macroeconomics: expectations matter; monetary policy cannot persistently trade off inflation for output gains.

## connections

- [[monetary-policy]] — the Phillips Curve was a guide for conducting it, and its failure reshaped monetary-policy theory.
- [[inflation]] — one variable on the curve's axes; its unpredictable behavior in the 1970s exposed flaws in the relationship.
- [[unemployment]] — the other variable on the curve; the idea of a stable trade-off was central to its appeal.
