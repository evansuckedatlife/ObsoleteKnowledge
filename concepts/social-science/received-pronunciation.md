# Received Pronunciation

## summary

*Received Pronunciation* (RP) is the prestige accent of English associated with educated speakers in southeastern England, historically taught in public schools and used by the British Broadcasting Corporation. Characterized by non-rhotic pronunciation and formal vowel distinctions, it remains the standard accent in formal British contexts despite now being spoken by only a minority of the population.

## you gotta know

- Originated as the accent of the English upper classes and public school system; no longer universally taught but remains the reference standard for British English.
- Non-rhotic: the final "r" in words like *car* and *park* is silent unless followed by a vowel (*car-ist*).
- Preserves distinctions between vowel pairs that other accents merge, such as *lot/cloth* and *trap/palm*.
- The "short u" is pronounced with lips spread rather than rounded, distinguishing *strut* from *foot*.
- Features a distinction between *nurse* and *square* that is lost in many other accents.
- Declining among younger generations in favor of Estuary English and regional alternatives, though still used in formal broadcasting and legal settings.

## connections

- [[cockney]] — its traditional working-class counterpart in London, historically marked by heavy h-dropping and glottal stops.
- [[estuary-english]] — the modern successor blending RP with London working-class speech patterns.
- [[general-american]] — the transatlantic prestige standard for American English.
- [[english-accents-and-dialects]] — one node within the broader landscape of English variation.
