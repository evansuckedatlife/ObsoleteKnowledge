# Bronisław Malinowski

## summary

*Bronisław Malinowski* (1884–1942) was a Polish-born British anthropologist who revolutionized ethnographic method through years of intensive participant observation in the Trobriand Islands. His insistence on long-term fieldwork, meticulous documentation, and the integration of culture as a functioning whole established the template for modern British anthropology and influenced the discipline globally.

## you gotta know

- Spent four years (1915–1918) in the *Trobriand Islands* conducting participant observation during World War I, documenting kinship, exchange systems, magic, and daily life in unprecedented detail.
- Championed *functionalism* — the idea that cultural practices and beliefs serve to meet psychological and social needs — shaping how anthropologists explain rituals, taboos, and institutions.
- Published *Argonauts of the Western Pacific* (1922), a monograph presenting ethnographic data with reflexivity about method, establishing the standard for ethnographic monographs.
- Developed the concept of *ethnographic realism*: immersive fieldwork required learning the language, living among the people, and abandoning external judgment.
- His study of the *kula* ring (ceremonial exchange) became a foundational example of how economic systems embed cultural and social meanings.
- Founded the London School of Economics' anthropology program, training a generation of British anthropologists including *Evans-Pritchard* and *Radcliffe-Brown*.
