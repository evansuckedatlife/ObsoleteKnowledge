# The Little Albert Experiment

## summary

*John B. Watson* and *Rosalie Rayner* demonstrated in 1920 that human emotions could be classically conditioned by frightening an infant named Albert with loud noise whenever he played with a white rat. Albert developed a fear of the rat and other white fluffy objects, showing that phobias may arise through association rather than instinct.

## you gotta know

- Albert B., an 11-month-old infant, initially had no fear of the white rat; Watson paired the rat with a deafening clanging sound.
- After repeated pairings, Albert came to cry and attempt to avoid the rat itself—the rat had become a conditioned stimulus for fear.
- The fear *generalized* to similar white fluffy stimuli: a rabbit, cotton wool, a white fur coat—demonstrating stimulus generalization.
- Watson argued this proved Freudian psychology was wrong; emotions were learned, not rooted in unconscious drives.
- The experiment was conducted at Johns Hopkins University and widely publicized, making it one of the first famous psychological studies.
- It raised serious ethical concerns: Albert developed a lasting phobia and was never desensitized (a major failure of the field).
- The study became a cornerstone of behaviourist child psychology and later a cautionary tale about research ethics.
