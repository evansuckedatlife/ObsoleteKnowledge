# The Asch Conformity Experiment

## summary

*Solomon Asch* conducted experiments in 1951–1956 in which participants were asked to match the length of a line against three comparison lines, often finding that they gave incorrect answers when confederates (secret research assistants) gave the same wrong answer first. The work revealed that group pressure causes individuals to conform, even when it contradicts sensory evidence.

## you gotta know

- A participant sat in a group apparently judging which of three lines matched a standard line; the other members were confederates instructed to give wrong answers.
- On critical trials, roughly one-third of participants conformed to the obviously wrong group answer; on average, participants conformed on about one-third of trials.
- Participants rarely conforming felt strong internal conflict and stress; many later reported doubting their own eyesight.
- When confederates gave correct answers, conformity dropped dramatically; when just one confederate dissented from the group, conformity fell sharply.
- Asch interviewed participants afterward; most said they knew the group was wrong but felt overwhelming social pressure to fit in.
- The experiments were replicated across cultures and decades, showing conformity is robust but not universal—personality, culture, and group composition all matter.
- The work became paradigmatic for studying social influence and laid groundwork for understanding groupthink and cult dynamics.
