# Jean Piaget

## summary

**Jean Piaget** (1896–1980) was a Swiss psychologist and founder of *developmental psychology*, whose theories of how children's minds grow and develop fundamentally changed education and our understanding of childhood. Unlike earlier views that children were simply miniature adults or passive recipients of knowledge, Piaget proposed that children actively *construct* their understanding of the world through interaction and play, progressing through distinct stages with qualitatively different cognitive abilities at each level. From infancy (sensorimotor) through concrete operations and finally abstract reasoning, Piaget mapped the child's journey toward adult thinking, revealing that what looks like a child's error often reflects not stupidity but a genuinely different way of understanding reality. His influence on education, child psychology, and humanistic approaches to learning is immeasurable.

## you gotta know

### Life and Career
- Born in Neuchâtel, Switzerland; began as a biologist, then turned to psychology after becoming fascinated by children's reasoning and mistakes.
- Founded the International Center for Genetic Epistemology in Geneva in 1955; spent decades conducting detailed observations of children solving problems.
- Conducted thousands of informal experiments, often using his own three children as subjects—not always by modern ethical standards, but with genuine curiosity and insight.

### Stages of Cognitive Development
- *Sensorimotor* (0–2 years): infants learn through sensation and movement; develop object permanence (understanding that objects exist when out of sight).
- *Preoperational* (2–7 years): children think with symbols and language but are *egocentric* (unable to take another's perspective) and fail *conservation* tasks (believe quantity changes when shape does).
- *Concrete operational* (7–11 years): children can perform logical operations on concrete objects; understand conservation; begin to decentrate (focus on multiple features at once).
- *Formal operational* (12+): adolescents and adults reason abstractly, hypothetically, and systematically—they can think about thinking.

### Key Concepts
- *Schema*: mental structures or frameworks for understanding the world; develop and become more complex with experience.
- *Assimilation*: incorporating new information into existing schemas.
- *Accommodation*: modifying schemas when new information doesn't fit.
- *Constructivism*: knowledge is not passively absorbed but actively constructed by the learner through interaction.
- *Conservation*: the understanding that quantity remains constant despite changes in appearance (water poured into a different-shaped glass is still the same amount).

## context

Piaget's revolution was philosophical: he elevated childhood from a preparatory stage to be endured into a fascinating realm of genuinely different—but no less sophisticated—thinking. This dignity and respect for children's minds reshaped education from rote memorization toward discovery, exploration, and problem-solving. His stages provided a framework that, while later refined (children's abilities are less stage-bound than Piaget believed), still anchors how educators and parents understand development. The conservation task became iconic—a simple, elegant demonstration that children don't think like adults. Piaget also pioneered a method of careful observation and questioning that became standard in developmental psychology. Though his theories have been revised (cognitive development is more continuous and flexible than he proposed; his underestimation of infants' capabilities has been corrected), his core insight remains: minds develop, mature, and transform qualitatively. Understanding how a child thinks—why they believe inanimate objects have feelings, why they struggle with multiple perspectives—opens pathways to teaching, parenting, and raising emotionally attuned humans.
