## summary

*Geordie* is the accent and dialect of Newcastle upon Tyne and Northeast England, marked by distinctive vowel pronunciations, rhotic speech, and strong working-class and regional identity. It preserves many historical features of Northern English and remains one of Britain's most distinctive and recognizable regional accents.

## you gotta know

- Rhotic pronunciation of "r" throughout, even word-finally and before consonants, as in Scottish English.
- Distinctive vowel shifts: *strut* vowel pronounced more toward *o*, making *bus* sound like *bos*; *face* toward the *trap* area.
- Consonant features include *th* sometimes becoming *d* at the start of words (*that* → *dat*) and soft *g* dropping in -ing endings (*running* → *runnin'*).
- Use of *gan* for "go" and distinctive grammatical forms like *yee* for "you" (plural).
- Intonation patterns with characteristic rising and falling movements distinct from RP and other accents.
- Strong local identity and cultural pride; the accent has become more acceptable and even fashionable in contemporary British media.

## connections

- [[scottish-english]] — another strongly rhotic Northern accent with regional pride and non-standard grammar.
- [[scouse]] — the Liverpool accent, similarly regionally distinctive and Northern-based.
- [[received-pronunciation]] — the prestige Southern standard against which Geordie has historically been marginalized.
