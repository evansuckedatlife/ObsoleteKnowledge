# John Maynard Keynes

## summary

*John Maynard Keynes* was a 20th-century British economist whose *General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money* (1936) revolutionized macroeconomics by showing that free markets do not automatically reach full employment. His theory provided the intellectual basis for government intervention through fiscal stimulus and shaped economic policy for decades.

## you gotta know

- Wrote *The General Theory* (1936), which shifted economic focus from individual markets to aggregate demand, employment, and national income.
- Introduced the concept of *aggregate demand*: the total spending in an economy, which may fall short of what's needed for full employment.
- Argued that *sticky wages* (wages do not fall quickly in recessions) mean unemployment can persist without government intervention—overturning classical economics.
- Developed the *multiplier* mechanism: a dollar of government spending generates more than a dollar of total income as workers spend their wages.
- Proposed that during recessions, government should run *budget deficits* to stimulate demand and employment—counter-intuitive to balanced-budget orthodoxy.
- Served as chief British negotiator at Bretton Woods (1944) and designed the International Monetary Fund and World Bank.

## connections

- [[milton-friedman]] — his principal intellectual challenger, who argued that monetary policy (not fiscal stimulus) is the proper tool.
- [[alfred-marshall]] — his Cambridge teacher; Keynes built the *General Theory* partly as a refutation of Marshallian equilibrium.
- [[thomas-malthus]] — Keynes admired Malthus's skepticism about say's law and market clearing.
- [[friedrich-hayek]] — contemporary critic from the free-market right; their debates defined mid-20th-century macroeconomics.
