# Stanley Milgram

## summary

**Stanley Milgram** (1933–1984) was an American social psychologist famous for his controversial experiments on obedience to authority, which revealed a disturbing willingness in ordinary people to inflict harm when ordered by an authority figure. In the 1960s, Milgram recruited participants to deliver what they believed were electric shocks to another person in a learning experiment; the shocking finding was that most participants obeyed the experimenter's commands to continue delivering increasingly severe shocks even as the "learner" (actually an actor) screamed in apparent agony. The experiments demonstrated that situational pressures and authority can override individual conscience—a chilling insight in the shadow of the Holocaust and other atrocities. Though ethically controversial (Milgram was eventually forbidden from using deceptive research methods), his work profoundly influenced how psychology understands conformity, authority, and moral responsibility.

## you gotta know

### Life and Achievements
- Born in New York; earned his PhD from Harvard, studying under Solomon Asch, a pioneer in conformity research.
- Conducted the obedience experiments at Yale University in 1961–1962, intending to test whether Americans would commit atrocities more readily than Germans (a post-WWII question).
- Later taught at CUNY Graduate Center; died of a heart attack at age 51.

### The Obedience Experiments
- *Setup*: participants were told they were in a "learning experiment"; they played the role of "teacher," giving shocks to a "learner" for wrong answers.
- *The learner's responses*: as shocks increased, the actor gave scripted protests (grunts, shouts, pleas to stop, then silence—implying unconsciousness or death).
- *Authority pressure*: when participants hesitated, the experimenter issued verbal prods ("You have no choice; you must continue").
- *Result*: 65% of participants delivered the maximum shock (450 volts), even after the learner went silent. Most were visibly distressed but obeyed.

### Key Variations and Findings
- Obedience dropped dramatically if the experimenter was absent or gave orders remotely (distance effect).
- Obedience remained high even when participants could see the learner's agony (spatial proximity did not override obedience to the experimenter).
- Obedience decreased when peers modeled disobedience or when participants had to physically hold the learner's hand on a shock plate.
- Obedience was not predicted by personality measures (the "authoritarian personality" was not the sole determinant).

### Other Contributions
- *Small-world phenomenon*: Milgram's experiments showing that people are connected through a surprisingly small number of social links; foundational to understanding social networks.
- *Cognitive overload*: Milgram theorized that people in urban environments process so much information that they become desensitized and less likely to help strangers.

## context

Milgram's obedience experiments became among the most famous and ethically fraught in psychology's history. They answered a pressing post-Holocaust question with a deeply uncomfortable answer: ordinary people will inflict pain on strangers if an authority figure orders them to do so and claims responsibility. This was not sadism or evil; it was obedience, the subordination of personal conscience to role and authority. The experiments suggested that atrocities emerge not from pathology but from normal psychology in abnormal situations. This insight redirected psychology toward understanding how situational factors override moral agency—a theme later explored by Philip Zimbardo's Stanford Prison Experiment and Hannah Arendt's "banality of evil." However, Milgram's methods became a watershed for ethical constraints on research: participants were deceived, exposed to psychological distress, and unable to truly withdraw (the experimenter's "You have no choice" overrode their autonomy). His work catalyzed the development of institutional review boards (IRBs) and informed-consent protocols. The controversy persists: some argue his deception was justified by the magnitude of insights gained; others contend that psychology should never inflict such distress, even for knowledge. Milgram also demonstrated the self-reflective power of experimental psychology: by showing people how readily they would obey, the experiments became self-aware commentaries on human nature and moral fragility.
