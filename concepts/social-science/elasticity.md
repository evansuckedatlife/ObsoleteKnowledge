# Elasticity

## summary

*Elasticity* measures the responsiveness of quantity demanded or supplied to changes in price (or income, or other factors). A good with *high price elasticity of demand* means consumers dramatically reduce purchases when the price rises. Conversely, *inelastic* goods see little quantity change despite price swings. Elasticity is typically expressed as a percentage: a 1% price change causing a 2% quantity change indicates elasticity of 2. This concept is crucial for firms setting prices and governments predicting the effects of taxes and regulation.

## you gotta know

### Price Elasticity of Demand
- *Elastic demand* (elasticity > 1): quantity demanded is very responsive to price; a 1% price increase causes a greater than 1% drop in quantity.
- *Inelastic demand* (elasticity < 1): quantity demanded is unresponsive to price; even large price changes cause small quantity swings.
- *Unit elastic* (elasticity = 1): quantity and price change proportionally.

### Determinants
- *Necessity vs. luxury*: necessities tend to be inelastic (people buy insulin regardless of price); luxuries are elastic (people cut back on vacations when budgets tighten).
- *Availability of substitutes*: goods with many substitutes are more elastic (switching costs are low); goods with few substitutes are inelastic.
- *Time horizon*: demand is more elastic over long periods (people adjust slowly to price hikes but eventually shift behavior).
- *Share of budget*: goods representing a large share of income are more elastic (consumers scrutinize the purchase); cheap items are inelastic.

### Supply Elasticity
- *Elastic supply*: producers can easily increase output in response to price rises (often true for manufactured goods).
- *Inelastic supply*: output is hard to expand quickly (true for land, minerals, or goods with long production times).

### Real-World Implications
- Firms with inelastic products (tobacco, pharmaceuticals) can raise prices without losing many customers.
- Taxes on elastic goods generate little revenue but large deadweight loss; taxes on inelastic goods generate substantial revenue.

## context

Elasticity transforms supply-and-demand analysis from a qualitative intuition into a quantifiable predictive tool. It explains why the OPEC cartel can restrict oil output and raise prices (crude oil is globally inelastic in the short term), why raising cigarette taxes doesn't cut smoking much (inelastic), and why competition in software (elastic due to substitutes) keeps prices lower than in pharmaceuticals (inelastic, no substitutes, regulatory barriers). It also highlights the tension between revenue and welfare: a government can maximize tax revenue on an inelastic good, but the policy may harm consumer surplus without changing behavior. Elasticity is a quiz-bowl staple because it connects price theory to observable markets.
