# Gross Domestic Product

## summary

*Gross Domestic Product (GDP)* is the total monetary value of all final goods and services produced within a country's borders during a specific period, typically one year. It is the most widely used measure of a nation's economic size and performance. GDP can be calculated three equivalent ways: the expenditure approach (consumption + investment + government spending + net exports), the income approach (sum of all incomes earned in production), and the output approach (sum of value added by all firms). A country's GDP growth rate is a key indicator of economic health; high growth signals expansion and rising living standards, while negative growth (recession) signals contraction.

## you gotta know

### Calculation Methods
- *Expenditure approach*: GDP = C + I + G + (X – M), where C is consumption, I is investment, G is government spending, X is exports, M is imports.
- *Income approach*: GDP = wages + rent + interest + profit (all incomes earned in production).
- *Output approach*: sum of value added at each stage of production to avoid double-counting (raw materials → processing → retail).
- All three methods yield the same GDP figure if measured correctly.

### Nominal vs. Real GDP
- *Nominal GDP*: measured at current prices; inflated by price increases.
- *Real GDP*: adjusted for inflation using a base year; reflects actual quantity changes, not price changes.
- *Real GDP growth* is the metric economists use to assess true economic expansion.

### Limitations
- GDP omits non-market activity (household labor, volunteering, underground economy) and environmental costs (pollution).
- *Per capita GDP*: dividing by population allows cross-country comparisons; high per capita GDP is associated with high living standards.
- GDP doesn't measure inequality, sustainability, or happiness; a country could have high GDP but poor income distribution.

### Key Components
- *Consumption* (typically 60–70% of GDP): spending by households on goods and services.
- *Investment* (typically 15–20%): spending by businesses on capital goods; highly cyclical.
- *Government spending* (typically 15–25%): purchases by government; varies with policy.
- *Net exports*: if a country imports more than it exports, this is negative and subtracts from GDP.

## context

GDP is the language of macroeconomic policy. Central banks, governments, and international organizations obsess over GDP growth because it correlates with employment, tax revenue, and political stability. A 2% growth rate is considered healthy; a 0% or negative rate signals recession and triggers policy interventions. However, GDP-centrism has drawbacks: it ignores income distribution (a country can grow richer while the poor get poorer), environmental sustainability (extracting and burning resources boosts GDP but depletes natural capital), and quality of life (leisure time, health, security). Alternatives like *Gross National Happiness* or *Human Development Index* attempt to measure welfare more broadly, but GDP remains the dominant metric. Understanding GDP is essential for interpreting economic news and understanding why growth obsession drives much of economic policy.
