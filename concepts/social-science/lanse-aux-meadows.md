# L'Anse aux Meadows

## summary

*L'Anse aux Meadows* is a Norse settlement on the northern tip of Newfoundland, Canada, dating to around 1000 CE, representing the only confirmed pre-Columbian European presence in North America. Discovered archaeologically in 1960, the remains of Viking sod-built structures and Norse artifacts provided the first definitive proof that Vikings reached the Americas five centuries before Columbus, matching descriptions in Norse sagas of a land called *Vinland*.

## you gotta know

- Located at the tip of the Great Northern Peninsula in Newfoundland, discovered by Norwegian explorer Helge Ingstad and his wife archaeologist Anne Stine Ingstad in 1960.
- The settlement contains the remains of eight Norse-built structures (longhouses) constructed from sod over wooden frames, similar to Viking buildings from Greenland and Iceland.
- Radiocarbon dating places the occupation around 1000 CE, during the time of Leif Erikson, the Norse explorer credited in medieval sagas with discovering *Vinland*.
- Archaeological finds include Norse artifacts (iron nails, tools, a weaving loom) and the only known Viking spindle whorl found outside Scandinavia, confirming European origin.
- The site shows evidence of Norse iron-working, food storage, and habitation but was apparently occupied for only a short period (estimated 10–20 years) before abandonment.
- The Icelandic sagas *Grænlendinga saga* and *Eirík's saga* contain narrative accounts of voyages to Vinland, providing historical context for the archaeological discovery.
- The settlement was likely a way-station or trading post between Greenland and indigenous North American peoples, though the reasons for its abandonment remain unclear.
