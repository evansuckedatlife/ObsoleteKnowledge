# Thorstein Veblen

## summary

*Thorstein Veblen* was a late 19th and early 20th-century American institutionalist economist who critiqued both classical economics and the wealthy leisure class, introducing concepts like "conspicuous consumption" and "conspicuous waste." His acerbic style and focus on social habits and business culture made him an outsider to mainstream economics but a towering influence on heterodox thought.

## you gotta know

- Coined *conspicuous consumption*: the practice of buying expensive goods not for utility but to signal wealth and status to others.
- Introduced *conspicuous waste*: the deliberate destruction or idleness (not working) as a signal of wealth—that you can afford not to produce.
- Argued that economics must be an *evolutionary* science focused on habits and institutions, not mathematical models of rational agents.
- Analyzed the *predatory* nature of business and finance, distinguishing between productive industry (engineering) and parasitic business (profit-seeking).
- Rejected the neoclassical assumption that humans are rational utility maximizers, emphasizing instead social instincts, emulation, and tribal rivalry.
- Wrote *The Theory of the Leisure Class* (1899), a scathing sociological anatomy of the American rich.

## connections

- [[alfred-marshall]] — Veblen rejected Marshall's mathematical formalism and assumptions of rational choice.
- [[john-kenneth-galbraith]] — intellectual heir to Veblen; extended his critique of consumerism and corporate power into the 20th century.
- [[karl-marx]] — shared Marx's skepticism about capitalist harmony, though Veblen focused on status rivalry rather than class struggle.
