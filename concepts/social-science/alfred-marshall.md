## summary

*Alfred Marshall* was a late 19th and early 20th-century British economist who synthesized classical political economy with marginal analysis, creating the framework of supply-and-demand curves that remains central to microeconomics. His textbook, *Principles of Economics* (1890), became the standard for nearly 50 years and established Cambridge economics as the global center of the discipline.

## you gotta know

- Pioneered *partial equilibrium analysis*: showing how supply and demand interact in a single market, with price as the equating mechanism.
- Introduced the concept of *elasticity*: the responsiveness of quantity demanded to price changes, quantifying the slope of demand curves.
- Developed the theory of *consumer surplus* and *producer surplus*, measuring the welfare gains from trade.
- Harmonized classical rent theory (Ricardo) with marginal utility theory, showing how all factor prices (wages, profits, rents, interest) are determined by marginal productivity.
- Advocated for *industrial organization*: systematic study of how firms compete, merge, and grow, laying foundations for later antitrust economics.
- Championed mathematics and graphs in economics, establishing the modern quantitative tradition (the Marshallian cross diagram of supply and demand).

## connections

- [[adam-smith]] — Marshall formalized Smith's vision of market equilibrium and the self-regulating economy.
- [[david-ricardo]] — incorporated Ricardian rent theory into a unified marginal framework.
- [[john-maynard-keynes]] — Marshall was Keynes's Cambridge teacher; Keynes later critiqued Marshall's assumption that markets always clear.
- [[thorstein-veblen]] — contemporary American institutionalist who rejected the mathematical formalism that Marshall championed.
