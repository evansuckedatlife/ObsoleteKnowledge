# Alfred Adler

## summary

**Alfred Adler** (1870–1937) was an Austrian psychiatrist and the third major figure in early 20th-century psychoanalysis, alongside Freud and Jung. Though originally part of Freud's circle, Adler developed *individual psychology*, a framework centered on the individual's felt sense of inferiority and the drive to overcome it through striving, goal-setting, and social connection. Unlike Freud's emphasis on sexual drives or Jung's mysticism, Adler emphasized consciousness, choice, and social factors. He popularized the concept of the *inferiority complex* and argued that birth order shapes personality—ideas that became widely influential in parenting, education, and popular psychology.

## you gotta know

### Life and Contributions
- Born in Vienna; studied medicine and became a practicing physician before joining Freud's circle around 1902.
- Left Freud's group in 1911 to establish his own school of individual psychology, which attracted a devoted following.
- Pioneered *child guidance clinics* and championed education reform; believed psychology should be accessible to the public, not confined to medical practice.
- Emigrated to the United States in 1935; died in Aberdeen, Scotland in 1937.

### Core Theories
- The *inferiority complex*: a pervasive sense of inadequacy or powerlessness that drives behavior; people are motivated to compensate, overcome, or "move upward."
- *Striving for superiority*: not narcissism, but a healthy drive to improve oneself, master challenges, and contribute meaningfully.
- *Birth order*: personality and behavior are shaped by one's position in the family (oldest, middle, youngest); different positions foster different psychological patterns and strengths.
- *Social interest*: the capacity to feel connected to others and contribute to the common good; mental health correlates with strong social interest.
- *Lifestyle*: the individual develops a coherent pattern of beliefs, goals, and behaviors (a "lifestyle") early in childhood, often in response to feeling inferior in some domain.

### Influence
- Individual psychology influenced humanistic psychology, existential therapy, and modern positive psychology.
- Adler's work on parenting, birth order, and inferiority complexes became ubiquitous in popular psychology and self-help.

## context

Adler's greatest gift was democratizing psychology. Where Freud built a medical specialty and Jung pursued esoteric spirituality, Adler insisted that psychological insight should inform parenting, teaching, and social policy. His focus on conscious goal-striving and social belonging felt more optimistic and empowering than Freudian determinism. The concept of inferiority complexes became so embedded in 20th-century thought that people now casually say "I have an inferiority complex," treating it as common psychological vocabulary. Though empirical evidence for birth order effects has proven mixed, Adler's larger insight—that position in the family shapes development—remains intuitive and culturally resonant. His emphasis on social interest and community also distinguished him; he saw mental health not as symptom-free isolation but as engagement with others and contribution to the greater good.
