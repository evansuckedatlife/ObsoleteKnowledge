# Cognitive science

## summary

*Cognitive science* is an interdisciplinary field integrating psychology, linguistics, philosophy, neuroscience, and artificial intelligence to study the mind and intelligence. Emerging in the 1950s–1960s, it treats cognition as computation—mental processes are rule-governed operations over representations—and rejects behaviorism's restriction to observable behavior.

## you gotta know

- Arose partly in reaction to behaviorism's rejection of mental states; proposes that unobservable cognitive processes (thinking, planning, learning) are scientifically tractable.
- *Noam Chomsky's* generative grammar—arguing that language reflects innate principles—was a catalyzing influence, demonstrating that behavior alone cannot explain language.
- Integrates insights from *Jean Piaget's* stage theory of cognitive development, showing how children's thinking structures change over time.
- *Artificial intelligence* and computational modeling provide a framework: if we can write a program that behaves like a cognitive system, we understand something about cognition.
- *Neuroscience* provides evidence about the brain mechanisms underlying perception, memory, and reasoning.
- Key concepts: *mental representation*, *schema*, *processing limitations*, *working memory*, *automaticity vs. controlled processing*.
- Cross-domain: cognitive principles apply to language, vision, reasoning, decision-making, and social understanding.

## connections

- [[linguistics]] — Chomsky's linguistic theory was foundational to cognitive science's emergence.
- [[noam-chomsky]] — his critique of behaviorism and work on universal grammar shaped the field.
- [[jean-piaget]] — pioneering developmental psychologist whose stage theory is central to cognitive science.
- [[behaviorism]] — cognitive science emerged as a direct response to behaviorism's limitations.
- [[generative-grammar]] — exemplar of the rule-based, computational approach cognitive science employs.
