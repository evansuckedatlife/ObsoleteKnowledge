# Adam Smith

## summary

**Adam Smith** (1723–1790) was a Scottish economist and moral philosopher whose *The Wealth of Nations* (1776) established the foundations of modern economics and capitalism. He introduced the concept of the "invisible hand" and argued that individuals pursuing self-interest in a competitive market produce the greatest good for society, laying the intellectual groundwork for free-market capitalism and liberalism. Smith's synthesis of moral philosophy and political economy proved extraordinarily influential: his vision of the market as a self-regulating system replaced mercantilist thought and shaped two centuries of economic policy and theory.

## you gotta know

### Foundational Economics
- Wrote *The Wealth of Nations* (1776), one of the first systematic treatises on economics and widely credited as the origin of economics as a formal academic discipline.
- Proposed the "invisible hand" metaphor: markets self-regulate through competition and self-interest without central government planning — an idea revolutionary in an age of mercantilism.
- Distinguished between the "natural price" (determined by labor costs, materials, and ordinary profit) and market price (set by supply and demand), anticipating modern supply-and-demand theory.

### The Division of Labor and Productivity
- Championed the division of labor as the key to productivity: he showed how specialization and task division increase output exponentially — his famous pin factory example demonstrated how 10 workers, when specialized, could produce 48,000 pins a day (vs. a few dozen if each made whole pins).
- Recognized that the division of labor, while productive, could be socially deadening, requiring workers to perform repetitive tasks without engaging their full intellectual capacities.

### Political Economy and Trade
- Argued against *mercantilism* (the belief that national wealth comes from hoarding precious metals and restricting trade) and *protectionism*, advocating instead for free trade between nations.
- Contended that free trade allows each nation to specialize in what it produces most efficiently, increasing total wealth — an idea foundational to modern international economics.
- Held that government should minimize intervention in markets, though he recognized limited roles for public investment in infrastructure and education.

### Moral Philosophy
- Claimed that *sympathy* (or empathy) is the foundation of moral philosophy, a theory advanced in *The Theory of Moral Sentiments* (1759), which preceded his economics work.
- Smith integrated his moral philosophy with his economics: self-interest is not selfish vice but a natural human drive that, properly channeled through market competition, serves the public good.

## context

Adam Smith is the intellectual founder of modern capitalist economics and liberal political philosophy. His work marked a civilizational shift from viewing economics through the lens of national power (mercantilism) to viewing it through individual incentive and market mechanism. The "invisible hand" became shorthand for the belief that unregulated markets allocate resources efficiently—an idea both profoundly influential and perpetually contested. Smith's actual thought is more nuanced than his popularizers suggest: he believed markets worked best when participants were educated and moral, and he warned against monopolies and merchant cartels. His influence on subsequent economics is immense: from *David Ricardo* and *John Stuart Mill* through *Alfred Marshall* to modern microeconomics, Smith's framework of self-interest, competition, and efficient allocation remains dominant. Yet his work also founded a tradition of critique: *Karl Marx* built his critique of capitalism partly against Smith, and later critics have questioned whether markets truly serve the "public good" when participants have radically unequal power. Smith remains simultaneously celebrated as capitalism's intellectual godfather and contested as the originator of flawed assumptions about human nature and market behavior.

## connections

- [[david-ricardo]] — developed Ricardian rent theory and comparative advantage from Smith's framework.
- [[alfred-marshall]] — formalized Smith's ideas into neoclassical supply-and-demand curves and mathematical economics.
- [[john-stuart-mill]] — adopted and extended Smith's liberal economics with concerns for individual liberty and utility.
- [[karl-marx]] — built a comprehensive critique of capitalist political economy partly in response to Smith.
- [[john-maynard-keynes]] — challenged Smith's assumption that markets automatically equilibrate and full employment occurs naturally.
- [[scottish-enlightenment]] — Smith was part of this broader intellectual movement including Hume, Hutcheson, and Kames.
- [[mercantilism]] — the economic doctrine Smith systematically refuted through historical and logical argument.
- [[capitalism]] — Smith is considered the intellectual founder of modern capitalism as a system.
- [[free-trade]] — Smith's advocacy for free trade became foundational to classical liberal political economy.
