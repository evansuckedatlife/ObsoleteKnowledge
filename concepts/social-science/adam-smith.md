# Adam Smith

## summary

*Adam Smith* was an 18th-century Scottish economist and moral philosopher whose *The Wealth of Nations* (1776) established the foundations of modern economics. He introduced the concept of the "invisible hand" and argued that individuals pursuing self-interest in a competitive market produce the greatest good for society, laying the intellectual groundwork for capitalist economics.

## you gotta know

- Wrote *The Wealth of Nations* (1776), one of the first systematic treatises on economics and the origin of the discipline as a formal study.
- Proposed the "invisible hand" metaphor: markets self-regulate through competition and self-interest without central planning.
- Championed the division of labor, showing how specialization increases productivity—his famous pin factory example demonstrated how 10 workers could produce 48,000 pins a day.
- Argued against mercantilism and protectionist trade policies, advocating for free trade between nations.
- Claimed that sympathy (or empathy) is the foundation of moral philosophy, advanced in *The Theory of Moral Sentiments* (1759), before his economics work.
- Distinguished between the "natural price" (labor costs, materials) and market price, anticipating supply-and-demand theory.

## connections

- [[david-ricardo]] — developed Ricardian rent theory from Smith's framework of market equilibrium.
- [[alfred-marshall]] — formalized Smith's ideas into neoclassical supply-and-demand curves.
- [[john-kenneth-galbraith]] — later critiqued the "invisible hand" as inadequate for modern corporations.
- [[karl-marx]] — built a critique of capitalist labor and surplus value partly in response to Smith's economics.
