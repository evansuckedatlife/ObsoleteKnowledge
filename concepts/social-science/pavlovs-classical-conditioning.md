# Pavlov's Classical Conditioning Experiments

## summary

*Ivan Pavlov* discovered classical conditioning through experiments with dogs in the 1890s–1900s, demonstrating that animals could learn to associate a neutral stimulus (a bell) with a natural response (salivation). His work became foundational to behaviorism and showed that learned behaviour arises through association rather than instinct alone.

## you gotta know

- Pavlov presented a bell sound followed by food powder; dogs eventually salivated at the bell alone—the neutral stimulus became a conditioned stimulus.
- He identified unconditioned stimuli (food naturally triggering salivation) and conditioned stimuli (the bell, learned).
- The phenomenon is called *acquisition*—the process by which the association forms—and occurs best when the neutral stimulus precedes the unconditioned stimulus by a short interval.
- *Extinction* occurs when the conditioned stimulus is presented repeatedly without the unconditioned stimulus, weakening the association.
- *Spontaneous recovery* happens when the conditioned response reappears after extinction following a rest period.
- His research earned him the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine in 1904.
- The work laid the groundwork for behaviourist psychology, influencing figures like *John B. Watson* and *B.F. Skinner*.
