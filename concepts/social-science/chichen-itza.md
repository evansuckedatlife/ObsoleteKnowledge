# Chichén Itzá

## summary

*Chichén Itzá* is a major ceremonial center in the Yucatan Peninsula, Mexico, representing the syncretism of Maya and Toltec cultures during the 10th–13th centuries CE. Its iconic El Castillo pyramid, complex astronomical alignments, evidence of human sacrifice, and advanced knowledge of mathematics and astronomy exemplify the intellectual and architectural sophistication of Mesoamerican civilization at its apex before Spanish conquest.

## you gotta know

- Located in Quintana Roo state in the northern Yucatan Peninsula, occupied from approximately 600 CE onward but reaching its peak during the Terminal Classic and Early Postclassic periods (10th–13th centuries).
- El Castillo (the Castle) is the site's most famous structure—a 79-foot stepped pyramid dedicated to Kukulkan, the feathered serpent deity, with architectural refinements creating visual serpent shadows during spring and autumn equinoxes.
- The Great Ball Court is the largest known Mesoamerican ball court, 545 feet long, where ritual ball games played a role in religious and political ceremonies; losers may have been sacrificed.
- Contains the Cenote of Sacrifice (Cenote Sagrado), a natural sinkhole where archaeological evidence shows humans and valuable objects were ritually thrown as offerings to Chaac, the rain god.
- Shows clear evidence of Toltec influence alongside Maya design—columns carved as feathered serpents, warrior imagery, and architectural styles differing from earlier Classic Maya sites.
- The Maya glyphs and astronomical alignments throughout the site demonstrate sophisticated calendrical knowledge and mathematical precision, including the 365-day solar year and the 260-day ritual calendar.
- One of Mexico's most visited archaeological sites and a UNESCO World Heritage Site, drawing millions of tourists annually.
