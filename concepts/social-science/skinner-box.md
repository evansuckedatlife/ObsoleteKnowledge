## summary

*B.F. Skinner*'s operant conditioning chamber, used from the 1930s onward, is an enclosed apparatus in which an animal (typically a rat or pigeon) learns to perform a behaviour (like pressing a lever) to receive reward or avoid punishment. It shifted psychology from passive classical conditioning to active instrumental learning shaped by consequences.

## you gotta know

- A rat in the box discovers that pressing a lever dispenses food pellets; it learns to press the lever repeatedly—the behaviour is *reinforced*.
- Positive reinforcement (reward following behaviour) increases the behaviour's frequency; negative reinforcement (removal of discomfort) also strengthens it.
- *Punishment*—adding something unpleasant after behaviour—and *extinction*—withdrawing reinforcement—both weaken behaviour.
- Skinner used *schedules of reinforcement*: continuous (every response rewarded), fixed-ratio (every Nth response), variable-ratio (unpredictable), and interval-based schedules, each producing different patterns of behaviour.
- The apparatus was neutral to context; the same principles applied to rats, pigeons, humans, and complex behaviours.
- Skinner argued that all behaviour—even language and moral conduct—is shaped by reinforcement, a view that became central to behaviourism.
- His work showed that behaviour could be precisely controlled and predicted through environmental manipulation.
