## summary

*Paul Grice* was a British philosopher of language whose theory of conversational implicature and the cooperative principle established pragmatics as a central domain in the study of meaning. He argued that what a speaker *means* often diverges from the literal, encoded meaning of their words — a recognition that transformed how linguists and philosophers understand communication.

## you gotta know

- Introduced the "cooperative principle" and the maxims of conversation (quality, quantity, relevance, and manner), explaining how speakers manage implied meaning while appearing to follow shared conversational rules.
- Developed the concept of "conversational implicature" — the difference between what is said (literal meaning) and what is implicated (intended meaning), showing how context and shared expectations generate unstated meaning.
- Distinguished between "natural" meaning (*the spots mean measles*) and "non-natural" meaning (*the words mean X*) — a philosophical distinction with wide implications.
- His work bridged formal semantics (logic-based meaning) and pragmatics (meaning in use), showing that neither alone explains linguistic communication.
- Though a philosopher by training, his insights became foundational to linguistics and cognitive science, particularly in understanding indirect speech, irony, and figurative language.
