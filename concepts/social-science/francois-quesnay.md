# François Quesnay

## summary

*François Quesnay* was an 18th-century French physician and economist who founded the *Physiocratic* school, which held that agriculture is the sole source of economic value and that a nation's wealth flows from the land. Though later eclipsed by Smith and Ricardo, the Physiocrats pioneered systematic economic analysis and influenced early political economy.

## you gotta know

- Founded the *Physiocratic* school (1750s–1770s), which believed agriculture alone produces a *net product* (surplus) beyond replacement of inputs, unlike manufactures.
- Created the *Tableau Économique* (Economic Table), an early input-output model showing how economic surplus flows between landowners, farmers, and artisans.
- Argued that only land-based output generates true wealth; manufacturing merely transforms existing materials and adds no net value.
- Advocated for a *single tax* on land—the only legitimate source of government revenue, since all other income derives from land value.
- Opposed feudal restrictions on grain trade and advocated freer commerce, aligning with Enlightenment reformism.
- Served as physician to Louis XV and had influence in French royal circles.

## connections

- [[adam-smith]] — Smith admired Quesnay's systematic approach and incorporated some Physiocratic ideas into *Wealth of Nations*, though he rejected their exclusive focus on agriculture.
- [[david-ricardo]] — inherited and refashioned Quesnay's rent theory into a more general framework.
- [[karl-marx]] — later Marxist theory owed debt to Physiocratic analysis of production and surplus.
