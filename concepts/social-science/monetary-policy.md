# Monetary policy

## summary

*Monetary policy* is the use of a central bank's tools—chiefly controlling the money supply and interest rates—to influence economic activity, inflation, and employment. A core lever of macroeconomic management, it reflects competing schools of thought about how money and credit shape the broader economy.

## you gotta know

- Central banks enact monetary policy by adjusting the *reserve requirement* (how much banks must hold), the *discount rate* (at which the central bank lends), and *open market operations* (buying and selling government securities).
- Expansionary policy (increasing money, lowering rates) aims to stimulate demand and reduce unemployment; contractionary policy (tightening money, raising rates) aims to combat inflation.
- *Milton Friedman* and monetarists emphasize the money supply as the primary driver of inflation and output; they favor steady, rule-based monetary growth.
- *John Maynard Keynes* and Keynesians argue that monetary policy is one tool among several; fiscal policy (government spending) can be equally or more effective.
- The *Phillips Curve* suggests a trade-off between inflation and unemployment—but the relationship broke down in the 1970s, challenging Keynesian monetary management.
- *Quantitative easing* (large-scale asset purchases) became a tool of monetary policy after interest rates hit zero during the 2008 financial crisis.
