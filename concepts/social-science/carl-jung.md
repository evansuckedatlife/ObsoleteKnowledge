# Carl Jung

## summary

**Carl Jung** (1875–1961) was a Swiss psychiatrist and one of the earliest and most original students of Freud, whom he eventually broke with to develop *analytical psychology*. While Freud rooted the unconscious in personal childhood and sexuality, Jung proposed a vast *collective unconscious* shared by all humanity, populated by universal symbols (archetypes) like the Hero, the Shadow, and the Self. Jung's ideas were more mystical and spiritual than Freud's mechanistic drive theory; he integrated Eastern philosophy, alchemy, and mythology into psychology. His framework—particularly the introvert/extrovert distinction and the Myers-Briggs personality framework derived from his work—became deeply embedded in popular psychology.

## you gotta know

### Life and Work
- Born in Switzerland; studied medicine and psychiatry, becoming director of the Burghölzli clinic in Zurich by age 30.
- Met Freud in 1907 and became his intellectual heir and closest collaborator; their relationship ruptured in 1913 over theoretical differences, particularly Jung's rejection of Freud's emphasis on sexual repression.
- Spent much of his career analyzing myths, religions, and symbols across cultures; traveled to Africa and America to study indigenous psychologies.

### Core Theories
- The *collective unconscious*: beneath the personal unconscious lies a deeper stratum of the mind shared by all humans, shaped by millennia of evolutionary and cultural history.
- *Archetypes*: universal symbols and character patterns (the Hero, the Wise Old Man, the Shadow, the Anima/Animus, the Self) that recur in myths, religions, and dreams across cultures.
- The *Self*: the goal of psychological development is integration and wholeness, symbolized by the mandala; the Self is the archetype of meaning and integration.
- *Individuation*: the psychological process of becoming fully oneself by integrating conscious and unconscious aspects of personality.
- Introversion/extroversion: fundamental personality orientations that determine how the psyche turns its energy inward or outward.

### Influence
- His work influenced depth psychology, Jungian analysis, and later humanistic psychology.
- The Myers-Briggs Type Indicator and modern personality frameworks owe much to Jung's typology.

## context

Jung's break with Freud was pivotal—it liberated psychology from a purely reductive, drive-based model and opened it to meaning, spirituality, and cross-cultural symbolism. In an era of increasing rationalism and materialism, Jung's willingness to take myth, religion, and the irrational seriously gave psychology a bridge to the humanities and spirituality. Though the collective unconscious cannot be empirically measured in the way Freud's theories could (or couldn't), Jung's archetypes have proven remarkably durable in literary analysis, mythology studies, and narrative design. His insistence that the goal of therapy is not symptom removal but *integration* and growth shaped humanistic psychology and continues to inform modern coaching and self-development. For many, Jung's psychology feels more liberating than Freud's because it posits not just conflict but growth and transcendence.
