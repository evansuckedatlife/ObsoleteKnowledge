# Tariff

## summary

A *tariff* is a tax imposed on imported goods when they cross a nation's border. Tariffs raise the price of foreign goods, making domestic producers more competitive. While tariffs protect domestic jobs and industries in the short run, they also reduce consumer choice, raise prices for consumers, and invite retaliation from trading partners. Tariffs are a tool of protectionism—the deliberate restriction of imports to shield domestic industries—and sit at the heart of trade policy debates, from mercantilism in the 17th century to modern trade wars between the U.S. and China.

## you gotta know

### Mechanics
- *Ad valorem tariff*: a percentage tax (e.g., 25% of the import price); the most common form.
- *Specific tariff*: a fixed dollar amount per unit of import (e.g., $2 per barrel of oil).
- *Effect*: raises the price of imported goods, shifting demand toward domestic substitutes; domestic producers expand output.

### Trade-Offs
- *Winners*: domestic producers in protected industries; workers in those industries (in the short run).
- *Losers*: domestic consumers (pay higher prices), foreign exporters (sell less), other domestic industries that rely on cheap imports as inputs.
- *Net effect*: a tariff typically causes deadweight loss—the gains to producers don't fully offset losses to consumers and the forgone benefits of trade.

### Historical Contexts
- *Mercantilist era*: tariffs were used to maintain trade surpluses and accumulate precious metals.
- *Smoot-Hawley Tariff (1930)*: U.S. raised tariffs to protect against Great Depression; trading partners retaliated, worsening the crisis.
- *GATT and WTO*: post-WWII trade agreements have gradually lowered tariffs, though many sectors remain protected.

### Strategic Uses
- Infant industry protection: tariffs shield new industries in developing countries until they gain competitiveness.
- National security: tariffs or quotas on critical goods (semiconductors, military equipment) protect domestic production.
- Negotiating leverage: tariff threats are used in trade negotiations.

## context

Tariffs embody a perennial tension: free trade increases overall economic efficiency and consumer welfare, but the benefits are diffuse while the costs are concentrated (a few industries lose jobs and market share). This asymmetry makes tariff protection politically durable despite its efficiency costs. The political appeal of "protecting American jobs" competes with the economic reality that tariffs ultimately hurt more consumers than they help workers in protected industries. Tariff policy is a litmus test for understanding the gap between political economy and textbook economics.
