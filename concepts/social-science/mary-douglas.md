# Mary Douglas

## summary

*Mary Douglas* (1921–2007) was a British anthropologist who analyzed how societies use classification systems, ritual, and concepts of purity and danger to establish order and control. Her work on symbolic systems, body symbolism, and the relationship between social boundaries and pollution beliefs transformed how anthropologists understand culture as a coherent meaning-making system.

## you gotta know

- Published *Purity and Danger* (1966), arguing that what a society labels "dirt" or "pollution" reveals its fundamental classification system and social boundaries.
- Analyzed the *Lele* peoples of the Congo, examining their food taboos, hunting rituals, and body practices to show how classification and ritual reinforce social order.
- Developed the concept of *liminality* in ritual (expanding on *Victor Turner*'s work), showing how rituals manage transitions and ambiguous categories.
- Applied her theoretical framework to Western societies, analyzing food taboos, household cleanliness, and consumption patterns to show that "primitive" and modern classification systems operate by the same principles.
- Wrote on gift-giving, debt, and exchange systems, challenging *Mauss's* gift theory with structural analysis of how societies establish obligation and reciprocity.
- Bridged anthropology and sociology, making her work influential across disciplines; her ideas shaped cognitive anthropology and cultural studies.
