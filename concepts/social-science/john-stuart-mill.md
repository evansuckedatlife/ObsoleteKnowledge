# John Stuart Mill

## summary

*John Stuart Mill* was a 19th-century British philosopher and economist who synthesized Ricardian political economy with utilitarian ethics, arguing that the goal of economics is to maximize human happiness rather than merely to describe markets. His *Principles of Political Economy* (1848) was the standard textbook of the era and introduced a more humane, reform-minded vision of capitalism.

## you gotta know

- Wrote *Principles of Political Economy* (1848), which refined and humanized Ricardian economics, acknowledging the role of custom and social arrangement in shaping production and distribution.
- Grounded economics in *utilitarianism*: the principle that the aim of all policy is the "greatest happiness for the greatest number."
- Argued that while the *laws of production* are fixed (like laws of nature), the *laws of distribution* are socially constructed and can be reformed.
- Advocated for *progressive taxation*, public education, and limits on property rights to reduce inequality.
- Championed *women's liberation*: wrote *The Subjection of Women* (1869) and argued for women's legal equality and voting rights.
- Served in Parliament and was a public intellectual across politics, philosophy, and economics.

## connections

- [[adam-smith]] — Mill extended Smith's moral philosophy and free-trade vision with a utilitarian framework.
- [[david-ricardo]] — Mill refined Ricardian theory, but moderated its pessimism with emphasis on social reform.
- [[john-maynard-keynes]] — Keynes admired Mill's ethical approach to economics and his skepticism of laissez-faire absolutes.
- [[karl-marx]] — contemporary; both critiqued capitalism but from opposite directions—Mill through reform, Marx through revolution.
