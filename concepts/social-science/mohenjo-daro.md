# Mohenjo-daro

## summary

*Mohenjo-daro* is the largest city of the Indus Valley Civilization, flourishing from approximately 2600 to 1900 BCE in what is now Pakistan. Its rectilinear street grid, standardized weights and measures, public baths, and sophisticated drainage systems demonstrate urban planning that rivals or exceeds contemporary Bronze Age cities, though its written script remains undeciphered, leaving much of its society shrouded in mystery.

## you gotta know

- Located in the Sindh province of Pakistan on the right bank of the Indus River, first excavated in the 1920s by archaeologists Rakhal Das Banerjee and Daya Ram Sahni.
- Covered approximately 300 acres and housed an estimated 40,000 residents at its peak, making it one of the largest cities of the Bronze Age world.
- The city is laid out on a regular grid pattern with streets running at right angles, suggesting centralized planning and strong civic authority.
- Features a "Great Bath"—a large public pool with sophisticated brick construction and drainage, possibly used for ritual purification or public gatherings.
- Contains standardized weights, measures, and tools indicating a complex economy and possibly a merchant class, yet shows little evidence of monumental religious architecture or royal palaces.
- The script appears on seals and pottery but remains undeciphered, preventing direct knowledge of the civilization's language, religion, or political structure.
- The civilization declined and was abandoned around 1900 BCE, with causes debated—possibly environmental change, floods, or the drying of the Ghaggar River to the east.
