# Abraham Maslow

## summary

**Abraham Maslow** (1908–1970) was an American psychologist and the founder of *humanistic psychology*, a movement that recentered psychology on human potential, growth, and meaning rather than pathology and dysfunction. Reacting against behaviorism's mechanistic view and psychoanalysis's focus on neurosis, Maslow asked: What does a healthy, flourishing person look like? His answer was the concept of *self-actualization*—the drive to become the fullest version of oneself, to actualize latent capacities and pursue meaning. His *hierarchy of needs* (the famous pyramid) proposed that human motivation progresses from basic physiological needs through safety, belonging, esteem, and finally self-actualization. Maslow studied not the mentally ill but high-functioning, creative, and fulfilled people—an inversion of psychology's traditional focus—and this reorientation opened pathways to positive psychology and the wellness movement.

## you gotta know

### Life and Contributions
- Born in Brooklyn to Russian-Jewish immigrant parents; earned his PhD from the University of Wisconsin in 1934.
- Taught at Brooklyn College for most of his career; founded humanistic psychology alongside Carl Rogers and others as a "third force" beyond behaviorism and psychoanalysis.
- Coined the term *self-actualization* and made it central to psychological theory.
- Studied exemplary figures (Eleanor Roosevelt, Albert Einstein, Frederick Douglass) to understand optimal human functioning.

### Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs
- *Physiological needs* (food, water, sleep): foundational; without these, higher needs cannot be pursued.
- *Safety needs*: security, stability, protection from harm.
- *Belonging and love*: connection, intimacy, family, community.
- *Esteem needs*: respect, recognition, autonomy, achievement.
- *Self-actualization*: becoming fully oneself, pursuing purpose, creativity, growth, fulfilling one's potential.

### Key Concepts
- *Self-actualization*: the highest human motivation; realizing one's unique capacities and living authentically.
- *Peak experiences*: moments of intense joy, insight, or connection that feel transcendent; Maslow believed these were central to psychological health.
- *Intrinsic motivation*: pursuing activities for their own sake (learning, creativity) rather than external reward—more psychologically nourishing than extrinsic motivation.
- *The self-actualized person*: creative, accepting, authentic, focused on problems outside the self, appreciative of life, capable of deep relationships.

## context

Maslow's humanistic psychology was countercultural—a radical assertion that psychology should study thriving, not merely reducing suffering. In an era when psychology was dominated by behaviorists mechanizing humans and Freudians diagnosing neurosis, Maslow insisted on human dignity, agency, and growth. His hierarchy of needs, though sometimes presented as rigid stages (a misreading), intuitively captures how human motivation expands once basic needs are met. The phrase "self-actualization" entered the cultural lexicon and continues to frame conversations about fulfillment, purpose, and success. His focus on peak experiences—those moments when life feels transcendent and meaningful—validated experiences that behaviorism dismissed as unscientific. Maslow's work opened pathways to later positive psychology, which studies flourishing, resilience, meaning-making, and strengths rather than pathology. Though his framework has been criticized for Western individualism (self-actualization may not resonate across all cultures) and for lack of empirical rigor, his reorientation of psychology toward human potential and meaning remains profoundly influential. He made it intellectually respectable to ask not "What's wrong with you?" but "What could you become?"
