# The Clark Doll Experiment

## summary

*Kenneth and Mamie Clark* conducted experiments in the 1940s asking Black children to choose between white and Black dolls, finding that the children often preferred the white dolls and attributed positive qualities to them. The work documented the psychological damage of racial segregation and became central evidence in *Brown v. Board of Education*.

## you gotta know

- Black children aged 3–7 were shown four dolls (identical except for skin colour) and asked which doll was "nice," "pretty," "good," and which they wanted to play with.
- Most children preferred the white dolls; many attributed positive traits to white dolls and negative traits to Black dolls.
- Clark interpreted this as evidence of internalized racial prejudice—segregation had damaged Black children's self-image and sense of worth.
- The children's awareness of race was sharp and their preference for whiteness was consistent, suggesting early and deep social conditioning.
- In 1954, the Supreme Court cited the Clark studies as evidence that "separate educational facilities are inherently unequal" in *Brown v. Board of Education*.
- Later critiques noted methodological limits: small samples, leading questions, and unclear causation (whether segregation caused the preference or reflected broader media influence).
- The study remains iconic for showing psychology's power to document social injustice, even if the mechanism remains debated.
