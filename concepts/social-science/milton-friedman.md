## summary

*Milton Friedman* was a 20th-century American economist and public intellectual who championed free-market capitalism and monetarism, arguing that the control of the money supply (rather than government spending) is the primary lever for economic policy. His ideas shaped conservative economics and challenged Keynesian consensus from the 1960s onward.

## you gotta know

- Developed *monetarism*: the theory that inflation is always and everywhere a monetary phenomenon, caused by excess money supply rather than by wage-price spirals or demand shocks.
- Criticized Keynesian economics and deficit spending, arguing that fiscal stimulus is ineffective and distorts market incentives.
- Championed the concept of the "natural rate of unemployment," arguing that the long-run Phillips curve is vertical and unemployment cannot be persistently reduced through inflation.
- Wrote *Capitalism and Freedom* (1962), a manifesto for minimal government, school choice, and voluntary exchange.
- Proposed the *negative income tax* as an alternative to the welfare bureaucracy—a precursor to modern universal basic income proposals.
- Served as an advisor to the Chilean government under Pinochet (1973–1988), a period of controversial "shock therapy" economic reform.

## connections

- [[john-maynard-keynes]] — his principal intellectual opponent; rejected Keynes's multiplier theory and government spending programs.
- [[friedrich-hayek]] — fellow free-market advocate; shared a skepticism of government planning.
- [[karl-marx]] — diametrically opposed: Marx saw capitalism as inherently exploitative; Friedman saw it as inherently liberating.
- [[john-kenneth-galbraith]] — contemporary critic who challenged Friedman's laissez-faire vision from the left.
