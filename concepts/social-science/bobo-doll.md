# The Bobo Doll Experiment

## summary

*Albert Bandura*, *Dorothy Ross*, and *Sheila Ross* conducted experiments in 1961 in which children watched a video or live model beating an inflatable *Bobo doll*, then were given the same doll to play with. Children who had observed aggression imitated it, demonstrating that behaviour can be learned through observation and modelling, not just through direct reinforcement.

## you gotta know

- Children aged 3–6 watched either live or filmed aggression toward the doll by an adult model; a control group saw no aggression.
- When given access to the doll and other toys, children who had observed aggression imitated it, hitting, kicking, and punching the doll.
- Children who observed the filmed model also imitated the aggression, showing that learning could occur through media—a finding with major implications for television.
- Girls imitated aggression if the model was the same sex; boys imitated regardless of model sex, suggesting gendered social norms around aggression.
- The effect was strongest in the immediate aftermath of observation, but children showed aggression even when the model was no longer present.
- Bandura argued that reinforcement was not necessary for learning; observation and imitation alone were sufficient (contra Skinner's behaviorism).
- The work became foundational to social learning theory and fuelled decades of research (and public concern) about media violence.
