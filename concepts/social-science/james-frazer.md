# James Frazer

## summary

*James Frazer* (1854–1941) was a Scottish anthropologist and classicist whose monumental *The Golden Bough* synthesized ethnographic reports, folklore, and classical texts to construct a universal narrative of how human thought evolved from magic through religion to science. Though his evolutionary framework has been superseded, his collections of comparative mythology and ritual practices remain influential and readable sources.

## you gotta know

- Authored *The Golden Bough* (1890, expanded to 12 volumes by 1915), an encyclopedic comparative study of mythology, magic, ritual, and religion across cultures.
- Proposed that human intellectual history progresses from *magic* (belief in sympathetic causation) through *religion* (propitiation of supernatural beings) to *science* (empirical understanding) — a thesis later critiqued by functionalists and relativists.
- Analyzed the king-priest figure in Mediterranean religion (the "golden bough" itself), linking ritualized regicide, fertility rites, and sacral kingship across cultures.
- Conducted no fieldwork himself; instead synthesized secondary sources, a method that became characteristic of early comparative anthropology but later seen as methodologically limited.
- His notion of *sympathetic magic* — that magical thinking assumes a connection between similar or contiguous objects — influenced later anthropological theories of symbolism and ritual.
- His work became influential in early modernist literature and psychology (*T.S. Eliot*, *Sigmund Freud*) alongside anthropology.
