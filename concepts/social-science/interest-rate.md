# Interest Rate

## summary

An *interest rate* is the cost of borrowing money, expressed as a percentage of the principal per unit time (usually per year). It compensates lenders for the time value of money—the fact that money available today is worth more than the same amount in the future—and for the risk that the borrower defaults. The *nominal interest rate* is the rate quoted in markets; the *real interest rate* is the nominal rate adjusted for inflation and reflects the actual increase in purchasing power a saver gains or a borrower pays. Central banks set short-term interest rates (overnight lending rates) to influence the broader economy; changes in rates ripple through mortgage rates, credit-card rates, and bond yields. Interest rates are one of the most direct tools of monetary policy.

## you gotta know

### Key Concepts
- *Nominal rate*: the stated rate; what you see quoted.
- *Real rate*: nominal rate minus inflation; reflects true purchasing power change. (Fisher equation: real rate ≈ nominal rate – inflation rate.)
- *Time value of money*: $100 today is worth more than $100 in one year because it can earn interest or be spent today.
- *Risk premium*: riskier borrowers (lower credit scores, volatile industries) pay higher rates; U.S. Treasury bonds are the "risk-free" benchmark.

### Determinants
- *Central bank policy*: the Federal Reserve, ECB, and other central banks set target rates; market rates adjust accordingly.
- *Inflation expectations*: if inflation is expected to rise, interest rates rise (lenders demand compensation).
- *Demand for credit*: booming economies (high investment demand) push rates up; contractions push them down.
- *Supply of credit*: banks' willingness to lend; constrained in financial crises.

### Economic Effects
- *Savers benefit* from high interest rates (earn more on savings); borrowers benefit from low rates.
- *Investment is sensitive* to interest rates; lower rates stimulate borrowing for equipment and construction; higher rates discourage it.
- *Asset prices* are inversely related to interest rates (as rates rise, future cash flows are discounted more heavily, lowering present value).

### Types of Rates
- *Overnight rate*: central bank target (e.g., federal funds rate in the U.S.).
- *Prime rate*: banks' best customers pay this; credit-card and variable mortgage rates are indexed to it.
- *Mortgage rates*: tied to 10-year Treasury yields and bank lending standards.
- *Yield curve*: plots interest rates across maturities (short-, medium-, long-term); inversion (short rates > long rates) often precedes recession.

## context

Interest rates are the transmission mechanism of monetary policy. By lowering rates, central banks make borrowing cheaper, stimulating spending and investment; by raising rates, they make borrowing more expensive, cooling demand and inflation. The 2008 financial crisis pushed rates to near-zero globally; central banks kept them low for years, spurring asset bubbles, cheap credit, and eventually inflation. The 2022–2023 rate hikes (from 0% to 4–5% in the U.S.) were the sharpest in decades, signaling the severity of inflation but also threatening economic slowdown. The political tension is stark: borrowers and those dependent on asset prices want low rates; savers and inflation hawks want high rates. Understanding interest rates—how they're set, how they affect different sectors (mortgages, tech valuations, commodity prices)—is essential to interpreting central bank actions and macroeconomic forecasts.
