## summary

*Cockney* is the traditional working-class accent of East London, historically marked by distinctive vowel shifts, glottal stops, and rhyming slang. Though often associated with poverty and uneducated speech, it carries strong community identity and has influenced modern London English and youth speech patterns throughout Britain.

## you gotta know

- Characterized by glottal stops replacing the "t" sound in words like *butter* (bu'er) and *bottle* (bo'ul).
- Fronting of vowels, particularly the *th* sound becoming *f* (*think* → *fink*) and *th* becoming *v* (*them* → *vem*).
- Rhyming slang tradition: using two-word phrases that rhyme with the intended word, e.g., *apple and pears* for "stairs," *trouble and strife* for "wife."
- Traditional Cockney speakers must be born within the sound of Bow Bells (St. Mary-le-Bow Church in the City of London), though this is now largely ceremonial.
- Strong distinction of long and short vowels, with *face* often pronounced closer to *fice* and *goat* toward *gote*.
- The "l" is often velarized (pronounced far back in the mouth), sometimes disappearing entirely before consonants.
