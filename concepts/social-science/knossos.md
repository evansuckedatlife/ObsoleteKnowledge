# Knossos

## summary

*Knossos* is the largest Minoan palace on the island of Crete, Greece, occupied from approximately 2000 to 1370 BCE. The sprawling multi-story complex with its distinctive pillar crypts, vivid frescoes, and sophisticated plumbing represents the cultural and political heart of the Minoan civilization, rivaling contemporary palace complexes in Egypt and Mesopotamia and inspiring later Greek myths of *Minos* and the Minotaur.

## you gotta know

- Located in northern Crete, first excavated in 1900 by British archaeologist Arthur Evans, who reconstructed significant portions of the palace using controversial methods.
- The palace complex covers roughly 5.5 acres and stands up to five stories tall in places, with multiple wings, courts, and ceremonial spaces arranged around a central courtyard.
- Known for distinctive features including tapered wooden columns (wider at the top than the bottom), throne rooms with sacred chambers, and underground storage areas (magazines) for grain and oil.
- Decorated with naturalistic frescoes depicting dolphins, octopi, plants, religious rituals, and courtly scenes—among the finest art of the Bronze Age Aegean.
- Possessed advanced plumbing systems including terra-cotta pipes and drainage channels, suggesting sophisticated urban engineering for the second millennium BCE.
- Destruction layers in the archaeological record show evidence of multiple destructions and rebuildings, with the final abandonment around 1370 BCE, possibly due to invasion or eruption of nearby *Santorini*.
- Its layout and imagery inspired later Greek myths, particularly the legend of *King Minos* and the Minotaur trapped in a labyrinth—a possible reference to the palace's complex corridors.
