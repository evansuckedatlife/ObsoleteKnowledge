# Unemployment

## summary

*Unemployment* is the condition of being without a job despite actively seeking employment. The *unemployment rate* is the percentage of the labor force that is unemployed. Unemployment has multiple types—frictional (job search), structural (skill/location mismatches), and cyclical (caused by recessions)—and reflects both individual hardship and broader economic health. Policymakers monitor unemployment as a key indicator of economic slack; too-high unemployment wastes productive potential, while too-low unemployment can trigger inflation. The relationship between unemployment and inflation (the *Phillips Curve*) is one of the foundational trade-offs in macroeconomic policy.

## you gotta know

### Types of Unemployment
- *Frictional unemployment*: workers between jobs during normal job search; exists even at full employment (typically 2–3%).
- *Structural unemployment*: mismatch between worker skills/location and available jobs; caused by technology shifts or industry decline.
- *Cyclical unemployment*: rise in joblessness during recessions; falls during expansions; the most economically painful type.
- *Seasonal unemployment*: predictable swings in employment due to seasonal industries (agriculture, retail).

### Measurement
- *Unemployment rate* = (unemployed / labor force) × 100; excludes discouraged workers and part-timers.
- *Labor force participation rate*: percentage of working-age population in the labor force (employed + actively seeking).
- Real unemployment is higher if discouraged workers are counted; "U-6" is a broader measure used by the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics.

### Economic Implications
- *Cost of unemployment*: lost output (workers aren't producing), lower consumer spending, skill atrophy, psychological harm.
- *Natural rate of unemployment*: the level consistent with stable inflation; estimated at 4–5% in modern U.S. economy.
- *Trade-off with inflation*: lower unemployment can push inflation higher (workers demand higher wages; firms pass costs to consumers).

### Policy Responses
- *Monetary policy*: central banks lower interest rates to stimulate hiring during high unemployment.
- *Fiscal stimulus*: government spending and tax cuts boost demand and employment.
- *Active labor market policies*: retraining, job placement services, public employment programs.

## context

Unemployment sits at the intersection of microeconomic hardship and macroeconomic stability. For individuals, joblessness is devastating—income loss, loss of benefits, psychological toll. For economies, high unemployment signals slack (resources underutilized) but also constrains inflation (workers have less bargaining power, firms face less wage pressure). The 2008 financial crisis and subsequent "Great Recession" left many economies with 8–10% unemployment for years, raising questions about structural damage and the efficacy of recovery policies. Unemployment is also culturally coded: the political meaning of unemployment depends on its perceived causes (individual failure vs. systemic injustice) and its distribution (whether concentrated in disadvantaged groups or spread broadly).
