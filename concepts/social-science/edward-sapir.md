## summary

*Edward Sapir* was an American linguist and anthropologist who pioneered the study of Native American languages and language's role in shaping thought and culture. His work established the field of linguistic anthropology and, through his collaboration with Benjamin Lee Whorf, raised foundational questions about linguistic relativity — the hypothesis that language structure constrains human cognition.

## you gotta know

- Conducted the first comprehensive linguistic documentation of Nootka, Takelma, Navajo, and other Native American languages, creating rigorous morphological and phonetic analyses.
- Argued that every language has a unique "genius" or internal logic that shapes how speakers conceptualise the world — laying groundwork for linguistic relativity.
- Collaborated with Benjamin Lee Whorf on the relationship between language and thought, culminating in what became known as the Sapir-Whorf hypothesis.
- Sought to understand language as embedded in culture and psychology, not as a discrete formal system — bridging linguistics and anthropology.
- His career spanned both academic linguistics and applied work in Native American language preservation, emphasising the cultural urgency of the task.
