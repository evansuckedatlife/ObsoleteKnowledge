## summary

*David Ricardo* was an early 19th-century British economist whose theories of rent, comparative advantage, and value dominated economics for most of the 1800s. He showed how even nations with absolute disadvantages in all goods benefit from trade by specializing in their comparative advantage, and he analyzed how economic growth distributes wealth among landowners, capitalists, and workers.

## you gotta know

- Developed the principle of *comparative advantage*: nations benefit from specializing in goods they produce relatively cheaply and trading for others, even if they are less efficient at everything.
- Created *Ricardian rent theory*: rent is paid on land because of its fixed supply and differential fertility, not because of landlords' productivity.
- Distinguished between *wages* (paid to laborers) and *profits* (paid to capitalists), showing tension between them as output increases.
- Argued that labor is the ultimate source of value in commodities (the *labor theory of value*), though he acknowledged modifications for durable goods.
- Predicted that as population grew and agriculture expanded onto poorer lands, rents would rise and profit rates would fall—leading eventually to a stationary state.
- Published *Principles of Political Economy and Taxation* (1817), which remained the standard textbook for nearly 50 years.

## connections

- [[adam-smith]] — Ricardo extended Smith's division of labor and free trade into a full theory of distribution.
- [[thomas-malthus]] — contemporary friend and correspondent; Malthus's population theory influenced Ricardo's pessimism about growth.
- [[alfred-marshall]] — later harmonized Ricardian rent and value theory with supply-and-demand curves.
- [[karl-marx]] — adopted Ricardo's labor theory of value and theory of falling profit rates as cornerstones of his critique.
