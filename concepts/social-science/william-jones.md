## summary

*William Jones* was a British philologist and judge in 18th-century India whose 1786 proposal of a genetic relationship between Sanskrit, Greek, and Latin founded the field of comparative linguistics and launched the modern investigation into Indo-European languages. His insight that these languages shared a common ancestor revolutionised the study of language and early human history.

## you gotta know

- Proposed in a famous address to the Asiatic Society of Bengal that Sanskrit, Greek, and Latin were descended from a common "parent language," establishing the Indo-European hypothesis.
- A polymathic scholar fluent in at least thirteen languages, including Sanskrit, Persian, Arabic, and Chinese — enabling him to perceive the deeper structural kinships others had missed.
- Served as a judge in Bengal, which gave him access to Sanskrit texts and opportunity to study the language deeply rather than relying on secondhand accounts.
- His comparative method (systematic alignment of cognates) became the empirical foundation for all subsequent historical linguistics.
- Though he did not live to build out the full comparative framework, his insight immediately inspired others to map out the entire Indo-European family.
