## summary

A *monopoly* is a market structure in which a single firm is the sole supplier of a good with no close substitutes. Unlike perfectly competitive markets where many firms take the market price as given, a monopolist is a "price maker"—it chooses the price-quantity combination that maximizes profit, knowing that lowering price will increase quantity demanded and vice versa. Monopolies typically arise from barriers to entry (patents, economies of scale, network effects, regulatory protections) that prevent competitors from entering the market. While monopolists can earn high profits, they produce less output and charge higher prices than would occur under competition, causing economic inefficiency.

## you gotta know

### Monopoly Characteristics
- *Single seller*: one firm supplies the entire market; no close substitutes exist.
- *Barriers to entry*: legal (patents, licenses), technological (economies of scale), or network-based (switching costs) that protect the monopolist from competition.
- *Price maker*: the monopolist faces the market demand curve and sets quantity and price to maximize profit; competitive firms instead set quantity and take price as given.
- *Economic profit in the long run*: barriers to entry allow the monopolist to sustain above-normal profits indefinitely.

### Monopoly Pricing
- A monopolist produces where marginal revenue equals marginal cost and sets price on the demand curve above that quantity.
- This results in higher price and lower quantity compared to perfect competition.
- Consumer surplus shrinks; some converts to monopolist profit, some is lost as deadweight loss (allocative inefficiency).

### Sources of Monopoly Power
- *Patents*: pharmaceutical firms have monopolies on patented drugs for 20 years.
- *Economies of scale*: natural monopolies (utilities, rail) have such high fixed costs that a single firm can serve the market at lower per-unit cost.
- *Network effects*: social media platforms become more valuable as membership grows, creating winner-take-all dynamics.
- *Licensing and regulation*: governments grant exclusive rights (broadcast licenses, utility franchises).

### Regulation and Antitrust
- *Antitrust law*: governments may block mergers, break up monopolies, or regulate pricing to protect consumer welfare.
- *Price regulation*: setting a price ceiling can reduce monopoly profit but may reduce output (if set too low) or do little if set above the monopoly price.

## context

Monopoly power is central to debates over fairness, efficiency, and the role of government in market economies. The fear of monopoly motivated U.S. antitrust law (Sherman Act, Clayton Act) and continues to animate antitrust scrutiny of tech giants. Yet monopolies aren't inherently evil: patents reward innovation; natural monopolies may be more efficient than multiple competing firms. The real economic harm comes from *productive inefficiency* (monopolists don't minimize costs) and *allocative inefficiency* (they produce too little at too high a price). Understanding monopoly vs. competition is foundational to understanding why some markets are regulated and why "breaking up big tech" remains politically contentious.
