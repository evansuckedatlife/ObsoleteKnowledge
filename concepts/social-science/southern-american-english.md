# Southern American English

## summary

*Southern American English* encompasses the accents and dialects of the American South, ranging from conservative plantation-influenced speech to modern urban Southern. Marked by distinctive prosody (melodic speech patterns), vowel shifts like the Southern Shift, and grammatical features including double modals, it carries strong regional identity and cultural associations.

## you gotta know

- The Southern Shift: systematic fronting of back vowels and backing of front vowels, making *fleece* sound more like *flice* and *face* toward *fice*.
- Monophthongization of *i* and *oi* sounds, so *time* becomes *tahm* and *oil* sounds like *ahl*.
- Intrusive "r" insertion, adding an *r* sound between vowels in words like *idea* (*idear*).
- Use of y'all (you all) as a plural second-person pronoun; ain't as a general negative.
- Double modals: constructions like *might could* or *might would* that are ungrammatical in standard English.
- Rhoticity varies regionally; traditional coastal Southern may be non-rhotic, while inland Southern is typically rhotic.

## connections

- [[general-american]] — the prestige American standard from which Southern English diverges.
- [[aave]] — shares historical roots in the South and some phonological patterns, though with distinct grammatical features.
- [[appalachian-english]] — a regional variant within broader Southern speech with additional archaic features.
