# Learned Helplessness Experiments

## summary

*Martin Seligman* and *Steven Maier* conducted experiments in the 1960s in which dogs exposed to inescapable electric shocks later failed to escape shocks even when escape was possible, instead remaining passive. The phenomenon, termed *learned helplessness*, revealed that organisms can lose motivation and agency after repeated exposure to uncontrollable aversive stimuli, with implications for depression and resilience.

## you gotta know

- Dogs were divided into three groups: some received escapable shocks, some inescapable, and a control group received no shocks.
- In a second phase, all groups were placed in a shuttle-box where they could escape shocks by jumping a barrier.
- Dogs that had previously experienced inescapable shocks showed no escape attempts and passively endured the new shocks; the other two groups quickly learned to escape.
- Seligman proposed that inescapable shocks taught the dogs that their actions were futile, producing a generalised expectancy of helplessness that persisted.
- Learned helplessness was not inevitable; dogs with some control history or prior success showed resilience, suggesting prior mastery confers protection.
- Seligman extended the model to depression in humans: loss, failure, and uncontrollability can produce passivity, learned helplessness, and depressive symptoms.
- The work became central to cognitive models of depression and spawned research on optimism, resilience, and the therapeutic value of agency.
