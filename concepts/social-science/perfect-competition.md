# Perfect competition

## summary

*Perfect competition* is an idealized market structure in economic theory where many buyers and sellers trade homogeneous goods, all have perfect information, and entry and exit are costless. Though rarely if ever seen in reality, perfect competition is the benchmark against which actual markets are measured and the foundation for much microeconomic analysis.

## you gotta know

- Requires: many small firms (no single firm can influence price), identical products, perfect information, free entry and exit, and perfectly elastic factor supplies.
- In perfect competition, price equals marginal cost (firms earn zero economic profit in long-run equilibrium), and allocative efficiency holds—no resources are wasted.
- Firms are *price-takers*, not price-makers; they cannot set prices; they can only choose quantity to supply at the market-determined price.
- *Adam Smith's* "invisible hand"—the idea that self-interest channels individual actions toward the common good—presupposes something like perfect competition.
- Real markets (monopoly, oligopoly, monopolistic competition) depart from perfect competition because of barriers to entry, product differentiation, and information asymmetries.
- Perfectly competitive outcomes are *Pareto efficient*—no reallocation can make someone better off without harming another—making the model a yardstick for policy evaluation.

## connections

- [[adam-smith]] — whose invisible-hand concept assumes competitive markets.
- [[david-ricardo]] — built comparative advantage theory on a foundation of competitive markets.
- [[alfred-marshall]] — formalized the mathematical model of perfectly competitive equilibrium.
- [[monopoly]] — the opposite extreme: a single seller with power to set price and earn monopoly profit.
- [[invisible-hand]] — the mechanism by which self-interest in competitive markets produces socially beneficial outcomes.
