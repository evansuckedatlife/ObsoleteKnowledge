# Lascaux

## summary

*Lascaux* is a cave in southwestern France containing some of the world's finest Paleolithic wall paintings, created roughly 17,000 years ago during the Upper Paleolithic period. Its vivid polychrome murals of horses, bison, deer, and abstract symbols represent the artistic pinnacle of Ice Age hunter-gatherers and provide unparalleled evidence of aesthetic sophistication, symbolic thinking, and perhaps spiritual or shamanic beliefs in prehistoric humans.

## you gotta know

- Located in the Dordogne region of southwestern France, discovered accidentally in 1940 by teenagers and a dog; the narrow entrance had been sealed by rockfall for millennia.
- Contains approximately 600 paintings and 1,500 engravings covering roughly 17,000 square meters of cave wall space, the majority of which remain in situ.
- The paintings use natural earth pigments (charcoal, iron oxide, manganese dioxide) applied with fingers, brushes made from plant material, and possibly blown through tubes to create spray patterns.
- Depicts predominantly large herbivores—horses are most common, followed by bison, deer, and ibex—with occasional predators and very few human figures, contrary to common misconception.
- Astronomically precise—certain panels are astronomically aligned to celestial events, suggesting seasonal gatherings or ritual calendars encoded in the paintings.
- The famous "Hall of the Bulls" features four enormous aurochs (wild cattle) up to 17 feet long, the largest known Paleolithic paintings.
- Closed to the general public in 1963 due to irreversible algae and fungal damage caused by visitor breath and body heat; a full-scale replica (Lascaux IV) opened in 2016 allowing continued study.
