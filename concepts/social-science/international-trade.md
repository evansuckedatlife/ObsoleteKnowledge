# International trade

## summary

*International trade* is the exchange of goods and services across national borders, governed by differences in comparative advantage, factor endowments, and trade policies. A foundational topic in economics, it addresses why nations trade, who benefits, and how tariffs, quotas, and trade agreements shape commerce.

## you gotta know

- *David Ricardo's* theory of *comparative advantage* (early 19th century) showed that nations gain from specializing in what they produce relatively cheaply, even if one is absolutely more productive.
- Countries export goods that use abundant factors of production intensively; import goods that use scarce factors intensively (Heckscher-Ohlin theorem).
- Trade barriers—tariffs, quotas, export subsidies—protect domestic producers from foreign competition but create deadweight loss; free trade maximizes global efficiency.
- *Comparative advantage* explains why trade is mutually beneficial even when one country is more productive at everything; both countries gain by specializing.
- Trade politics: import-competing industries lobby for protection; export industries advocate for open markets; consumers benefit from lower prices.
- Modern trade: multilateral agreements (GATT, WTO), regional trade blocs (EU, NAFTA/USMCA), supply chains spanning multiple countries.

## connections

- [[david-ricardo]] — originator of comparative advantage theory, the centerpiece of trade economics.
- [[adam-smith]] — earlier advocate of free trade; his division of labor extends across national borders.
- [[comparative-advantage]] — the foundational principle explaining gains from international trade.
- [[tariff]] — a chief trade policy tool; debates over tariffs center on protection vs. free-trade gains.
- [[friedrich-hayek]] — defender of free markets and critic of trade protectionism on efficiency grounds.
