## summary

*Pirahã* is an Amazonian language spoken by the Pirahã people in Brazil, famous in linguistic circles for its alleged absence of color terms, number words, and recursive grammar—properties once thought universal to all human languages. Whether these properties hold remains contested, but Pirahã has become a focal point in debates over linguistic universals and the relationship between language and cognition.

## you gotta know

- Spoken by roughly 300–400 speakers on the Maici River in Amazonas State, Brazil; classified as critically endangered.
- Lacks consistent tense marking; events are described in terms of immediate experience rather than abstract temporal categories.
- Supposedly lacks numerals and color terms, expressing quantity and hue through other linguistic means; linguist Daniel Everett argued it lacks recursion, challenging Chomskyan universals.
- Uses a tonal system with evidential markers (prefixes indicating source of knowledge: direct experience, inference, hearsay).
- Everett's claims about Pirahã sparked decades of debate and critique from other linguists, who contested whether the language truly lacks these features or simply expresses them differently.
- High rate of language shift toward Portuguese among younger speakers; fluent native speaker numbers declining.
