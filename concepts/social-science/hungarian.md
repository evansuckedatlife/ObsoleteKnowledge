## summary

*Hungarian* is a Uralic language spoken primarily in Hungary and neighboring regions, linguistically isolated within Central Europe by being one of the few non-Indo-European languages in the region. It is celebrated for its agglutinative morphology, with extensive case systems and postpositions that challenge learners from Indo-European backgrounds.

## you gotta know

- A Uralic language with about 10 million native speakers concentrated in Hungary, plus diaspora communities; most remotely related to Finnish and Estonian.
- Uses an agglutinative structure with 18–20 noun cases (depending on classification) and numerous postpositions rather than prepositions, creating lengthy, information-packed words.
- Vowel harmony is a prominent feature: vowels in suffix patterns agree with the vowels in the stem (front vs. back vowels).
- Word order is flexible (SVO, SOV, and other patterns occur naturally); verbs conjugate for person, number, and definiteness (whether the object is definite or indefinite).
- Has an extensive vocabulary of Finno-Ugric roots alongside significant borrowing from Slavic, Germanic, and Romance languages due to geographic contact.
- Spoken continuously in the Carpathian Basin since the Magyar migration in the 9th century; the language has evolved little in core structure across that millennium.

## connections

- [[uralic-languages]] — the most widely spoken Uralic language; its nearest relatives are Finnish and Estonian.
- [[agglutinative-languages]] — exemplifies productive agglutination with complex case systems.
- [[language-isolation]] — linguistically unique within Central Europe; caused some medieval scholars to speculate on non-Indo-European origins.
- [[linguistic-typology]] — a standard example in typological surveys of agglutinative and postpositional languages.
