## summary

*Ferdinand de Saussure* was a Swiss linguist whose *Course in General Linguistics* (published posthumously in 1916) established the foundational principles of structural linguistics and semiotics. He reframed language as a self-contained system of signs, distinguishing sharply between the system (langue) and individual utterance (parole), creating the framework that dominated 20th-century linguistics.

## you gotta know

- Distinguished between *langue* (the underlying system or code of a language) and *parole* (individual speech acts) — a distinction fundamental to modern linguistic thought.
- Defined the sign as comprising two inseparable parts: the *signifier* (the sound/form) and the *signified* (the concept), establishing semiotics as a discipline.
- Argued that linguistic values are purely relational — the meaning of a word arises not from its content but from its difference from other words (*différence*).
- Emphasised the arbitrary relationship between signifier and signified (there is no inherent reason "dog" sounds like that and means that animal).
- His work was published posthumously by students from lecture notes, yet it reshaped how linguists conceptualise language as a system rather than a collection of words.
