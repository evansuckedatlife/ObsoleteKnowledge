# Çatalhöyük

## summary

*Çatalhöyük* is a Neolithic settlement in central Turkey, occupied from approximately 7500 to 5700 BCE, representing one of the world's oldest cities and earliest examples of urban living. Its tightly packed mud-brick dwellings arranged in a honeycomb pattern, with shared walls but individual hearths and no streets, reveal radical experiments in communal living and social organization that predate the civilizations of Mesopotamia.

## you gotta know

- Located on the Konya Plain in central Anatolia, one of the largest Neolithic settlements ever discovered, covering 32 acres at its peak.
- Occupied continuously for nearly 2,000 years (c. 7500–5700 BCE) with multiple layers of habitation, providing an unparalleled archaeological record.
- Houses were mud-brick structures built directly adjacent to one another with no street access—inhabitants entered through roof hatches and used roofways to move between buildings.
- Walls were decorated with elaborate murals featuring hunting scenes, geometric patterns, and hand prints; some of the oldest known monumental art.
- Burial practices included placing the dead beneath house floors, sometimes within months of death, suggesting strong ties between the living and ancestor veneration.
- Population estimates suggest 5,000–10,000 inhabitants at peak, rivaling or exceeding the size of contemporary settlements in Mesopotamia.
- Excavations by archaeologist James Mellaart (1961–1965) and renewed work under Ian Hodder revealed sophisticated gender roles, communal grain storage, and early religious practices.
