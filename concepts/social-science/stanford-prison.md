# The Stanford Prison Experiment

## summary

*Philip Zimbardo* conducted an experiment in 1971 in which college students were randomly assigned to play prisoners or guards in a mock prison built in the basement of Stanford's psychology building. The guards quickly became authoritarian and abusive, while prisoners became submissive and traumatized, leading Zimbardo to conclude that situations, not personalities, drive evil behaviour.

## you gotta know

- Twenty-four male undergraduates were randomly assigned to be prisoners or guards; participants were screened for psychological health beforehand.
- Guards were given uniforms, dark sunglasses, and billy clubs; prisoners wore smocks and chains, and were subjected to dehumanizing procedures (strip searches, numbering).
- Within days, guards engaged in psychological abuse, humiliation, and arbitrary punishment; prisoners became compliant, depressed, and emotionally broken.
- One prisoner had a mental breakdown and was released; another led a rebellion that guards violently suppressed; others became passive and dependent.
- Zimbardo planned a two-week study but halted it after six days when a graduate student (Christina Maslach) challenged the ethicality of what she witnessed.
- Zimbardo concluded that ordinary people become perpetrators of abuse when placed in dehumanizing roles, even without prior inclination toward cruelty.
- The study became iconic for the "Lucifer Effect"—the idea that situations can corrupt good people—but has faced methodological and replication critiques in recent decades.
