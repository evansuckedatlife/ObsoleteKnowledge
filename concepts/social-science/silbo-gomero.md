# Silbo Gomero

## summary

*Silbo Gomero* is a whistled language spoken on the island of La Gomera in the Canary Islands, used by shepherd and fishermen communities to communicate across long distances that would be inaudible if spoken normally. It encodes Spanish phonologically but in whistled form, and was nearly extinct before being designated a UNESCO Intangible Cultural Heritage and reintroduced into school curricula.

## you gotta know

- A whistled version of Spanish that encodes phonetic information through pitch and duration variations; speakers can communicate over distances of 1–2 kilometers.
- Developed in isolation on La Gomera as a practical tool for rural communities separated by deep valleys; shepherds and agricultural workers used it for coordination.
- Encodes Spanish consonants and vowels through whistle patterns; different whistle pitches correspond to different phonemes.
- Nearly extinct by the 1980s as younger generations abandoned shepherding and rural life; only a few hundred fluent speakers remained.
- Revitalized through UNESCO recognition in 2001 and formal reintroduction to school curricula in the 2000s; now taught to maintain the tradition.
- Related whistled languages exist in Mexico (*Zapotec whistled speech*), Papua New Guinea, and a few other communities; Silbo Gomero is the most documented and institutionally supported.
