# Inflation

## summary

*Inflation* is the general and sustained increase in the price level of goods and services in an economy over time. It is measured as a percentage rate per year; for example, 3% inflation means the average price of a bundle of goods rises by 3% in a year. Inflation erodes the purchasing power of money—$100 today buys less next year if inflation is positive. While moderate inflation (1–3%) is often considered healthy and is compatible with steady growth, high inflation (above 5–10%) creates uncertainty and distorts economic decisions. *Deflation* (negative inflation) is rare in modern economies but can be economically damaging, trapping consumers and firms in a cycle of deferred spending.

## you gotta know

### Measurement
- *Consumer Price Index (CPI)*: tracks price changes in a basket of goods and services bought by households.
- *Producer Price Index (PPI)*: tracks prices at the wholesale/production level.
- *Inflation rate* = (price level this year – price level last year) / price level last year × 100.
- *Core inflation*: excludes volatile items (food and energy) to reveal underlying inflationary pressure.

### Causes
- *Demand-pull inflation*: "too much money chasing too few goods"—aggregate demand exceeds aggregate supply; often occurs in booms.
- *Cost-push inflation*: rises in production costs (wages, oil prices) push firms to raise prices.
- *Monetary inflation*: rapid growth in the money supply without corresponding growth in output.

### Effects
- *Winners*: borrowers (who repay loans with devalued currency); firms that can raise prices quickly.
- *Losers*: savers (whose savings lose purchasing power); creditors; workers with fixed wages.
- *Moderate inflation*: often accepted as the cost of full employment; encourages spending and investment.
- *High inflation*: creates uncertainty, erodes real wages, triggers wage-price spirals (workers demand higher wages, firms raise prices).

### Policy Trade-offs
- *Phillips Curve*: there is a trade-off between low unemployment (which raises inflation) and low inflation (which requires accepting higher unemployment).
- Central banks raise interest rates to cool inflation by reducing spending; lower rates stimulate inflation.

## context

Inflation is the great trade-off of monetary policy. After decades of (mostly) low inflation following the 1980s, the 2021–2023 surge in inflation above 5–8% worldwide forced central banks to aggressively raise interest rates, slowing growth and raising unemployment. This reignited the unemployment-inflation debate: can central banks bring inflation down without causing a recession? Historically, the answer has often been no—aggressive inflation fighting (as in the Volcker era of the early 1980s) required severe recessions. Inflation also has cultural resonance: high inflation is often blamed on governments, unions, or "greedy" firms and becomes a symbol of economic failure, even if its root cause is monetary excess or external shocks. Understanding inflation is essential to understanding monetary policy, real vs. nominal interest rates, and why savers and borrowers have opposite preferences about inflation.
