# Australian English

## summary

*Australian English* is the accent and dialect of Australia, marked by a distinctive vowel system, non-rhotic pronunciation influenced by British English, and colloquial vocabulary unique to Australia. Though historically stigmatized as inferior compared to British English, it has gained acceptance and has become a symbol of Australian identity, with younger generations showing increasing pride in the accent.

## you gotta know

- Non-rhotic like RP: the "r" is silent except before vowels, so *car* is pronounced *cah*.
- The short "i" vowel shifts toward "e" sound, making *kit* sound like *ket* and *bid* like *bed*.
- Distinctive rising intonation on statements, making many declarative sentences sound like questions (*High Rising Terminal*).
- Vowel shifts in other positions: *trap* shifts toward *drip* and *strut* toward *sport*.
- Colloquial vocabulary and diminutives: *servo* (service station), *brekkie* (breakfast), *arvo* (afternoon).
- Rhotic variants exist in Tasmania and some rural areas, but non-rhotic prestige speech dominates urban centers like Sydney and Melbourne.

## connections

- [[general-american]] — the major prestige American standard; Australian English maintains stronger British influence.
- [[received-pronunciation]] — Australian English descends from British English and shares the non-rhotic feature.
- [[new-zealand-english]] — a closely related variety in the Oceania region with many shared features.
