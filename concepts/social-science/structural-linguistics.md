# Structural linguistics

## summary

*Structural linguistics* is an approach to the study of language that treats it as a system of interrelated elements—sounds, words, and structures—whose meaning derives from their relations to one another rather than from inherent properties. Dominant from the early 20th century through the 1950s, it laid the groundwork for modern linguistic science before being reshaped by generative approaches.

## you gotta know

- Pioneered by *Ferdinand de Saussure*, who distinguished between langue (the system of language) and parole (actual speech).
- Analyzes language as a *closed system*—a network of binary oppositions and relations where meaning emerges from differences (e.g., `/p/` vs. `/b/` create distinct meanings).
- *Leonard Bloomfield* developed American structural linguistics, emphasizing observable, distributional methods and rejecting introspection.
- Extends beyond words to entire systems: phonological, grammatical, and semantic structures all exhibit hierarchical organization.
- Influenced broader structuralist movements in anthropology (Claude Lévi-Strauss), psychoanalysis, and literary criticism.
- *Noam Chomsky* critiqued structural approaches as inadequate to explain language's recursive depth and productivity.

## connections

- [[linguistics]] — the broader discipline of which structuralism is a historical and methodological school.
- [[ferdinand-de-saussure]] — founder of modern structural linguistics and semiotics.
- [[leonard-bloomfield]] — American structuralist who systematized the approach through distribution and phoneme analysis.
- [[noam-chomsky]] — transformed linguistics by rejecting strict behaviorism and structural methods in favor of generative principles.
- [[claude-levi-strauss]] — applied structural methods to myth and kinship, extending the paradigm beyond language.
