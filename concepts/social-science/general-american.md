## summary

*General American* is the prestige accent of the United States, standardized in network television and radio broadcasting in the mid-20th century. Rhotic, with relatively uniform vowel values and minimal regional coloring, it lacks the strong local identity markers of regional accents and remains the reference standard for American English in formal and educational contexts.

## you gotta know

- Rhotic pronunciation: the "r" is pronounced in all positions, including after vowels (*car*, *here*) and word-finally (*butter*).
- Cot-caught merger: *cot* and *caught* are pronounced identically, a feature absent from RP and conservative American accents.
- Flapping: the "t" and "d" sounds become a single tap in unstressed syllables, so *butter* and *udder* sound nearly identical.
- Tense-lax vowel distinctions are relatively symmetrical and clear compared to British English vowel systems.
- The "r" is typically pronounced as a retroflex approximant (tongue curled back), less prominent than in rhotic British accents.
- Emerged as a broadcast standard partly due to its perceived neutrality and lack of strong regional or class associations.

## connections

- [[received-pronunciation]] — the British prestige standard; General American and RP are the two main reference accents for global English.
- [[southern-american-english]] — shows systematic vowel shifts and grammatical structures distinct from General American.
- [[upper-midwestern-american-english]] — a regional variant that approaches General American closely, historically preferred in broadcasting.
