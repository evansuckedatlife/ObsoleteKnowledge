# Tết

## summary

**Tết**, or Tết Nguyên Đán ("Festival of the First Morning"), is the Vietnamese New Year celebrated at the same moment as Chinese New Year, marking the start of the lunar calendar. The holiday spans several days and emphasizes family reunion, ancestor veneration, and renewal. It is the most important festival in Vietnamese culture, featuring special foods, cleaning rituals, and visits to temples and family shrines.

## you gotta know

- Celebrated on the second new moon after the winter solstice, occurring on the same date as Chinese New Year (late January or February).
- The word *Tết* means "festival," but Tết Nguyên Đán ("Tết of the first morning") is the primary New Year observance.
- Families prepare *bánh chưng* (square sticky rice cakes wrapped in dong leaves) and *bánh dày* (round glutinous rice cakes), which are essential ceremonial foods.
- Before Tết, homes are meticulously cleaned and decorated with yellow flowers (*hoa mai* orchids or *hoa đào* peach blossoms).
- Ancestor altars are tended carefully; families bring offerings of fruit, flowers, incense, and food to honor the deceased.
- Children receive *lì xì* (lucky money in red envelopes) from elders, similar to Chinese *hongbao*.
- The first visitor to enter a home on New Year's Day is believed to influence the household's fortune for the year—traditionally someone happy and successful is invited.
