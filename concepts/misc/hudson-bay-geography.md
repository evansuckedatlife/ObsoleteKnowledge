# Hudson Bay Geography

## summary

A common misconception places *Hudson Bay* in the Arctic Ocean or confuses it with Hudson Strait. Hudson Bay is a large inland sea in northern Canada, connected to the Atlantic Ocean via Hudson Strait; it is not an Arctic water body proper but a subarctic embayment surrounded by land on three sides, and it is not a narrow strait but a vast bay with an area of roughly 475,000 square kilometers.

## you gotta know

- *Hudson Bay* is a large body of water in northern Canada, roughly bordered by Manitoba, Ontario, Quebec, and Nunavut; it opens to the Atlantic Ocean through Hudson Strait.
- The bay is *not* part of the Arctic Ocean itself; it is a subarctic marine environment distinct from true Arctic waters, though it contains seasonal pack ice and supports Arctic wildlife.
- *Hudson Strait*, which connects the bay to the Atlantic, is often confused with Hudson Bay itself—the strait is a narrow passage, whereas the bay is a vast inland sea.
- The explorer *Henry Hudson* sought the Northwest Passage in the early 1600s and discovered the bay and strait; he was set adrift and perished, and the waters were named in his honor posthumously.
- Hudson Bay was historically crucial to the North American fur trade; the Hudson's Bay Company (chartered 1670) controlled vast territories and dominated trade for over two centuries.
- The bay's waters are among the most productive in North America ecologically, supporting whales, seals, polar bears, and bird populations despite cold temperatures and seasonal ice.
