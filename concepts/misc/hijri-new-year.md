# Hijri New Year

## summary

The **Hijri New Year** (Islamic New Year), also called the celebration of *Muharram*, marks the beginning of the Islamic lunar calendar and commemorates the *Hijra*—Prophet Muhammad's migration from Mecca to Medina in 622 CE. Unlike Western and most lunar new years, the Hijri New Year is observed quietly and spiritually rather than with festive celebrations. It occurs on the first day of *Muharram*, the first month of the Islamic calendar, advancing approximately eleven days earlier each Gregorian year.

## you gotta know

- The Islamic calendar is purely lunar, comprising twelve months of 29 or 30 days, causing the Hijri New Year to occur roughly eleven days earlier each Gregorian year.
- The Islamic calendar is dated from the *Hijra* (migration), the year 622 CE in the Gregorian calendar; 2024–2025 corresponds to Islamic year 1446.
- The observance is primarily spiritual and contemplative; many Muslims spend the day in prayer, reflection, and remembrance of the Hijra's historical and religious significance.
- *Muharram* is one of the four sacred months (*Ashhur al-Hurum*) in Islam, during which warfare and hunting are traditionally forbidden.
- The tenth day of Muharram, called *Ashura*, is particularly significant in *Shia Islam* as the day of mourning for *Imam Hussain*, the grandson of Muhammad, who was killed in battle.
- The New Year is observed across the Islamic world with prayer gatherings, Quranic recitations, and reflection, though public festivities are more subdued than Western New Year celebrations.
- Islamic societies traditionally mark the occasion with family gatherings and special foods, though customs vary widely by region and sect.
