# Five Classical Orders of Architecture

## summary

The *Five Classical Orders of Architecture* are the standardized systems of proportion, style, and ornamentation used in ancient Greek and Roman buildings, codified by Renaissance and Baroque theorists. Three orders—*Doric*, *Ionic*, and *Corinthian*—originated in classical Greece; two later composite orders—*Tuscan* and *Composite*—were developed by Romans. These orders have dominated Western architecture for over two millennia, providing a shared visual language for columns, capitals, and overall structure.

## you gotta know

- *Doric* order: the simplest and most austere, featuring fluted columns with no base, plain echinus capitals, and a sturdy male-coded aesthetic; employed in Greek temples and Roman civic structures.
- *Ionic* order: more slender and graceful than Doric, with a base, elaborate capital crowned by scrolls (volutes), and fluted shafts; associated with female elegance in classical aesthetics.
- *Corinthian* order: the most ornate and luxurious, with an ornamental capital decorated with acanthus leaves, used extensively in Roman temples and later aristocratic buildings.
- *Tuscan* order: a Roman simplification of the Doric, with unfluted columns, a base, and minimal ornament; practical and economical for utilitarian Roman structures.
- *Composite* order: a Roman synthesis combining the Ionic scrolls with the Corinthian acanthus leaves; used in triumphal arches and grand imperial buildings.
- The orders are defined by strict mathematical proportions (height-to-width ratios, capital height, entablature composition) and were systematized in treatises by Vitruvius, Palladio, and later theorists.
