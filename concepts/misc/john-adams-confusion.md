# John Adams Confusion

## summary

Multiple historical figures named John or Adams from the early American era are frequently conflated. *John Adams* was the second U.S. president and Founding Father; his son *John Quincy Adams* was the sixth president; and *Samuel Adams* was a different Founding Father and organizer of the Boston Tea Party. These three figures are distinct and played different roles, yet their similar names and overlapping historical period cause confusion.

## you gotta know

- *John Adams* (1735–1826) was a diplomat, lawyer, and the second U.S. president (1797–1801); he was a signer of the Declaration of Independence and a negotiator of the Treaty of Paris ending the Revolutionary War.
- *Samuel Adams* (1722–1803) was John Adams's cousin and a key organizer of anti-British resistance including the Boston Tea Party (1773); he was a statesman but never served as president.
- *John Quincy Adams* (1767–1830) was the son of John Adams and served as the sixth U.S. president (1825–1829); after his presidency, he served in the House of Representatives and was an abolitionist.
- The confusion intensifies because both John Adams and John Quincy Adams were presidents, making chronological and electoral distinctions easy to muddle.
- Samuel Adams and John Adams were contemporaries and worked together during the Revolution, but Samuel Adams held fewer federal offices; he was a state politician in Massachusetts.
- John Quincy Adams outlived his father John Adams by three years; both died in 1826, the 50th anniversary of the Declaration of Independence—a notable coincidence that historically intertwines their legacies.
