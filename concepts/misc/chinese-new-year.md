# Chinese New Year

## summary

**Chinese New Year**, also called the Spring Festival or Lunar New Year, is the most important celebration in Chinese culture, marking the beginning of the lunar calendar. Families gather for elaborate feasts, clean their homes to sweep away bad luck, and exchange red envelopes filled with money for prosperity. The celebration spans fifteen days and involves dragon parades, fireworks, and the reunion of families across generational lines.

## you gotta know

- Occurs on the second new moon after the winter solstice, falling between January 21 and February 20 on the Gregorian calendar.
- The zodiac cycles through twelve animals (*rat, ox, tiger, rabbit, dragon, snake, horse, goat, monkey, rooster, dog, pig*), and the year is named for whichever animal rules that year.
- Red symbolizes luck and joy; red envelopes (*hongbao*) containing money are given to children and the unmarried by elders and bosses.
- Families prepare special dishes with auspicious meanings—whole fish (*abundance*), tangerines (*wealth*), and long noodles (*longevity*).
- The house is thoroughly cleaned before New Year's Day to sweep away misfortune; sweeping on the holiday itself is forbidden to avoid sweeping luck away.
- Dragon dances involving multi-person puppets and elaborate costumes are paraded through streets to bring prosperity and ward off evil.
- Fireworks and firecrackers were historically lit to scare away the *Nian* demon; modern fireworks remain a central spectacle.
