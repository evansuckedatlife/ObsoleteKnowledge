# Platonic Solids

## summary

The *Platonic Solids* are the five three-dimensional geometric shapes in which all faces are identical regular polygons, all edges are equal, and all angles are equal. Named after the ancient Greek philosopher *Plato*, these solids have fascinated mathematicians and natural philosophers since antiquity and represent the only possible configurations satisfying these symmetry constraints. They appear throughout nature—in crystal structures, viral particles, and atomic geometry—and have inspired artistic and mystical interpretations for thousands of years.

## you gotta know

- The *Tetrahedron*: four equilateral triangular faces, four vertices, six edges; the simplest Platonic solid and traditionally associated with fire.
- The *Cube* (Hexahedron): six square faces, eight vertices, twelve edges; the most familiar solid and associated with earth.
- The *Octahedron*: eight equilateral triangular faces, six vertices, twelve edges; symmetrical like two square pyramids base-to-base, associated with air.
- The *Dodecahedron*: twelve regular pentagonal faces, twenty vertices, thirty edges; the most complex and associated with the cosmos or universe as a whole.
- The *Icosahedron*: twenty equilateral triangular faces, twelve vertices, thirty edges; associated with water.
- Euclid proved these are the only five regular polyhedra—no sixth exists—making them a complete and finite set.
- They were known to ancient Greek mathematicians and appear in Pythagorean and Platonic cosmology, though modern crystallography and chemistry confirm their deep mathematical necessity.

## connections

- [[plato]] — philosopher after whom they are named, though known long before him.
- [[euclid]] — mathematician who proved their completeness in the *Elements*.
- [[pythagoras]] — ancient mystic who associated them with classical elements.
- [[geometry]] — the mathematical discipline in which they are studied.
- [[symmetry]] — the principle underlying their perfection.
