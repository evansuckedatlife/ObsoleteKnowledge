# Et tu, Brute?

## summary

The famous phrase "Et tu, Brute?" (And you, Brutus?) is attributed to Julius Caesar upon recognizing his ally *Marcus Brutus* among his assassins. However, there is no historical evidence Caesar actually said this; William Shakespeare invented the line for his 1599 play *Julius Caesar*, and it has since become the canonical final words, conflating Shakespeare's theatrical fiction with historical fact.

## you gotta know

- The Latin phrase "Et tu, Brute?" does not appear in any contemporary historical account of Caesar's assassination in 44 BCE—it is entirely Shakespeare's invention for dramatic effect.
- *Marcus Brutus*, a respected senator and a friend of Caesar, was indeed part of the conspiracy against Caesar; his betrayal is central to the tragedy.
- Shakespeare placed the phrase in Act III, Scene 2 of *Julius Caesar* to add pathos—Caesar's shock at Brutus's involvement amplifies his tragic downfall.
- Popular culture has adopted the phrase as historical fact, with countless references treating it as a genuine quotation from Caesar's final moments.
- The conspiracy against Caesar was driven by senators' fears of his accumulating power and desire to restore the Republic, not personal animosity from Brutus alone.
- The assassination itself occurred on the *Ides of March* (March 15), and Caesar was stabbed by multiple senators, including Brutus.
