# Painting Titles

## summary

The *titling of famous paintings* creates confusion because artworks are often known by multiple names—popular titles differ from official ones, artists did not always title their works, and translations or scholarly re-naming complicate identification. This leads to persistent errors in art history quizzes and cultural references.

## you gotta know

- Many Old Masters did not give their paintings formal titles; titles were assigned later by curators, collectors, or art historians based on subject matter or provenance, so the same painting may have several names.
- Official museum titles often differ from popular names (e.g., Edvard Munch's work is famously called *The Scream*, but its formal Norwegian title is *Skrik*, literally "Shriek" or "Cry").
- Translation introduces ambiguity: *Les Demoiselles d'Avignon* (Picasso) translates as "The Young Ladies of Avignon" or "The Avignon Prostitutes" depending on translator's interpretation of "demoiselles."
- Some paintings are titled after their owners or locations rather than content (e.g., *The Rokeby Venus* by Velázquez is named for its former location at Rokeby Hall, not its subject).
- Restoration and cleaning of paintings sometimes reveal original signatures or dated inscriptions that affect how a work is identified; reattribution of authorship can change the registered title.
- Common mistakes: calling Kandinsky's *Composition VII* by vague names like "abstract painting"; confusing Franz Hals's *Laughing Cavalier* with Rembrandt; referring to paintings by generic descriptions (e.g., "the one with the melting clocks") rather than proper titles.
