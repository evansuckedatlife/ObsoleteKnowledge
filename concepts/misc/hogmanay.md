# Hogmanay

## summary

**Hogmanay** is the Scottish celebration of New Year on December 31 and January 1, featuring street festivals, midnight bonfires, and communal singing. The word's origin is uncertain, though it may derive from Old French or Norse roots. Hogmanay traditions—particularly the "first footing" custom and the singing of "Auld Lang Syne"—have become emblematic of Western New Year celebration worldwide.

## you gotta know

- The main celebration occurs on December 31, with festivities continuing into January 1 and sometimes beyond.
- "First footing" (the first person to cross a household's threshold after midnight) is believed to influence the household's fortune; traditionally a tall dark-haired man is preferred.
- Bonfires are traditionally lit on hilltops to ward off evil and welcome the new year; Edinburgh's Hogmanay festival includes massive bonfire spectacles.
- The song "Auld Lang Syne," written by Scottish poet *Robert Burns*, is sung at midnight, symbolizing remembrance of past friendships and good times.
- Homes are cleaned and debts settled before the holiday, echoing older pagan purification traditions.
- Seenie-stones (pebbles) are thrown into the fire for symbolic cleansing, and doorways are marked with ash for protection.
- Street parties and processions occur in Scottish towns and cities, with particular grandeur in Edinburgh, Glasgow, and Stirling.
