# Songkran

## summary

**Songkran**, known as the Water Festival, is the Thai New Year celebration observed from April 13–15, marking the beginning of the Buddhist calendar and the astronomical arrival of spring. The festival is characterized by joyful water splashing—a symbolic cleansing ritual—elaborate parades, merit-making ceremonies at temples, and family gatherings. Songkran is considered one of the world's wettest and most exuberant celebrations, transforming Thai cities into water-soaked festivities.

## you gotta know

- Celebrated from April 13–15 annually, marking the Thai solar new year and the beginning of the Buddhist calendar year.
- Water symbolizes purification and renewal; splashing water on others is the central tradition, believed to wash away misfortune and negativity from the old year.
- The festival is rooted in *Theravada Buddhism*; many Thai people begin by pouring scented water over Buddha images and monk statues as an act of merit-making.
- Families gather to celebrate, elders are honored by having water poured over their hands, and young people ask for blessings by sprinkling water on the elderly.
- Elaborate parades feature floats, traditional Thai music, and performances; dancers wear colorful traditional dress (*sabai thaweechai*).
- Thailand's streets become impromptu water battlegrounds with water guns, buckets, and hoses used indiscriminately on passersby—even tourists are fair targets.
- Special foods include *khao pad* (fried rice), glutinous rice wrapped in banana leaves, and sweets made with coconut as offerings.
