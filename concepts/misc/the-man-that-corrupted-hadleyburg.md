# The Man That Corrupted Hadleyburg

## summary

*The Man That Corrupted Hadleyburg* is a short story (1899) by Mark Twain satirizing small-town American hypocrisy, virtue-signaling, and greed. It is frequently misremembered as a novel, attributed to the wrong author, or confused with other Twain works like *The Gilded Age* or *Connecticut Yankee*.

## you gotta know

- Written by *Mark Twain* (Samuel Clemens), not Bret Harte or another contemporary; it appeared in *Harper's Magazine* in 1900.
- The story is a short story (roughly 40 pages), not a novel; Twain's major novels are *The Adventures of Tom Sawyer*, *Adventures of Huckleberry Finn*, and *A Connecticut Yankee in King Arthur's Court*.
- The plot involves a stranger who arrives in the virtuous town of Hadleyburg with a scheme to expose its residents' hidden greed and moral weakness through a rigged contest over a bag of gold coins.
- The story is a sharp critique of American civic morality and the contradiction between public virtue and private self-interest—not a celebration of either virtue or corruption.
- Hadleyburg is entirely fictional, not based on a specific small town, though Twain drew on his observations of American communities in Nevada and California.
- The ending reveals that the townspeople's reputations are destroyed, but Twain's irony suggests that moral corruption was always present beneath the surface of respectability.
