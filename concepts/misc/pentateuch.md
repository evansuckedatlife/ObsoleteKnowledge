# Pentateuch

## summary

The *Pentateuch* (Greek for "five scrolls") is the first five books of the Hebrew Bible—*Genesis*, *Exodus*, *Leviticus*, *Numbers*, and *Deuteronomy*—collectively known in Jewish tradition as the *Torah* or *Law*. These texts form the foundational scriptures of Judaism and are central to Jewish religious practice, law, and identity. Christian traditions include these same five books as the opening of the Old Testament, making the Pentateuch one of the most influential religious texts in Western civilization.

## you gotta know

- *Genesis*: the creation narrative, the stories of the patriarchs (*Abraham*, *Isaac*, *Jacob*), and the account of humanity's origins and the covenant with *Abraham*.
- *Exodus*: the liberation of the Israelites from Egyptian slavery under *Moses*, the Sinai covenant, and the giving of the *Ten Commandments*.
- *Leviticus*: detailed laws of priests, sacrifice, purity, and holiness; the most legal and ritual-focused of the five books.
- *Numbers*: the wilderness wanderings of the Israelites for forty years, census-taking, and laws governing the community and priesthood.
- *Deuteronomy*: Moses' farewell speeches restating the law, the covenant renewal, and the entry into the promised land.
- Jewish tradition ascribes authorship to *Moses* (hence "Books of Moses"), though modern scholarship attributes the texts to later editors and multiple sources (JEDP hypothesis).
- The Pentateuch is read cyclically in synagogues, with the yearly cycle completing on *Simchat Torah*.
