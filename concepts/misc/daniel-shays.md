# Daniel Shays

## summary

*Daniel Shays* (1747–1825) was a Massachusetts militia officer and farmer who led a rebellion (1786–1787) against debt collection and foreclosures. Shays' Rebellion is frequently misremembered as a major revolution, forgotten entirely in broad historical survey, or conflated with other post-Revolutionary uprisings.

## you gotta know

- Shays was a captain in the Continental Army during the American Revolution, fought at Bunker Hill, and commanded at Saratoga—he was a legitimate war veteran, not a radical outsider.
- Shays' Rebellion (1786–1787) was primarily a protest against debtor's prisons and farm foreclosures in Massachusetts following the Revolutionary War; veterans and rural poor were economically squeezed by creditors and the state.
- The rebellion involved armed militia blockading courthouses to prevent debt trials and foreclosure proceedings, but it was *not* an attempt to overthrow the government; it was an appeal for debt relief and economic justice.
- The uprising was suppressed by the Massachusetts militia under General Benjamin Lincoln; Shays fled to Vermont and later New York to escape execution.
- Though localized to Massachusetts, Shays' Rebellion significantly influenced the framers of the Constitution; it raised fears of social disorder and weak government, contributing to arguments for a stronger federal system.
- Shays received a pardon in 1788 and later petitioned Congress for a pension for his Revolutionary service; he died in obscurity, his rebellion often overshadowed by the Constitutional Convention that followed it.

## connections

- [[american-revolution]] — Shays was a Revolutionary veteran; his later rebellion arose from Revolutionary-era dislocations.
- [[articles-of-confederation]] — the weak federal government under the Articles was unable to address economic crises that sparked the rebellion.
- [[constitutional-convention]] — Shays' Rebellion was invoked as a cautionary example of the chaos that could result from weak central authority.
