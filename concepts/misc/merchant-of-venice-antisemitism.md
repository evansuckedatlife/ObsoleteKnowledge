# The Merchant of Venice and Antisemitism

## summary

*The Merchant of Venice* is frequently misread as a redemptive or sympathetic portrait of the Jewish moneylender *Shylock*, when in fact Shakespeare's 1596 play reinforces antisemitic tropes of the era. While modern productions often sympathize with Shylock's plight and rejection, the original text frames him as a villainous usurer, and his forced conversion at the play's end was intended as a "merciful" conclusion in its time but represents religious coercion from a modern perspective.

## you gotta know

- *Shylock* is a Jewish moneylender in Venice who demands a pound of flesh as collateral for a loan to Antonio, the titular merchant, setting up the play's central conflict and spectacle.
- The play reflects Elizabethan antisemitism: Shylock is depicted as miserly, vengeful, and inhuman, with much of the comedy deriving from Christian characters' mockery of him and his religion.
- Shylock is forced to convert to Christianity at the play's conclusion—presented as merciful resolution but actually an act of coerced religious assimilation that erases his identity.
- Modern interpretations, especially since the Holocaust, have reframed Shylock as a tragic victim whose isolation and humiliation justify his bitterness, inverting the play's original moral framework.
- The "pound of flesh" plot device is not original to Shakespeare; it derives from earlier folklore and exempla, but Shakespeare's execution weaponizes it for comedy at a Jewish character's expense.
- The play's other Jewish figures are entirely absent, leaving Shylock as the sole representation—a reduction that amplifies negative stereotyping.
