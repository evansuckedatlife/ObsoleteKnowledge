# Shogatsu

## summary

**Shogatsu**, the Japanese New Year, is celebrated on January 1 and is the most important holiday in the Japanese calendar. The celebration involves household purification, shrine visits, family gatherings, and special foods with auspicious symbolism. While traditionally linked to lunar calendars, modern Japan observes Shogatsu on the Gregorian January 1, making it distinct from other East Asian lunar new years celebrated by neighboring cultures.

## you gotta know

- Celebrated on January 1 of the Gregorian calendar (since the Meiji Restoration), unlike Chinese New Year and Tết which follow the lunar calendar.
- Homes are meticulously cleaned before January 1 in a ritual called *susuharai* (soot sweeping) to purify the space and remove the old year's misfortune.
- *Kadomatsu* (ornamental pine and bamboo) and *shimenawa* (sacred rope) are placed at home entrances to welcome *toshigami* (the kami of the new year).
- Families visit *Shinto* shrines and *Buddhist* temples on New Year's Day in the custom of *hatsumôde* (first shrine visit) to pray for the year's blessings.
- *Osechi* dishes—elaborately arranged foods in lacquered boxes—contain items with symbolic meanings: herring roe for fertility, black beans for health, and burdock root for grounding.
- *Mochi* rice cakes (sometimes containing a coin for luck) are eaten; the ritual eating is called *kagami biraki* (mirror breaking).
- *Otoshidama* (money gifts in decorative envelopes) are given to children by family elders, similar to red envelopes in other Asian cultures.
