# Five Senses

## summary

The *Five Senses*—*sight*, *hearing*, *taste*, *smell*, and *touch*—are the classical categorization of human sensory systems, through which perception of the external world is mediated. This taxonomy, established in ancient philosophy and refined by modern physiology, reflects the primary channels through which sensory organs (eyes, ears, tongue, nose, skin) transmit chemical and physical stimuli to the brain for conscious and unconscious processing. Though neuroscience has since identified additional sensory modalities (balance, proprioception), the classical five remain the foundation of sensory understanding in philosophy, medicine, and everyday language.

## you gotta know

- *Sight* (vision): perception of light and color via photoreceptors (rods and cones) in the retina; the most informational sense in most humans.
- *Hearing* (audition): detection of sound waves via the cochlea and hair cells in the inner ear; crucial for communication and spatial awareness.
- *Taste* (gustation): detection of chemical compounds in food and liquid via taste buds on the tongue; sensitive to sweet, salty, sour, bitter, and umami.
- *Smell* (olfaction): detection of volatile chemical molecules via olfactory receptors in the nasal mucosa; the sense most directly linked to memory and emotion.
- *Touch* (tactile sensation): detection of pressure, temperature, and pain via mechanoreceptors and thermoreceptors distributed across the skin.
- Aristotle codified the classical five in ancient Greek philosophy; modern neuroscience has identified additional senses (balance, proprioception, hunger) but retains the classical taxonomy.
