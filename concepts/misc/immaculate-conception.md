# Immaculate Conception

## summary

The *Immaculate Conception* is the Catholic and Eastern Orthodox Christian doctrine that *Mary*, the mother of Jesus, was born without original sin. It is frequently and persistently confused with the *Virgin Birth*—the belief that Jesus was born of a virgin mother—which are two entirely different theological concepts separated by a generation.

## you gotta know

- The Immaculate Conception refers to Mary's *conception* (her birth), not Jesus's conception; it asserts that Mary herself was conceived without original sin and was preserved in perfect purity from that moment.
- The Virgin Birth refers to *Jesus's* conception and birth; it is the claim that Jesus was born of a virgin mother (Mary) without a biological father—asserted in Matthew and Luke, though not mentioned in Mark or John.
- Both doctrines are stated in Christian scripture and tradition, but they address different theological questions: the Immaculate Conception concerns Mary's sinlessness, while the Virgin Birth concerns Jesus's divine nature and conception.
- The Immaculate Conception was not formally defined Catholic doctrine until Pope Pius IX declared it in 1854 (*Ineffabilis Deus*), though it was widely believed and taught much earlier.
- The Virgin Birth is older in Christian tradition (stated in the Gospels, early creeds) but is not universally accepted among Christian denominations; many Protestants affirm it, while some liberal theologians question it.
- Eastern Orthodoxy affirms Mary's perpetual virginity and holiness (*Theotokos*, "God-bearer") but does not use the terminology "Immaculate Conception" in the same way; the traditions diverged on precise formulations.
