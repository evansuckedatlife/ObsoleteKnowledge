# The Invisible Man Authorship

## summary

Two major novels titled *The Invisible Man* or *Invisible Man* exist by different authors in different centuries, and they are often confused or conflated. H.G. Wells's 1897 novella *The Invisible Man* is a science-fiction parable about a scientist who discovers invisibility; Ralph Ellison's 1952 novel *Invisible Man* is a philosophical Bildungsroman about race and identity in America. Beyond the title, they share virtually nothing in premise, theme, or era.

## you gotta know

- *The Invisible Man* by *H.G. Wells* (1897) concerns a scientist named Griffin who makes himself invisible through scientific experimentation and descends into madness and violence; it is science fiction and social critique.
- *Invisible Man* by *Ralph Ellison* (1952) follows an unnamed Black protagonist through American society, exploring racism and the erasure of Black identity; the invisibility is metaphorical and existential, not literal.
- Wells's novel is a brief, action-driven story exploring what happens when a man seizes ultimate power through invisibility; Ellison's is a sprawling 600-page introspection on American racism.
- Wells wrote his story during the late Victorian era and was influenced by emerging scientific romance; Ellison wrote after World War II and drew from modernist literature and African-American tradition.
- The titles are often confused in educational and cultural contexts, leading to mistaken expectations or misidentification of which work is being referenced.
- Both novels are considered classics, but for entirely different reasons: Wells for pioneering science-fiction concepts, Ellison for landmark contributions to American literature and racial discourse.
