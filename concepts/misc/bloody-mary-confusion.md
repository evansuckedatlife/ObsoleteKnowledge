# Bloody Mary Confusion

## summary

A frequent conflation mistakes *Mary I of England* with *Mary, Queen of Scots* or uses "Bloody Mary" to refer to both indiscriminately. These are distinct historical figures: Mary I ruled England (1553–1558) and earned the "Bloody Mary" epithet for Protestant persecutions; Mary, Queen of Scots was a separate figure who ruled Scotland and was executed in England decades later. Confusion arises because both were queens named Mary and lived during the same tumultuous era.

## you gotta know

- *Mary I of England* (1516–1558), also called "Bloody Mary," was the Catholic daughter of Henry VIII who ruled England and attempted to restore Catholicism after Edward VI's Protestant reign.
- *Mary, Queen of Scots* (1542–1587) was the Catholic queen of Scotland who had claims to the English throne; she fled Scotland, was imprisoned in England, and was executed by her cousin Elizabeth I.
- Mary I earned the "Bloody Mary" nickname for the execution of approximately 300 Protestants during her reign, aimed at Catholic restoration but resulting in Protestant backlash and her enduring negative legacy.
- Mary, Queen of Scots played a different tragic role: her imprisonment and execution became a symbol of Catholic martyrdom, and she had no part in Mary I's persecutions.
- Mary I died in 1558 and was never called "Bloody Mary" in her own time; the epithet emerged after her death and solidified through later Protestant historians' writings.
- The two Marys were cousins and lived overlapping lifespans, but Mary I never ruled Scotland, and Mary of Scots never ruled England, though both had claims and ambitions tied to both realms.
