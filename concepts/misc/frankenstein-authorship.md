# Frankenstein Authorship Myth

## summary

A widespread misconception holds that *Frankenstein* refers to the monster itself; in fact, *Frankenstein* is the name of the scientist, *Victor Frankenstein*, and the creature he creates is the *monster* or remains nameless. This confusion persists because the creature has become iconic while its creator's name has faded from popular memory.

## you gotta know

- *Victor Frankenstein* is the scientist, not the monster—the novel is his story, told in first person through letters and narrative.
- The creature itself is never given a name in the novel; it is referred to only as "the creature," "the monster," or "the being"—calling it "Frankenstein" fundamentally misidentifies the work's protagonist.
- Mary Shelley published the novel in 1818 when she was just 20 years old, with no author attribution on the first edition.
- The creature is intelligent and articulate in the novel, capable of speech and philosophical reflection—far different from the inarticulate brute of popular culture and stage adaptations.
- The Gothic tradition of the 1800s often adapted stories loosely for dramatic effect, and stage versions (beginning with *Frankenstein; or, The Modern Prometheus* in 1823) shifted focus to the monster, reinforcing the name confusion.
- The creature's anguish stems from abandonment and rejection by his creator and society, making him tragic rather than purely evil.

## connections

- [[mary-shelley]] — the author, who conceived the novel during the famous 1816 summer at Lake Geneva.
- [[gothic-literature]] — the tradition within which *Frankenstein* sits, influencing horror and science fiction deeply.
- [[prometheus]] — the mythological parallel referenced in the novel's subtitle.
