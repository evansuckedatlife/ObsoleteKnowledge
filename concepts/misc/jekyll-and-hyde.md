# Jekyll and Hyde

## summary

A persistent confusion mistakes *Dr. Jekyll* and *Mr. Hyde* for the same person, when in fact they are distinct personalities inhabiting one body. Robert Louis Stevenson's 1886 novella explores duality—how a respectable man and his repressed, violent alter ego can coexist—through a transformation via an experimental potion, not a supernatural split or possession.

## you gotta know

- Dr. *Henry Jekyll* and Mr. *Edward Hyde* are one person—Jekyll is the scientist and physician; Hyde emerges as his darker, hedonistic self through the effects of a chemical draught.
- Jekyll consciously takes the potion to separate his moral from his immoral impulses, intending to indulge in vice as Hyde without tarnishing his respectable identity.
- As the story progresses, Hyde becomes dominant; Jekyll loses voluntary control of the transformation and spends more time as Hyde, suggesting the depth of his repressed desires.
- Many popular adaptations portray them as literally two minds fighting for control, but the novella frames them as facets of one psyche—Jekyll's duality made physical.
- The transformation is not reversible at the end; Jekyll is unable to suppress Hyde, and ultimately takes his own life to prevent Hyde from acting further.
- The tale is a cautionary meditation on Victorian repression, suggesting that the denial of human desire does not eliminate it but amplifies and distorts it.
