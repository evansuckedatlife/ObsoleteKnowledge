# Junkanoo

## summary

**Junkanoo** is a vibrant Bahamian street parade celebrating the New Year (held on Boxing Day and New Year's Day), featuring elaborate costumes, floats, horns, drums, and dancing. The festival is rooted in African and Caribbean traditions and reflects the cultural heritage of enslaved peoples who were historically given a few days off around Christmas and New Year. Junkanoo has become a symbol of Bahamian identity and one of the Caribbean's most colorful celebrations.

## you gotta know

- Celebrated on *Boxing Day* (December 26) and *New Year's Day* (January 1); the January parade is the larger and more famous celebration.
- The name's origin is debated—possibly derived from *John Canoe*, an African tribal chief, or from French *gens inconnus* (unknown people), reflecting the tradition of partygoers wearing masks.
- Participants construct elaborate paper-mache floats and costumes featuring brilliant colors, geometric patterns, and imaginative designs crafted over months of planning.
- Distinctive instruments include cowbells, whistles, drums, conch shells, and metal horns creating a cacophonous celebratory sound throughout Nassau and other Bahamian islands.
- Groups compete for prizes; each parade group (called a "rush") comprises dozens or hundreds of costumed dancers and musicians dancing behind their float.
- The festival directly roots in African diaspora traditions and the freedom days historically granted to enslaved people to celebrate with family.
- Steel bands, gospel choirs, and traditional rake-and-scrape music blend with modern Caribbean sounds.
