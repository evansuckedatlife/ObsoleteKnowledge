# Enharmonic Notes

## summary

*Enharmonic notes* are two different pitch notations that sound identical on a piano or most instruments but have different names and spellings—for example, C-sharp and D-flat. Confusion arises because people often treat them as the same pitch when theoretically they serve different functions in music.

## you gotta know

- Enharmonic equivalents sound the same in equal temperament (the modern tuning system) but are written differently—C♯ vs. D♭, E vs. F♭, B vs. C♭.
- On a piano, the same physical key plays both enharmonic pitches; wind and string players must adjust intonation slightly differently depending on which enharmonic the composer writes.
- Enharmonic spelling is *not* arbitrary—it depends on the key signature and harmonic context; B♯ and C are enharmonic, but B♯ appears in the key of C♯ major, not the key of C major.
- In historical tuning systems (just intonation, Pythagorean tuning), enharmonic notes were *not* identical in pitch—they had subtle but audible differences in intonation.
- Enharmonic confusion affects music theory reading and composition; a musician who misreads C♯ as D♭ may play the right pitch but analyze the harmony incorrectly.
- The word "enharmonic" comes from ancient Greek music theory but had a different meaning—it refers to microtonal distinctions, not exact pitch equivalence.
