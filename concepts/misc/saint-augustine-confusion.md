## summary

Two major figures named Saint Augustine lived over two centuries apart, and they are frequently conflated despite very different roles and legacies. *Augustine of Hippo* (354–430) was a North African theologian whose writings shaped Christian doctrine; *Augustine of Canterbury* (c. 540–604) was a missionary who brought Christianity to Anglo-Saxon England. Confusion is especially common because both are venerated as saints and both profoundly influenced Christianity, yet in entirely different contexts.

## you gotta know

- *Augustine of Hippo* (354–430) was born in Roman North Africa, converted to Christianity as an adult, became a bishop, and wrote *Confessions* and *City of God*—foundational texts of Christian theology.
- *Augustine of Canterbury* (c. 540–604) was an Italian-born Benedictine monk sent by Pope Gregory I to evangelize the Anglo-Saxons; he established the Archbishop of Canterbury and is considered the founder of the English church.
- Augustine of Hippo lived in late antiquity and the early medieval period; Augustine of Canterbury lived in the early medieval period, separated from the first by over a century.
- Augustine of Hippo's influence was primarily theological and philosophical, shaping doctrines of original sin, grace, and the nature of the church; Augustine of Canterbury's influence was primarily ecclesiastical and missionary.
- Augustine of Hippo is the more famous and widely cited figure in academic and theological contexts; Augustine of Canterbury is primarily significant in British and English church history.
- Both are commemorated as saints; Augustine of Hippo's feast day is August 28, while Augustine of Canterbury's is May 26.
