## summary

**Achilles** was the mightiest warrior of the *Trojan War*, nearly invulnerable and unmatched in combat prowess. The son of *Thetis*, a sea-nymph, and *Peleus*, a mortal king, he represents the apex of martial excellence and also the tragedy of a life cut short by glory. His wrath—particularly at *Agamemnon* and over the death of *Patroclus*—drives the central narrative of Homer's *Iliad*. Though destined for a short life, Achilles achieved immortal fame through both his deeds and his unflinching acceptance of his fate. His character embodies the heroic code of ancient Greece: honor, prowess, and defiance in the face of inevitable doom.

## you gotta know

### Birth and Invulnerability
- Born to *Thetis*, a sea-nymph, and *Peleus*, a mortal king of Thessaly.
- Made invulnerable by his mother, who dipped him in the river *Styx* as an infant—except for his heel, where she held him.
- Trained by the wise centaur *Chiron* in martial arts, medicine, and wisdom from childhood.
- Destined for either a long, forgettable life or a short, glorious one; he chose glory.

### The War and His Rage
- The greatest warrior at *Troy*, slaying countless Trojan soldiers and their champions throughout the ten-year siege.
- Withdrew from battle after *Agamemnon* seized his captive *Briseis*, nearly causing Greek defeat in his absence.
- Returned to war only after his closest companion *Patroclus* was killed by *Hector*, wearing Achilles' own armor.
- Killed *Hector* in single combat and dishonored the corpse by dragging it thrice around Troy's walls.

### Death and Legacy
- Died when *Paris* shot a poisoned arrow through his heel, the only vulnerable part of his body.
- His death marked the beginning of Troy's fall, though he did not live to see the city's conquest.
- Became a symbol of martial excellence, tragic fate, and the warrior code in Western literature.

## context

Achilles stands at the foundation of Western heroic tradition, embodying the ancient ideal that a short life lived with honor surpasses a long one lived in obscurity. His story—told most fully in Homer's *Iliad* and later retellings by Aeschylus, Sophocles, and countless Renaissance poets—raises profound questions about fate, choice, and the cost of glory. His heel became metaphorical; the term "Achilles' heel" now refers to a hidden weakness in anything seemingly invulnerable, a legacy that extends from military strategy to business and psychology. Psychologically, Achilles has fascinated modern thinkers as an archetype of the warrior trapped between duty and desire, masculine violence and human tenderness. His relationship with Patroclus has been reinterpreted across centuries, sometimes as a mentor-student bond, sometimes as a romantic love, making him a contested figure in debates about heroism and identity.
