# The Iliad

## summary

The Iliad is an ancient Greek epic poem in dactylic hexameter, traditionally attributed to the blind poet Homer. Set during the final weeks of the ten-year Trojan War, the epic focuses on the wrath of the Greek champion Achilles after he is dishonored by Agamemnon, and the devastating consequences of his withdrawal from battle. It is one of the oldest and most foundational works of Western literature.

## you gotta know

- The epic's central and guiding theme is introduced in its very first line: the *rage (menis) of Achilles* and the death and destruction it brings to the Achaean forces.
- The narrative begins *in media res* in the tenth year of the war with a dispute between Achilles and *Agamemnon*, who refuses to return Chryseis, leading Achilles to withdraw his Myrmidons from the fighting.
- The turning point occurs when Hector kills Achilles's close companion *Patroclus*, who had entered the battle wearing Achilles's armor to rally the faltering Greeks.
- Achilles returns to the fray in new armor forged by Hephaestus, slays the Trojan champion *Hector*, and desecrates his corpse by dragging it behind his chariot for twelve days.
- The poem concludes not with the fall of Troy or the Trojan Horse, but with the funeral of Hector, after King *Priam* secretly visits Achilles's tent to ransom his son's body.
- The gods are active participants in the poem, physically engaging in combat and taking sides, such as Athena aiding Achilles and Apollo protecting Hector's corpse.
