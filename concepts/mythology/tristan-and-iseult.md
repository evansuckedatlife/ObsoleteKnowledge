## summary

The tragic lovers Tristan and Iseult (also Isolde) represent one of medieval literature's greatest love stories—a passionate romance that defies duty, honor, and propriety. Bound together by a magical love potion, they became icons of romantic devotion willing to sacrifice everything for their forbidden union.

## you gotta know

- Tristan was a renowned knight of *King Arthur's* court, though his greatest fame stems from his love rather than his valor
- Iseult was a princess betrothed to Tristan's uncle, King Mark of Cornwall, in a political union meant to secure peace
- A magic love potion, accidentally consumed by Tristan and Iseult during a voyage, bound them in undying passion and transformed their entire lives
- Their affair, conducted in secret and in the open, defied both their vows and the authority of King Mark, who pursued them relentlessly
- Multiple versions exist of their fate: some claim they were caught and executed; others that they fled to live apart; still others that they died separately, united only in death
- Often portrayed with deep mutual devotion and genuine affection despite the chaos their love caused around them
- Became archetypal figures for romantic love in Western literature, representing the power of passion to override all other obligations and hierarchies
