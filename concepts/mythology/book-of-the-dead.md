# Book of the Dead

## summary

The Book of the Dead is an ancient Egyptian funerary text used from the beginning of the New Kingdom around 1550 BC to about 50 BC. Consisting of a collection of magical spells, incantations, and formulas, it was placed in the tombs of the deceased to assist their souls in navigating the dangers of the underworld (*Duat*) and achieving a blessed afterlife.

## you gotta know

- Its original Egyptian title translates to the *Book of the Coming Forth by Day* or *Book of Spells for Coming Forth by Day*.
- It was typically written in hieroglyphic or hieratic script on papyrus scrolls, often decorated with elaborate illustrations called vignettes showing the journey of the soul.
- It contains the crucial instructions for the *Weighing of the Heart* ceremony, where the jackal-headed god Anubis weighs the deceased's heart against the feather of Ma'at (truth and justice).
- The text lists the "Negative Confession" or Declaration of Innocence, where the deceased must deny committing a list of 42 specific sins before the 42 judges of the underworld.
- It was traditionally authored and maintained by the ibis-headed god *Thoth*, the patron deity of writing and scribes.
- A famous surviving copy is the *Papyrus of Ani*, a lavishly illustrated version created around 1250 BC for a royal scribe named Ani.

## connections

- [[osiris]] — the supreme judge of the dead before whom the deceased must plead their case.
- [[anubis]] — the god who guides the deceased to the weighing scales.
- [[thoth]] — the divine scribe who records the results of the weighing ceremony.
- [[maat]] — the goddess of justice whose feather is the standard of judgment.
- [[ra]] — the sun god whom the deceased hopes to accompany on his daily voyage.
- [[nephthys]] — the goddess of mourning who protects the deceased during their trial.
