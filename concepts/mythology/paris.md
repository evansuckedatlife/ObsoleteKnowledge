# Paris

## summary

**Paris** was the Trojan prince whose abduction of *Helen* of Sparta triggered the Trojan War, one of antiquity's most destructive conflicts. Originally named *Alexandros* and raised in exile as a shepherd due to prophecy, he is remembered as both the man of legendary beauty who judged the goddesses and the catalyst for Troy's destruction. Though less formidable in battle than his brother *Hector*, his pivotal role in the war's causation, and his killing of the invulnerable *Achilles*, secure him a place in mythology's central narrative. Paris embodies a tragic contradiction: the beautiful but flawed hero whose personal desires unleash catastrophic consequences for an entire civilization. His story explores the tension between individual choice and collective fate, between personal honor and civilizational ruin.

## you gotta know

### Origins and Exile
- Originally named *Alexandros*, son of King *Priam* and Queen *Hecuba*.
- A prophecy foretold he would bring destruction to Troy; he was therefore exposed as an infant and raised by shepherds in exile on Mount Ida.
- Despite humble upbringing, his exceptional beauty was recognized, and he was eventually restored to the royal court.

### The Judgment of the Goddesses
- Chosen by *Zeus* to judge which of three goddesses—*Athena*, *Hera*, or *Aphrodite*—was most beautiful.
- *Aphrodite* bribed him with the love of Helen, the world's most desired woman, so he crowned her the fairest.
- The Judgment of Paris triggered divine wrath from the scorned goddesses and set in motion all the events leading to the war.

### Helen and the War
- Abducted *Helen* from her husband *Menelaus*, king of Sparta, and brought her to Troy.
- His action prompted the thousand-ship Greek invasion and the ten-year Trojan War that claimed tens of thousands of lives.
- Lacked the martial prowess of his brother *Hector* and other great Trojan warriors, making him a liability in actual combat.

### Death
- Killed the invulnerable *Achilles* with an arrow to his heel, the Greek's only vulnerable point—perhaps his sole moment of genuine warrior achievement.
- Later killed by *Philoctetes'* bow and poisoned arrows in the final stages of the war, a fitting irony for one who killed with arrows.

## context

Paris has long been read as a symbol of the costs of unchecked desire and the powerlessness of individuals against fate. Medieval and Renaissance literature treated him as a cautionary figure of the consequences of pride and lust, while Romantic writers found in him a tragic figure torn between desire and duty. Goethe's *Faust* invokes the Judgment of Paris as a scene of worldly temptation, and modern psychology has examined him as representing the tension between individual autonomy and social obligation. The phrase "judgment of Paris" itself became a byword for impossible choices and the impossibility of satisfying all parties. His role as unwitting instrument of divine will—choosing beauty through a bribe, committing an act that triggers war—has made him a perennial figure in discussions of moral agency and fate.
