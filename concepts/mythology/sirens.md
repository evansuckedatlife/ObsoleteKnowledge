# Sirens

## summary

Supernatural beings that blended feminine beauty with bestial form—variously described as women with bird wings or fish tails—the *Sirens* were famous for their irresistible song that lured sailors to their doom. *Odysseus* famously resisted their call by having his crew plug their ears with wax while he himself was bound to the mast, determined to hear their legendary voices without perishing.

## you gotta know

- Their voices possessed magical properties that filled any listener with an overwhelming compulsion to approach, regardless of reason or danger
- In early accounts, they were depicted as winged women; in later versions, they became fish-tailed maidens closer to what we call mermaids today
- Their exact number varied—some sources name two, others three, and still others describe more; the most famous are *Leucosia*, *Ligeia*, and *Parthenope*
- They inhabited a rocky island (variously located in different parts of the Mediterranean) where the bones of shipwrecked sailors accumulated
- *Orpheus*, the legendary musician, was said to have protected the *Argonauts* by singing even more beautifully than the *Sirens*, drowning out their call
- Unlike monsters driven purely by hunger or malice, the *Sirens* seemed motivated by loneliness and a desire for companionship, however destructive
- Some tales describe them as having originally been companions of *Persephone* before being transformed as punishment for failing to prevent her abduction
- Ancient sailors viewed them as both deadly hazards and objects of fascination, representing the seductive dangers lurking on maritime journeys
