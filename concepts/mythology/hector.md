## summary

**Hector** was the mightiest warrior of Troy and the tragic heart of the *Trojan War*. As the eldest son of King *Priam* and the supreme commander of the Trojan forces, he embodied both martial prowess and human virtue—honor, devotion to family, and duty to his city. Though ultimately defeated and killed by *Achilles*, Hector is portrayed with such nobility and dignity by Homer that he emerges as the moral center of the *Iliad*, perhaps more sympathetic than the Greeks themselves. His awareness of his own doom, his tender farewell to his wife *Andromache* and infant son *Astyanax*, and his dignity in death make him a figure of timeless tragic grandeur. His death signals Troy's inevitable fall, yet his legacy endures across all of Western literature as the archetype of the noble warrior facing unavoidable fate.

## you gotta know

### Family and Kingship
- Son of *Priam* and *Hecuba*, eldest prince of Troy and supreme commander of the Trojan forces.
- Married to *Andromache*, princess of a neighboring kingdom, and father of young *Astyanax*.
- Deeply devoted to his family and city; his love for them motivates his heroic stand against impossible odds.

### Deeds in War
- Slew *Patroclus* in single combat while the latter wore *Achilles'* armor, not knowing he was killing Achilles' beloved companion.
- Defended Troy's walls for ten years, leading the Trojan defense with strategic brilliance and personal valor.
- Killed many Greek champions; his reputation alone terrified the Greek forces.

### The Final Duel
- Faced *Achilles* in the climactic duel of the *Iliad*, fully knowing it would be his last fight and that Troy could not survive his death.
- Despite his valor, he was outmatched by Achilles and fell, pierced through the throat.
- Dragged around Troy's walls three times by Achilles' chariot as a final indignity, denied proper burial rites.
- Eventually ransomed by *Priam* himself, who humbled himself before his son's killer to restore his body for funeral rites.

### Character and Legacy
- Described by both Greeks and Trojans as noble, honorable, and devoted to duty—the moral superior of many Greeks.
- His death symbolizes Troy's inevitable doom and the triumph of fate over human effort.

## context

Hector represents a uniquely sympathetic portrayal of a defeated enemy in ancient epic literature. Unlike many warriors immortalized in mythology, Hector is never portrayed as a villain or a savage; even his opponents respect him. Renaissance and Enlightenment thinkers saw in Hector a model of virtue and civic duty, quite distinct from the individualistic heroism of Achilles. Artists from medieval illuminators to neoclassical painters depicted his farewell to Andromache as one of literature's most poignant scenes, exploring themes of duty, loss, and the human cost of war. In modern scholarship and popular culture, Hector has been reexamined as embodying a form of heroism based on communal obligation and family rather than personal honor and glory. His contrast with Achilles—the superior warrior versus the superior human—remains a fertile ground for moral philosophy and literature.
