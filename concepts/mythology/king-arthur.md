# King Arthur

## summary

**King Arthur** is the legendary king of Britain whose reign represents the height of medieval chivalry and whose tragic downfall defines the Arthurian cycle. Founded on magical birth, raised in exile, and proven legitimate by drawing Excalibur from stone, Arthur establishes the *Round Table* as a revolutionary vision of knightly fellowship without hierarchy—yet that vision is ultimately destroyed by the very passions and flaws that make his knights human. His legend, with roots possibly in the Dark Ages but elaborated through medieval romance, has become the defining myth cycle of Britain and Western chivalry, repeatedly retold and reinterpreted across eight centuries.

## you gotta know

### Birth and Rise to Power
- Arthur is the illegitimate son of *Uther Pendragon* and *Igraine*, conceived through *Merlin's* magic when Uther disguised himself as Igraine's husband.
- Raised in secret by *Merlin* and the knight *Ector*, Arthur's true identity remained hidden until he proved his right to the throne by drawing *Excalibur* from the stone—the only feat that could legitimize a king.
- *Merlin*, the wise and powerful sorcerer, served as Arthur's advisor and magical protector throughout his early reign, counseling him on matters of state and destiny.

### The Round Table and Camelot
- Arthur founded *Camelot*, his legendary seat of power, and established the *Round Table*, where knights sat as equals without precedence or hierarchy—a revolutionary vision of fellowship.
- The Round Table, gifted to Arthur as part of *Guinevere's* dowry, could accommodate 150 knights, and the *Siege Perilous* (the empty seat) was reserved for the knight pure enough to achieve the *Holy Grail*.
- *Camelot* became the symbol of idealized medieval chivalry: a place of beauty, magic, justice, and knightly virtue—though increasingly troubled by internal conflicts.

### The Kingdom's Downfall
- Arthur married *Guinevere*, the beautiful and noble queen, but her love affair with *Lancelot*, Arthur's greatest and most trusted knight, brought festering discord to the realm.
- *Mordred*, Arthur's own son (or in some versions nephew), was fathered on *Morgause* and harbored bitter resentment toward Arthur; he eventually led a rebellion against the king.
- The final betrayal came at the Battle of *Camlann*, where Mordred mortally wounded Arthur; Arthur returned the wound, killing Mordred, but the fellowship of the Round Table shattered beyond repair.

### Arthur's Legacy and Fate
- Rather than dying, Arthur was spirited away by the *Lady of the Lake* to the mythical isle of *Avalon*, where he sleeps, destined to return when Britain is in its greatest need—making him the "*once and future king*."
- *Excalibur*, Arthur's magical sword (which cannot be sheathed without drawing blood), was returned to the Lady of the Lake, who caught it from the dying king's hand.
- Despite his tragic end, Arthur's legend inspired medieval chivalric ideals and became a symbol of lost golden ages and the possibility of heroic restoration.

### The Knights and Fellowship
- *Gawain*, his loyal nephew, served as one of his most formidable knights, though his strength waned as the day progressed.
- Other knights like *Galahad*, *Lancelot*, *Bedivere*, and *Percival* pursued the *Holy Grail*, a spiritual quest that divided the fellowship's focus from worldly kingship.

## context

**King Arthur** represents a uniquely British mythology that bridges legendary history with fantasy and spiritual quest. His sources are disputed—some scholars posit a historical 6th-century Dark Age British war leader, while others see him as purely legendary. The medieval elaboration of Arthur by writers like *Geoffrey of Monmouth* (12th century), Thomas Malory (*Le Morte d'Arthur*, 15th century), and T.H. White (20th century) transformed him into an archetype of the noble king brought down by passion and fate. The Arthurian cycle became the template for exploring contradictions: the ideal versus the real, the individual passion versus social duty, the quest for meaning in a fallen world. Tennyson, Wagner, and modern adaptations (film, literature, games) continue to mine Arthur's story for its psychological and spiritual complexity. The Round Table symbolizes egalitarian leadership; Camelot represents lost paradise; and Arthur's return myth encodes hope for renewal after catastrophe—all deeply resonant in Western culture.
