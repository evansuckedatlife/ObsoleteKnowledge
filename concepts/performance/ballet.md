# Ballet

## summary

Ballet is a formalized system of theatrical dance originating in Renaissance Italy and developed into its classical form in France and Russia during the 18th and 19th centuries. Defined by turned-out legs, vertical alignment, precise arm positions, and ethereal lightness, ballet became the preeminent vehicle for storytelling through movement in the Western tradition. It remains the foundation upon which most Western concert dance rests.

## you gotta know

- Ballet emerged in Italian Renaissance courts as an entertainment combining music, dance, and spectacle; the term "ballet" comes from the Italian *balletto* (a dancing figure).
- **French codification** (17th–18th centuries): King [[louis-xiv]] of France, a dancer himself, established the Académie Royale de Danse (1661), which systematized ballet technique and terminology (five positions of the feet, port de bras, etc.).
- The **five positions of the feet** — the foundational stances in which the dancer's feet are arranged (heels together or apart, toes turned out at ninety degrees) — are the grammar of ballet and have remained unchanged for 350 years.
- Ballet moved from court entertainment to theatrical spectacle; [[jean-philippe-rameau]] and other Baroque composers created ballet works that were early operas-ballets.
- **Romantic ballet** (1830s–1870s): works like [[la-sylphide]] and [[giselle]] emphasized supernatural, ethereal imagery; dancers wore white tutus and danced en pointe (on the tips of their toes in special shoes), creating an illusion of weightlessness and otherworldliness.
- **Tchaikovsky's masterworks** — [[swan-lake]], [[the-nutcracker]], [[sleeping-beauty]] — elevated ballet to the highest artistic level, combining symphonic orchestration with profound emotional and narrative depth.
- **Russian Imperial Ballet** (St. Petersburg and Moscow, 19th–early 20th centuries): [[marius-petipa]] created the classical vocabulary; works like *Swan Lake* and *The Sleeping Beauty* defined the canon.
- **Sergei Diaghilev's Ballets Russes** (1909–1929): revolutionary company that commissioned [[igor-stravinsky]] to write [[the-firebird]] and [[the-rite-of-spring]], and worked with painters (Picasso, Matisse) and choreographers ([[vaslav-nijinsky]], [[george-balanchine]]).
- **George Balanchine** (1904–1983) created neoclassical ballet, stripping away narrative and emphasizing pure movement and music; his works dominate the modern ballet repertoire.

## connections

- [[swan-lake]] — the canonical Romantic ballet, a masterpiece of Tchaikovsky and choreography.
- [[the-nutcracker]] — a holiday perennial and beloved introduction to ballet and classical music.
- [[sleeping-beauty]] — Tchaikovsky's first and longest ballet, an epic of classical technique.
- [[the-firebird]] — Stravinsky's early masterwork, bridging Romantic and modernist ballet.
- [[the-rite-of-spring]] — Stravinsky and Nijinsky's revolutionary work that presaged modern dance.
- [[giselle]] — a Romantic ballet of supernatural romance and betrayal.
- [[la-sylphide]] — the founding work of Romantic ballet, emphasizing ethereal imagery.
- [[pyotr-ilyich-tchaikovsky]] — the composer who elevated ballet to symphonic seriousness.
- [[marius-petipa]] — the choreographer who codified classical ballet vocabulary.
- [[george-balanchine]] — the 20th-century reformer who created neoclassical ballet.
- [[modern-dance]] — the 20th-century break from ballet's rigid formalism.
