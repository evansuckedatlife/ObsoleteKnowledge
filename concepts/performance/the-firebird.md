## summary

*The Firebird* is a one-act ballet by Igor Stravinsky and Michel Fokine, premiered in 1910 by the Ballets Russes, drawing on Russian fairy tales and mythology. The ballet features a magical bird with burning plumage that enchants a prince and leads him to rescue a princess from an evil sorcerer, combining spectacle, oriental exoticism, and Stravinsky's vivid orchestration.

## you gotta know

- *Igor Stravinsky* composed the score with a shimmering, modern idiom that marks the birth of his revolutionary voice; the music combines Russian folk elements with impressionistic and atonal textures.
- *Michel Fokine* choreographed the work and danced the Prince in the premiere, establishing his style of interpretive, naturalistic ballet freed from rigid classical vocabulary.
- The Firebird herself is a tour-de-force female role: she leaps, flutters, and pirouettes with supernatural speed and grace, earning applause for her spectacular jumps and flowing port de bras.
- The Prince's capture and taming of the Firebird by her feathers drives the magical rescue plot; she helps him vanquish the sorcerer *Koschei the Deathless* and win the princess.
- The famous *Firebird jump* — a series of quick, explosive leaps in a single direction — became a technical signature of the role and a benchmark for male dancers.
- The ballet showcases immense spectacle: elaborate costume designs, set changes, and a large corps, epitomizing the lavish, exotic aesthetic the Ballets Russes brought to Paris.
- It premiered at the Théâtre du Châtelet in Paris on 25 June 1910 and became an immediate sensation, establishing Stravinsky and Fokine as masters of modernist ballet.

## connections

- [[stravinsky]] — the composer whose revolutionary score launched his fame.
- [[fokine]] — the choreographer-dancer who created and premiered the role.
- [[ballets-russes]] — the impresario company that brought Russian ballet to the West.
