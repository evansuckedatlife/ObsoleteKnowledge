# Bob Fosse

## summary

*Bob Fosse* was an American choreographer, dancer, and director who created a distinctive theatrical style blending jazz, vaudeville, and contemporary movement. His signature *Fosse style*—characterized by turned-in knees, angular arm positions, and sharp isolations—transformed both Broadway musicals and film, earning him a record three Tony Awards in a single year.

## you gotta know

- Born in Chicago in 1927; trained in vaudeville and burlesque before breaking into Broadway in the 1950s.
- Developed the *Fosse technique*, marked by flexed wrists, hunched shoulders, splayed hands, and intricate footwork performed with seeming casualness and dark humor.
- Choreographed and directed *Chicago* (1975), which revived the musical theater and won the Tony Award for Best Musical; his staging became a benchmark for theatrical innovation.
- Won an unprecedented three Tony Awards in one year (1973) for choreography and direction of *Pippin* and *A Little Night Music*, establishing him as Broadway's pre-eminent figure.
- Directed the film *Cabaret* (1972), whose choreography revolutionized how dance was integrated into cinema; the film won eight Academy Awards.
- Created *All That Jazz* (1979), a semi-autobiographical film that blended his dance vocabulary with existential filmmaking, becoming a cult classic.
- Known for incorporating sexuality, irony, and darkness into musical theater, breaking the bright, optimistic conventions of mid-century Broadway.
