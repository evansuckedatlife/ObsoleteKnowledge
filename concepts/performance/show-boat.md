# Show Boat

## summary

*Show Boat* is a 1927 musical by Jerome Kern (music) and Oscar Hammerstein II (lyrics) based on Edna Ferber's novel, set along the Mississippi River and aboard a showboat. It tells the interconnected stories of performers, riverboat workers, and the community affected by the showboat's arrival, dealing frankly with miscegenation laws, racial prejudice, and the human cost of segregation in the American South.

## you gotta know

- Composed by *Jerome Kern* with book and lyrics by *Oscar Hammerstein II* — a foundational collaboration that elevated musical theatre to serious social drama.
- The opening number "Ol' Man River" (sung by *Joe*, a Black dock worker) is both a lament and a spiritual, and remains one of the most powerful songs in musical-theatre history.
- Other major songs include "Make Believe," "Why Do I Love You?", "Can't Help Lovin' Dat Man," and "Life Upon the Wicked Stage."
- The plot centers on *Magnolia*, a showboat performer who falls in love with *Ravenal*, a riverboat gambler; their marriage is disrupted when *Ravenal* abandons her, forcing her to rebuild her life.
- A subplot involving a racially mixed couple being forced to flee illustrates the show's unflinching portrayal of Jim Crow laws and racial violence—unprecedented for Broadway at the time.
- Opened at the Ziegfeld Theatre in 1927 and became a landmark: the first musical to combine operatic music with social realism rather than purely comedic or romantic ends.
- The 1936 film adaptation (and later revivals) altered or downplayed the racial storyline, but the original stage version's social conscience remains its defining feature.

## connections

- [[jerome-kern]] — the composer whose sophisticated harmonic language defined early American musical theatre.
- [[oscar-hammerstein-ii]] — the librettist whose skill at weaving serious themes into theatrical form pioneered the American musical.

- [[american-racism]] — the historical context that the musical confronts directly.
