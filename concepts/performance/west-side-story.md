# West Side Story

## summary

*West Side Story* is a 1957 musical by Leonard Bernstein (music), Stephen Sondheim (lyrics), and Arthur Laurents (book) that transplants Shakespeare's *Romeo and Juliet* to 1950s New York City, replacing feuding families with rival street gangs—the Jets (white) and the Sharks (Puerto Rican)—and exploring themes of prejudice, youth, and violence in an urban American setting.

## you gotta know

- Composed by *Leonard Bernstein* with lyrics by the young *Stephen Sondheim* — a landmark collaboration that elevated musical-theatre music to symphonic complexity.
- Contains some of the most famous musical-theatre songs: "Somewhere," "America," "Tonight," "Officer Krupke," and the instrumental "Rumble."
- The "Rumble" scene replaces a sword fight with a choreographed gang battle set to orchestral music — a fusion of dance, drama, and symphonic underscoring.
- Opens with a brief overture and uses *Bernstein*'s jazz and Latin influences to capture 1950s urban America; the score won the Tony Award and was adapted into an Academy Award-winning film (1961).
- The plot follows *Tony* (an ex-Jet) and *Maria* (a Shark's sister) who fall in love despite gang loyalty, leading to tragedy and social commentary rather than romantic resolution.
- Directed and choreographed by *Jerome Robbins*, whose innovative dance vocabulary became inseparable from the show's identity.
- Won 10 Tony Awards in 1958; the 1961 film won 10 Academy Awards, making it one of the most decorated theatrical works in American history.
