# Chicago

## summary

*Chicago* is a cynical 1996 musical revival by John Kander and Fred Ebb, originally written in 1975, that captures the sleaze and spectacle of 1920s Chicago's criminal underworld and justice system. Choreographed by Ann Reinking in the style of Bob Fosse, it won six Tony Awards and became the longest-running revival in Broadway history, redefining how modern audiences engage with classic musicals.

## you gotta know

- Composed by *John Kander* with lyrics by *Fred Ebb*, the original 1975 musical had a brief run; the 1996 revival by director Walter Bobbie became a phenomenon through stripped-down staging emphasizing dance and black humor.
- The narrative centers on Roxie Hart, a chorus girl who murders her lover, and Velma Kelly, an incarcerated vaudeville star, who become rivals fighting for tabloid attention and legal representation in a corrupt system.
- **"All That Jazz"** opens the show with steely, rhythmic sophistication; **"Razzle Dazzle"** presents a lawyer's cynical philosophy—that perception and spectacle matter more than truth in the legal system.
- The revival's minimalist aesthetic, influenced by *cabaret* staging, strips away elaborate sets for spotlight choreography in the Fosse style—sharp, angular movement that creates visual and narrative tension.
- The show explicitly critiques American justice, media culture, and the commodification of violence and sex, framing all three as inseparable from entertainment.
- The 1996 revival surpassed the original in every respect and became the longest-running musical revival; the 2002 film adaptation won the Best Picture Oscar.
