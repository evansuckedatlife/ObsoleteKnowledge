# My Fair Lady

## summary

*My Fair Lady* is a 1956 musical by Alan Jay Lerner (lyrics) and Frederick Loewe (music) adapted from George Bernard Shaw's *Pygmalion*. The story follows *Henry Higgins*, an arrogant linguistics professor, who makes a wager that he can transform *Eliza Doolittle*, a Cockney flower girl, into a refined society lady indistinguishable from a duchess—only to discover that art and science cannot account for human feeling.

## you gotta know

- *Alan Jay Lerner* wrote both book and lyrics; *Frederick Loewe* composed the music — a major achievement in the postwar American musical, earning eight Tony Awards (1957).
- Features the unforgettable "I Could Have Danced All Night," "On the Street Where You Live," "The Rain in Spain," "Wouldn't It Be Loverly?", and "Why Can't the English?"
- The song "Why Can't the English?" establishes *Higgins*' obsession with linguistic precision and social order, establishing both comedy and the conflict that drives the plot.
- Based directly on *Shaw*'s 1913 play *Pygmalion*, which itself drew on the Greek myth — Lerner and Loewe added romantic tension and musical sophistication that *Shaw* deliberately withheld.
- Won the 1959 Academy Award for Best Picture; the film starred *Rex Harrison* (as *Higgins*) and *Audrey Hepburn* (as *Eliza*), cementing both in cultural memory.
- Ran on Broadway for 2,717 performances (1956–1962), the second-longest run at the time, and has been revived repeatedly since.
- The ending — *Eliza*'s assertion of independence and *Higgins*' reluctant recognition of her as an equal — remains a subtle rejection of simple romantic resolution.
