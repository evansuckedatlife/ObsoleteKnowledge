## summary

A beloved two-act classical ballet composed by Pyotr Ilyich Tchaikovsky, *The Nutcracker* has become a staple Christmas tradition across the Western world. Based on E. T. A. Hoffmann's short story "The Nutcracker and the Mouse King" as adapted by Alexandre Dumas, it follows young Clara Stahlbaum on a magical Christmas Eve journey. After defending her new nutcracker doll from the Mouse King, she travels to the Land of Sweets with the Prince. Despite its initial premiere in 1892 receiving mixed reviews, the ballet is now celebrated worldwide for its vibrant choreography, festive themes, and orchestrations. It is particularly famous for introducing the celesta to Russian audiences in its musical score.

## you gotta know

### act i: christmas eve & the battle

- Young *Clara Stahlbaum* (sometimes named Marie) receives a wooden nutcracker from her mysterious godfather, *Herr Drosselmeyer*, at a family party.
- Clara's brother *Fritz* accidentally breaks the nutcracker, which Drosselmeyer quickly repairs with a handkerchief.
- At midnight, the Christmas tree grows to gigantic proportions, and Clara is caught in a battle between toy soldiers and the *Mouse King*.
- Clara throws her slipper at the Mouse King to distract him, enabling the *Nutcracker* (who has come to life) to slay him.

### act ii: the land of sweets

- The Nutcracker transforms into a handsome Prince and leads Clara through the Pine Forest, where the *Waltz of the Snowflakes* is performed, to the *Land of Sweets*.
- They are welcomed by the *Sugar Plum Fairy*, who hosts a series of national dances representing sweets: Spanish chocolate, Arabian coffee, Chinese tea, and Russian trepak.
- Features the *Waltz of the Flowers*, led by the *Dewdrop Fairy*, followed by a grand *pas de deux* between the Sugar Plum Fairy and her Cavalier.
- The score famously features the *celesta* to create the ethereal, tinkling sound for the "Dance of the Sugar Plum Fairy".

## connections

- [[swan-lake]] — Tchaikovsky's first ballet, whose definitions of classical structures paved the way for The Nutcracker.
- [[sleeping-beauty]] — Tchaikovsky's second ballet, which also premiered at the Mariinsky Theatre.
- [[the-rite-of-spring]] — a contrasting, modernist Russian ballet that eschewed the festive fantasy of works like The Nutcracker.
- [[petrushka]] — another Russian ballet set during a festive holiday (Shrovetide Fair) that features puppets coming to life.
- [[coppelia]] — a classical ballet featuring toys and mechanical dolls coming to life, similar to the Nutcracker.
- [[giselle]] — a classical ballet whose use of leitmotif influenced Tchaikovsky's narrative scoring in The Nutcracker.
- [[don-quixote]] — a classical Spanish-themed ballet choreographed by Petipa, who outlined the original scenario for The Nutcracker.

## context

While *The Nutcracker* was not an immediate triumph in late-nineteenth-century St. Petersburg, its twentieth-century revivals—most notably George Balanchine's 1954 production for the New York City Ballet—solidified it as an annual holiday institution. It represents a major economic driver for modern ballet companies, often accounting for a significant portion of their ticket sales. In quiz bowl, the work is highly clued by its narrative beats (Drosselmeyer, Fritz, the Mouse King), its specific national dances, and Tchaikovsky's use of the celesta.
