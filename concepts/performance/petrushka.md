# Petrushka

## summary

*Petrushka* is a one-act ballet by Igor Stravinsky and Michel Fokine, premiered in 1911 by the Ballets Russes, based on a Russian puppet-show character. The ballet dramatizes the tragic tale of a wooden puppet who awakens to feelings and unrequited love, set against the vivid bustle of a St. Petersburg carnival, with music that captures the chaos and pathos of the fair and its inhabitants.

## you gotta know

- Stravinsky's score employs bold dissonance and rhythmic innovation, using leitmotifs for each character and setting the tone for twentieth-century modernist ballet music.
- The puppet *Petrushka* is a tragic figure: animated by a sorcerer to perform for a crowd, he gains consciousness and desires the *Ballerina* (another puppet), only to be heartbroken when she prefers the *Moor*.
- The role of Petrushka is a male dancer's vehicle for mime, pathos, and controlled movement; Fokine's choreography blends pantomime with balletic technique, making the puppet's humanity visible.
- The famous *Russian Dance* in the first act features folk steps and character work, displaying peasant energy and vivid Russian carnival color.
- Act II reveals three separate puppet vignettes: Petrushka's chamber, the Ballerina's room, and the Moor's chamber, each with distinct music and movement idiom reflecting personality.
- The final scene at the carnival's conclusion shows Petrushka's apparent death and a supernatural moment where his ghost rises above his collapsed form, asserting his spiritual transcendence over wooden captivity.
- Premiered at the Théâtre du Châtelet on 13 June 1911, *Petrushka* deepened the Ballets Russes' reputation for narrative innovation and psychological depth.
