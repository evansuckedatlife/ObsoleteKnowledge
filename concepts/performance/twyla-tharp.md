# Twyla Tharp

## summary

*Twyla Tharp* is an American dancer and choreographer who seamlessly blended contemporary dance, ballet, and popular culture, creating a movement vocabulary that is both athletic and emotionally direct. Her prolific output and fearless genre-hopping—from concert dance to Broadway to film—established her as one of the most innovative choreographers of the late twentieth century.

## you gotta know

- Born in 1941 in Indiana; studied ballet, tap, jazz, and modern dance, synthesizing these vocabularies into a distinctly American style.
- Formed the *Twyla Tharp Dance Company* in 1965; created over 130 works that ranged from pure-dance abstractions to narrative pieces with popular music.
- Collaborated extensively with *Mikhail Baryshnikov*, including the groundbreaking *Push Comes to Shove* (1976), which introduced ballet audiences to postmodern movement.
- Created *Hair* (1987), *Singin' in the Rain* (1985), and *Movin' Out* (2002), each redefining how dance could work in theatrical contexts.
- Known for using popular music—including Billy Joel, Frank Sinatra, and David Byrne—as equally valid material for dance; broke the hierarchy between "serious" and "entertainment" art.
- Directed and choreographed films including *Amadeus* (1984), *White Nights* (1985), and *Hair* (1979), bringing dance to cinematic audiences.
- Published an influential memoir, *Push Comes to Shove*, and wrote extensively about dance process and creativity.

## connections

- [[mikhail-baryshnikov]] — her greatest male collaborator; their partnership reinvigorated ballet.
- [[bob-fosse]] — contemporary musical theater innovator; both bridged concert and commercial dance.
- [[merce-cunningham]] — predecessor in dance abstraction; Tharp extended his vocabulary into pop contexts.
- [[alvin-ailey]] — shared her commitment to inclusive, genre-blending movement vocabularies.
- [[billy-joel]] — composer whose music became a vehicle for her choreography.
