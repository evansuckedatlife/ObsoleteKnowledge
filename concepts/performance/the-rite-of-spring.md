## summary

Composed by Igor Stravinsky and originally choreographed by Vaslav Nijinsky, *The Rite of Spring* (*Le Sacre du printemps*) is a revolutionary 1913 modernist ballet that transformed twentieth-century music and dance. Produced by Sergei Diaghilev's Ballets Russes, the work depicts various pagan rituals in ancient Russia celebrating the arrival of spring, culminating in a young girl dancing herself to death as a sacrifice to the god of spring. Its Paris premiere at the Théâtre des Champs-Élysées famously provoked a near-riot due to its avant-garde choreography and dissonant music. The score's complex rhythmic structures and aggressive polytonality broke entirely with classical conventions, making it one of the most influential compositions of the modernist era. The ballet's visual design was created by the archaeologist and painter Nicholas Roerich, who also co-wrote the scenario.

## you gotta know

### choreography & premiere

- Choreographed by *Vaslav Nijinsky*, who rejected classical ballet positions in favor of turned-in feet, bent knees, and heavy, stomping movements.
- The premiere on *May 29, 1913*, led by conductor *Pierre Monteux*, was met with loud protests, derisive laughter, and physical altercations in the audience.
- Commissioned and presented by impresario *Sergei Diaghilev* as part of his *Ballets Russes* season in Paris.
- The original choreography was lost for decades but was painstakingly reconstructed by Millicent Hodson and Kenneth Archer for the Joffrey Ballet in 1987.

### musical structure & themes

- Begins with a famous, extremely high-register *bassoon solo* playing a Lithuanian folk-inspired melody.
- Divided into two parts: *Adoration of the Earth* (*L'Adoration de la Terre*) and *The Sacrifice* (*Le Sacrifice*).
- Features the "Augurs of Spring" (*Les Augures printaniers*) section, characterized by heavy, repetitive, syncopated chords accented by French horns.
- The final dance, the "Sacrificial Dance" (*Danse sacrale*), features rapidly shifting time signatures and chaotic accents that exhaust the solo dancer.

## context

*The Rite of Spring* is a landmark of high modernism, widely regarded as the moment classical music and ballet broke into the twentieth century. Stravinsky's score introduced polyrhythms, polymeters, and bi-tonality (such as the famous "Rite chord" which combines an E-flat dominant seventh with an F-flat major triad) to the orchestral vocabulary. In quiz bowl, the work is frequently clued by the details of the Paris riot, the opening bassoon solo, its division into two parts, and the specific sections like the "Augurs of Spring" and the "Sacrificial Dance."
