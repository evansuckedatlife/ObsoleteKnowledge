# Arthur Miller

## summary

*Arthur Miller* (1915–2005) was an American playwright whose dramas explore moral crisis, social responsibility, and the collision between individual conscience and collective pressure. His plays *Death of a Salesman* and *The Crucible* are canonical works of American drama; they move audiences while raising questions about ethics, ambition, and complicity. He was as much a public intellectual and activist as a playwright.

## you gotta know

- *Death of a Salesman* (1949) — tragedy of a travelling salesman's delusion and decline, Pulitzer Prize winner; defined the American dream and its betrayals.
- *The Crucible* (1953) — play set during the Salem witch trials but written as allegory for McCarthyism; explores mass hysteria and principled resistance.
- *A View from the Bridge* (1955) — tragedy of an Italian-American longshoreman's jealous passion, Shakespearean in its compression and inevitability.
- Won the Pulitzer Prize twice (1949, 1953); remains one of the most-performed American playwrights.
- Public intellectual: testified before the House Un-American Activities Committee; refused to name names during the Red Scare.
- His plays combine social realism with symbolic depth; characters grapple with conscience in systems designed to elicit complicity.
