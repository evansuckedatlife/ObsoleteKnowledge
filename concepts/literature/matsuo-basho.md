# Matsuo Bashō

## summary

**Matsuo Bashō** (1644–1694) was Japan's preeminent master of haiku and poet of the Edo period whose spare, nature-inflected verse transformed poetry into a meditation on impermanence and the paradoxes of perception. His travelogues, blending haiku with prose commentary, pioneered a hybrid literary form that influenced world poetry and aesthetics.

## you gotta know

- Perfected and canonised the *haiku*, a three-line poem of 5-7-5 syllables, establishing it as the highest form of Japanese poetry and the standard by which all haiku are measured.
- Composed *"Frog jumping into the old pond / water's sound"*—perhaps the most famous haiku, exemplifying his principle that a poem should capture a single moment of insight or beauty (*satori*, enlightenment) with absolute economy.
- Authored *The Narrow Road to the Deep North* (*Oku no Hosomichi*, 1694), a travelogue blending haiku, prose description, and philosophical reflection as he journeyed through remote Japan seeking spiritual understanding.
- Founded the *Shōmon* school of haiku, gathering students and establishing a poetic lineage that standardised conventions of composition and *hokku* (opening stanza of collaborative poetry chains).
- Embraced *karumi* (lightness) as an aesthetic principle—the idea that true depth emerges from simplicity, absence, and the reader's own interpretation rather than authorial elaboration.
- Lived as a mendicant poet, wandering Japan and composing in response to nature, Buddhist philosophy, and the transience of human life—embodying in his life the very impermanence his verse explores.
