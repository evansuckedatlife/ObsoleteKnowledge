# Lady Macbeth

## summary

**Lady Macbeth** is Macbeth's wife and the architect of Duncan's murder, a woman of steel who suppresses her own humanity to drive her husband toward kingship. She calls upon darkness to "unsex" her and fill her with cruelty, then manipulates Macbeth through questions of manhood and honour. Yet her ambition exacts a price: consumed by guilt, she descends into madness, sleepwalking with invisible bloodstains on her hands before taking her own life—a fall as tragic as Macbeth's own.

## you gotta know

### Ambition Unleashed
- Lady Macbeth receives Macbeth's letter about the witches' prophecy and resolves immediately that Duncan must die; she is more eager for the crown than Macbeth himself.
- She invokes supernatural powers: "Come, you spirits / That tend on mortal thoughts, unsex me here, / And fill me from the crown to the toe / Top-full / Of direst cruelty."
- This invocation is unique in the play—it reveals that she sees womanhood as a barrier to ruthlessness, a constraint she explicitly rejects.

### The Murder and Its Aftermath
- When Macbeth hesitates ("We will proceed no further in this business"), she employs psychological manipulation: "When you durst do it, then you were a man."
- She drugs Duncan's guards with wine and smears their hands with blood to frame them; Macbeth does the deed, returning with bloody hands that trouble him immediately.
- She tells him to wash the blood away: "A little water clears us of this deed"—but this proves prophetic, as water will never clear either of them.

### The Unravelling
- After the murder, Lady Macbeth vanishes from sight for much of the play, suggesting that guilt is already working upon her mind even in her silence.
- In Act V, she is shown sleepwalking, attempting to wash imaginary blood from her hands: "Out, damned spot! Out, I say!"—a reversal of her earlier insistence that blood washes away easily.
- She speaks aloud of the murders (Duncan's, Banquo's, Macduff's family), revealing that her mind has fractured under the weight of guilt.

### The Death
- Macbeth learns that Lady Macbeth is dead (presumably by her own hand, though the play is ambiguous).
- His response—"Life's but a walking shadow"—suggests that her death empties the purpose he had in securing the throne; they no longer share ambition, only ruin.

### Gender and Power
- Lady Macbeth's character raises questions about whether female ambition is unnatural (as she herself fears) or whether the patriarchal world makes it tragic.
- She uses femininity as a tool (feigning illness, weeping, seduction) and explicitly rejects it as a liability, revealing the double bind of her position.

## context

Lady Macbeth is Shakespeare's most complex female villain because she is not motivated by hatred or lust for power *for its own sake*, but by the desire to elevate her husband—a motive that, in early modern terms, was thought to be properly feminine. Yet she pursues it with such ruthlessness that she becomes monstrous. Her fall into madness and suicide is not divine punishment in a morality play but a tragic consequence: her attempt to suppress her humanity to achieve ambition succeeds initially but destroys her. She is central to quiz-bowl discussions of gender, guilt, conscience, and the cost of moral transgression.
