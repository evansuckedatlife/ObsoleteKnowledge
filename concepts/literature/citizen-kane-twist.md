## summary

Orson Welles' *Citizen Kane* is a landmark film that chronicles the rise and fall of Charles Foster Kane, a newspaper magnate and media tycoon, through multiple perspectives and flashbacks. The film structures its investigation of Kane's life as a mystery—who is Kane, really? What defines him? The final twist reveals that "Rosebud," the cryptic word Kane whispered on his deathbed, refers to his childhood sled—a symbol of lost innocence before his ambition and power consumed him. The twist recontextualises Kane's entire trajectory as the tragedy of a man who lost his humanity in pursuit of control.

## you gotta know

- Charles Foster Kane grows from a poor boy into a newspaper publisher and media mogul who shapes American public opinion through propaganda.
- The film opens with Kane's death and the mystery of his final word: "Rosebud," which reporters attempt to decode.
- Through interviews and flashbacks, Kane's life unfolds: his marriage, infidelities, political campaigns, affairs, and accumulation of wealth and art.
- Kane is portrayed as increasingly isolated, manipulative, and consumed by his own power, despite moments of apparent vulnerability.
- The final scene reveals that "Rosebud" is Kane's childhood sled—the last memory of happiness before he was sent away to pursue wealth.
- The sled is burned at the end, destroying the key to understanding Kane; the revelation suggests his essential humanity was irretrievable.
- Directed by Orson Welles and released in 1941; considered one of cinema's greatest achievements.

## connections

- [[narrative-cinema]] — the film pioneered multiple-narrator structure and non-linear editing in mainstream cinema.
- [[orson-welles]] — director and star; made Citizen Kane as his directorial debut.
- [[media-manipulation]] — Kane's newspapers and propaganda represent the power and corruption of media.
- [[loss-of-innocence]] — the sled symbolises the child Kane before ambition consumed his life.
- [[biography-as-mystery]] — the film deconstructs the biographical form, suggesting some truths remain unknowable.
