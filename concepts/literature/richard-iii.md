## summary

**Richard III**, the hunchbacked Duke of Gloucester, is Shakespeare's most self-aware villain—a man who owns his malevolence even as he seduces a widow and murders two children to claim the English throne. Brilliant, witty, and conscious of his deformity's social ostracism, he declares his intent to villainy in the play's opening ("I am determined to prove a villain") and then methodically eliminates every obstacle between himself and kingship: he manipulates his brother, imprisons his nephews, and orchestrates their murder in the Tower. Yet his reign is short-lived; the ghosts of his victims haunt him on the eve of battle, and Henry Richmond (Henry VII) defeats him, restoring legitimate rule.

## you gotta know

### Deformity and Ambition
- Richard is explicitly described as a hunchback ("crook-backed," "bunch-backed"), and he claims his deformity has twisted his spirit: "I that am curtailed of this fair proportion... am not shaped for sportive tricks."
- He frames his villainy as a response to his physical alienation, though the play suggests this is partly excuse and partly genuine resentment of the world that rejected him.
- His opening soliloquy promises to "prove a villain," setting him apart from Macbeth (who becomes a villain reluctantly) and Iago (who acts from wounded pride).

### The Path to Kingship
- Richard's older brother is King Edward IV; Richard eliminates him by encouraging his alliance with Margaret of Anjou, then arranges his imprisonment and death.
- He seduces the widow Lady Anne (widow of Edward, the Yorkist prince) by sheer force of personality and flattery, despite her husband's death at Richard's own hands; she agrees to marry him out of paralysis or Stockholm syndrome.
- He imprisons his young nephews (Edward V and Richard, Duke of York) in the Tower of London, ostensibly for safekeeping until Edward V comes of age.

### The Princes' Murder
- The two boys vanish from the Tower; they are presumed murdered on Richard's orders, though he publicly maintains plausible deniability.
- Richard is crowned king; the princes' fate haunts the play and becomes central to the witches' curses and the final battle.
- Historically (and in the play), Richard's usurpation and the princes' disappearance delegitimized his rule and rallied opposition.

### The Fall
- On the eve of the Battle of Bosworth, Richard is haunted by the ghosts of all his victims: the princes, Edward IV, Lady Anne, Buckingham, and others.
- He cries out in the night, "A horse! A horse! My kingdom for a horse!"—desperate to escape when surrounded.
- Richmond defeats him; Richard dies in battle, his body stripped and dragged through the streets. Richmond becomes Henry VII and marries Elizabeth of York, uniting the houses of Lancaster and York.

### Psychology and Speech
- Richard is the most Machiavellian of Shakespeare's villains—he *enjoys* his own evil and speaks directly to the audience, inviting complicity and admiration.
- His wit is infectious; he converts his enemies to allies through charm before revealing himself, making him dangerous in a way that brute force could never be.

## context

*Richard III* is based on Thomas More's *History of Richard III* and reflects Tudor propaganda (the play was written after Henry VII won the throne and was the grandmother of Elizabeth I). Richard becomes the embodiment of an illegitimate, unnatural monarch whose deformity mirrors his moral deformity—a problematic equation that the play endorses. Yet Shakespeare also makes Richard so charming and self-aware that modern productions sometimes read him as victim as much as villain. The play is central to quiz-bowl discussions of villainy, legitimacy, historical fiction, and the Tudor myth.
