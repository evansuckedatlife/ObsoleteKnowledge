# The Oresteia

## summary

*The Oresteia* is Aeschylus' monumental trilogy—the only surviving Greek dramatic trilogy—spanning three generations of the House of Atreus from the aftermath of the Trojan War to the establishment of Athenian justice. It traces a cycle of revenge (murder begetting murder) that is finally broken when *Orestes* is tried in Athens under human law, resolving the intervention of the Furies. The trilogy moves from divine curse to rational jurisprudence, enacting the birth of civilization itself.

## you gotta know

- *Agamemnon* (first play): Agamemnon returns from Troy; his wife *Clytemnestra* murders him in his bath as revenge for his sacrifice of their daughter *Iphigenia*.
- *The Libation Bearers* (second play): Orestes, their son, kills his mother and her lover *Aegisthus* to avenge his father, but is pursued by the *Furies*—spirits of vengeance embodying the unforgiving rules of blood debt.
- *The Eumenides* (third play): Orestes is tried by *Athena* and the Areopagus (Athens's law court); the Furies are transformed into *Eumenides* (benevolent spirits), ushering in rational justice over endless vendetta.
- The trilogy emphasises *Zeus* as the architect of justice through suffering (*pathei mathos*—learning through pain).
- *Cassandra*, the captured Trojan prophet, speaks truth that none believe and is killed alongside Agamemnon.
- The play climaxes with the binding of the Furies, a symbolic transformation from primal justice to civic law.

## connections

- [[aeschylus]] — author of the only surviving trilogy; a foundational work of Western drama.
- [[orestes]] — the protagonist whose deed and trial drive the second and third plays.
- [[clytemnestra]] — the queen who breaks the cycle by her audacious murder of Agamemnon.
- [[agamemnon]] — the king whose sacrifice and return echo through the trilogy's moral structure.
- [[electra]] — his sister, referenced as Orestes' helper; also subject of tragedies by Sophocles and Euripides.
