# The Frogs

## summary

*The Frogs* is Aristophanes' satirical comedy in which *Dionysus* descends to the Underworld to bring back a dead playwright to save Athens from cultural decline. The play stages a witty agon (formal debate) between *Aeschylus* and *Euripides*, two of the greatest Greek tragedians, judging which poet's work better serves the state. Written after Euripides' death and Aeschylus' departure, it is a clever meditation on literary merit, politics, and cultural authority.

## you gotta know

- Dionysus, god of theatre, is so dismayed by living playwrights that he journeys to Hades to retrieve one of the dead masters.
- The journey involves crossing the *Styx* with a chorus of frogs, whose croaking song gives the play its name and comic delight.
- In the Underworld, Aeschylus and Euripides compete in a formal debate (*agon*) over whose plays are superior.
- Aeschylus represents grand, moral tragedy; Euripides represents innovative, psychologically complex drama with realistic dialogue.
- Dionysus ultimately chooses Aeschylus, valuing his political and moral gravity over Euripides' cleverness.
- The play is a meta-commentary on theatre itself, with parodies of both tragedians' styles and jokes about Athenian politics.
- Aristophanes uses the competition to argue that theatre should educate and elevate citizens, not merely entertain.

## connections

- [[aristophanes]] — author; a celebrated example of Old Comedy's blend of fantasy and political critique.
- [[aeschylus]] — one of the two competing playwrights, representing grave and moral drama.

- [[dionysus]] — the god-protagonist whose journey frames the entire plot.
- [[greek-tragedy]] — the play's subject and Aristophanes' target for both praise and mockery.
