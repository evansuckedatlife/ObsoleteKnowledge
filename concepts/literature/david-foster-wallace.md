# David Foster Wallace

## summary

*David Foster Wallace* (1962–2008) was an American novelist, essayist, and short-story writer who synthesized postmodern narrative difficulty with earnest engagement with consciousness, suffering, and meaning-making. His masterwork *Infinite Jest* (1996) is a thousand-plus-page postmodern epic; his essays on literature, tennis, and American culture are canonical; his voice—erudite, funny, desperate—defined a generation's confrontation with information overload, entertainment, addiction, and the search for authentic connection.

## you gotta know

- *Infinite Jest* (1996) is a sprawling, encyclopedic novel set in a near-future North America dominated by corporate entertainment and addiction; it braids together tennis academies, drug rehabilitation, wheelchair-bound assassins, and the lethal power of a film so entertaining it kills viewers.
- Employs nested narratives, hundreds of footnotes, and a dense, maximalist prose style; the novel is formally challenging and deliberately overwrought, treating postmodern excess as both aesthetic and thematic problem.
- His essay *A Supposedly Fun Thing I'll Never Do Again* (on a luxury cruise) and *Consider the Lobster* (on the Maine Lobster Festival) blend reportage, digression, and philosophical inquiry; his essays are literary performances.
- Treats postmodern irony and detachment as moral and psychological crises; his work argues *for* sincerity, emotion, and connection despite (or through) postmodern skepticism.
- *The Pale King* (posthumously published) is an unfinished novel about IRS workers; fragments explore boredom, attention, and bureaucratic consciousness.
- His suicide in 2008 cast his work's engagement with depression, addiction, and meaninglessness as tragically prophetic; he became a symbol of postmodern disaffection and its costs.
