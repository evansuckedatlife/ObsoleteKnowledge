# Zeami

## summary

**Zeami Motokiyo** (1363–1443) was a transformative theatre artist and theorist who perfected and codified Nō theatre during the Muromachi period of feudal Japan. His plays, theories of performance, and analytical writings established Nō as Japan's most refined dramatic form and created a vocabulary for aesthetics (*yūgen*, *ma*, transience) that profoundly influenced not only theatre but all Japanese arts.

## you gotta know

- Developed Nō theatre into its classical form by synthesizing earlier forms of masked dance, mimetic performance, and poetic language into an austere, highly stylized dramatic art.
- Wrote over 100 plays for Nō, many of which remain in the standard repertory; examples include *Matsukaze* (ghost story of two sisters), *Atsumori* (warrior's redemption through buddhist mercy), and *Hagoromo* (celestial maiden and human love).
- Authored *Fushikaden* (*The Flower of Acting*) and other treatises on performance theory, introducing concepts of *yūgen* (profound grace and subtle beauty), *ma* (negative space), and the paradox of *imitation revealing truth*.
- Served as actor and playwright under the patronage of shogun *Ashikaga Yoshimitsu*, whose political power allowed him to stage performances for the elite and refine the art form.
- Pioneered the use of *mugen nō* (dream plays), a structural innovation in which a contemporary character encounters a ghost or spirit in a vision, allowing exploration of karma, redemption, and the supernatural.
- His theoretical writings emphasize economy of gesture, the power of what is *not* shown, and the emotional resonance of silence and stillness—principles that became foundational to Japanese aesthetics.
