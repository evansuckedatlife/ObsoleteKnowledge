# T. S. Eliot

## summary

*T. S. Eliot* (1888–1965) was an American-born poet and critic who became the defining intellectual voice of Anglo-American modernism. Educated at Harvard and Oxford, he spent his mature career in London, where he reinvented himself as a defender of tradition, orthodoxy, and formal order. His poem *The Waste Land* (1922) is modernism's most influential text—a fragmented, allusive masterpiece of cultural despair—while his criticism shaped literary taste for a generation and his later conversion to Anglo-Catholicism redirected his artistic vision toward spiritual transcendence.

## you gotta know

### Life & Literary Career
- Born in St. Louis, Missouri to an old New England family; felt perpetually displaced, seeking cultural authority in Europe.
- Studied philosophy at Harvard; met and married Vivienne Haigh-Wood in London (a marriage of mutual unhappiness and psychological turbulence).
- Worked as a banker and then editor at the *Criterion*, a journal that became the mouthpiece of modernist aesthetics and his own intellectual vision.
- Became a British citizen in 1927 and entered the Anglican Church in the same year—a religious conversion that marked a turn toward traditionalism and formalism.
- Won the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1948; his later plays (*Murder in the Cathedral*) were commercially and critically successful.

### Major Works
- *Prufrock and Other Observations* (1917): early poems including "The Love Song of J. Alfred Prufrock," modernism's ur-text of paralysis and indecision.
- *The Waste Land* (1922): 433 lines of fragmented, multilingual verse depicting post-World War I spiritual desolation; draws on myth (Arthurian legend, Dante, Sanskrit); footnoted extensively; a central text of modernism.
- *The Hollow Men* (1925): meditation on spiritual emptiness and meaninglessness; haunting, ritualistic.
- *Four Quartets* (1943): four long meditative poems on time, spiritual experience, and the nature of language; his most mature and celebrated work.

### Literary Philosophy & Criticism
- Advocate of *impersonality* in art—the artist should disappear behind the work; emotion should be expressed through objective correlatives (external situations that evoke feeling).
- Emphasis on *difficulty* and *allusiveness*; believed great literature draws on the entire European tradition; readers must be erudite to understand fully.
- *Unified sensibility*: the romantic poets had dissociated feeling from thought; metaphysical poets and the Elizabethans unified them—a model for modern recovery.
- Conservative culturally and religiously; skeptical of democracy, mass culture, and modernism's transgressive impulses (despite being modernism's architect).

## context

Eliot's influence on 20th-century literary culture is almost impossible to overstate. *The Waste Land* defined how modernism understood itself: fragmented, erudite, alienated, yet rich with cultural memory. His criticism—especially *The Sacred Wood* (1920) and essays on the *metaphysical poets*—created the canon that universities taught for decades. Yet Eliot's legacy is deeply contested: his anti-Semitism, his skepticism of democracy, and his dismissal of women writers have made him a figure of ongoing scholarly debate. He represents both the heights of modernist accomplishment and its elitism and conservatism. In quiz bowl, Eliot is the quintessential high modernist: difficult, allusive, personally reserved, spiritually serious.
