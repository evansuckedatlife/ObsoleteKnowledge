# The Haunting of Hill House

## summary

Shirley Jackson's 1959 novel tells the story of Eleanor Vance, a lonely woman invited to participate in a parapsychological investigation of the notorious Hill House, a mansion built on a site of tragedy. As the investigation progresses, Eleanor becomes increasingly unmoored from reality, unable to distinguish between the house's actual supernatural manifestations and her own psychological projections, culminating in an ambiguous climax that may be either salvation or destruction.

## you gotta know

- Eleanor is chosen by Dr. Montague, a parapsychologist, to join a team investigating Hill House, a place with a documented history of strange deaths and violent phenomena.
- The house itself seems alive—doors lock, cold spots manifest, and sounds echo through its architecture in ways that defy rational explanation.
- Eleanor becomes increasingly attached to the house and begins experiencing phenomena: mysterious knockings, a phantom hand, and the sensation that the house is trying to communicate with her.
- A secondary character, Theodora, manipulates Eleanor's isolation and vulnerability, driving a wedge between Eleanor and the other investigators.
- The novel employs unreliable narration and shifts between third-person omniscience and Eleanor's fractured perception, making readers uncertain whether the house is genuinely haunted or Eleanor is experiencing a complete mental breakdown.
- The ending offers no resolution: Eleanor drives away from Hill House, perhaps to safety, perhaps to death, with the narrator's final line leaving ambiguous whether she escapes or is claimed by the house.
- Jackson's novel redefined American Gothic by grounding supernatural horror in psychological realism and feminist themes of women's isolation and powerlessness.
