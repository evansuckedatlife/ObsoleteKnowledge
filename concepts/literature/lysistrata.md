# Lysistrata

## summary

*Lysistrata* is Aristophanes' farcical antiwar comedy where the women of Athens and Sparta refuse sexual relations with their husbands until they end the devastating Peloponnesian War. Performed in 411 BCE during the 20-year conflict, the play uses obscene humour and sexual politics to critique endless warfare, staging a radical fantasy of female collective action ending male militarism. It remains one of antiquity's boldest comedies.

## you gotta know

- Lysistrata, an Athenian woman, organises women across hostile Greek city-states to withhold sex from soldiers until peace is negotiated.
- The women seize the Acropolis and its treasury to prevent further military funding; their husbands, sexually frustrated, sue for peace.
- The play is bawdy and explicit, featuring erect male actors in comedic phallus costumes symbolising frustrated military masculinity.
- Aristophanes juxtaposes the women's political ingenuity against male military obsession, questioning whether endless war serves the state.
- The chorus splits between old men (pro-war) and old women (pro-peace), staging a mock battle of political ideology.
- The fantasy resolution—peace achieved through female coordinated action—was outrageous propaganda for democracy and women's collective power.
- The play mocks both Athenian and Spartan leaders, hinting at the desperation of the war's final years.
