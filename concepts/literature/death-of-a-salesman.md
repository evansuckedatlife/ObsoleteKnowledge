## summary

Arthur Miller's tragedy follows *Willy Loman*, an aging traveling salesman whose mental and financial decline culminates in suicide. Through fragmented scenes mixing past and present, the play explores the collision between Willy's delusional faith in the American Dream and the harsh realities of capitalism and human limitation.

## you gotta know

- Premiered on Broadway in 1949; won the Pulitzer Prize for Drama that year and became one of the most frequently performed American plays.
- Willy's final act is suicide by car crash, designed to make it look accidental so his family can collect insurance money.
- The character *Biff*, Willy's favorite son, was expected to succeed but became a drifter; their relationship fragments over Biff's discovery of Willy's infidelity.
- Staged without a conventional set; the playwright used lighting and sound design to shift between Willy's present humiliation and memories of his past ambitions.
- The play introduced the term "anti-hero" to American drama—Willy is not admirable but sympathetic, undone by forces larger than himself.
- *Happy*, Willy's younger son, remains trapped in his father's delusional worldview, promising to vindicate Willy's failing legacy.

## connections
