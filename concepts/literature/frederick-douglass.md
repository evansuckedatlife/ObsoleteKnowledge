# Frederick Douglass

## summary

*Frederick Douglass* (1818–1895) was an escaped enslaved American who became the most prominent Black intellectual and orator of the 19th century. His autobiography, *Narrative of the Life of Frederick Douglass, an American Slave* (1845), stands as a masterpiece of American prose and a foundational text of abolitionist literature, proving through his very eloquence the lie of African inferiority.

## you gotta know

- Published his *Narrative* (1845) while enslaved, describing his brutal experiences in bondage and his self-taught path to literacy and freedom—the book became an immediate bestseller and cornerstone of abolitionist writing.
- His oratorical power made him a leading voice of the abolitionist movement, traveling internationally to advocate for emancipation and racial equality.
- Wrote two additional autobiographies—*My Bondage and My Freedom* (1855) and *Life and Times of Frederick Douglass* (1881)—expanding and revising his life story across decades.
- Advocated not only for emancipation but for full political participation, women's rights, and economic justice, making him a radical intellectual figure.
- Held several government positions after the Civil War, including Ambassador to Haiti, demonstrating both political achievement and the ongoing battles for Black dignity.
- His essays and speeches on literacy, freedom, identity, and American democracy remain essential to understanding 19th-century political thought.

## connections

- [[abolitionism]] — central figure in the movement; his writings and speeches galvanized anti-slavery sentiment.
- [[slave-narratives]] — his autobiography helped establish and legitimize the genre.
