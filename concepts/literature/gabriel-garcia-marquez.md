# Gabriel García Márquez

## summary

*Gabriel García Márquez* (1927–2014) was a Colombian novelist and short-story writer who became the voice of magical realism, blending the everyday with the mythical so seamlessly that the impossible becomes inevitable. His masterpiece *One Hundred Years of Solitude* rewrote the possibilities of the novel by treating a small village's multigenerational saga as both intimate family chronicle and cosmic fable, weaving cyclical time, incest, and transformation into prose of crystalline clarity. His work demonstrates that realism and the fantastic are not opposites but siblings.

## you gotta know

- *One Hundred Years of Solitude* (1967) — his epic novel following the Buendía family through seven generations in the isolated village of Macondo, where events repeat and the past bleeds into the present.
- Pioneered the literary technique of *magical realism*: treating fantastical events (a woman ascends to heaven while folding laundry, a character returns from the dead) with the matter-of-fact tone of documentary realism.
- Nobel Prize in Literature (1982) — awarded for his novels and short stories in which the fantastic and the realistic are woven into a richly coloured whole.
- *Love in the Time of Cholera* (1985) — a love story spanning fifty years, a meditation on time, memory, and fidelity shot through with the poignancy of aging and loss.
- Worked as a journalist throughout his life, convinced that storytelling and reportage were inseparable; his fiction carries the precision of witnessed detail.
- Drew deeply from Colombian violence and folklore — the banana-company massacre, the civil wars, the mythology of his Caribbean coast homeland.
