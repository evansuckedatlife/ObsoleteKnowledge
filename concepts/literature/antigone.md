# Antigone

## summary

*Antigone* is Sophocles' tragedy about defiance, duty, and the collision between familial piety and political law. Set after the civil war in Thebes, it follows Antigone as she buries her treasonous brother against Creon's explicit decree, accepting death rather than abandon her family obligation. The play interrogates whether human law or divine law carries ultimate authority.

## you gotta know

- Antigone's brothers—*Eteocles* and *Polynices*—kill each other in a war over Thebes; Creon forbids the burial of Polynices under pain of death.
- Antigone defies the decree and buries her brother with ritual, driven by religious duty and blood kinship.
- Creon condemns her to entombment alive; his son *Haemon*, betrothed to Antigone, pleads for her life and then dies with her.
- Creon's wife *Eurydice* kills herself upon learning of her son's death, compounding the tragedy.
- The blind prophet *Tiresias* warns Creon that his rigidity has angered the gods; Creon relents too late.
- The chorus represents the tension between obedience to law and loyalty to family—a central theme of the play.
- *Haemon* embodies youth and mercy, contrasting Creon's hardened authoritarianism.

## connections

- [[sophocles]] — author; part of the Theban trilogy (though not a direct sequel).
- [[creon]] — Theban ruler whose inflexibility drives the tragedy.
- [[haemon]] — Creon's son and Antigone's fiancé, caught between duty to father and love.
- [[oedipus-rex]] — shares the same city, lineage, and explores similar tensions between fate and choice.
- [[the-oresteia]] — Aeschylus's parallel meditation on family obligation versus civic law.
