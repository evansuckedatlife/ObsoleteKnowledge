# Is this a dagger which I see before me

## summary

*Macbeth's* soliloquy immediately before murdering *King Duncan*, in which he confronts a vision of a floating dagger—real or imaginary—as the instrument of regicide. The speech merges perception, desire, and the blurring of fantasy and murder.

## you gotta know

- Occurs in Act II as *Macbeth* steels himself to carry out the murder his wife has urged upon him.
- The dagger appears to float before him; *Macbeth* questions whether it is real or a hallucination born of his fevered mind.
- "Is this a dagger which I see before me, the handle toward my hand?"—ambiguity about the weapon's ontological status.
- *Macbeth* interprets the dagger as a sign from the witches and fate, validating his resolve to murder.
- The speech conflates the imaginary dagger with the real bloody deed to come, suggesting that thought and action blur in guilt.
- *Macbeth* addresses the invisible dagger as his guide, inviting it to lead him to *Duncan's* chamber.

## connections

- [[macbeth]] — the speaker, whose ambition and susceptibility to supernatural visions drive the tragedy.
- [[lady-macbeth]] — who has pushed him toward the murder and controls his will through manipulation and seduction.
- [[regicide]] — the act the speech precedes and psychologically frames as inevitable, fated, and already accomplished.
- [[hallucination]] — the dagger exemplifies the play's deep concern with perception, reality, and mental disturbance.
- [[witches]] — whose prophecies may be orchestrating *Macbeth's* actions or merely confirming his desires.
