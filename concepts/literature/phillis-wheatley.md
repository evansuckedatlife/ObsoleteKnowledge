# Phillis Wheatley

## summary

*Phillis Wheatley* (c. 1753–1784) was an enslaved African-American poet who became the first Black person and one of the first women to publish a book in the American colonies. Her work, composed while enslaved in Boston, demonstrated intellectual prowess and challenged prevailing assumptions about African inferiority and women's literary capacity.

## you gotta know

- Published *Poems on Various Subjects, Religious and Moral* (1773) while enslaved, making her the first African-American and first Black woman to publish a book in North America.
- Her poems addressed George Washington, the Earl of Dartmouth, and other prominent figures, establishing her as an intellectual voice during the Revolutionary era.
- Wrote in the neoclassical style of the 18th century, employing classical allusions, Christian themes, and formal verse structures that demonstrated mastery of the literary establishment's conventions.
- Was owned by Boston merchant John Wheatley, who recognized her talent and allowed her education—highly unusual and contested among those who questioned whether Africans were capable of intellectual achievement.
- Addressed slavery obliquely in her work, constrained by her position and the literary conventions of her time, yet her very existence as a published poet was a refutation of racist ideology.
- Lived briefly in freedom after manumission but died in poverty at 30, her legacy obscured for generations before being recovered by African-American literary historians.
