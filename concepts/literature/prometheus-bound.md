# Prometheus Bound

## summary

*Prometheus Bound* is Aeschylus' stark tragedy of defiance and suffering, depicting the Titan *Prometheus* chained to a rock as punishment for stealing fire for humanity and granting them knowledge. With minimal plot but maximum existential weight, the play stages Prometheus's refusal to submit to *Zeus*'s tyranny across aeons of torture, making him an archetype of heroic resistance against unjust authority. Its authorship has been disputed, but it stands as a cornerstone of Western rebellion myth.

## you gotta know

- Prometheus is bound to a rock in the Caucasus; each day an eagle devours his liver, which regenerates each night, an infinite punishment.
- He stole fire from the gods and gave it to humans, enabling them to advance technologically and spiritually—an act Zeus forbade.
- Prometheus refuses to reveal a secret knowledge he possesses: the identity of a woman whose son will overthrow Zeus.
- Various figures visit him—*Oceanus*, *Io* (a woman transformed into a heifer by Zeus's jealousy), and the Furies—but none can convince him to submit.
- Prometheus remains defiant despite torture, declaring his suffering righteous and Zeus's rule tyrannical.
- The play's ending is ambiguous: Prometheus is driven into the earth by a cataclysm, his resistance unbroken.
- *Heracles* is prophesied to eventually free Prometheus, though the original trilogy's conclusion is lost.
