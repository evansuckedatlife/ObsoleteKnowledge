## summary

*At Swim-Two-Birds* is Flann O'Brien's 1939 satirical novel set in Dublin, following a unnamed undergraduate who writes a book about characters who rebel against him. A dizzying metafictional work that layers narratives within narratives, it blends Arthurian legend, Irish folklore, modern Dublin comedy, and wildly experimental prose to create one of the most inventive and playful modernist novels in English literature.

## you gotta know

- Published in 1939; initially failed to find an audience but was later revived and recognized as a major modernist achievement by writers including James Joyce and Jorge Luis Borges.
- The novel's central conceit: a student-narrator writes a novel whose fictional characters—including King Arthur and Finnegan—become conscious and plot against their author, creating a nested rebellion against authorial authority.
- O'Brien's prose shifts wildly between registers: Dublin slang, archaic Irish English, pseudo-academic apparatus, literary pastiche, and long passages of pure comic invention.
- The book includes an entire subplot involving a drunken man named Trellis and his boarding-house guests, alongside retellings of Irish myth and modern Dublin low-life, all colliding chaotically.
- The epigraph from Euripides warns that a book should be a monster, a structure of intertwined passages and reactions—O'Brien takes this literally, constructing a novel of deliberate chaos and formal contradiction.
- O'Brien's comic genius lies in his willingness to break narrative convention entirely, interpolating untranslatable Irish passages, absurdist character descriptions, and philosophical non-sequiturs.
- The work predates much of postmodern literature but contains many of its central techniques: metafiction, narrative unreliability, intertextuality, and the collapse of authorial control.

## connections

- [[flann-o-brien]] — the Irish writer who published under multiple pseudonyms and was a pioneer of postmodern fiction.
- [[dublin]] — the setting whose low-life culture and bohemian characters O'Brien captures with affection and ridicule.
- [[stream-of-consciousness]] — the modernist technique O'Brien both employs and parodies throughout the novel.
- [[metafiction]] — the self-conscious narrative game O'Brien plays with his own novel and its characters.
- [[irish-mythology]] — the folklore and legend O'Brien collages into his contemporary satire.
