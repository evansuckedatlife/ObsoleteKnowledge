## summary

*Romeo and Juliet* is a tragedy by *William Shakespeare*, written early in his career (c. 1595), depicting the doomed love of two young people from warring families in Verona, Italy. The play explores themes of fate, family hatred, sexual desire, and the destructive power of vengeance; through the lovers' brief, intense passion and its tragic conclusion, Shakespeare examines how private emotion collides with public feuding, making it one of literature's most famous meditations on love and death.

## you gotta know

- Set in *Verona*, *Italy*, among two feuding noble families—the *Montagues* and the *Capulets*—whose hatred poisons the whole city and ultimately destroys their children.
- *Romeo* (a Montague) and *Juliet* (a Capulet) meet at a masked ball, fall instantly in love, and secretly marry the next day; their union is a rebellion against family duty and ancient grudge.
- *Friar Lawrence* facilitates their marriage and devises a plan to reunite them: Juliet will drink a potion to feign death, Romeo will retrieve her from the tomb, and they will flee together.
- The plan fails due to miscommunication: Romeo, believing Juliet truly dead, kills himself at her tomb; Juliet wakes to find him dead and kills herself in despair.
- The lovers' deaths finally shame the *Montagues* and *Capulets* into ending their feud, but at the cost of two innocent lives—the play's tragic irony.
- Themes of *fate* run throughout: the lovers are called "star-crossed," and the play explores whether their doom is written or self-inflicted through rash action.
- The play is renowned for its *poetic language*—sonnets, metaphors of light and darkness, passionate soliloquies—and its depiction of youthful, all-consuming romantic love.

- [[tragedy]] — the form that *Romeo and Juliet* embodies, a narrative of noble characters brought to ruin by fate and passion.
- [[hamlet]] — Shakespeare's later tragedy, more psychologically complex but similarly exploring how private moral struggles collide with external forces.
- [[othello]] — Shakespeare's tragedy of jealousy and manipulation, like *Romeo and Juliet* showing how destructive emotion overwhelms reason.
- [[the-tempest]] — Shakespeare's late play that, like *Romeo and Juliet*, explores love and reconciliation but resolves differently.
