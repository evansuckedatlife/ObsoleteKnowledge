# Once more unto the breach

## summary

*King Henry V's* rallying cry to his soldiers as they assault the breach in the walls of Harfleur, demanding they harden their resolve and channel their warrior spirit. The speech emphasizes physicality, courage, and the transformation of common men into instruments of war and glory.

## you gotta know

- Delivered in Act III as *Henry's* army prepares its frontal assault on the French fortress of Harfleur.
- "Once more unto the breach, dear friends, once more" echoes the relentless cycle of combat and the will to overcome fear.
- *Henry* commands soldiers to steel their faces, "imitate the action of the tiger," and summon animal fury and courage.
- Frames manhood itself as contingent on willingness to fight: "Now is it time to stir your blood in your veins."
- Contrasts the brutality required in war with the civility of peacetime, suggesting soldiers must temporarily abandon their humanity.
- The speech is more explicitly violent and martial than the later *Saint Crispin's Day* speech, emphasizing action over philosophy.
