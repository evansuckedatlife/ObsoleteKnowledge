# Salman Rushdie

## summary

*Salman Rushdie* (b. 1947) is a British-Indian novelist whose defiant blending of magical realism, postmodern narrative, and postcolonial critique has established him as a major voice in contemporary literature. His novels—especially *Midnight's Children* and *The Satanic Verses*—collapse historical realism into mythic excess, treat language as performative and unstable, and interrogate the legacies of empire, identity, and cultural hybridity from a diasporic perspective.

## you gotta know

- *Midnight's Children* (1981) won the Booker Prize; it narrates the lives of children born at the moment of Indian independence, blending historical epic with magical realism and narrative self-consciousness.
- *The Satanic Verses* (1988) provoked the Iranian fatwa (1989) against Rushdie; the novel blends realism and fantasy, addresses migration, identity, and religious hybridity; became a symbol of free expression vs. religious censorship.
- Employs *magical realism*—the seamless integration of fantastic and realistic elements—to represent postcolonial consciousness and the instability of "official" narratives of history and identity.
- His prose is digressive, densely allusive, and playful with language; he treats English as a postcolonial inheritance to be stretched, deformed, and made anew.
- *Shame* (1983) is an allegorical political novel set in a fictional South Asian nation, combining satire, family saga, and metafiction.
- His work foregrounds diaspora, migration, and the construction of identity across cultural borders; he writes from exile (forced and chosen) with sympathy for the displaced.
