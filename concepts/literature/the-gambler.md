## summary

*The Gambler* (1867), a short novel set in a decadent European casino town, chronicles the descent of *Alexei*, a poor tutor caught in obsessive gambling and a volatile love affair with the imperious *Polina*. Through the perspective of escalating desperation and illusory hope, Dostoevsky portrays gambling as a psychological disorder—a substitute for authentic feeling and will that consumes the self.

## you gotta know

- *Alexei* is a weak man of ambition without agency, drawn into the orbit of a wealthy Russian family and Polina, who tests his devotion through cruelty and demand.
- His gambling is not calculation but compulsion—an attempt to transcend his powerlessness and win Polina's love through sudden fortune, a fantasy of willful transformation.
- Polina sends him to the casino to win large sums to free her from debt; his wins and losses become a mirror of his emotional state—inflated hope, devastating shame.
- The setting is a fashionable spa-town casino (inspired by Wiesbaden), where wealthy Russians waste fortunes and moral standards corrode under perpetual temptation.
- Dostoevsky wrote the novella under financial pressure and partly from personal experience with gambling addiction; its urgency is autobiographical.
- The protagonist's voice becomes increasingly fragmented and manic as compulsion deepens, rendering the psychological texture of obsession.

## connections

- [[fyodor-dostoevsky]] — semi-autobiographical and born from his own gambling crisis in 1863.
