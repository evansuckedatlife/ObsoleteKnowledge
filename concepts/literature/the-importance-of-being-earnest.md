# The Importance of Being Earnest

## summary

*The Importance of Being Earnest* is Oscar Wilde's 1895 comedy of errors and witty repartee, in which two men invent fictional identities to escape their duties and win the affections of women who are charmed by the name "Earnest." A masterpiece of comic dialogue, it is Wilde's most performed play, celebrated for its epigrams, paradoxes, and the gleeful subversion of Victorian propriety and hypocrisy.

## you gotta know

- Premiered in 1895 as "A Trivial Comedy for Serious People"; written in four acts and subtitled to joke at its own apparent frivolousness.
- The plot hinges on a "bunbury"—a fictitious persona. Jack Worthing poses as his wealthy brother Earnest to woo Gwendolen, while his friend Algernon impersonates Earnest in the country to win Cecily.
- Famous for its razor-sharp epigrams: "The truth is rarely pure and never simple"; "To lose one parent may be regarded as a misfortune, to lose both looks like carelessness"; "All women become like their mothers. That is their tragedy."
- Wilde exploits the class anxieties and romantic conventions of Victorian England, revealing that identity and propriety are performances rather than essences.
- The character of Lady Bracknell is an iconic comic creation—a formidable aunt whose social snobbery is both exaggerated and instantly recognizable.
- Wilde's technique involves layering witticisms throughout; nearly every exchange contains a joke or paradox, making the play endlessly quotable.
- The resolution, surprisingly tender for a farce, reveals that Jack is actually Algernon's older brother—the search for an "earnest" identity resolves into biological fact, subverting the entire conceit.
