# Absurdism

## summary

Absurdism was a 20th-century literary and theatrical movement that depicted human existence as inherently meaningless, irrational, and farcical, employing fragmented dialogue, illogical plots, and darkly comic situations to convey the breakdown of communication and the fundamental disconnection between human yearning for meaning and the universe's silence. Emerging in the post-World War II era, it challenged conventional dramatic form and narrative coherence to capture the spiritual and intellectual crisis of the modern age.

## you gotta know

- Grew from *existential philosophy*, particularly *Albert Camus*'s notion that the universe is indifferent to human meaning-seeking; the term "absurd" refers to the collision between human desire for rational order and an inherently chaotic world.
- Rejected conventional *plot*, *character development*, and *narrative causality*; plays often featured circular action, repetition, non-sequiturs, and dialogue that fails to communicate, mirroring existential isolation.
- Major figures: *Samuel Beckett* (*Waiting for Godot*, *Endgame*), *Eugène Ionesco* (*The Bald Soprano*, *Rhinoceros*), *Harold Pinter* (*The Caretaker*), and *Tom Stoppard*, who redefined what drama could express.
- *Waiting for Godot* (1953)—two vagrants waiting for a mysterious Godot who never arrives—became the movement's masterpiece and paradigm, with minimal action and profound thematic emptiness.
- Characterized by *dark comedy* and *grotesqueness*: suffering and death rendered as comedy, normal social conventions revealed as arbitrary and absurd, language itself breakdown and lose meaning.
- The movement was partly a response to *post-war disillusionment*: the horrors of World War II and the Cold War undermined faith in rational progress, leaving artists to depict a world of meaninglessness and despair.
- Influenced later postmodern and experimental theater; its techniques of fragmentary dialogue and illogic became foundational to 20th-century avant-garde drama.

## connections

- [[samuel-beckett]] — Irish playwright whose *Waiting for Godot* and *Endgame* are foundational absurdist masterworks.
- [[ionesco]] — Romanian-French dramatist known for surreal, repetitive comedies that deconstruct language and social ritual.
- [[harold-pinter]] — British playwright of menace and ambiguity, master of the "pregnant pause" and coded violence.
- [[albert-camus]] — philosopher whose *The Myth of Sisyphus* and essay on the absurd provided intellectual foundation for the movement.
- [[existentialism]] — philosophical movement emphasizing individual freedom and meaninglessness that absurdism dramatizes.
- [[waiting-for-godot]] — the canonical absurdist play, emblem of the movement's themes and innovations.
- [[kafka]] — precursor whose nightmarish, illogical narratives anticipated absurdist sensibility.
