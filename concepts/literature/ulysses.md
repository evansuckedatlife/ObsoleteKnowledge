## summary

*Ulysses* is James Joyce's 1922 modernist masterpiece, a revolutionary novel that follows Leopold Bloom through Dublin on June 16, 1904, mirroring Homer's *Odyssey* in the span of a single day. Famous for its experimental stream-of-consciousness technique, fragmented narrative, and linguistic innovation, it transformed what literature could be and remain one of the most influential—and challenging—novels ever written.

## you gotta know

- Published in 1922; initially banned in many countries for obscenity; represents the apex of modernist fiction.
- Structured as a paralleled journey: the wanderings of Leopold Bloom (and Stephen Dedalus) through Dublin echo Odysseus's voyage home, collapsing epic into urban dailiness.
- Employs stream-of-consciousness narration—the unfiltered interior monologue of its characters—as its primary narrative mode, capturing thought exactly as it occurs, including fragmented, digressive, and even ungrammatical phrasing.
- The "Proteus" and "Circe" episodes are among the most formally audacious: shifting styles, scripts, lists, and modes of address within a single chapter.
- Bloom and Stephen represent opposite poles: the former a practical, sensual, compassionate everyman; the latter a young artist-intellectual struggling with exile and identity.
- Contains no chapters or traditional narrative breaks—divided instead into 18 "episodes," each with its own stylistic signature and (sometimes) Homeric parallel.
- Famous for passages like "Molly Bloom's soliloquy," an 40-page interior monologue by Bloom's wife that is virtually unpunctuated and captures raw female desire and bodily consciousness.

## connections

- [[james-joyce]] — its author, who spent most of his life in self-imposed exile from Ireland.
- [[a-portrait-of-the-artist-as-a-young-man]] — Joyce's earlier novel, which introduces Stephen Dedalus (who reappears in *Ulysses*).
- [[dublin]] — the city that is the work's true subject, mapped precisely across the novel's timeline.
- [[the-odyssey]] — Homer's epic, whose structure and themes the novel deliberately repurposes for the 20th century.
- [[stream-of-consciousness]] — the narrative technique that defines its form and influence.
- [[modernism]] — the literary movement it exemplifies and in some ways completes.
