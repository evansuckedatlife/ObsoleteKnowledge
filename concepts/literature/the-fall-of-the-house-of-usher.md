## summary

Edgar Allan Poe's 1839 masterwork of psychological terror follows an unnamed narrator's visit to a childhood friend, Roderick Usher, whose family mansion is collapsing—both physically and mentally. The story explores themes of decay, mental illness, and the blurred boundary between the living and the dead, culminating in the literal collapse of the house as Roderick's fragile sanity and his twin sister's curse break free.

## you gotta know

- The narrator arrives at the House of Usher to find his friend Roderick in a state of acute nervous affliction, hypersensitive to light, sound, and touch.
- Roderick's twin sister, Madeline, suffers from a wasting disease, and her cataleptic illness—so profound she appears dead—symbolizes the house's decay.
- Madeline is entombed alive within the house, and her return from the vault precipitates the final horror.
- The house itself is personified as a living entity, with cracks spreading through its foundations matching Roderick's mental deterioration.
- Poe uses the unreliable narrator technique: readers cannot fully trust whether the horrors are external or products of Roderick's diseased mind.
- The story epitomizes the American Gothic: a decaying aristocratic family, psychological isolation, and the failure of reason to contain supernatural terror.
- Published in *Burton's Gentleman's Magazine*, it became one of the most frequently anthologized tales in American literature.
