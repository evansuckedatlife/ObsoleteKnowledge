## summary

The Septuagint (LXX) is a Greek translation of the Hebrew scriptures produced in Alexandria, Egypt around the 3rd and 2nd centuries BCE for Greek-speaking Jews who no longer understood Hebrew. Begun during the reign of Ptolemy II, this translation became the Bible of Hellenistic Judaism and was the scriptural foundation of the early Christian church before most Christians read Greek.

## you gotta know

- Tradition holds that 70 (or 72) Jewish scholars were imprisoned on the island of Pharos and independently produced identical translations, hence "Septuagint" (Latin for 70).
- The actual translation was likely gradual, piecemeal work over a century across different translators of varying skill; the name is more mythic than historical.
- Produced in Alexandria for Jews whose native language was Greek, not Hebrew, making the scriptures accessible to the diaspora who could no longer read the original.
- Contains books not in the Hebrew Masoretic Text, including Wisdom, Sirach (Ecclesiasticus), and the Books of Maccabees, which were included in later Christian Old Testaments (Catholic and Orthodox canons) but excluded by Rabbinic Judaism.
- Earlier and sometimes more reliable than the Masoretic Text for reconstructing the original Hebrew scriptures; modern critical scholarship often prefers Septuagint readings.
- The early Christian church (1st–2nd centuries CE) used the Septuagint as its Old Testament and quoted it in the New Testament, especially in Paul's epistles and Hebrews.
- Lost in the Christian Western tradition until recovered from ancient manuscripts in the 17th century; now edited from Dead Sea Scroll fragments and medieval Greek manuscripts.
