# Gaudy Night

## summary

Dorothy L. Sayers's 1935 novel centers on Lord Peter Wimsey's investigation of mysterious threats and acts of vandalism at her old college, Shrewsbury, at Oxford University. The mystery unfolds against a backdrop of intellectual women scholars navigating academia, marriage, and independence, making the novel as much about female intellect and autonomy as about the crime itself.

## you gotta know

- The novel opens with Harriet Vane, a mystery writer and don at Shrewsbury College, asking Lord Peter Wimsey to help investigate threats and sabotage targeting the college.
- The vandalism escalates: anonymous letters, obscene messages, defaced manuscripts—all directed at undermining the women scholars and their work.
- Wimsey spends time at the college, observing the scholars, their relationships, and their tensions, while Harriet investigates privately.
- The investigation reveals that the sabotage stems from personal grievances—jealousy, romantic disappointment, and resentment of women's intellectual authority.
- The culprit proves to be one of the servants, whose daughter was rejected by the college; driven by rage and class resentment, she targets the scholars.
- The resolution intertwines with a romantic subplot between Wimsey and Harriet, who must reconcile their professional identities with personal desire.
- The novel ultimately affirms the value of female scholarship and intellectual community, despite the conflicts within it.
