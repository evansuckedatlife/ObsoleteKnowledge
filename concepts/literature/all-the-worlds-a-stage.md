# All the world's a stage

## summary

*Jacques's* meditation on the seven ages of human life, spoken in *As You Like It* as he observes the world as a stage and all humans as actors playing their parts. The speech moves from infancy through old age, each stage defined by diminished capacity and growing irrelevance, culminating in senility and death.

## you gotta know

- Delivered in Act II in the Forest of Arden, where *Jacques* has retreated from court.
- Opens with the metaphor "All the world's a stage, and all the men and women merely players"—humans are actors in a cosmic drama.
- Maps life into seven ages: infant, schoolboy, lover, soldier, justice, pantaloon, and finally second childlessness ("sans teeth, sans eyes, sans taste, sans everything").
- Each age has its characteristic dress, speech, and preoccupations; the soldier burns with ambition; the justice grows prosperous and cautious.
- The final age strips away all human dignity and agency, returning the old to infancy—a melancholic reversal.
- The speech reflects Renaissance ideas about theatrum mundi (the world as a stage), blending philosophy, psychology, and performance.

## connections

- [[as-you-like-it]] — the play in which *Jacques* delivers this meditation on escapism and the human condition.
- [[theatrum-mundi]] — the philosophical conceit that life is a stage and humans are players without free will.
- [[existentialism]] — the speech's focus on meaninglessness and the absurdity of human ambition.
- [[seven-deadly-sins]] — the ages implicitly embody various human failings and follies.
- [[aging]] — a central preoccupation; *Jacques* sees age as progressive diminishment rather than wisdom.
