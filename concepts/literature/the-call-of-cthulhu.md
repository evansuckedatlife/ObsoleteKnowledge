# The Call of Cthulhu

## summary

H.P. Lovecraft's 1928 novella introduces *Cthulhu*, an ancient cosmic entity sleeping beneath the Pacific Ocean, and establishes the tradition of "cosmic horror"—the terror that arises not from personal evil but from humanity's insignificance in a universe ruled by indifferent, ancient powers. The narrative, framed as a scholar's piecing together of fragmented accounts, reveals a global cult devoted to an entity that threatens human sanity and civilization itself.

## you gotta know

- The protagonist discovers a collection of documents and relics suggesting a worldwide religion devoted to Cthulhu, a sleeping god imprisoned beneath the sea.
- Cthulhu is depicted as an immense entity—part octopus, dragon, and humanoid—whose very appearance can drive humans to madness or death.
- The story incorporates the Necronomicon, a fictional grimoire of occult knowledge that Lovecraft reused across his fiction, establishing an interconnected universe of cosmic terror.
- A cult called the Order of Dagon has performed rituals and sacrifices across continents in service to Cthulhu's eventual awakening.
- Lovecraft uses the framing device of discovered journals and reports to create verisimilitude, blending academic discourse with mounting dread.
- The novella established the subgenre of "cosmic horror": terror derived not from evil human action but from encounters with entities whose nature is fundamentally incomprehensible and hostile to human existence.
- *The Call of Cthulhu* became the founding text of Lovecraftian fiction, inspiring countless writers and entire shared fictional universes.
