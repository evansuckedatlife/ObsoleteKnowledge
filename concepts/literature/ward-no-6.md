## summary

*Ward No. 6* is Chekhov's searing critique of institutional indifference, set in a provincial Russian hospital's isolation ward for psychiatric patients. When the compassionate physician Dr. Ragin begins to philosophize with an intelligent inmate, he gradually abandons his professional distance, only to find himself trapped on the ward's benches as a patient himself—stripped of authority, dignity, and reason.

## you gotta know

- Dr. *Ragin* runs a dreary ward housing six patients in squalid conditions, including *Ivan Dmitrich*, an educated man who articulates eloquent critiques of society and oppression.
- Ragin begins to engage with Ivan as an intellectual equal, visiting the ward regularly to discuss philosophy and social injustice, blurring the boundary between doctor and patient.
- Ivan recognizes in Ragin a kindred spirit—someone aware of the world's corruption but resigned to it—and influences the doctor's worldview, deepening his philosophical detachment.
- Ragin's colleagues, alarmed by his eccentricity and growing identification with the inmates, conspire to have him declared unfit and committed to Ward No. 6.
- Once confined, Ragin discovers the unbridgeable gulf between philosophical acceptance of suffering and the immediate reality of physical abuse and degradation.
- He dies under mysterious circumstances, his fate a grim reversal—the doctor becomes the object of institutional cruelty, his reason useless against systemic brutality.
- Chekhov's ending refuses catharsis or redemption, instead indicting the resigned complicity of those who rationalize injustice.
