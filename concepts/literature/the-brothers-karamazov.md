# The Brothers Karamazov

## summary

*The Brothers Karamazov* (1880) is Dostoevsky's final and most ambitious novel, exploring faith, doubt, and morality through three brothers and their dissolute father. The murder of their father sets off a spiritual and philosophical crisis, reaching its climax in Ivan's rebellion against God and Alyosha's path of redemptive love—a testament to the author's lifelong wrestle with theodicy and grace.

## you gotta know

- The three brothers embody different responses to faith: *Dmitri* is passionate and sensual; *Ivan* is intellectual and atheistic; *Alyosha* is spiritual and forgiving.
- *Ivan's Grand Inquisitor* parable—a story-within-the-story where Christ returns in medieval Spain—is one of literature's greatest statements on freedom, faith, and suffering.
- The father *Fyodor Karamazov* is a libertine whose murder becomes the pivot: Dmitri is arrested; Ivan descends into existential fever; Alyosha tends to both.
- *Sonya*, a local girl, loves Alyosha and represents the possibility of love transcending intellectual doubt.
- The novel interrogates whether morality requires God: Ivan argues that "if God does not exist, all things are permitted"; Alyosha answers through active love.
- Published serially in *The Russian Messenger* and left incomplete at Dostoevsky's death; the epilogue hints at Dmitri's spiritual awakening in exile.
