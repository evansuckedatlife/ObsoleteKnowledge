# In Cold Blood

## summary

Truman Capote's 1966 novel is a true-crime narrative account of the 1959 murder of a Kansas farm family and the investigation leading to the capture and trial of the killers. Capote pioneered "narrative nonfiction," blending meticulous journalism with literary technique to create a work that reads as both detective story and psychological study.

## you gotta know

- The Clutter family—Herb, Bonnie, Nancy, and Kenyon—are murdered in their rural Kansas home on November 15, 1959, apparently by burglars.
- The crime appears random; the killers seem to have no connection to the family and flee the state, leaving investigators with few leads.
- Two drifters, Richard Hickock and Perry Smith, are eventually identified through fingerprints and witness testimony.
- Capote tracks the investigation alongside the killers, presenting their perspectives, childhoods, and psychological makeup as the mystery unfolds.
- The narrative builds to the capture of Hickock and Smith in Las Vegas, followed by their trial in Kansas.
- Capote reveals that the two men are partners in crime with starkly different personalities and motivations, yet bound together by their crime.
- The novel concludes with their execution, offering neither judgment nor sentimentality, but a complex portrait of violence and human nature.

## connections

- [[truman-capote]] — author; his immersion in the case and literary technique redefined true crime.
- [[true-crime]] — the pioneering example of literary nonfiction true-crime narrative.
- [[investigation]] — the detective work parallels the psychological study of the criminals.
- [[kansas]] — the setting; rural America becomes as essential to the narrative as the crime itself.
- [[richard-hickock]] and [[perry-smith]] — the real criminals; Capote humanizes them without excusing their acts.
