# Sei Shōnagon

## summary

**Sei Shōnagon** (966–1017 CE) was a Heian court noblewoman and lady-in-waiting whose *Makura no Soshi* (*The Pillow Book*) is one of the foundational texts of Japanese literature and a prized document of aristocratic life. Her aphoristic, fragmentary style—lists of beautiful things, personal observations, and witty commentary—established a literary form that influenced Japanese aesthetics for centuries.

## you gotta know

- Authored *The Pillow Book* (*Makura no Soshi*), a collection of essays, lists, and observations of court life, written in the vernacular *kana* script around 1010 CE.
- *The Pillow Book* is organized as scattered thoughts and thematic lists ("Things that are elegant," "Things that are unpleasant," "Suitable and unsuitable things") rather than a chronological narrative, creating an intimate portrait of Heian sensibility.
- Served as lady-in-waiting to Empress Teishi, observing and recording court ceremonies, seasonal rituals, aesthetic refinements, and the petty drama of aristocratic competition.
- Her writing is noted for its wit, irony, and incisive social commentary—she does not shy from criticism of her peers or pointed observations about vanity and human nature.
- *The Pillow Book* preserves detailed information about Heian fashion, poetry contests, courtship rituals, and the intellectual life of high-ranking women—invaluable historical testimony.
- Lived contemporaneously with Murasaki Shikibu; both women represent the peak of female literary authority in the Heian court and influenced the canonisation of women's writing in Japanese letters.
