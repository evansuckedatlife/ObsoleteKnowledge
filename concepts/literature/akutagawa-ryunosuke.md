# Akutagawa Ryūnosuke

## summary

**Akutagawa Ryūnosuke** (1892–1927) was a seminal figure in early twentieth-century Japanese literature who pioneered the modern short story, often set in historical Japan, and explored psychological ambiguity, guilt, and the unreliability of perception. His luminous, intellectually rigorous prose and tragic early death have secured him a towering place in the Japanese literary canon.

## you gotta know

- Authored *"In a Grove"* (1922), a story of multiple conflicting testimonies about a crime in medieval Japan that becomes a formal meditation on the impossibility of objective truth—the basis for Akira Kurosawa's film *Rashomon* (1950).
- Wrote prolifically in the short-story form, publishing stories that revisited classical Japanese tales and legends through a modern, psychologically penetrating lens (*"Rashomon"*, *"The Nose"*, *"Hell Screen"*).
- His story *"Rashomon"* (1915) depicts a servant driven by hunger and survival to rationalize moral compromise, exploring the corruption of ethics under material desperation.
- Studied English literature and Western philosophy intensely, synthesizing European modernism with classical Japanese aesthetics and narrative techniques.
- Struggled with drug addiction, depression, and spiritual malaise—themes that permeate his late work, culminating in his suicide at age 35 in 1927.
- Established the literary prize bearing his name (*Akutagawa Prize*), Japan's most prestigious award for short fiction, awarded twice yearly and given to emerging writers of distinction.
