# American Literature

## summary

American literature, beginning as a derivative outgrowth of English Romanticism, developed a distinctive voice in the 19th century through figures like *Nathaniel Hawthorne*, *Herman Melville*, *Walt Whitman*, and *Edgar Allan Poe*, who forged literary traditions out of American experience: the frontier, slavery, democracy, and the individual's relationship to an expanding nation. The 20th century saw American literature achieve world dominance through *F. Scott Fitzgerald*, *Ernest Hemingway*, *William Faulkner*, and others who absorbed modernism, African American voices, and immigrant perspectives into a capacious national tradition. American literature is characterized by a preoccupation with freedom, reinvention, violence, race, and the meaning of national identity.

## you gotta know

### 19th-Century Emergence (American Renaissance)
- *Ralph Waldo Emerson* (1803–1882): philosopher and poet; *transcendentalism*; individualism, nature as moral teacher, self-reliance; foundational to American intellectual tradition.
- *Nathaniel Hawthorne* (1804–1864): *The Scarlet Letter* (1850); Puritan New England; sin, guilt, and the individual vs. society; psychological realism.
- *Herman Melville* (1819–1891): *Moby-Dick* (1851); epic novel of obsession, ambition, and metaphysical inquiry; the whale as sublime, unknowable force.
- *Walt Whitman* (1819–1892): poet of American democracy; *Leaves of Grass* (1855); free verse, expansive catalogues, the democratic bard celebrating American diversity and bodily experience.
- *Edgar Allan Poe* (1809–1849): master of psychological horror and the grotesque; *The Fall of the House of Usher*; influence on detective fiction and modernism.

### Civil War & Reconstruction Era
- *American Civil War* (1861–1865) as historical and literary watershed; the war destroyed the notion of American innocence.
- *Mark Twain* (*Adventures of Huckleberry Finn*, 1884): vernacular American voice; race, freedom, and moral education; canonical American novel.
- *Emily Dickinson* (1830–1886): poetry of introspection and compression; unconventional form; metaphysical inquiry; largely unpublished in her lifetime.

### 20th-Century Modernism & After
- *F. Scott Fitzgerald* (*The Great Gatsby*, 1925): Jazz Age America; the American dream as corruption and illusion; lyrical prose; emotional authenticity beneath surfaces.
- *Ernest Hemingway* (1899–1961): minimalist prose; the iceberg principle (what is left unsaid); *The Sun Also Rises*, *For Whom the Bell Tolls*; influence on American masculinity and cool detachment.
- *William Faulkner* (1897–1962): Southern gothic; stream-of-consciousness; *Yoknapatawpha County* saga; modernist experimentalism applied to American South and race.
- *African American literature*: *Harlem Renaissance* (1920s); *Langston Hughes*, *Zora Neale Hurston*; assertion of Black cultural identity and literary voice.
- *Native American literature*: *N. Scott Momaday*, *Leslie Marmon Silko*; reclamation of indigenous perspectives and storytelling traditions.

### Literary Preoccupations
- *The frontier*: westward expansion, manifest destiny, the individual against nature; ambivalence toward progress.
- *The American dream*: upward mobility, self-invention, material success; literature often treats the dream as mirage or corruption.
- *Race and slavery*: from *Hawthorne*'s *Scarlet Letter* through *Faulkner* and *Morrison*; the stain of slavery on the national conscience.
- *The individual vs. society*: tension between democratic freedom and social conformity; the nonconformist as hero and outcast.
- *Violence*: American literature is marked by a preoccupation with violence—individual, racial, historical; neither condemned nor glamorized but recognized as constitutive.

### Formal Innovation
- *Vernacular voice*: American writers embrace dialect and colloquial speech as legitimate literary language (*Twain*, *Whitman*).
- *Modernist absorption*: 20th-century American writers absorb European modernism but inflect it distinctly (Faulkner's stream-of-consciousness differs from Joyce's).
- *Sprawling, encyclopedic forms*: *Whitman*'s catalogues, *Melville*'s digressions, *Faulkner*'s fragmentation; resistance to European formal restraint.

## context

American literature's rise to world dominance in the 20th century mirrors the United States' emergence as a global superpower. The earlier 19th-century achievement—*Moby-Dick*, *The Scarlet Letter*—were not initially widely read; American literature had to assert itself against British cultural authority. By the 1920s, with *Fitzgerald*, *Hemingway*, and the *Harlem Renaissance*, American literature had become a major force; modernism itself was substantially shaped by American writers. Yet American literature remains marked by questions of authenticity and address: who is the American writer writing for? Which America—the promised land of freedom or the historical reality of slavery and indigenous genocide? Whose stories constitute the canon? The 20th century saw successive waves of voices—African American, Latinx, Asian American, LGBTQ—challenging the hegemony of white male perspectives.
