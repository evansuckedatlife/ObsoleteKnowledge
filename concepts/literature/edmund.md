# Edmund

## summary

**Edmund**, the bastard son of the Earl of Gloucester, is a schemer who exploits his illegitimate status as an excuse for ruthlessness, pitting his legitimate elder brother Edgar against their father and seducing both of King Lear's cruel daughters. He embodies the theme of nature versus legitimacy: though he calls himself "nature's" bastard child (as opposed to society's), his actions are purely calculated manipulation. His betrayals destroy Gloucester and set in motion the final catastrophe, though he is ultimately defeated by Edgar in combat and dies confessing his crimes.

## you gotta know

### The Bastard's Complaint
- Edmund opens the play with a soliloquy declaring his resentment: he is the bastard son of Gloucester, born outside marriage, and thus barred from inheritance by legitimate succession.
- He frames his villainy as a response to social injustice: "Why bastard? Wherefore base?... I grow; I prosper."
- Yet his actions suggest his villainy is less born of necessity than of naked ambition; he is intelligent and articulate, capable of advancement through merit, but chooses manipulation instead.

### The Frame Against Edgar
- Edmund forges a letter from his brother Edgar, suggesting Edgar is plotting to kill their father and seize the earldom.
- He then confronts Edgar with the letter and, when Edgar flees in panic, Edmund helps his father hunt Edgar down (who goes mad and disguises himself as "Poor Tom").
- Edmund sends his father to Lear on the heath, where Gloucester is captured and blinded; Edmund benefits by having his father's lands forfeit to the crown.

### The Seduction of Two Sisters
- Edmund seduces both Goneril and Regan, King Lear's cruel elder daughters, promising marriage to whichever gains power first.
- When Goneril poisons Regan (who loves Edmund), Goneril stabs herself; Edmund is now the sole obstacle to his succession.
- Yet both daughters' affection for him leads to mutual destruction, suggesting that Edmund's charm (like Iago's or Richard III's) masks an emptiness—he uses love as a tool.

### The Unravelling
- Edmund is made Earl of Gloucester and temporarily rules; his ascent is meteoric, unchecked by conscience or fear.
- Edgar (still disguised as Poor Tom) defeats Edmund in single combat and reveals himself; Edmund, mortally wounded, finally confesses his crimes.
- Edmund even orders the death of Lear and Cordelia before dying, but his order is too late—Cordelia is already hanged.

### Illegitimacy and Nature
- Edmund's invocation of "nature" (he calls himself "nature's bastard") is a deliberate inversion: he claims that nature, not law, should rule—that the strongest should govern.
- Yet the play refutes him: *nature* in the play is represented by Cordelia's love and Edgar's endurance, not by Edmund's cunning.
- His illegitimacy is the occasion for his ambition but not its justification.

## context

Edmund is less elaborated than Iago or Macbeth but is thematically crucial to *King Lear*'s exploration of *what is natural*. The play is obsessed with nature—Lear invokes it, the storm rages as nature's fury, and legitimate bonds (parent-child, king-subject) are appealed to as "natural." Edmund's explicit rejection of these bonds, his claim that nature *permits* ruthlessness, represents a philosophical threat that the play spends five acts refuting. In quiz bowl, Edmund is central to discussions of legitimacy, the social construction of villainy, and the nature of natural law.
