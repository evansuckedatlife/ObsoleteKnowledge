# Our Town

## summary

Thornton Wilder's 1938 play is a three-act meditation on small-town American life, set in the fictional *Groves Corners, New Hampshire* in 1901-1913. Narrated by the Stage Manager, the play moves from community observation to intimate scenes of the Gibbs and Gibbs families, culminating in marriage and death.

## you gotta know

- Won the Pulitzer Prize for Drama in 1938; has been revived continuously and is one of the most produced plays in American high schools and community theaters.
- The play is almost entirely without scenery or props; actors mime opening doors and stirring milk, forcing audiences to imaginatively complete the stage world.
- The Stage Manager breaks the fourth wall throughout, commenting on the action and the human condition; he becomes a kind of witness to mortality and the preciousness of ordinary moments.
- The second act depicts a dual wedding, with scenes of the two couples on their wedding mornings interspersed—a meditation on love, commitment, and the passage of time.
- The third act moves to the cemetery; the dead are present, and the living confront the reality that their current love and domestic comfort are temporary.
- Emily's final cry, "Oh, Mama, just look at me one more time, Mother!"—uttered as she relives her ninth birthday—crystallizes the play's theme: we fail to appreciate the present while we live it.
