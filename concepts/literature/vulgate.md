# Vulgate

## summary

The Vulgate is a Latin translation of the Hebrew Bible and New Testament completed by Jerome between 390 and 405 CE, commissioned by Pope Damasus to provide a unified, authoritative scripture for the Western church. It became the official Bible of the Roman Catholic Church for over 1,500 years and shaped Christian theology, liturgy, and Latin itself through its phrasings.

## you gotta know

- Commissioned by Pope Damasus I to replace the Old Latin translations, which were fragmentary and inconsistent across different scriptural manuscripts.
- Jerome, a Church Father and linguist, translated the Hebrew Bible directly from Hebrew (not the Septuagint), and revised the New Testament from Greek manuscripts.
- Completed over 15 years; Jerome's process was meticulous, comparing manuscripts and consulting Jewish scholars to understand difficult Hebrew words.
- The term "Vulgate" comes from the Latin *vulgata* (common, common tongue), reflecting its purpose: scripture in the language of everyday Latin-speaking Christians.
- Officially adopted as the exclusive biblical text of the Catholic Church at the Council of Trent (1545–1563), cementing its authority for four centuries.
- Medieval scholars added the fourfold method of biblical interpretation (literal, allegorical, moral, anagogical) to commentaries on the Vulgate, shaping Christian exegesis.
- Translated into hundreds of vernacular languages during the Reformation; Protestant translators like Tyndale and Luther used it as a reference alongside the original Hebrew and Greek.
- The Sixtine (1590) and Clementine (1592) editions standardized the Vulgate text for the modern Catholic Church.
