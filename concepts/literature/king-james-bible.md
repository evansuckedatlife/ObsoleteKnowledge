## summary

The King James Bible is a 1611 English translation of the Hebrew Bible and Christian New Testament commissioned by King James I of England. Its majestic, cadent prose established a register of English that would dominate religious and literary thought for centuries, becoming the most influential translation in the English-speaking world and shaping the language itself through thousands of phrases now embedded in vernacular speech.

## you gotta know

- Published in 1611 and authorized as the official Bible of the Church of England; undertook 47 translators working in teams for over a decade.
- Translated from the Textus Receptus (Greek New Testament) and Hebrew Massoretic texts available at the time, not the earliest or most reliable manuscripts.
- Famous for its rhythmic, archaic English: "In the beginning was the Word" and "I am the resurrection and the life" became canonical phrasings.
- Introduced or cemented hundreds of phrases in English: "salt of the earth," "the writing on the wall," "go the extra mile," "knock and it shall be opened unto you."
- The Geneva Bible (1560) was the scholarly alternative favored by Protestants; the KJV was less scholarly but far more resonant and eventually superseded it.
- Underwent 54 "authorized editions" with minor corrections between 1611 and 1769 (the Blayney revision is the standard modern text).
- Challenged in the 19th and 20th centuries by newer translations using earlier manuscripts (the Revised Version, the RSV, modern translations), yet remains culturally dominant.
