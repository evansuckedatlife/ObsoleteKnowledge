# Long Day's Journey into Night

## summary

Eugene O'Neill's 1956 play (published posthumously) is a four-act family tragedy set over a single day in a Connecticut summer home. The work traces the Tyrone family's slow unraveling as addiction, disease, regret, and hidden resentments surface, culminating in confession and despair.

## you gotta know

- Largely autobiographical: O'Neill drew on his own family's struggles with morphine addiction, tuberculosis, and failed dreams to create the play.
- The protagonist is not a single character but the *family itself*—the father James, mother Mary (addicted to morphine), and two sons (one tubercular, one an alcoholic drifter).
- The play's title echoes Dante's *Inferno*; the journey is downward, each act pulling the family deeper into honesty and pain.
- Premiered in 1956, after O'Neill's death; it won the Pulitzer Prize and is considered one of the greatest works of American drama.
- The dialogue is verbose and repetitive, mirroring the circularity of family trauma and the way grievances resurface without resolution.
- Set almost in real time: the action spans from morning to late night, intensifying the sense of inexorable decline.
