# The Idiot

## summary

*The Idiot* (1869) follows *Prince Myshkin*, a saint-like innocent returning to St. Petersburg after recovery from epilepsy abroad, whose Christian virtue and unselfishness make him catastrophically vulnerable in a corrupt, materialistic society. His doomed love for the passionate *Nastasya Filipovna* and his witnessing of human cruelty underscore Dostoevsky's bitter vision: goodness may be noble but is powerless against worldly vice.

## you gotta know

- *Prince Myshkin* is almost pathologically good—honest, compassionate, forgiving—making him seem foolish to ruthless society; his "idiocy" is moral rather than intellectual.
- *Nastasya Filipovna* is a beautiful woman of ambiguous virtue, kept by the wealthy *Totsky* and adored by both Myshkin and the rapacious *Rogozhin*, who finally murders her.
- The novel's structure mimics Myshkin's disorientation: sudden tonal shifts, fragmented narrative, psychological depths that defy easy resolution.
- Myshkin's epileptic fits are literal and symbolic—attacks of transcendent vision followed by vulnerability and anguish.
- The work was serialized in *The Russian Messenger* and remains incomplete in some editions; Dostoevsky abandoned plot to excavate psychology.
- Myshkin's failure to redeem anyone—his goodness met with mockery, abandonment, and tragedy—reflects Dostoevsky's doubt about idealism in modern life.
