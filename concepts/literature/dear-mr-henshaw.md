# Dear Mr. Henshaw

## summary

*Dear Mr. Henshaw* is Beverly Cleary's epistolary novel told through the letters and diary entries of Leigh Botts, a sixth-grader who begins writing to his favorite author, Boyd Henshaw, as a way of processing his parents' divorce and his own loneliness. The novel is a masterwork of emotional authenticity, showing how writing becomes a lifeline for a grieving, isolated child.

## you gotta know

- Leigh starts as a fan-boy writing to his idol, Boyd Henshaw, but gradually transforms his correspondence into a vehicle for processing his deepest feelings about his parents' separation and his father's absence.
- The novel is structured entirely through Leigh's letters and diary entries, allowing readers to witness his unfiltered thoughts, anger, confusion, and slow emotional maturation without authorial commentary.
- Mr. Henshaw's role is minimal but crucial—his kind, brief responses encourage Leigh to continue writing and to explore his own feelings, modeling how mentorship can be quiet and generative.
- The "Dear Mr. Henshaw" device becomes a metaphorical bridge between Leigh's broken home and a wider, kinder world where adults listen and validate a child's experience.
- Won the Newbery Medal in 1984, celebrated for its authenticity in depicting childhood emotion and its innovative use of the epistolary form.
- Cleary captures the texture of a child's interior life—the small humiliations, the anger at adults, the confusion of loyalties, the slow growth of wisdom—with remarkable tenderness.
- The novel suggests that writing itself is therapeutic and transformative; Leigh becomes a writer not as a career but as a way of surviving and understanding.
