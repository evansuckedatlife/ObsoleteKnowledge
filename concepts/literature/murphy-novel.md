# Murphy

## summary

*Murphy* is Samuel Beckett's 1938 debut novel about Murphy, an Irishman in London who seeks to escape the world's chaos by sitting in a rocking chair and dwelling in his own mind. A darkly comic meditation on the mind's refuge from external suffering, the novel combines slapstick farce with philosophical meditation, establishing many themes Beckett would develop throughout his career: the failure of communication, the mind's retreat from reality, and the comedy of human desperation.

## you gotta know

- Published in 1938, Beckett's first novel; though not immediately successful, it established his distinctive voice: tragicomic, philosophical, and bleakly funny.
- Murphy abandons a love interest (Celia) and becomes a male nurse in a London asylum, where he encounters patients who paradoxically possess the mental peace he seeks.
- The novel's opening is famous for its Cartesian anxiety: "The sun shone, having no alternative, on everything"; everything proceeds from this premise of cosmic indifference.
- Murphy's fantasy is to achieve a state of mental vacancy—what Beckett calls "the big world" of pure mind—while the external "little world" of bodies and relationships continually disrupts this dream.
- The asylum chapters contain some of Beckett's sharpest satirical writing, depicting modern institutional life with acid humor while suggesting that madness may be saner than sanity.
- Beckett's style is already apparent: long sentences interspersed with fragments, abrupt tonal shifts, and a narrator who comments on his own narrative inadequacy.
- The ending—Murphy's accidental death—is bathetic and absurd, but it offers a kind of release the living cannot attain.
