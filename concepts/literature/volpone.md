## summary

Ben Jonson's *Volpone, or The Fox* is a ruthless satirical comedy exposing avarice, deceit, and social parasitism in Venice. The wealthy invalid *Volpone* fakes terminal illness to lure legacy-hunters into increasingly absurd acts of flattery and depravity, and the play's dark comedy lies in watching greed strip away every pretence of virtue until all characters become willing dupes in their own humiliation.

## you gotta know

- *Volpone* (the Fox), a Venetian merchant, pretends to be dying to attract fortune-hunters who compete to inherit his wealth through increasingly shameless pandering.
- His servant *Mosca* engineers the deceptions, playing each suitor against the others and pocketing substantial bribes in the process; his wit and malice are the play's engine.
- Each suitor — *Corvino* the merchant, *Corbaccio* the miser, *Voltore* the lawyer — brings gifts and moral outrages in hopes of the inheritance, revealing their cores as pure appetite.
- *Corvino* even prostitutes his own wife to Volpone in hopes of gaining favour, a transgression that shifts the comedy toward darker satire of male possession.
- Volpone and Mosca's scheme unravels when Volpone's appetite outpaces caution; his final attempt to seduce Corvino's wife precipitates their exposure.
- The Venetian magistrates' punishment is swift: Volpone is exposed as a fraud and sentenced; Mosca's betrayal of his master seals both their ruin.
- The play ends not with redemption but with cold justice: every character is punished or humiliated in proportion to their greed, and the audience delights in their comeuppance.
