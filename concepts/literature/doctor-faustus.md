# Doctor Faustus

## summary

*Doctor Faustus* is Christopher Marlowe's tragic play about an overreaching scholar who sells his soul to the devil for forbidden knowledge and magical power, only to descend into despair and damnation. Written in the 1590s, it established the Faustian bargain as a central myth of ambition outrunning morality, and the protagonist's final hour of anguish remains one of English drama's most powerful meditations on mortality and regret.

## you gotta know

- Faustus, a learned scholar, summons *Mephistopheles* and signs a pact: his soul for twenty-four years of unlimited knowledge and power.
- His famous opening speech: "Is not philosophy but divinity of highest kind?" — the tragedy of intellectual pride seeking to transcend human limits.
- The final monologue, beginning "See, see, where Christ's blood streams," depicts his last hour as the clock strikes midnight, evoking genuine terror of damnation.
- The play explores the Renaissance hunger for knowledge against Christian doctrine — the clash of humanism and theology that defines the era.
- *Mephistopheles*, the devil's agent, is less tempter than Faustus's willing accomplice, warning him even as Faustus descends.
- Despite gaining magical command, Faustus's desires grow petty — levitating spirits, pranking the Pope — squandering eternity on triviality.
- A morality play structure (devils and angels contend for his soul) combines with proto-modern psychology: Faustus's damnation is as much self-inflicted as imposed.
