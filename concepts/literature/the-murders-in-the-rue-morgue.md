# The Murders in the Rue Morgue

## summary

Edgar Allan Poe's 1841 short story about a seemingly impossible double murder in Paris—a mother and daughter killed in a locked room high above the street with no apparent means of entry or weapon. C. Auguste Dupin, an amateur detective with extraordinary analytic powers, solves the crime through reason and observation alone, establishing the template for the modern detective story.

## you gotta know

- Published in 1841 in *Graham's Magazine*; widely considered the first modern detective story, predating Arthur Conan Doyle's *Sherlock Holmes* by decades.
- C. Auguste Dupin, a reclusive Parisian gentleman with a passion for analysis, is the first detective protagonist in Western fiction.
- The victims are Madame L'Espanaye and her daughter Camille, brutally murdered in a fourth-floor apartment on Rue Morgue.
- No clear entry point; the room's windows are too high, the door was locked from inside—the crime scene appears physically impossible.
- The weapon and killer are initially a mystery; suspicion falls on various suspects, all of whom have solid alibis.
- Dupin solves the crime through close observation of details overlooked by police: a tuft of hair, the strength required for the murders, and eyewitness accounts of odd language.
- The killer is revealed to be an escaped orangutan, brought to Paris on a ship—a shocking solution that makes the "impossibility" suddenly logical.
