# Langston Hughes

## summary

*Langston Hughes* was an American poet, writer, and cultural innovator central to the Harlem Renaissance, known for infusing his poetry with jazz rhythms, blues idiom, and vernacular speech. His work celebrated Black culture while critiquing American racism, and his prolific output—poetry, short stories, drama, essays—made him one of the most influential voices of 20th-century American letters.

## you gotta know

- Pioneered "jazz poetry," incorporating the rhythms and improvisational spirit of jazz music into verse, making formal poetry accessible to Black audiences.
- Published *The Weary Blues* (1926), his debut collection, which brought Harlem nightlife, blues feeling, and African-American voice to literary prominence.
- Wrote the poem "Harlem" (1951), asking "What happens to a dream deferred?"—a stark, memorable exploration of delayed Black aspiration that became canonical.
- His short stories collected in works like *The Ways of White Folks* (1934) examined racism and racial hypocrisy with wit and moral clarity.
- Wrote plays, translations, children's literature, essays, and journalism across a 50-year career, making him a cultural polymath.
- Centered Black joy, resilience, and dignity alongside critique of oppression, refusing a purely victimological framing of Black life.
