# William Faulkner

## summary

*William Faulkner* (1897–1962) was an American modernist novelist who brought stream-of-consciousness and radical structural experimentation to the American South. Born in Mississippi, he spent much of his life there, creating the fictional county of Yoknapatawpha as a microcosm of the region's history, violence, and moral complexity. His novels—particularly *The Sound and the Fury* and *Absalom, Absalom!*—are celebrated for their stylistic innovations and despised for their difficulty; his work explores Southern Gothic themes of family decay, racism, and the legacy of slavery with uncompromising psychological depth.

## you gotta know

### Life & Region
- Born in New Albany, Mississippi; moved to Oxford at age 5; spent most of his life there despite travels and brief Hollywood screenwriting work.
- Fought in World War I (or claimed to); influenced by Hemingway and other expatriate modernists, though he chose to remain in the South.
- Explored themes of family, decline, and the South's relationship to its past—particularly slavery and the Civil War—with unprecedented moral seriousness and narrative complexity.
- Won the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1949; his acceptance speech articulated a humanistic vision of literature.

### Major Works
- *The Sound and the Fury* (1929): four-part novel following the Compson family across decades; first three sections told from perspectives of three sons (one intellectually disabled, one obsessed with his sister's sexuality, one bitter and resentful); the final section told in third-person; uses stream-of-consciousness, interior monologue, and fragmented time.
- *As I Lay Dying* (1930): 59 brief, stream-of-consciousness monologues from 15 characters about a dying mother and her family's journey to bury her; formally experimental; darkly comic.
- *Absalom, Absalom!* (1936): the history of Thomas Sutpen and his family across generations, told through multiple narrators' interpretations; explores racism, pride, and the South's original sin.
- *Light in August* (1932): interconnected narratives involving a pregnant woman, a light-skinned Black man, and a reverend; themes of alienation, sexuality, and racial identity.

### Literary Method
- *Stream of consciousness*: Faulkner renders the unfiltered, digressive thoughts of characters; time loops and fragments; consciousness itself becomes the primary subject.
- *Multiple perspectives*: the same events seen through different characters' eyes; truth is plural and subjective; no omniscient narrator to settle meaning.
- *Southern Gothic*: decay, grotesquerie, violence, and psychological darkness; the South's present haunted by its past.
- *Formal innovation*: non-linear chronology; parenthetical asides; minimal punctuation; stream-of-consciousness monologues that blur interior and exterior reality.

## context

Faulkner brought modernist experimentation to American regional fiction, proving that the South and its traumas were fit subjects for the most demanding literary forms. His work forced American literature to confront slavery, racism, and the South's mythologized past through the lens of radical consciousness-exploration. He is less academic and allusive than Eliot, less linguistic and playful than Joyce or Stein, but equally committed to rendering consciousness itself as the true subject of fiction. His influence is felt throughout American fiction—in writers like Flannery O'Connor, Carson McCullers, and later novelists who inherit his use of stream-of-consciousness and Gothic Southern settings. In quiz bowl, Faulkner represents modernism's American wing: regional, psychologically intense, morally serious.
