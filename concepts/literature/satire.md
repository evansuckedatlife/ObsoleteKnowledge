# Satire

## summary

Satire is a literary mode that uses wit, irony, ridicule, and dark humor to critique and expose human vice, folly, corruption, or social injustice. Employing indirect methods of mockery and exaggeration rather than direct moral argument, satire ranges from gentle mockery to savage denunciation, and has been a powerful tool of social commentary from classical antiquity to the present day.

## you gotta know

- Originated in *classical Rome*: *Horace* (mild, conversational satire) and *Juvenal* (bitter, scathing indictment) established two poles of the form that persist in all later satire.
- *Central technique: irony*—saying the opposite of what is meant, or appearing to endorse vice to expose its absurdity; this indirection allows satire to evade censorship and engage readers intellectually.
- Uses *exaggeration* and *grotesque distortion* to render the absurdity of vices visible; through hyperbole, social conventions reveal themselves as arbitrary or repugnant.
- *Jonathan Swift*'s *A Modest Proposal* (1729)—proposing the eating of Irish children as a solution to poverty—became the paradigm of mordant satirical logic, using deadpan reason to condemn inhumanity.
- Targets vary: *personal satire* mocks individual folly or vice (Pope's *Dunciad*); *social satire* exposes class hypocrisy, pretense, and corruption (*Voltaire*'s *Candide*); *political satire* attacks rulers and systems (*Orwell*'s *Animal Farm*).
- Distinct from *comedy* (which aims chiefly to amuse) and *parody* (which imitates a specific work); satire aims at moral correction and social reform through ridicule.
- Often dangerous to satire's author: censorship, libel suits, and political persecution have accompanied satire throughout history, from *Juvenal* to *Rushdie*.

## connections

- [[jonathan-swift]] — master of ironic satire whose *A Modest Proposal* remains the gold standard of savage critique.
- [[alexander-pope]] — English Augustan poet whose *The Rape of the Lock* and *Dunciad* satirize literary and social pretense with formal brilliance.
- [[voltaire]] — French Enlightenment satirist whose *Candide* uses mock-optimism to demolish philosophical complacency.
- [[mark-twain]] — American satirist whose *Huckleberry Finn* and *Connecticut Yankee* critique racism and imperialism through vernacular humor.
- [[george-orwell]] — 20th-century political satirist who used animal fable (*Animal Farm*) and dystopia (*1984*) to expose totalitarianism.
- [[juvenal]] — classical Roman satirist of bitter, invective style; established the model of satirical denunciation.
- [[horace]] — classical Roman satirist of urbane, conversational tone; model for gentler, more personal satire.
