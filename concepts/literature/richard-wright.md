# Richard Wright

## summary

*Richard Wright* (1908–1960) was an American novelist whose unsparing social realist works depicted Black poverty, racism, and existential alienation in mid-20th-century America. His groundbreaking novel *Native Son* (1940) became a watershed text in American literature, establishing him as a major voice articulating the psychological toll of systemic racism.

## you gotta know

- *Native Son* (1940) tells the story of Bigger Thomas, a poor Black man in Chicago whose accidental killing of a white woman becomes the catalyst for exploring racism, desperation, and the making of a criminal.
- The novel was a bestseller and influential on American letters, though controversial for its unflinching depiction of Black criminality and racial violence.
- Published *Black Boy* (1945), a powerful autobiography detailing his childhood poverty in the Jim Crow South and his journey to literacy and artistic consciousness.
- Initially engaged with the Communist Party as offering solidarity against racism, though he later grew disenchanted and documented this in *The God That Failed* (1949).
- Emigrated to France in 1946, seeking escape from American racism, where he spent the final decade of his life writing and engaging with existentialist philosophy.
- Influenced by naturalism, existentialism, and Marxist analysis; his work synthesized these traditions with urgent attention to American racial reality.

## connections

- [[james-baldwin]] — younger contemporary novelist influenced by Wright but critical of his naturalism; both explored Black identity under American racism.
- [[gwendolyn-brooks]] — Chicago poet-contemporary; both documented Black Chicago with unflinching realism.
- [[ralph-ellison]] — contemporary novelist; Ellison both admired and diverged from Wright's literary approach.
- [[existentialism]] — Wright's later work engaged with existentialist philosophy, especially after moving to France.
