# Arthur Conan Doyle

## summary

**Sir Arthur Conan Doyle** (1859–1930) was a Scottish writer and physician best known as the creator of Sherlock Holmes, whose detective stories revolutionized the mystery genre and made Holmes the most famous fictional character of his era. Beyond Holmes, Doyle was a prolific author of historical novels, science fiction, and adventure tales, yet his legacy rests almost entirely on his brilliant invention of the consulting detective and his faithful chronicler Dr. Watson.

## you gotta know

- Doyle trained as a doctor and practiced medicine, an experience that informed his character's precision, his understanding of poison and anatomy, and his occasional frustration with public adulation overshadowing his other literary work.
- He first introduced Sherlock Holmes in the 1887 novel *A Study in Scarlet*; Holmes then appeared in serialized short stories beginning in 1891, becoming an immediate sensation.
- The 1892 collection *The Adventures of Sherlock Holmes* cemented Holmes's place in popular culture; the stories popularized detective fiction and established conventions—the brilliant deducer, the loyal companion-narrator, the seemingly impossible case solved through logic—that defined the genre.
- Despite Holmes's enormous success, Doyle attempted to kill off the character in *The Final Problem* (1893), having the detective apparently fall to his death at Reichenbach Falls while battling the criminal mastermind Professor Moriarty.
- Public outcry was so fierce that Doyle eventually resurrected Holmes, returning him in 1901 with *The Hound of the Baskervilles*, one of his most celebrated tales, blending Gothic atmosphere with Holmesian deduction.
- Beyond Holmes, Doyle wrote historical novels, tales of adventure (*The Lost World*, featuring the dinosaur-filled plateau), and science fiction, but none achieved Holmes's cultural penetration.
- Doyle was fascinated by spiritualism in later life, writing works defending the spiritualist movement and investigating alleged mediums; this preoccupation sometimes overshadowed his fiction writing.
