# The Crucible

## summary

Arthur Miller's 1953 play dramatizes the 1692 *Salem witch trials*, depicting a community consumed by fear, accusation, and moral compromise. The protagonist *John Proctor*, a farmer drawn into the hysteria, becomes a test case for integrity and the consequences of bearing witness to injustice.

## you gotta know

- Written as an allegory for McCarthyism and the anti-communist witch hunts of 1950s America; its critique of mass hysteria and coerced confession parallels contemporary political persecution.
- John Proctor, accused alongside many townspeople, initially considers confessing to save his life but ultimately refuses to sign a false confession, choosing execution over moral surrender.
- The play's title refers both to the literal crucible (melting pot) and to John Proctor's crucifixion—a spiritual reckoning where integrity requires sacrifice.
- Abigail Williams, a servant girl whose accusation begins the trials, is depicted as manipulative and driven by desire for John Proctor, suggesting personal vengeance beneath the religious fervor.
- The play's final act in the courthouse shows how the machinery of law and authority becomes a tool for injustice when driven by panic and self-interest.
- Miller's historical research grounded the play in actual trial records; it became one of the most frequently taught works in American high schools.
