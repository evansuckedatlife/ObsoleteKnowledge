# Now is the winter of our discontent

## summary

*Richard III's* opening soliloquy, in which the deformed, ambitious duke announces his intention to manipulate, seduce, and murder his way to the throne. He frames his ambition as a response to nature's cruelty in making him ugly—casting himself as both victim and villain.

## you gotta know

- Delivers the speech alone on stage at the play's outset, establishing his character and intentions with no intermediary.
- Opens with "Now is the winter of our discontent" metaphorically—*Richard* claims the Wars of the Roses are ending and peace dawning, but he will prevent that peace.
- *Richard's* physical deformity is his defining characteristic; he blames it for his ambition and cruelty, inverting victim and perpetrator.
- Announces his seduction plot against *Anne* (widow of the man he murdered) and his schemes against his brother *George* and the king.
- The speech establishes *Richard* as charming, witty, self-aware, and utterly amoral—he knows he is evil and relishes it.
- Famous for its energy and rhetorical command; audiences are drawn to *Richard* despite his villainy.
