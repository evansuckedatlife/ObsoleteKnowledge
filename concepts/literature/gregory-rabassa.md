# Gregory Rabassa

## summary

Gregory Rabassa (1922–2016) was an American translator of Latin American literature whose translations of García Márquez's *One Hundred Years of Solitude* and Cortázar's *Hopscotch* became the definitive English versions, introducing generations of English readers to the magical realism and experimental form of the Latin American boom. His career spanned six decades and over 40 books translated, making him indispensable to how English speakers encountered Spanish-language fiction.

## you gotta know

- Born in Yonkers, New York; studied Romance languages and developed fluency in Spanish through immersion and scholarship rather than childhood bilingualism.
- Translated García Márquez's *One Hundred Years of Solitude* (1970), the novel that defined magical realism for English readers; his rendering of the Buendía family's cyclical history became the standard text.
- His translation of Cortázar's *Hopscotch* (1966) captured the novel's ludic, philosophical tone and its experimental structure, allowing English readers to experience the book's formal innovation.
- Also translated Cortázar's short stories, essays by Vargas Llosa, and major works by Guimaraes Rosa and other Latin American authors, shaping the reception of the boom across the English-speaking world.
- Known for linguistic precision and cultural sensitivity; he insisted on understanding not just Spanish but the specific regional idioms and literary references each author embedded.
- Taught translation theory and practice for decades, mentoring younger translators and writing about the philosophy of translation—arguing that fidelity meant capturing spirit and intent, not literal word-for-word equivalence.
- Received the National Book Award and the PEN/Ralph Manheim Medal for Translation; his versions remained in print and widely assigned long after his death.
