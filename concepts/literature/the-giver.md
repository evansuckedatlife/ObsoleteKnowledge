# The Giver

## summary

*The Giver* is Lois Lowry's dystopian novel about a seemingly perfect, colorless society where all pain, choice, and emotion have been eliminated through conformity and controlled medication. Twelve-year-old Jonas is selected to become the Receiver of Memory, inheriting all the hidden history and emotional truth that the community has suppressed, and must decide whether to preserve this dangerous knowledge or escape.

## you gotta know

- Jonas lives in a uniform, orderly society where precision language eliminates ambiguity, color has been engineered out of perception, and citizens are assigned their life roles at a ceremonial age.
- He is chosen to be the Receiver of Memory and begins training with the Giver, an elderly man who transfers all of human history—joy, suffering, beauty, and pain—directly into Jonas's mind.
- Jonas discovers that the Giver carries the emotional and historical weight of the entire civilization; the Giver has seen color, felt love, experienced music—all of which are invisible to the rest of the community.
- The society maintains order through enforced sameness: color-blindness, sexuality suppression, regulated emotions, and euthanasia of those deemed "imperfect" or inconvenient.
- Won the Newbery Medal in 1994, becoming one of the most widely taught children's books and sparking debate about choice, freedom, and the price of safety.
- Jonas learns that his friend Fiona is scheduled for "release" (death) along with his father, a Nurturer, as part of the society's management of population and undesirables.
- The ending is deliberately ambiguous—Jonas escapes with a baby and finds either freedom or death, suggesting that choice and risk are inseparable from meaning and humanity.

## connections

- [[lois-lowry]] — author, winner of the Newbery twice, known for exploring difficult themes with clarity and moral depth.
- [[dystopian-fiction]] — exemplary work of dystopian literature that examines the cost of safety achieved through conformity.
- [[science-fiction]] — envisions a technologically controlled future as a parable for human choice and autonomy.
- [[newbery-medal]] — 1994 winner; landmark book that expanded the range of what children's literature could address.
