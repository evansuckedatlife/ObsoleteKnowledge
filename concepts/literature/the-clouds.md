# The Clouds

## summary

*The Clouds* is Aristophanes' vicious satire of intellectualism and the sophists, featuring *Socrates* as a ridiculous figure running a "thought-shop" where he teaches students to win arguments through rhetorical tricks rather than truth. Performed in 423 BCE, the comedy mocks the philosophical fashion of Athens, portraying Socrates levitating and measuring flea jumps, and ultimately shows a student using sophistic argumentation to justify beating his father. The play was performed again in revised form and became historically notorious as a precursor to Socrates' trial.

## you gotta know

- An elderly farmer, *Strepsiades*, seeks Socrates' teaching to learn how to cheat his creditors with clever arguments.
- Socrates is depicted as absurdly impractical—suspended in a basket, studying clouds and insects, obsessed with abstract questions.
- The Chorus of Clouds represents shifting, insubstantial ideas; they teach that *Right Reason* and *Wrong Reason* are equally valid debating techniques.
- Strepsiades' son *Pheidippides* learns sophistic rhetoric so well he beats his father and argues philosophically that patricide is justified.
- The play criticises the *sophists*—teachers who claimed to sell rhetorical skill and moral relativism—and associates Socrates with them.
- Aristophanes uses scatological humour, slapstick, and direct attack on a living philosopher in ways that blended comedy with serious critique.
- The play reflects anxiety about the decay of traditional values and the power of words divorced from moral truth.
