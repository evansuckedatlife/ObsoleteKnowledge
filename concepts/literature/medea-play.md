## summary

*Medea* is Euripides' shocking tragedy of a spurned woman who commits infanticide rather than bear the humiliation of her husband's abandonment. A sorceress princess who sacrificed her home and family to help Jason win the Golden Fleece, Medea kills her own children when Jason announces he will marry a younger princess for political advantage. The play subverts expectations of motherhood and feminine victimhood, presenting a protagonist both sympathetic and terrifying.

## you gotta know

- Jason, seeking to advance his position in Corinth, plans to marry the king's daughter and abandon Medea, mother of his two sons.
- Medea initially considers suicide but pivots to revenge, deciding to destroy everything Jason loves, including his future children.
- She poisons a wedding gift—a robe and diadem—that kill the princess and burn down the palace, also murdering the king.
- Medea then kills her own sons with a dagger, denying Jason heirs and legacy; she escapes in a dragon-drawn chariot.
- The nurse and chorus foreground Medea's isolation as a foreign woman in Greece, sympathising with her plight even as she becomes monstrous.
- Euripides portrays her with intelligence and eloquent justification—she is neither pure victim nor pure villain.
- The play shattered contemporary norms by making a woman the protagonist and refusing to punish her directly.

## connections

- [[euripides]] — author; one of his most performed and psychologically acute plays.
- [[jason]] — her husband, whose pragmatic marriage abandons her and drives her to revenge.
- [[the-argonauts]] — the quest Medea aided, establishing her as a figure of power undone by love.
- [[corinth]] — the setting where Greek norms and an outsider collide.
- [[oedipus-rex]] — contemporary tragedy; both explore the destructive power of knowledge and agency.
