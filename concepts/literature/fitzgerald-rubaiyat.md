# FitzGerald's Rubáiyát of Omar Khayyám

## summary

Edward FitzGerald's *Rubáiyát of Omar Khayyám* (1859) is an English verse translation and radical reinterpretation of a collection of Persian quatrains attributed to the medieval mathematician and poet Omar Khayyám. FitzGerald's free, poetic rendering—weaving together Persian verses into a unified narrative of wine, love, and mortality—became the dominant way English readers encountered Omar Khayyám, though it bears little resemblance to the original's fragmented nature.

## you gotta know

- Published anonymously in 1859; FitzGerald revised it five times over his lifetime, constantly tinkering and reordering the quatrains into an increasingly sophisticated whole.
- The original Persian *Rubáiyát* (quatrains) is a dispersed collection of independent four-line stanzas, often misattributed to Omar Khayyám; FitzGerald assembled them into a narrative arc about seizing pleasure before death.
- Rendered in interlocking rhyme scheme (AABA), FitzGerald's couplets are mellifluous and English; the original Farsi rubaí is much tighter and more aphoristic.
- Contains explicit hedonism—wine, love, sensory joy—and a cynicism about fate that scandalized some Victorian moralists, making the book daringly subversive for its time.
- FitzGerald's fame revived interest in Omar Khayyam across Europe; modern scholars now argue that many stanzas may not be his at all, and that FitzGerald invented more of the "character" than was present in the sources.
- The translation became iconic in late Victorian and Aesthetic movement culture, quoted and anthologized widely; its opening ("Awake! for Morning in the Bowl of Night") is its most famous passage.
- Later scholars and translators have offered more literal, scholarly versions, revealing the gap between FitzGerald's poetic liberty and philological accuracy.
