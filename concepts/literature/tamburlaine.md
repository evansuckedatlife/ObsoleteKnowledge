# Tamburlaine

## summary

Christopher Marlowe's *Tamburlaine the Great*, written in two parts (1587–1588), is an epic chronicle of a Scythian shepherd who rises through military genius and will-to-power to conquer much of the known world. The play launches Marlowe's characteristic "mighty line" of blank verse and celebrates an amoral conqueror who brooks no rival, embodying Renaissance admiration for power and the charismatic individual unconstrained by morality or divine sanction.

## you gotta know

- *Tamburlaine*, a nomadic shepherd, conquers armies across Asia, Persia, Egypt, and the Ottoman Empire through tactical brilliance, personal magnetism, and ruthless ambition.
- Marlowe's blank verse—the "mighty line"—elevates Tamburlaine's rhetoric to near-lyrical heights; his speeches assert the self-made man and the conquest of fate through will.
- The play celebrates power itself: Tamburlaine defeats not one rival but successive kingdoms; he speaks of *Zenocrate*, a Damascus princess, with sensual reverence, yet subordinates love to conquest.
- Tamburlaine defeats Bajazeth, the Ottoman emperor, and humiliates him into slavery, forcing the divine-right monarch to serve as his footstool—a theatrical emblem of might overturning hierarchy.
- Part 2 introduces the limits of will: Tamburlaine ages, grows ill, and confronts death; his son *Calyphas* proves a coward, triggering Tamburlaine's rage and the first signs of his own mortality.
- The play features spectacular staging—walls burning, kings on golden thrones pulled by conquered monarchs—rendering Tamburlaine's dominion in visual grandeur.
- Tamburlaine never repents; he dies in harness, still commanding armies, embodying the protagonist for whom conquest is both means and end.
