# Greek Tragedy

## summary

Greek tragedy was the dominant literary form of ancient Athens, primarily during the 5th century BCE, in which playwrights explored themes of fate, human suffering, divine justice, and moral consequence through dramatic narratives featuring noble or legendary figures. Performed at religious festivals like the *City Dionysia*, these plays established the fundamental structures and conventions—protagonist, chorus, tragic flaw, catharsis—that have defined drama for over two millennia.

## you gotta know

- Emerged in the 6th century BCE and reached its peak in the *Classical period* (mid-5th century), when *Aeschylus*, *Sophocles*, and *Euripides* dominated Athenian theater.
- Performances were part of *religious festivals*, most notably the *City Dionysia*, dedicated to the god Dionysus; playwrights competed for prizes and prestige.
- Typical structure included a *chorus* (group of actors commenting on action), *episodes* (scenes featuring principal characters), and *stasimon* (choral odes); plays were performed in day-long trilogies or groups.
- Central to the form was the concept of the *tragic flaw* (*hamartia*)—a character's fatal mistake or moral blindness leading to inevitable downfall and suffering, often culminating in recognition (*anagnorisis*) and reversal (*peripeteia*).
- *Aristotle* later codified tragedy's rules in the *Poetics*, defining *catharsis*—the purification of audience emotion through pity and fear—as the form's purpose.
- Women and slaves, though excluded from performing or attending, were frequently the subjects of drama; female characters often embodied transgression, passion, or divine retribution.
- The *three tragedians*—Aeschylus (*The Oresteia*, *Agamemnon*), Sophocles (*Oedipus Rex*, *Antigone*), and Euripides (*Medea*, *The Bacchae*)—left fewer than 50 surviving plays out of thousands originally written.

## connections

- [[aeschylus]] — the earliest of the three great tragedians, pioneer of dramatic structure with multiple speaking actors.
- [[sophocles]] — master of the form, known for psychological complexity and exploration of destiny and human dignity.
- [[euripides]] — innovator who questioned divine justice and gave tragic voice to women, slaves, and marginal figures.
- [[aristotle]] — philosopher who systematized tragedy's rules and purpose in *Poetics*, profoundly influencing later Western drama.
- [[the-oresteia]] — Aeschylus's trilogy of vengeance and justice, the grandest surviving Greek tragic cycle.
- [[oedipus-rex]] — Sophocles' masterpiece exploring fate, knowledge, and the corruption of royal power.
- [[medea]] — Euripides' portrait of a woman's rage and the cost of transgression.
- [[catharsis]] — the emotional and moral purification that tragedy aims to produce in its audience.
