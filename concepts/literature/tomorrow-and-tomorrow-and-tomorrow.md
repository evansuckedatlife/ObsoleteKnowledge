## summary

*Macbeth's* bleak soliloquy after learning of *Lady Macbeth's* death, in which he articulates the meaninglessness of life and the futility of human striving. Time stretches endlessly into insignificance; all human actions are merely sound and fury signifying nothing.

## you gotta know

- Spoken in Act V after *Macbeth* hears of *Lady Macbeth's* suicide, the moment his ambition crumbles.
- The repetition of "Tomorrow, and tomorrow, and tomorrow, creeps in this petty pace from day to day" conveys the tedious, mechanical passage of time.
- *Macbeth* describes life as a "tale told by an idiot, full of sound and fury, signifying nothing"—existence is mere noise and spectacle.
- Reflects *Macbeth's* psychological collapse: he has murdered to seize power, yet all of it feels hollow and purposeless.
- The speech marks the inversion of the play's opening obsession with ambition—*Macbeth* now sees all striving as vanity.
- "Out, out, brief candle" describes life as a fragile, short-lived light extinguished by death.

## connections

- [[macbeth]] — the speaker, reduced from ambitious tyrant to nihilist prophet of emptiness.
- [[lady-macbeth]] — whose death catalyzes his despair and the breakdown of his will to fight.
- [[ambition]] — which *Macbeth's* speech reveals to be self-defeating and corrosive to meaning.
- [[nihilism]] — the speech is a Shakespearean articulation of the meaninglessness of existence.
- [[fate-vs-free-will]] — *Macbeth* grasps that his own choices may have been predetermined illusions.
