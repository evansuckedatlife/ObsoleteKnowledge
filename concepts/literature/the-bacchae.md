# The Bacchae

## summary

*The Bacchae* is Euripides' final masterpiece, a tragedy of religious ecstasy and divine vengeance in which the god *Dionysus* punishes the Theban king *Pentheus* for denying his worship. As Dionysus drives the women of Thebes into ecstatic frenzy and Pentheus spies on them in disguise, the play culminates in his ritual dismemberment by his own mother, who mistakes him for a wild beast. It is a profound exploration of religious experience, masculinity, and the dangerous boundaries between civilization and nature.

## you gotta know

- Pentheus refuses to acknowledge Dionysus as a god, leading Dionysus to punish Thebes by driving women (including Pentheus's mother *Agave*) into bacchic ecstasy.
- Dionysus appears disguised as a young priest, seducing Pentheus into spying on the women's secret rites on Mount Cithaeron.
- The women, in bacchic frenzy, tear Pentheus limb from limb, mistaking him for a wild animal; Agave carries his head triumphantly as a trophy.
- Agave's recognition—realising she holds her own son's head—is the play's tragic anagnorisis, shattering her divine rapture.
- The play presents Dionysian worship as simultaneously ecstatic liberation and violent transgression of social order.
- Euripides blurs distinctions between male and female, civilization and wilderness, reason and ecstasy—destabilising Pentheus's attempt to control reality.
- The play's ambiguous ending questions whether Dionysus's revenge is justified or monstrous.
