# Gulliver's Travels

## summary

*Gulliver's Travels* is Jonathan Swift's 1726 satirical voyage narrative, in which Lemuel Gulliver undertakes four journeys to fantastical lands—Lilliput (inhabited by tiny people), Brobdingnag (inhabited by giants), Laputa (a land of obsessed scholars), and the country of the Houyhnhnms (governed by rational horses). A cutting political allegory and philosophical satire, it exposes human folly, hypocrisy, and moral degradation beneath the veneer of civilization.

## you gotta know

- Published anonymously in 1726; initially read as a straightforward adventure tale but recognized as Swift's most elaborate satire of contemporary politics, science, and human nature.
- Gulliver's first voyage to Lilliput contains thinly veiled references to 18th-century British politics; the diminutive Lilliputians parody courtly intrigue and petty faction.
- The second voyage to Brobdingnag inverts the scale, forcing Gulliver (and the reader) to see humanity from the perspective of the giants, who regard human customs and politics with disgust.
- Laputa satirizes the Royal Society and contemporary scientific obsessions; its inhabitants are so absorbed in abstract mathematics and music that they neglect practical life and governance.
- The final voyage to the land of the Houyhnhnms is Swift's harshest critique: rational horses govern their society while debased humans (Yahoos) represent humanity stripped of reason and virtue.
- Gulliver's transformation throughout the novel—from confident observer to horrified misanthrope—reflects Swift's growing contempt for human civilization itself.
- The prose, despite its fantastical content, is matter-of-fact and journalistic, lending the work an unsettling plausibility.
