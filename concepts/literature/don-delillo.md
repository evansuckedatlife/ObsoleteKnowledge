# Don DeLillo

## summary

*Don DeLillo* (b. 1936) is an American novelist celebrated for sprawling, densely plotted narratives that anatomize American paranoia, consumer culture, technology, and the specter of catastrophe. His novels—*White Noise*, *Libra*, *Underworld*—combine encyclopedic detail, tonal control, and a fascination with systems (media, surveillance, nuclear anxiety) that have made him a defining postmodern voice in American literature.

## you gotta know

- *White Noise* (1985) follows a blended family navigating consumerism, media saturation, and existential dread; the novel treats television, supermarkets, and fear as the textual fabric of American life.
- *Libra* (1988) reimagines the Kennedy assassination through multiple perspectives and historical detail, treating history itself as an unstable text subject to reinterpretation and conspiracy.
- *Underworld* (1997) spans Cold War America; opens with the 1951 Giants-Dodgers baseball game, then spirals backward and forward through decades of nuclear anxiety, waste, and urban decay.
- Master of *free indirect discourse* and long accumulative paragraphs; his prose is baroque, digressive, cataloging the clutter of American material and informational excess.
- Treats major historical events (Kennedy, 9/11 implicitly in *Point Omega*) as narrative events subject to textual instability and competing interpretations.
- His novels resist closure and moral clarity; they are formal experiments in representing contemporary consciousness under conditions of information overload and paranoia.
