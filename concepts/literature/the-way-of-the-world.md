# The Way of the World

## summary

William Congreve's *The Way of the World* (1700) is a brilliant Restoration comedy of wit, intrigue, and marriage negotiation set among the fashionable society of London, centring on the courtship of *Mirabell* and *Millamant* and their navigation of relatives, rivals, and social protocols to achieve matrimony on their own terms. The play epitomizes Restoration comedy's celebration of verbal cleverness and amoral sexual politics, culminating in a marriage contract as witty and contested as a treaty between equal powers.

## you gotta know

- *Mirabell*, a wealthy rake, loves *Millamant*, a rich widow with her own fortune and fierce independence; both insist on marriage contracted between equals rather than mastery.
- *Lady Wishfort*, Millamant's formidable aunt and guardian of her wealth, despises Mirabell and prefers the foppish *Sir Wilfull* Witwoud as a suitor for her nephew and her own purposes.
- Mirabell employs *Waitwell*, a servant, to woo Lady Wishfort under a false identity, extracting leverage; the subplot of servant-intrigue parallels the main romance.
- The play's famous proviso scene: Mirabell and Millamant negotiate the terms of their union, each stipulating conditions (she retains social freedom and independence; he accepts her terms) in a mock-legal exchange.
- *Fainall*, Mirabell's rival, is married to the deceitful *Mrs. Fainall* and schemes to control Millamant's fortune; his plot unravels when his own infidelities are exposed.
- The denouement exposes Fainall's intrigue; Mirabell and Millamant triumph through combined wit, honesty between themselves, and the exposure of their rivals' hypocrisy.
- The play's language is dazzlingly clever: puns, epigrams, and verbal sparring dominate; dialogue is a form of courtship and combat, and victory belongs to the quickest wit.

## connections

- [[william-congreve]] — the dramatist; his masterpiece and the apex of Restoration comedy.
- [[restoration-comedy]] — the genre; Congreve perfected its emphasis on wit, sexual intrigue, and verbal virtuosity.
- [[mirabell]] — the hero; intelligent, honest with himself and Millamant, and contemptuous of hypocrisy.
- [[millamant]] — the heroine; witty, wealthy, and insistent on her own terms; arguably the first truly autonomous female comic protagonist.
- [[lady-wishfort]] — a comic grotesque; her age, vanity, and sexual desperation are objects of satire yet also sympathetic.
- [[wit-and-marriage]] — the play's central negotiation; marriage as a pact between equals rather than a transfer of property.
