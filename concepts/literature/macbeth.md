# Macbeth

## summary

**Macbeth** is the ambitious Scottish thane whose murders transform him from a celebrated warrior into a paranoid tyrant. Spurred by his wife's ambition and a prophecy from three witches, he murders King Duncan to seize the throne, then commits further atrocities to secure his rule—killing Banquo, ordering the massacre of Macduff's family, and consulting the witches again for false reassurance. His descent from honour to tyranny charts how ambition corrodes conscience, and his eventual defeat by Macduff (born not of woman) demonstrates that the witches' promises were twisted truths, not guarantees.

## you gotta know

### The Prophecy and the Crime
- Three witches prophesy that Macbeth will become King of Scotland and that Banquo's descendants will be kings; Macbeth is currently the Thane of Glamis.
- Lady Macbeth, hearing of the prophecy, resolves to kill King Duncan and urges her husband to do the deed, questioning his manhood: "Are you a man?"
- Macbeth murders Duncan in his sleep, then is crowned king; the murder is blamed on Duncan's guards, whom Macbeth kills to prevent questions.

### Paranoia and Expansion
- Macbeth learns that Banquo was also present when the witches spoke, and grows fearful that the prophecy will raise Banquo's *children* to kingship, not his own; he orders Banquo's assassination.
- Banquo is killed, but his son Fleance escapes; Macbeth remains haunted by this failure and consults the witches again.
- They tell him three seemingly impossible things: "none of woman born / Shall harm Macbeth," "Macbeth shall never vanquished be until / Great Birnam Wood to high Dunsinane Hill / Shall come against him," and his crown is safe.

### The Reign of Terror
- Macbeth orders the massacre of Macduff's wife and children on suspicion of treachery—an act of cruelty driven entirely by paranoia.
- Lady Macbeth, tormented by guilt, descends into madness and commits suicide (offstage).
- Macbeth becomes a tyrant hated by the Scottish nobles, who ally with the English forces under Malcolm (Duncan's son).

### The Downfall
- Macduff, learning of his family's slaughter, joins Malcolm's army and marches on Dunsinane; soldiers cut branches from Birnam Wood as camouflage, fulfilling the prophecy literally.
- Macduff defeats Macbeth, and Macbeth learns that Macduff was "from his mother's womb / Untimely ripped"—born by Caesarean section, thus "not of woman born" in the witches' twisted meaning.
- Macbeth dies in battle, his body displayed as a traitor; Malcolm becomes the rightful king.

### Psychology of Ambition
- Macbeth's initial hesitation ("We will proceed no further in this business") reveals his moral sense, but Lady Macbeth's manipulation and his own ambition overwhelm it.
- Each murder is committed to secure the last, showing how evil compounds: Duncan's murder requires Banquo's, which requires Macduff's family, until Macbeth becomes a monster.

## context

*Macbeth* is Shakespeare's most psychologically brutal exploration of ambition's corruption. The witches raise the question of free will—are they prophets of fate, or did Macbeth's own desires twist their words? The tragedy lies not in the prophecies themselves but in Macbeth's interpretation of them: he chooses murder as the instrument to fulfil them. In quiz bowl, Macbeth is central to discussions of tragic flaw, the nature of ambition, witchcraft as dramatic device, and the political theology of legitimate kingship in early modern England.
