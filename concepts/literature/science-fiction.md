# Science Fiction

## summary

Science fiction is a literary mode that imagines worlds and futures shaped by scientific discovery, technological innovation, or speculative science. Rather than depicting the impossible as supernatural (as fantasy does), SF grounds its speculation in plausible scientific extrapolation: faster-than-light travel, artificial intelligence, genetic engineering, time manipulation, alternate histories, or contact with alien civilizations. Originating with *Mary Shelley*'s *Frankenstein* (1818), systematized by *Jules Verne* and *H. G. Wells* in the late 19th century, and matured in the 20th century through pulp magazines and literary innovators, SF became a dominant mode for exploring the social and existential consequences of technological change.

## you gotta know

### Foundational Figures & Works
- *Mary Shelley*'s *Frankenstein* (1818): proto-SF; scientific ambition (creating life through galvanism) leads to catastrophe; explores technology's ethical costs.
- *Jules Verne* (*Twenty Thousand Leagues Under the Sea*, 1870; *Journey to the Center of the Earth*, 1864): adventure SF; extraordinary voyages enabled by speculative technology; the submarine, the submarine captain *Captain Nemo*, and underwater/underground worlds.
- *H. G. Wells* (*The Time Machine*, 1895; *The War of the Worlds*, 1898; *The Island of Doctor Moreau*, 1896): SF as social allegory; time travel, alien invasion, and genetic experimentation as vehicles for exploring class, empire, and scientific ethics.

### Science vs. Technology SF
- *Hard SF*: prioritizes scientific accuracy and speculation grounded in physics, astronomy, biology; *Isaac Asimov* and *Arthur C. Clarke* are exemplars.
- *Soft SF*: emphasizes social, psychological, and cultural speculation; less concerned with technical plausibility; often explores linguistics, sociology, psychology.
- *Cyberpunk*: late 20th-century SF; fusion of computers, virtual reality, corporate power, and dystopian futures; *William Gibson*.
- *Post-human SF*: AI, genetic modification, digital consciousness; questions the boundaries of humanity itself.

### Major Themes
- *Technological consequences*: how technology reshapes society, psychology, and human nature; utopian and dystopian trajectories.
- *Alien encounter*: first contact with non-human intelligence; what does meeting the alien reveal about ourselves?
- *Time travel and causality*: temporal paradoxes; if you can change the past, is the future fixed or mutable?
- *Dystopia*: totalitarian futures, environmental collapse, technological surveillance; SF as warning.
- *Space exploration*: the frontier; escape from Earth; colonization of other worlds.

### Literary Heritage
- *Pulp magazines*: *Amazing Stories* (1926 onward); SF as popular, commercial form; distinct from high-literary modernism.
- *Golden Age SF* (1930s–1940s): adventure, wonder, and optimism about technological progress; *Isaac Asimov*, *Ray Bradbury*.
- *New Wave SF* (1960s): literary experimentation; formal innovation and psychological depth; *Harlan Ellison*, *Ursula K. Le Guin*, *Samuel R. Delany*.
- *Contemporary SF*: mainstream literary integration; *Margaret Atwood*, *N. K. Jemisin*, *Ted Chiang*.

### Key Concepts
- *Worldbuilding*: SF requires consistent, detailed imaginative worlds; the fictional world is a thought-experiment.
- *The novum*: the central speculative innovation that drives the narrative; what is imagined differently from our reality?
- *Estrangement*: SF makes the familiar strange; by imagining futures or alien worlds, it illuminates our own time.

## context

Science fiction occupies a paradoxical position: it is the most commercially successful literary genre globally, yet often dismissed as "low" literature by literary establishments. Yet SF has profoundly shaped how we imagine technology, the future, and human possibility. *Frankenstein* is now canonical high literature, but SF as a whole remains caught between pulp origins and literary legitimacy. SF's influence on philosophy, cognitive science, and futurism is immense; scenarios imagined in SF shape how we think about AI, climate, biotechnology, and space. The genre embodies Enlightenment rationality applied to the future; it asks "what if?" with scientific rigor. In quiz bowl, SF is a major cultural force: canonical works (*The Time Machine*, *1984*), iconic figures (*Isaac Asimov*, *Ray Bradbury*), and themes that recur across literature and culture.
