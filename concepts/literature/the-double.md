# The Double

## summary

*The Double* (1846), Dostoevsky's second published work, presents *Yakov Petrovich Golyadkin*, a meek copying clerk whose fragile sanity unravels when an exact double of himself appears. The doppelganger is everything Golyadkin desires and fears—confident, socially ascendant, ruthlessly selfish—making the novella a hallucinatory descent into paranoia and the fracture of identity.

## you gotta know

- *Golyadkin* is a petty bureaucrat tormented by social anxiety and wounded pride, desperate to advance and be accepted; his double embodies the ruthless will he cannot access.
- The double initially appears as his "brother" but gradually supplants him in society, at work, even claiming his identity; Golyadkin cannot convince anyone the double is not himself.
- The narrative voice becomes increasingly unreliable as Golyadkin's grasp on reality weakens; readers cannot be certain whether the double is external or a projection of disintegrating consciousness.
- The novella blends the grotesque realism of *Poor Folk* with psychological distortion; it anticipates modernist interior monologue and the exploration of mental breakdown.
- Dostoevsky was influenced by E.T.A. Hoffmann's romanticism and the literary tradition of doppelgangers; his version is uniquely social and political—the double as the triumph of social conformity and competitive will.
- Published in *Notes of the Fatherland*; early critics found it confusing; its reputation grew as psychological realism became a central literary concern.
