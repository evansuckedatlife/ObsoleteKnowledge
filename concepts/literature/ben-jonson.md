# Ben Jonson

## summary

**Ben Jonson** (1572–1637) was an English dramatist, poet, and literary critic who was Shakespeare's contemporary and rival, yet ultimately his greatest champion and friend. Jonson was known for his satirical comedies featuring complex schemes and morally corrupt characters driven by obsession and vice, his mastery of Latin and classical learning (which he wielded to reinvent English comedy), and his role as editor of the First Folio of Shakespeare's works, without which many of Shakespeare's plays would have been lost. Jonson's comedies of *humours*—plays in which characters embody obsessive traits or follies—established a tradition of satirical comedy that influenced English drama for centuries.

## you gotta know

- Jonson was born in 1572 and came to London as a young man; he worked as a bricklayer, actor, and eventually playwright, rising to prominence in the early 1600s.
- *Volpone* (1605) is his masterpiece, a comedy of schemes and seduction in which a wealthy Venetian merchant feigns terminal illness to trick his greedy acquaintances into lavishing gifts upon him; the play is a brilliant satire of greed, lust, and human folly.
- *The Alchemist* (1610) is equally acclaimed, set in plague-ridden London where a con artist and his cohorts pose as an alchemist and his assistant, duping gullible victims into paying for promises of gold and spiritual transformation.
- Jonson introduced the *comedy of humours*—a mode of satire in which characters embody excessive traits (avarice, vanity, lust, pedantry) treated as pathological obsessions; this approach influenced character-driven comedy for centuries.
- Unlike Shakespeare, Jonson was highly learned in classical literature and theory; he consciously imitated Roman comedy (Plautus, Terence) and built his comedies on classical models, elevating English comedy to classical dignity.
- Jonson was also a celebrated poet and wrote masques—elaborate theatrical entertainments combining music, dance, and spectacle for the royal court—works that showcased his learning and wit.
- He was fiercely competitive with Shakespeare during their lives but became Shakespeare's greatest posthumous advocate, editing the First Folio (1623) and writing a prefatory poem praising Shakespeare as "not of an age, but for all time."

## context

Jonson emerged during the height of English Renaissance drama, a period when theatrical competition was fierce. While Shakespeare was beloved for his emotional depth and linguistic beauty, Jonson was admired for his intellectual rigor, classical learning, and satirical bite. He represented a different ideal of dramatic art—one grounded in classical precedent and rational comic structure rather than romantic imagination. Yet the two men respected each other, and when Shakespeare died in 1616, Jonson helped ensure his legacy by editing the First Folio. Jonson's own reputation declined somewhat in later centuries (overshadowed by the Romantic admiration for Shakespeare), but his plays have experienced sustained rediscovery and revaluation, and his impact on English comedy—its satirical tradition, its use of character-based humour, its intellectual ambitions—is profound.
