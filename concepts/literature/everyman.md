# Everyman

## summary

*Everyman* is a late-medieval morality play (c. 1495) of unknown authorship in which the representative human figure *Everyman* is summoned to death and must account for his deeds before God. As he prepares for judgment, his friends and possessions abandon him, yet *Good Deeds* alone remains loyal, offering redemption through honest living and Christian penance—a stark allegory of human isolation at death and the only true wealth is virtue.

## you gotta know

- *Death*, personified as a messenger from God, arrives suddenly to call *Everyman* to judgment; the play's entire action unfolds in the hours before this reckoning.
- *Everyman* seeks companions for his journey to the grave: *Fellowship*, *Kinship*, *Cousin*, and *Goods* (material wealth) all refuse him, exposing the illusion of worldly bonds.
- *Strength*, *Beauty*, *Discretion*, and *Five-Wits* (the senses and knowledge) also abandon him at the brink of death; only *Good Deeds* remains, having been nourished by his virtuous acts.
- *Knowledge* guides Everyman to *Confession*, where he repents and performs penance; through sacramental grace, *Good Deeds* is restored to strength.
- The play ends with Everyman's death and ascent to heaven, accompanied only by *Good Deeds*; the Doctor (a priestly speaker) delivers a coda on the lesson: only virtue survives the grave.
- The language is plain and repetitive, suited to popular audiences; the allegory is transparent, each character a moral abstraction rather than psychological depth.
- The play's power lies in its existential clarity: the levelling of all ranks before death, the stripping away of illusions, the solitary journey each soul must take.
