# Realism

## summary

Literary realism was a dominant movement of the 19th century that rejected the idealism and emotional excess of Romanticism in favor of precise, detailed depictions of ordinary life and contemporary society. Realist writers aimed to represent the world as it actually existed, focusing on middle-class and working-class characters, psychological interiority, and the social forces shaping human behavior, creating a new novelistic form that prioritized observation and moral seriousness.

## you gotta know

- Emerged in mid-19th century France as a deliberate reaction against *Romanticism*'s idealization of nature, emotion, and the exotic; *Gustave Flaubert*'s *Madame Bovary* (1857) became the movement's defining work.
- Central tenet: *fidelity to observable fact*—the author should record life with scientific precision, avoiding sentimentality, melodrama, and authorial judgment; dialogue and setting were rendered with meticulous detail.
- Realist novels typically featured *protagonists of ordinary or modest station*—clerks, provincial merchants, governesses, peasants—rather than aristocrats or extraordinary figures; their inner lives and moral struggles were treated with full seriousness.
- *Balzac*, *Flaubert*, *George Eliot*, *Leo Tolstoy*, and *Dostoevsky* were among the major practitioners; each developed the novel's capacity to explore psychology, society, and ethical complexity.
- The movement expanded the novel's scope to encompass *social analysis*: novels became accounts of how class, economics, marriage law, education, and institutions shaped individual destiny.
- Related movement of *Naturalism* (late 19th century) pushed realism further, treating human behavior as determined by heredity and environment, employing quasi-scientific method in fiction.
- By century's end, realism had become the dominant mode of serious fiction in Europe; even writers who moved beyond it (symbolists, modernists) remained indebted to its formal innovations.

## connections

- [[gustave-flaubert]] — pioneer of realism whose perfectionism and objectivity became the model for the movement.
- [[balzac]] — precursor and contemporary whose vast social panorama *The Human Comedy* mapped 19th-century society.
- [[leo-tolstoy]] — Russian realist who used the novel form to explore vast historical and psychological terrain in *War and Peace* and *Anna Karenina*.
- [[george-eliot]] — English novelist who brought philosophical and psychological depth to realist fiction.
- [[fyodor-dostoevsky]] — Russian realist who synthesized social realism with profound exploration of consciousness and moral philosophy.
- [[naturalism]] — later movement that radicalized realism's determinism and scientific method.
- [[romanticism]] — the aesthetic movement realism explicitly rejected in favor of observation over imagination.
