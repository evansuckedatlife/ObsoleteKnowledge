# Christopher Marlowe

## summary

**Christopher Marlowe** (1564–1593) was an English dramatist and poet who, though he died at 29 under mysterious circumstances, profoundly influenced the development of English theatre and established the archetype of the tragic overreacher. His works—especially *Tamburlaine* and *Doctor Faustus*—featured protagonists driven by ambition, appetite, and a hunger for absolute power, introducing a psychological and linguistic complexity that paved the way for Shakespeare's tragic heroes and established conventions that dominated Elizabethan drama.

## you gotta know

- Marlowe was born in 1564, the same year as Shakespeare, and died in 1593 at age 29 under circumstances never fully clarified—possibly a tavern brawl, possibly political murder; the mystery has spawned centuries of speculation.
- He attended King's School in Canterbury and later Cambridge University, where he may have worked as a spy for the Privy Council—an intriguing but unproven claim.
- *Tamburlaine the Great* (c. 1587), his first major work, introduced the *mighty line*—blank verse of exceptional force and beauty—and the archetype of the *tragic overreacher*: an ambitious protagonist whose desire for power and glory leads him to transgress moral and natural boundaries.
- *Doctor Faustus* (c. 1588) is a masterpiece of ambition and damnation: the scholar Faustus sells his soul to the devil for knowledge and power, then spends the play desperately trying to escape his fate, only to be dragged to hell as his contract expires.
- *Malta of the Jew* (*The Jew of Malta*, c. 1590) features the villainous Barabas, a brilliant but amoral schemer who manipulates Christians and Turks alike; the play influenced Iago and other Shakespearean villains.
- Marlowe's innovation was linguistic: his blank verse was more flexible, forceful, and expressive than anything in English drama before him, establishing the idiom Shakespeare would perfect.
- His themes—ambition, appetite, the limits of knowledge, the nature of evil—were psychological and modern in ways that anticipated later drama; his characters are driven by internal conflict rather than external fate.

## context

Marlowe emerged in the early Elizabethan period as English drama was establishing itself as a serious art form. His immediate success—his plays were performed frequently and made theatrical companies wealthy—demonstrated that English theatre could rival classical drama in grandeur and psychological depth. Though Shakespeare would ultimately eclipse him in reputation and output, Shakespeare's tragedies and villain-driven dramas owe fundamental debts to Marlowe's invention of the ambitious, passionate protagonist. Marlowe died before Shakespeare's career fully launched, but his influence was immense: he showed that English dramatists could write powerful plays in English (not Latin), that blank verse could be a vehicle for profound emotion, and that theatrical characters could embody internal psychological conflict. In quiz bowl, Marlowe is essential as a precursor to Shakespeare, an innovator of English drama, and the creator of archetypal tragic figures.
