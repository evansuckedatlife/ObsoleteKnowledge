# The Purloined Letter

## summary

Edgar Allan Poe's 1844 short story in which C. Auguste Dupin once again solves an impossible case—the theft of a compromising letter from the Queen's chambers. Rather than relying on forensic evidence, Dupin deduces the criminal's identity through psychological insight and understands that the best hiding place is in plain sight.

## you gotta know

- A letter of political importance is stolen from a royal apartment, and the thief's identity remains unknown despite an intensive police search.
- The Prefect of Police brings the case to Dupin, puzzled and frustrated by his inability to recover the document.
- Dupin reasons that the thief must be a person of intellect—a match for the criminal mind, not a common burglar.
- The solution: the letter has been hidden in the most obvious place—on display in the criminal's residence in a letter rack, unremarkable to observers searching for something concealed.
- The tale illustrates Dupin's principle that even the most brilliant criminals are often undone by their assumptions about how others think.
- Explores themes of power, secrecy, and the psychology of the criminal mind rather than physical evidence.
