# Gwendolyn Brooks

## summary

*Gwendolyn Brooks* was an American poet known for her precise, accessible verse that examined the lives of ordinary Black Chicagoans with both intimacy and social conscience. She became the first Black American to win the Pulitzer Prize and later championed younger Black writers and the Black Arts Movement.

## you gotta know

- Won the Pulitzer Prize for Poetry in 1950 for *Annie Allen*, becoming the first African-American to receive this honor.
- Her collection *A Street in Bronzeville* (1945) presented vivid, character-driven portraits of residents in a Chicago neighborhood, blending lyric poetry with social realism.
- Wrote the narrative poem *Maud Martha* (1953), exploring a Black woman's interior life, small joys, and encounters with racism and self-doubt.
- Known for formal mastery — sonnets, quatrains, and traditional verse forms applied to contemporary Black experience, making her work both aesthetically rigorous and culturally resonant.
- Later became increasingly political, declaring herself a "Black poet" after the Watts riots (1965) and actively mentoring younger Black writers.
- Published dozens of collections and mentored generations through workshops and teaching, making her a living bridge between literary eras.
