# To be or not to be

## summary

The most celebrated soliloquy in English literature, spoken by *Hamlet* as he contemplates suicide while wrestling with inaction and madness. In this moment, he debates whether existence itself is worth the pain of living, weighing life's sorrows against the unknown terror of death and the afterlife.

## you gotta know

- Opens *Hamlet* Act III, as *Hamlet* stands alone, caught between his desire for revenge and his paralysis.
- The speaker confronts the fundamental human choice: whether to endure suffering or to end it, using death as "sleep" as a metaphor for oblivion.
- References "the slings and arrows of outrageous fortune," life's random cruelties and injustices.
- Fears of the afterlife — "the undiscovered country from whose bourn no traveller returns" — trap the speaker in indecision.
- The soliloquy exemplifies *Hamlet's* philosophical bent and inability to act decisively, defining the play's tragic inertia.
- Sets the stage for *Hamlet's* subsequent actions and his eventual downfall, shaped by delay and doubt.
