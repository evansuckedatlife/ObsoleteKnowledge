# The quality of mercy

## summary

*Portia's* eloquent appeal to *Shylock* to show mercy in *The Merchant of Venice*, delivered in the courtroom as *Antonio* faces the penalty of his failed debt. She argues that mercy is not weakness but a higher virtue than strict legal justice, and that it ennobles both giver and receiver.

## you gotta know

- Occurs in Act IV in the trial scene where *Shylock* demands his "pound of flesh" as payment for the defaulted loan.
- *Portia* (disguised as a male lawyer) argues that mercy "droppeth as the gentle rain from heaven upon the place beneath"—it is a gift, not compulsion.
- Contends that mercy is an attribute of God, and that a ruler who shows mercy is "greater than a king"—echoing divine authority.
- The speech frames mercy as paradoxical: "It blesseth him that gives and him that takes"—both parties benefit spiritually.
- *Shylock* refuses mercy, insisting on his legal right to exact the bond, hardening the play's central conflict between law and compassion.
- The speech is often read as Shakespeare's meditation on forgiveness and the limits of law, though *Shylock's* refusal complicates any simple moral.
