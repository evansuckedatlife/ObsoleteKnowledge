## summary

Agatha Christie's *The Murder of Roger Ackroyd* is a detective novel narrated by Dr. James Sheppard, who assists the famous detective Hercule Poirot in investigating the murder of a wealthy man named Roger Ackroyd. The novel follows the traditional mystery structure, with Poirot methodically uncovering clues and narrowing suspects. The shocking twist reveals that Dr. Sheppard himself—the trustworthy narrator and Poirot's companion throughout the investigation—is the murderer. The revelation recontextualises every detail the narrator has reported, making the reader complicit in his deception.

## you gotta know

- Roger Ackroyd, a wealthy industrialist, is discovered murdered in his locked study at Fernly Park.
- Dr. Sheppard, a local physician, becomes the primary narrator, recounting his investigation with Hercule Poirot, who is staying in the village.
- Through Poirot's questioning and investigation, suspicion falls upon various suspects—Ackroyd's relatives, servants, and former associates.
- Sheppard narrates Poirot's deductions as the detective inches closer to identifying the killer, maintaining the reader's trust in his honesty.
- The final revelation: Dr. Sheppard murdered Ackroyd. His narration has been selectively truthful—he reports what he witnessed but omits his own guilt.
- The twist is revolutionary for detective fiction: the narrator is unreliable not through obvious deception but through omission and misdirection.
- Published in 1926; considered one of Christie's greatest works and a cornerstone of detective fiction.
