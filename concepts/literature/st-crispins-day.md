# Saint Crispin's Day speech

## summary

*King Henry V's* rousing address to his outnumbered troops before the *Battle of Agincourt*, transforming despair into glory by promising that survival in this battle will grant eternal fame. The speech is a masterclass in leadership rhetoric and patriotic fervor, defining the English warrior ideal.

## you gotta know

- Occurs in Act IV as *Henry's* army, vastly outnumbered by the French, prepares for battle.
- Opens with the famous line "We few, we happy few, we band of brothers," elevating the ragtag army to a fellowship of heroes.
- *Henry* promises that those who survive will be revered forever; even the wounded will wear their scars "like badges" of honour.
- References *Saint Crispin's Day* (the day of the battle, October 25), when men will remember the fight and toast their comrades' names.
- Inverts the soldiers' numerical disadvantage into a virtue: fewer soldiers means greater share of the glory and honor.
- The speech unites social classes—nobles and commoners fight as equals—making it an anthem for martial brotherhood.
