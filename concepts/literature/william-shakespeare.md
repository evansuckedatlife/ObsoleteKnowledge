# William Shakespeare

## summary

*William Shakespeare* (1564–1616) was an English playwright and poet whose 39 plays and 154 sonnets are widely considered the greatest achievement in English literature and perhaps in all literature. Born in the market town of Stratford-upon-Avon, he moved to London in his late twenties and became the preeminent dramatist of the Elizabethan and Jacobean eras, shaping the template for tragedy, comedy, and history plays that has never been surpassed. His fusion of linguistic exuberance, psychological penetration, and moral ambiguity created characters and scenes that have resonated for four centuries.

## you gotta know

### Life & Career
- Born in Stratford-upon-Avon to John Shakespeare, a glovemaker, and Mary Arden; educated in Latin and rhetoric at the local grammar school, but did not attend university.
- Married Anne Hathaway at 18 (she was already pregnant with their daughter); left Stratford for London in the late 1580s, leaving family behind.
- Became an actor and playwright in the newly robust London theatre scene; by 1592 was established enough that rival playwrights were envious of his success.
- Joined the Lord Chamberlain's Men (later the King's Men after James I's accession), a shareholder in the *Globe Theatre* (built 1599), where most of his plays premiered.
- Wrote continuously for three decades: early comedies and history plays in the 1590s, great tragedies 1600–1608, final romances 1609–1613. Returned to Stratford around 1613, died at 52.

### Canonical Plays (Major Tragedies)
- *Hamlet* (c. 1600): the Danish prince feigns madness while avenging his father's murder; indecision and introspection are the action. Contains more famous lines and speeches than any other play.
- *Macbeth* (c. 1606): a Scottish general murders the king after witches' prophecies; guilt-driven descent into paranoia and tyranny, the shortest tragedy, often called the most intense.
- *Othello* (c. 1603–1604): a Moorish general is driven to murder his innocent wife by the manipulations of *Iago*; racism, jealousy, and manipulation as psychological engines.
- *King Lear* (c. 1605–1606): an aging king divides his kingdom among his daughters based on flattery, loses power, and descends into madness on the heath; often cited as the greatest tragedy.
- *Romeo and Juliet* (c. 1595): young lovers from feuding families fall in love and die; the definitive love tragedy, though written relatively early in his career.

### Canonical Plays (Comedies & Romances)
- *A Midsummer Night's Dream* (c. 1595–1596): lovers and fairies in an enchanted forest; romance, magic, and comic confusion resolved by dawn.
- *The Merchant of Venice* (c. 1596–1597): *Portia*, an heiress, is bound by her father's will to marry the suitor who chooses the correct casket; *Shylock*, a Jewish moneylender, demands a "pound of flesh" from the merchant *Antonio*.
- *The Tempest* (c. 1610–1611): the sorcerer *Prospero*, rightful Duke of Milan, uses magic to shipwreck his brother on an island to restore his daughter *Miranda* to her rightful place; often read as Shakespeare's farewell to theatre.

### Shakespearean Innovation (Form & Language)
- *Iambic pentameter*: unrhymed lines of ten syllables (five iambs) as the backbone of dramatic verse. Gives speech a natural rhythm while allowing flexibility for rhetorical emphasis and emotional variation.
- *Soliloquy as introspection*: characters address the audience directly, revealing inner conflict and psychological nuance. Hamlet's "To be or not to be," Macbeth's descent into nihilism, Iago's motiveless malignity—all dramatized through soliloquy.
- *Linguistic abundance*: puns, wordplay, neologisms, and metaphorical denseness. Shakespeare expanded English vocabulary dramatically, coining or first recording hundreds of words.
- *Psychological realism*: characters act from mixed motives, evolve through the play, contradict themselves. Not stock figures but complex, self-aware beings with desires, doubts, and rationalizations.
- *Structural flexibility*: blend of verse and prose, serious and comic scenes, main plot and subplots; a fluidity that gives the impression of life rather than mechanical design.

## context

Shakespeare is the supreme literary genius of English culture and arguably of world civilization. His plays became the text of English education and cultural transmission; every educated English speaker knows passages, allusions, and plot summaries. His influence is total: he defined what theatre could be (psychological depth + philosophical reach + linguistic exuberance), and his template of tragedy and comedy has been the measure ever since. The Romantic poets elevated him to quasi-religious status (Coleridge, Hazlitt treated him as superhuman); the Victorians made him the apex of literature itself. The 20th century deconstructed him but never escaped him: modernists grappled with his authority, post-colonialists reckoned with *The Tempest* and *The Merchant of Venice*, feminist critics reclaimed female characters, and the plays continued to be performed and reinterpreted in every era. Quiz bowl's reverence for Shakespeare is nearly absolute—his plays and characters are assumed knowledge, his imagery and language recurrent in high-level questions. He stands with Homer and Dante as a writer whose work has shaped the possibilities of literature itself.
