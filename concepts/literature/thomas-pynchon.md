# Thomas Pynchon

## summary

*Thomas Pynchon* (b. 1937) is an American novelist of legendary reclusion whose encyclopedic, labyrinthine novels treat entropy, paranoia, and systems breakdown as the condition of postmodern consciousness. His masterwork *Gravity's Rainbow* (1973) is a canonical difficult postmodern text; his oeuvre probes the intersections of history, technology, physics, and narrative, all rendered in darkly comic, digressive prose bristling with footnotes and temporal jumps.

## you gotta know

- *Gravity's Rainbow* (1973) follows *Tyrone Slothrop* in World War II, ostensibly tracing a rocket's trajectory but actually mapping the paranoid consciousness of wartime and the dawning nuclear age; the novel resists linear reading and conventional plot.
- A notoriously private author; has never been photographed; grants no interviews; his invisibility has become part of his literary mythology.
- Employs *metafiction*, *anachrony*, and *paranoid hermeneutics*; his novels position readers as detective-interpreters searching for patterns that may or may not exist.
- *Entropy* (1960), an early story, uses the physical law of entropy (disorder increase) as metaphor for meaning-collapse and information decay in human systems.
- Other major novels include *The Crying of Lot 49* (1966), a short postmodern mystery, and *Against the Day* (2006), another encyclopedic historical epic.
- His work is notoriously difficult, allusive, and technical; reading Pynchon is treated as an intellectual challenge; his novels resist paraphrase and demand rereading.
