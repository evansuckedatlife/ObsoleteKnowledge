# Don John

## summary

**Don John**, the illegitimate brother of the Prince of Aragon, is a malcontent plotter whose sole ambition is to disrupt the happiness of others, specifically to prevent the marriage of Hero and Claudio by spreading a false rumour of Hero's infidelity. Unlike Iago (who acts from wounded pride about rank) or Macbeth (who desires the throne), Don John acts from a kind of existential resentment—he simply dislikes being happy and decides to destroy the happiness of those around him. His plot succeeds temporarily, causing Claudio to publicly shame Hero on their wedding day, but the deception is quickly uncovered, Don John flees, and the couples marry happily—making *Much Ado* the one Shakespearean tragedy that is not a tragedy at all.

## you gotta know

### The Malcontent
- Don John is introduced as a bastard, a status that excuses his sourness (as he sees it): "I cannot hide what I am. I must be sad."
- Yet the play suggests his sadness is chosen, not forced—he is a malcontent who takes pleasure in being at odds with the world.
- He has recently been defeated in war and captured by the Prince; the Prince has pardoned him and brought him to Sicily to attempt reconciliation, but Don John is unredeemable.

### The Plot
- Don John learns that Claudio is to marry Hero (the daughter of a gentleman of Sicily); he resolves to prevent the marriage by convincing Claudio that Hero has been unfaithful.
- He enlists Borachio (a gentleman of dubious morals) to stage a scene in which Borachio, disguised as Claudio, woos Hero at her window at night, making it appear that Hero is meeting a lover.
- Don John shows this scene to Claudio and the Prince, who are both convinced that Hero is disloyal.

### The Disruption
- At the wedding ceremony, Claudio publicly rejects Hero, calling her a "rotten orange" and accusing her of infidelity before the assembled church.
- Hero faints and is believed to be dead; her father is so shamed that he wishes her dead rather than dishonoured.
- The plot has succeeded: the marriage is prevented, Claudio is wracked with guilt (he is told later that she *is* dead), and Hero is defamed.

### The Unravelling
- The town watch arrests Borachio (who is drunk and boasting about the plot); he confesses, and the deception is revealed.
- Claudio and the Prince realize their error and are overcome with remorse; they publicly proclaim Hero's innocence.
- Hero is alive (she was not killed in fainting) and, extraordinarily, forgives Claudio and agrees to marry him anyway.
- Don John attempts to flee but is captured; he is threatened with execution (though the play ends before his sentence is carried out).

### The Lightness of Evil
- Don John's evil is not motivated by grand passion or philosophical principle—it is motivated by spite, a desire to mar beauty and happiness because they offend him.
- His dialogue is thin compared to other villains; he lacks Iago's eloquence or Macbeth's philosophy.
- Yet his ordinariness—he is simply a bitter man who cannot bear happiness—makes him, in a different way, as threatening as the more elaborate villains.

## context

*Much Ado About Nothing* is Shakespeare's lightest comedy, and Don John is its only villain—a malcontent whose evil is almost a parody of villainy. The play asks whether society has a place for those like Don John: he has been pardoned, brought into the court, given a chance at reconciliation, but he refuses it. His fate (execution is implied) is harsh, suggesting that some forms of unredeemable bitterness cannot be tolerated. In quiz bowl, Don John represents a different species of Shakespearean villainy: not the ambitious (Macbeth), not the racist manipulator (Iago), not the sadist (Aaron), but the simple malcontent who hates happiness.
