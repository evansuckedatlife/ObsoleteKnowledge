# Saul Bellow

## summary

*Saul Bellow* (1915–2005) was an American novelist whose work crystallises the anxieties of urban intellectual life and the search for meaning in a morally ambiguous world. His protagonists—often Jewish men navigating between tradition and modernity—grapple with alienation, desire, and the limits of reason. He is among the most influential American writers of the postwar era.

## you gotta know

- *The Adventures of Augie March* (1953) — picaresque novel of a Chicago hustler's exuberant wanderings, established him as a major voice and won the National Book Award.
- *Humboldt's Gift* (1975) — meditation on artistic ambition, failed mentorship, and the costs of genius, won the Pulitzer Prize.
- *The Victim* (1947) — early novella exploring paranoia and the uncanny intimacy between antagonists.
- *Seize the Day* (1956) — concentrated tragedy of a man's single day of reckoning, often taught as modernist masterpiece.
- Won the Nobel Prize in Literature (1976), rare honour for an American of his generation.
- His prose blends high modernist technique with colloquial American speech, creating a distinctive intellectual voice.
- Taught at the University of Chicago for decades; influenced generations of writers on aesthetics and moral philosophy.
