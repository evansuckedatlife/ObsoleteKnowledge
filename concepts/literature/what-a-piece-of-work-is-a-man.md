# What a piece of work is a man

## summary

*Hamlet's* eloquent meditation on human dignity and potential in *Hamlet*, in which he praises mankind as "noble in reason," "infinite in faculties," and "expres'd in action" like an angel and an ape. The speech radiates Renaissance optimism about human capacity—until *Hamlet's* melancholic reversal reveals his despair at humanity's depravity.

## you gotta know

- Delivered in Act II as *Hamlet* speaks to the visiting players *Rosencrantz* and *Guildenstern*.
- Opens with wonder at the human form and mind: "What a piece of work is a man! how noble in reason! how infinite in faculty!"
- Compares humans to angels ("in apprehension") and catalogs human potential: intellect, perception, beauty, grace.
- *Hamlet* then abruptly inverts his praise: "and yet, to me, what is this quintessence of dust? man delights not me."
- The reversal reflects *Hamlet's* depression and alienation; the same creature that embodies potential appears to him as worthless.
- The speech encapsulates the play's central tension between idealism and despair, potential and paralysis.

## connections

- [[hamlet]] — the speaker, whose psychological complexity drives his shifts between admiration and contempt.
- [[humanism]] — the Renaissance celebration of human potential *Hamlet* initially articulates, only to reject.
- [[depression]] — *Hamlet's* inability to sustain hope or meaning, expressed as contempt for humanity itself.
- [[rosencrantz-and-guildenstern]] — the old friends whose presence prompts *Hamlet's* meditation on change and decay.
- [[melancholia]] — the temperament that defines *Hamlet's* character and his rejection of the world's apparent goodness.
