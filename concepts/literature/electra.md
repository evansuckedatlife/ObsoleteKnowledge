# Electra

## summary

*Electra* exists as two separate tragic treatments—one by *Sophocles* and one by *Euripides*—both centered on the loyal daughter of *Agamemnon*, who aids her brother *Orestes* in avenging their father's murder by *Clytemnestra* and her lover *Aegisthus*. While both plays share the basic plot, Sophocles' version emphasises redemptive justice and emotional catharsis, whereas Euripides' portrays the revenge as spiritually hollow and psychologically damaging. Both make Electra herself the play's moral center and suffering conscience.

## you gotta know

- *Sophocles' Electra*: Electra waits patiently for Orestes' return; when he arrives disguised, she initially mistakes a false report of his death, then rejoices in his revelation and helps him kill their mother and Aegisthus. The revenge succeeds cleanly, and Electra's vindication is the play's climax.
- *Euripides' Electra*: Electra lives in misery, married off to a peasant by Aegisthus; Orestes' return leads to calculated murder disguised as ritual hospitality. After killing Clytemnestra, both siblings are struck with horror and remorse, cursed rather than vindicated.
- Both plays feature Electra's isolation and unrelenting grief as the primary emotional register; her loyalty to her dead father becomes the play's moral anchor.
- The plays differ in their conclusion: Sophocles ends with justice restored; Euripides leaves the siblings traumatised and pursued by the Furies.
- Electra's famous monologue of lamentation establishes her as a character of passionate conviction, not merely a supporting figure.
- The plays pit loyalty to family against obedience to political authority and divine will.
