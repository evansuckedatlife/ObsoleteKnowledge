# Aaron the Moor

## summary

**Aaron** is the moor in Shakespeare's *Titus Andronicus*, a sadistic servant and lover of the queen who orchestrates a cascade of atrocities—rape, murder, dismemberment, infanticide, and cannibalism—in an almost mechanical display of malice. Unlike villains motivated by ambition or wounded pride, Aaron acts from what appears to be pure, almost giddy evil; he gloats over his crimes and laughs at the suffering of others. He is also the one sympathetic figure in the play (contradictorily): when his mixed-race child is born, he defends it fiercely against a society that would destroy it, revealing that beneath the sociopath beats a heart capable of love.

## you gotta know

### The Lover and the Conspirator
- Aaron is explicitly a moor—a man of African descent—and is the lover of Tamora, the Goth queen captured by Titus Andronicus after a military campaign.
- When Tamora becomes empress (through marriage to the new Roman emperor), Aaron gains influence and begins to orchestrate a revenge against Titus, who has slaughtered her firstborn son in ritual.
- Unlike Iago or Edmund, Aaron has no personal grievance against Titus; he acts from a kind of existential malice, a desire to cause suffering for its own sake.

### The Machinery of Atrocity
- Aaron engineers the rape of Lavinia (Titus's daughter) by Tamora's sons, Demetrius and Chiron; they mutilate her afterward, cutting off her hands and tongue so she cannot identify them.
- He then frames Titus's sons for the rape, causing them to be executed; he brings Titus their severed heads as a "gift."
- When Titus's last son Lucius kills Demetrius and Chiron and grinds their bones into a bloody paste, Aaron bakes them into a pie and feeds it to Tamora—a revenge so baroque it surpasses even the original atrocities in absurdity.

### The Black Child
- Tamora gives birth to a child fathered by Aaron; the child is black, revealing the affair.
- Aaron's response is extraordinary: he defends the child ferociously, threatening to kill anyone who harms it, stealing it away and raising it himself.
- This moment—almost the only human gesture in a play of monsters—suggests that Aaron's evil is performative, a mask for an intelligence and sensitivity that emerges only in this one act of protection.

### The Confession
- At the end of the play, Aaron is captured and sentenced to death.
- He confesses to all his crimes, listing them with evident satisfaction; when asked if he repents, he declares: "No, not a jot" and wishes he had committed a thousand times more evil.
- He is either buried alive or hanged, depending on the production; his last act is to spit in the face of his executioners.

### Racism and the Villain
- Aaron's blackness is explicitly invoked as part of his villainy in the dialogue (Tamora calls him her "coal-black lover"), reflecting early modern English prejudices about blackness and evil.
- Yet Shakespeare complicates this: Aaron is intelligent, articulate, and—in his defense of his child—capable of genuine human feeling in a way that the "civilized" Romans (who practice human sacrifice and cannibalism) are not.
- The play's racism and its complexity about Aaron exist simultaneously, uneasily.

## context

Aaron is the most cartoonishly evil character in Shakespeare, yet also (paradoxically) the most humanized at the moment he acts from love. *Titus Andronicus* is Shakespeare's most extreme play—so violent and baroque that it has often been questioned as authentically his. Aaron represents a kind of theatrical villainy that anticipates the Gothic and horror fiction. In quiz bowl, Aaron is relevant to discussions of Shakespearean villainy, racism in early modern literature, and the question of whether evil can be redeemed by a single act of human feeling.
