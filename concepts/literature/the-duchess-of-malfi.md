# The Duchess of Malfi

## summary

John Webster's *The Duchess of Malfi* is a Jacobean revenge tragedy of obsession, corruption, and misogyny, centring on a duchess who secretly marries against her brothers' will and suffers their savage retribution. Its nightmarish imagery, tortured psychology, and baroque intensity make it a landmark of early modern English drama, depicting a world of deceit where virtue itself becomes fatal.

## you gotta know

- The Duchess marries her steward *Antonio* in defiance of her brothers, who seek control of her wealth and sexuality; her secret marriage becomes her doom.
- Her older brother *Ferdinand*, seemingly motivated by lust-tinged possessiveness, obsesses over her sexuality and orchestrates her destruction with sadistic fervor.
- The Cardinal, her other brother, aids the plot with cold calculation; both brothers hire the assassin *Bosola* to spy and eventually murder the Duchess and her children.
- Bosola delivers madness to the Duchess before executing her — he shows her a severed hand (supposedly Antonio's) and stages her murder of her children to break her mind.
- The Duchess's final composure in the face of death — her refusal to despair — stands as a human affirmation against the male violence arrayed against her.
- Bosola's eventual remorse and violence against his employers give the tragedy a reckoning, yet no true justice; the plot ends in mutual destruction.
- The play features grotesque imagery: the waxwork corpses, the madmen's parade, the nightmarish hospital scenes — all rendering psychological torment in visceral theatre.
