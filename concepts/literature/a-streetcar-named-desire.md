# A Streetcar Named Desire

## summary

Tennessee Williams' 1947 play depicts the collision between *Blanche DuBois*, a fading Southern belle seeking refuge with her sister in New Orleans, and her brother-in-law *Stanley Kowalski*, a crude, brutish working man. The clash of refinement and raw physicality, combined with Blanche's precarious mental state, builds to a catastrophic ending.

## you gotta know

- Won the Pulitzer Prize for Drama in 1948 and became a cornerstone of American realism; the 1951 film adaptation was equally acclaimed.
- Blanche arrives at her sister's cramped apartment with secrets: she lost her plantation home and has been drifting as a schoolteacher and sex worker.
- Stanley's discovery of Blanche's past drives her further into delusion; he ultimately rapes her, an act the play handles with shocking directness.
- The play's famous line is Blanche's desperate cry, "I have always depended on the kindness of strangers"—uttered as she's taken away to a mental institution.
- The streetcar named "Desire" is a literal New Orleans transit line but functions as a symbol of inescapable fate and base human appetite.
- Williams uses Southern Gothic imagery—decay, sensuality, madness—to dissect the gap between illusion and reality.
