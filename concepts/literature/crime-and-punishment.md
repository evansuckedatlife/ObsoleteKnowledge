# Crime and Punishment

## summary

*Crime and Punishment* is Dostoevsky's monumental psychological novel (1866) following *Raskolnikov*, an impoverished St. Petersburg student who commits murder to test his theory that extraordinary individuals transcend conventional morality. His descent into guilt, paranoia, and spiritual crisis becomes a meditation on conscience, redemption, and the limits of rationalist justification for transgression.

## you gotta know

- *Raskolnikov* murders an elderly pawnbroker and her sister to fund his studies and prove his theory of moral exceptionalism; the act becomes psychologically unbearable rather than liberating.
- *Sonya Marmeladova*, a virtuous young woman forced into prostitution to support her family, becomes Raskolnikov's confessor and embodies Christian redemption through suffering.
- The novel is structured as a psychological cat-and-mouse game between Raskolnikov and *Porfiry Petrovich*, a clever police investigator who gradually extracts a confession.
- Raskolnikov's attempted justification—that superior individuals may act beyond morality—echoes Nietzschean ethics and reflects 19th-century Russian nihilist movements.
- The epilogue's redemptive turn toward Christian faith was controversial; readers divided over whether it represents Dostoevsky's genuine message or a concession to censors.
- Original publication in *The Russian Messenger* serialized; the novel grew in revision and remains a cornerstone of psychological realism.
