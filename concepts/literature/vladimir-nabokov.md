# Vladimir Nabokov

## summary

*Vladimir Nabokov* (1899–1977) was a Russian-American novelist and writer celebrated for his elaborate wordplay, psychological complexity, and experiments with form. His most famous novel, *Lolita*, scandalized readers with its unreliable narrator and baroque prose style, establishing him as a master of metafiction and linguistic innovation who influenced generations of postmodern writers.

## you gotta know

- Born in czarist Russia, emigrated after the revolution, lived in Berlin and Paris before settling in America; his exile shaped his outsider perspective on language and belonging.
- Wrote *Lolita* (1955), narrated by the predatory *Humbert Humbert*; the novel's formal brilliance and moral ambiguity made it a landmark of modern fiction despite initial scandal.
- Pioneered *metafiction* — novels about the act of writing itself, characters aware of their fictionality, footnotes that undermine the text (especially *Pale Fire*, 1962).
- Created intricate wordplay and multilingual puns; his prose is densely composed, demanding rereading; he considered himself a "novelist of ideas, not ideas of a novelist."
- A lepidopterist (butterfly collector) who wove scientific precision and taxonomic obsession into his literary aesthetics.
- Spent decades perfecting *Pale Fire*, a novel disguised as a scholarly edition with a foreword, poem, and cranky commentary by an unreliable editor.
