# Arthur Waley

## summary

Arthur Waley (1889–1966) was a British translator and orientalist whose renderings of Chinese and Japanese classics—including *The Tale of Genji*, *The Dream of the Red Chamber*, and numerous Chinese poems—introduced English readers to East Asian literary traditions. Over 50 years, Waley produced some 30 books of translation, establishing a standard for how Chinese and Japanese verse and prose could be rendered into elegant, readable English.

## you gotta know

- Self-taught in Chinese and Japanese; began at the British Museum cataloguing Oriental manuscripts and developed his linguistic abilities through immersion in texts rather than formal training.
- His translation of Murasaki Shikibu's *The Tale of Genji* (1921–1928) was the first complete English version and remains canonical; he rendered the 11th-century court narrative into flowing, accessible English.
- Translated *The Dream of the Red Chamber* (Cao Xueqin's 18th-century novel), capturing its episodic structure and domestic intricacy; the translation made this Chinese masterpiece available to English readers for the first time.
- Translated hundreds of Chinese poems, particularly from the Tang and Song dynasties, into English free verse that captured the suggestiveness and imagistic precision of classical Chinese poetry without forcing rhyme.
- Known for readability and elegance rather than literal fidelity; modern scholars note he sometimes romanticized or simplified Chinese and Japanese syntax to suit English taste.
- Declined academic positions to remain independent; worked as a translator and cultural intermediary, writing essays on East Asian aesthetics and philosophy alongside his translations.
- His versions remained the standard for decades; though later scholars produced more literal and academically rigorous translations, Waley's remain the most widely read and cited in general audiences.
