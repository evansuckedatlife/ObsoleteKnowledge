# Regan

## summary

**Regan**, the younger of King Lear's two cruel daughters, matches her sister Goneril in callousness but surpasses her in pure violence. Where Goneril uses strategy and seduction, Regan is more brutally direct—she orchestrates the blinding of the Earl of Gloucester (tearing out his eyes with her own hands or urging her husband Cornwall to do so), conspires with her husband to poison her sister over Edmund, and shows little of the psychological complexity that makes Goneril fascinating. Yet her swift death (poisoned by Goneril) suggests that her villainy is unsustainable; in a play obsessed with the naturalness of bonds, her violation of every natural duty is swiftly punished.

## you gotta know

### The False Love and the Betrayal
- Like Goneril, Regan falsely professes her love for Lear at the play's opening: "I am made of that self metal as my sister, / And prize me at her worth."
- She joins her sister in reducing Lear's retinue and denies him hospitality; she is not content to limit him to a small number of followers but demands he give up all his knights.
- When Lear attempts to stay with her after being rejected by Goneril, she tells him to return to her sister; she is indifferent to his suffering.

### The Blinding of Gloucester
- When Gloucester attempts to help Lear reach safety on the coast, Regan (often with her husband Cornwall) arranges for his capture.
- They brutally interrogate him about his support for Lear; when Gloucester persists, one of them (or both, depending on the production) plucks out his eyes, leaving him blind and broken.
- Regan's participation in this mutilation is more direct than Goneril's in any crime; she actively tortures her victim rather than manipulating others to do so.

### The Love of Edmund
- Regan, like Goneril, falls in love with Edmund and becomes his lover.
- Yet her love is possessive and jealous; she is less willing than Goneril to accept Edmund's dual courtship.
- When she learns that Goneril has also written to Edmund, she becomes enraged and suspicious.

### The Poisoning and Death
- Regan poisons Goneril (or is suspected of poisoning her), and Goneril retaliates by stabbing Regan and then herself.
- Regan dies, presumably from the poison, before Edmund is defeated in combat.
- Her death is less dramatically staged than Goneril's (we are told of it rather than shown it), suggesting that she matters less to the play's conclusion.

### Cruelty Over Strategy
- Unlike Goneril, who calculates and manipulates, Regan is simply cruel—she enjoys Gloucester's suffering and does not scheme around it.
- Yet this directness also makes her more obviously monstrous; the play offers less psychological explanation for her villainy than for her sister's.
- Her husband Cornwall is often presented as the more active agent in Gloucester's blinding, but Regan is complicit and, in many productions, the more vicious of the two.

## context

Regan is less explored by critics and actors than Goneril, often read as a simpler version of her sister's villainy. Yet her brutality to Gloucester—the mutilation and the blinding—represents a violation of humanity that even Goneril does not quite match. She embodies the play's central concern: ingratitude and filial cruelty as a violation of natural law, leading to the dissolution of all bonds and hierarchies. In quiz bowl, Regan is relevant alongside Goneril as a study of female ambition and cruelty, and alongside Gloucester as a victim whose blindness is both literal and metaphorical.
