# Poor Folk

## summary

*Poor Folk* (1846), Dostoevsky's debut novel composed as an epistolary exchange, depicts the quiet tragedy of *Makar Devushkin*, a impoverished copying clerk, and *Varvara Dobroselova*, a orphaned woman he loves. Their tender, self-sacrificing correspondence reveals the spiritual dignity of the poor against their material degradation—an announcement of themes that would dominate his career.

## you gotta know

- Told entirely through letters between Makar and Varvara, the novel eschews plot for the revelation of inner states: longing, shame, hope, resignation.
- Makar is hypersensitive and proud, torn between the need to accept charity and the desire to maintain dignity; he copies bureaucratic documents for mere survival.
- Varvara's background includes a seducer and a dishonorable past; her correspondence with Makar is innocent yet speaks to her yearning for genuine human connection.
- The novel captures St. Petersburg as a labyrinth of poor lodgings and shabby streets where human kindness is rarer than hunger; Dostoevsky depicts poverty's spiritual toll, not just material facts.
- Published in *The Petersburg Miscellany*; the young critic *Belinsky* hailed it as a new voice in Russian realism, promising far greater works.
- Dostoevsky returned to the epistolary form in later stories; this novella remains tender and intimate compared to his later grandeur.
