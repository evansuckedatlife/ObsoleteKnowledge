# Revenge Tragedy

## summary

Revenge tragedy is a dramatic form in which the protagonist's quest for vengeance against a murderer or wrongdoer drives the plot toward catastrophic, often bloody resolution. Originating in Senecan Roman drama and flourishing in Elizabethan and Jacobean England, the revenge tragedy typically features a wronged protagonist who delays, plots, and finally executes a murderous revenge, often at great cost to themselves and innocents. *William Shakespeare*'s *Hamlet* is the supreme literary example, but the form encompasses *Thomas Kyd*'s *The Spanish Tragedy* and anonymous *The Revenger's Tragedy*. Revenge tragedy explores the psychology of vengeance, the failure of justice systems, and the poison that the demand for revenge spreads through a society.

## you gotta know

### Formal Properties
- *The ghost or revelation of injustice*: typically, a crime (murder, rape, betrayal) is revealed to the protagonist, often by a supernatural agent (the victim's ghost).
- *The protagonist's task*: to avenge the wrong; this becomes an obsession that consumes and corrupts the revenger.
- *Delay and hesitation*: the protagonist repeatedly postpones revenge, doubts its justice, considers alternatives; this hesitation is the psychological core.
- *Feigned madness*: the revenger often adopts madness as a mask; genuine and feigned mental states blur.
- *Bloody resolution*: climax typically involves multiple deaths, including the revenger's; the quest for justice becomes mass slaughter.

### Historical Emergence
- *Senecan models*: Roman dramatist *Seneca* influenced Renaissance revenge tragedy with his emphasis on intense emotion, rhetoric, and spectacle.
- *Thomas Kyd*'s *The Spanish Tragedy* (c. 1587): foundational English revenge tragedy; establishes the template of a father avenging his murdered son; Machiavellian plotting and delayed justice.
- *Elizabethan and Jacobean flowering* (1590s–1620s): revenge tragedy becomes dominant; dozens of plays exploit the form; increasing sophistication of psychological exploration.
- *William Shakespeare*'s *Hamlet* (c. 1600): transcends the formulaic; the protagonist's philosophical doubts about revenge, action, and mortality elevate the form.

### Key Themes & Psychology
- *Justice vs. vengeance*: the law is ineffectual or corrupt; private vengeance becomes necessary but morally suspect.
- *Revenge as contamination*: the demand for vengeance poisons the revenger and spreads violence to innocents; no clean justice possible.
- *Madness and feigning*: the revenger's sanity is questioned; is the madness real or performed? What is the difference?
- *Inaction and action*: philosophical exploration of whether to act, when to act, what action means in a corrupted world.
- *Cyclical violence*: vengeance provokes counter-vengeance; blood calls for blood; violence spirals.

### Major Works & Figures
- *The Spanish Tragedy* (*Thomas Kyd*, c. 1587): *Hieronimo* seeks vengeance for his murdered son against the Spanish royal court; embedded play-within-a-play; proto-Hamlet.
- *Hamlet* (*William Shakespeare*, c. 1600): Prince *Hamlet* is charged by his father's ghost to avenge his murder; philosophical investigation of action, hesitation, madness, and meaning.
- *The Revenger's Tragedy* (anonymous, c. 1606): *Vindice* avenges his fiancée's violation and death; baroque excess; moral corruption at court.
- *The Duchess of Malfi* (*John Webster*, c. 1614): revenge elements woven into a tragedy of passionate marriage and tyrannical control.

### Philosophical Implications
- *Crisis of authority*: in revenge tragedy, legitimate authority (king, law) fails; the protagonist must become the instrument of justice.
- *The revenger's corruption*: in pursuing vengeance, the revenger becomes what he opposes; moral clarity dissolves.
- *Action in an uncertain world*: *Hamlet* in particular raises the question: how do you act when you cannot be certain of truth, meaning, or consequence?

## context

Revenge tragedy is the Elizabethan and Jacobean form most preoccupied with justice, morality, and the limits of action. It emerges from a period of religious and political instability (Protestant Reformation, regicide anxieties, succession wars) where questions of legitimate authority and private justice were urgent. The form's psychological depth—its exploration of hesitation, madness, and the revenger's corruption—prefigures the modern psychological novel. *Hamlet*, the supreme revenge tragedy, has come to represent not the form at its most typical but the form transcended into philosophy: the play turns revenge tragedy into a meditation on consciousness, uncertainty, and the nature of action itself. The form declined after the 1620s but never disappeared; it recurs in modern literature and film whenever revenge becomes the engine of narrative. In quiz bowl, revenge tragedy marks the height of Elizabethan and Jacobean drama and the form most preoccupied with philosophical and psychological depth.
