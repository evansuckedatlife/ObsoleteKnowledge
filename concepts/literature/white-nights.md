## summary

*White Nights* (1848), a novella set during St. Petersburg's luminous midnight season, follows an unnamed dreamer's brief romantic awakening when he meets a young woman and they share nightly walks along the Neva. The encounter ends in disappointment as she departs with another; the dreamer is left to contemplate the beauty and cruelty of fleeting human connection and the distance between fantasy and reality.

## you gotta know

- The narrator is a solitary visionary who has lived in romantic imagination rather than real life, finding beauty in city streets and invented narratives.
- Over four consecutive nights of the white (nearly sunlit) nights of June, he meets a young woman and experiences genuine emotional connection—a crack in his isolation.
- The woman confesses she is waiting for a lover; the narrator's brief hope that she might return his affection dissolves when the lover reappears and she leaves with him.
- The novella's lyricism captures the dreamlike quality of the white nights themselves—suspended time, atmospheric beauty, the sense that ordinary life has been transcended.
- Dostoevsky explores the pathos of the dreamer, neither wholly tragic nor sympathetic—a figure wasting authentic life on reverie.
- Published in *The Petersburg Miscellany*; it remains one of his most poetically achieved and melancholic shorter works.
