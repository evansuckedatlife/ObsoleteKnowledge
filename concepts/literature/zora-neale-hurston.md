# Zora Neale Hurston

## summary

*Zora Neale Hurston* was an American novelist, folklorist, and anthropologist who celebrated Black Southern vernacular culture and folk traditions at a time when such material was often denigrated. Her novel *Their Eyes Were Watching God* (1937) stands as a modernist masterpiece centering a Black woman's voice, agency, and self-discovery.

## you gotta know

- *Their Eyes Were Watching God* uses stream-of-consciousness and Black vernacular speech to tell the story of Janie Crawford's three marriages and her journey toward autonomy and self-understanding.
- Trained as an anthropologist, she collected African-American folklore, folk songs, and oral traditions in the South and Caribbean, bringing scholarly rigor to material often dismissed by the academy.
- Her use of dialect and vernacular was politically charged—celebrating Black speech as literary-worthy rather than apologizing for or erasing it.
- Wrote short stories, essays, and plays exploring themes of love, independence, community, and the spiritual dimensions of Black life.
- Faced relative obscurity and poverty later in life, partly because her lyrical, folk-centered work was eclipsed by the protest literature of the 1940s and 1950s.
- Her reputation was revived by feminist and Black literary scholars in the 1970s-1980s, restoring her as a crucial figure in American modernism.
