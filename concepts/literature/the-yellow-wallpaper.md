# The Yellow Wallpaper

## summary

Charlotte Perkins Gilman's 1892 short story traces a woman's psychological descent while confined to a room for rest cure treatment prescribed by her physician husband. The protagonist becomes obsessed with the pattern of the room's yellow wallpaper, eventually believing a woman is trapped behind it, leading to her complete mental fracture and a chilling final act of liberation through madness.

## you gotta know

- The narrator is forbidden from working, writing, or engaging in intellectual activity by her physician husband, a "rest cure" standard for treating women's mental illness in the 1890s.
- She is isolated in a room with barred windows and yellow wallpaper, which she finds increasingly disturbing and hypnotic.
- The narrator begins seeing a creeping figure trapped behind the wallpaper's pattern, becoming convinced it is a woman trying to break free.
- As her mental state deteriorates, the figure becomes more vivid, and the narrator's own sense of self dissolves into identification with the trapped woman.
- The story's final lines reveal the narrator has torn down the wallpaper and is crawling around the room, having physically and psychologically crossed into madness.
- Published in *The New England Magazine*, the story was initially dismissed but became central to feminist critique of medical paternalism and women's autonomy.
- It is considered an early masterpiece of psychological horror fiction grounded in social critique.
