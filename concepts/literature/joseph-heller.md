# Joseph Heller

## summary

*Joseph Heller* (1923–1999) was an American novelist best known for *Catch-22*, a landmark anti-war satire that revolutionized postmodern fiction through circular logic, black comedy, and the collision of bureaucratic absurdity with human mortality. His novel became a cultural touchstone, introducing the titular paradox into everyday language and establishing him as a key voice in postwar American literature.

## you gotta know

- *Catch-22* (1961) follows *Captain Yossarian*, a bombardier in World War II fighting not the enemy but the illogic of military bureaucracy; the novel's circular structure mirrors its central paradox (one cannot be grounded for insanity if one requests it, proving one is sane).
- The novel introduced "Catch-22" as a metaphor for institutional double-bind—rules that trap the individual regardless of compliance; the term entered common parlance to denote logical traps.
- Employs *anachrony*, digression, and repetition; characters and scenes recur in fragmented sequence, reflecting the chaos and trauma of war rather than linear progression.
- Combines farcical humor with brutal depictions of death; tonal shifts between comedy and horror embody the postmodern collapse of unified tone and meaning.
- Later novels (*Something Happened*, *Good as Gold*) extended his satirical range to corporate life and political hypocrisy, though none achieved *Catch-22*'s cultural impact.
- Influenced a generation of American satirists and writers of absurdist fiction; the novel is canonical in postmodern literature courses.

## connections

- [[kurt-vonnegut]] — fellow American postmodernist interrogating war, absurdity, and institutional power through dark comedy.
- [[catch-22]] — his masterwork and most widely read novel; the paradox itself has become a cultural artifact.
- [[don-delillo]] — both American writers treating systems, war, and institutional breakdown through black comedy.
