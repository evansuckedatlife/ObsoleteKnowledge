# Friends, Romans, countrymen

## summary

*Mark Antony's* funeral oration for *Julius Caesar*, a masterpiece of rhetorical manipulation in *Julius Caesar*. Though *Caesar* is dead and *Antony* seemingly powerless, he uses flattery, irony, and emotional appeal to turn the mob against the conspirators who murdered his leader.

## you gotta know

- Delivered over *Caesar's* body in Act III, with the conspirators (*Brutus*, *Cassius*) watching.
- Begins with apparent deference to *Brutus* and the others, calling them "honourable men"—a phrase repeated with mounting sarcasm.
- Catalogs *Caesar's* achievements and refutes the accusation that ambition drove him: "Did this in *Caesar* seem ambitious?"
- Produces *Caesar's* will, promising to read it but withholding its contents, leaving the crowd desperate to know what *Caesar* left them.
- The crowd's emotional transformation—from accepting the conspiracy to demanding vengeance—marks *Antony's* triumph and *Brutus's* fatal miscalculation.
- Exemplifies how language and performance can override truth, violence, and established power.
