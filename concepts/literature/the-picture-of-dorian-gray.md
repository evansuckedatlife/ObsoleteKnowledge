# The Picture of Dorian Gray

## summary

*The Picture of Dorian Gray* is Oscar Wilde's 1890 philosophical novel about Dorian Gray, a young man of exceptional beauty who becomes obsessed with preserving his youth and indulges in sensual excess. He makes a pact—his portrait will age while he remains eternally beautiful—and the consequence of his moral decay becomes visible only in the painting's corruption, a haunting meditation on beauty, morality, and the price of hedonism.

## you gotta know

- Published as a short story in 1890; expanded to a novel in 1891 with additional chapters that deepened its philosophical resonance.
- Wilde coined the phrase "art for art's sake"—the aesthetic philosophy that dominates the novel through the character of Lord Henry Wotton, who argues that morality is irrelevant to beauty.
- The central bargain: Dorian wishes his portrait to bear the marks of aging and sin while he remains untouched; the supernatural mechanism through which this occurs is never explained, leaving it ambiguous and darkly magical.
- Dorian's trajectory moves from innocence (seduced by Lord Henry's nihilism) through hedonistic indulgence to murder and spiritual corruption—all invisible to the world until the painting reveals the truth.
- The novel contains some of Wilde's most famous aphorisms: "There is no such thing as a moral or an immoral book. Books are well written, or badly written. That is all."
- The portrait's corruption parallels Dorian's moral descent; by the novel's end, the painting is so grotesque it serves as a confessional mirror he cannot escape.
- The ending—Dorian's futile attempt to destroy the portrait—results in his own death, leaving observers to wonder whether redemption and truth were always possible.
