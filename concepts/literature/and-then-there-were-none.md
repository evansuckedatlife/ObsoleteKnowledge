# And Then There Were None

## summary

Agatha Christie's 1939 novel is considered her masterpiece: ten strangers are lured to an island, each accused of a past crime, and begin to die one by one according to a sinister nursery rhyme. With no detective to solve the murders and all potential victims dying, the mystery becomes inverted—the reader must figure out not just who the killer is, but how anyone is killing anyone at all.

## you gotta know

- Ten strangers are invited to an island under false pretenses, each harboring a hidden secret that might constitute murder or manslaughter.
- Each person is accused of a past crime by a voice on the island, and each insists on their innocence.
- The deaths begin and accelerate, each matching the verses of the nursery rhyme "Ten Little Soldiers," which also counts down from ten.
- As the survivors dwindle, suspicion and paranoia intensify; no one knows who among them is the killer, or if the killer is even present.
- The isolation of the island means no help can arrive; the survivors must rely entirely on themselves.
- The solution is extraordinary: one of the dead victims arranged the entire scheme before his own death, orchestrating a justice beyond the law.
- The novel ends with no survivor; all ten die, making it perhaps the darkest and most complete mystery in detective fiction.
