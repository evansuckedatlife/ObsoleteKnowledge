## summary

*Hamlet*, William Shakespeare's tragedy written early in the 17th century, tells of Prince Hamlet of Denmark who is visited by his father's ghost and commanded to avenge the king's murder—a task that fills him with philosophical uncertainty, feigned madness, and ultimately leads to the destruction of nearly the entire Danish court. The play explores themes of revenge, madness, mortality, and moral paralysis through its introspective protagonist and is considered one of the greatest works in English literature and a cornerstone of Western drama.

## you gotta know

### The Setup
- The ghost of Hamlet's father appears to his son, revealing that he was murdered by Hamlet's uncle *Claudius*, who has now married Hamlet's mother *Gertrude* and taken the throne.
- Hamlet is ordered by his father's ghost to "revenge his foul and most unnatural murder," a command that troubles the young prince with moral questions and doubt.
- Hamlet feigns madness to mask his investigation, adopting erratic behaviour that confuses the court and distances him from those around him, including the innocent *Ophelia*.

### The Investigation
- When a travelling troupe of actors arrives at Elsinore castle, Hamlet arranges for them to perform *The Mousetrap*, a play closely mirroring his father's murder, to test Claudius's guilt.
- Claudius's horrified reaction during the poisoning scene confirms the ghost's account, and Hamlet's rage intensifies, though his madness (real or feigned) prevents him from acting decisively.

### The Tragedy Unfolds
- Hamlet kills *Polonius*, father of Ophelia and brother of *Laertes*, thinking him to be spying; the murder triggers Ophelia's descent into actual madness and her drowning.
- Claudius manipulates Laertes into arranging a duel with Hamlet, poisoning Laertes' sword as insurance; the plan results in a poisoned drink meant for Hamlet killing his mother Gertrude instead.
- In the final swordfight, the poisoned blade wounds both Hamlet and Laertes; Hamlet, before dying, finally kills Claudius, completing his revenge but at the cost of the entire Danish royal line.

### Themes and Legacy
- The play is central to discussions of *the tragic flaw* (Hamlet's indecision), *moral ambiguity*, and *the cost of revenge*.
- Famous soliloquies such as "To be, or not to be" and "What a piece of work is a man" are among the most quoted lines in English literature.
- The character of Hamlet has become archetype for the intellectual, melancholic, conscience-stricken avenger—the *Hamlet complex* in psychology refers to paralysis through overthinking.

## context

*Hamlet* is Shakespeare's most performed and most analyzed play, standing at the centre of the Western literary canon. Its exploration of interiority—the notion that characters have rich inner lives—revolutionized drama and influenced centuries of literature, philosophy, and psychology. The play's meditations on death, madness, authenticity, and the meaning of action have made it a template for examining the human condition.
