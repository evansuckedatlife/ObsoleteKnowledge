# Angels in America

## summary

Tony Kushner's 1991-1993 two-part play, subtitled "A Gay Fantasia on National Themes," interweaves the personal collapse of four New Yorkers devastated by HIV/AIDS with a larger vision of American identity and redemption. The work combines kitchen-sink realism with apocalyptic fantasy, exploring grief, faith, and the possibility of change.

## you gotta know

- Premiered in 1991 (*Millennium Approaches*) and 1993 (*Perestroika*); won the Pulitzer Prize for Drama in 1993 and became the most celebrated American play of its era.
- The protagonist *Prior Walter*, a gay man dying of AIDS, is visited by an *Angel*—a biblical messenger announcing revelation and demanding that Prior stop the dying; he refuses.
- *Roy Cohn*, the real-life disgraced lawyer and closeted homosexual, is a central character; his death and moral reckoning serve as an indictment of power, hypocrisy, and complicity.
- The play explicitly addresses the Reagan administration's negligence during the AIDS crisis, transforming personal tragedy into political critique.
- *Hannah Pitt*, the mother of a closeted Mormon lawyer, gradually becomes an unlikely ally; the play suggests reconciliation is possible even across deep ideological divides.
- The final image is transcendent: Prior, now a survivor, addresses the audience with a vision of hope and reconstruction ("The Great Work Begins").
