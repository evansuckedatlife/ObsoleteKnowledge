# Emma Lazarus

## summary

*Emma Lazarus* (1849–1887) was an American poet and essayist whose most famous work, "The New Colossus," became the defining poem of American immigration and welcome. Writing in 19th-century New York, she explored Jewish identity, classical learning, and the possibility of an American sanctuary for the persecuted. Though she died young, her poetry and advocacy made her a pivotal figure in Jewish-American letters.

## you gotta know

- "The New Colossus" (1883) — sonnet commissioned for the Statue of Liberty, inscribed on its base; its phrase "your tired, your poor, your huddled masses yearning to breathe free" defines America's self-image.
- Wrote poetry in multiple modes: translations of Hebrew verse, dramatic monologues, philosophical meditations on suffering and redemption.
- *Songs of a Semite* (1874) — collection centring Jewish identity, written after the Russian pogroms renewed her commitment to her heritage.
- Intellectually ambitious: studied languages, translated from Hebrew and German, engaged with classical and contemporary literature.
- Died of Hodgkin lymphoma at 38, leaving behind a body of work of classical rigor and moral urgency.
- Her invocation of the "huddled masses" has made her the poet of immigration itself, though this fame came long after her death.
