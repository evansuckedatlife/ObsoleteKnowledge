# Isaac Bashevis Singer

## summary

*Isaac Bashevis Singer* (1904–1991) was a Polish-born writer who made Yiddish—a language other Jewish intellectuals were abandoning—the vehicle for profound literary modernism. His stories and novels blend folklore, demonology, and psychological realism into a unique vision of Eastern European Jewish life and its American diaspora. He won the Nobel Prize in Literature, the only writer primarily in Yiddish to do so.

## you gotta know

- Won the Nobel Prize in Literature (1978), honouring Yiddish literature at a moment when few believed it could be canonical.
- *The Magician of Lublin* (1960) — picaresque novel of a con artist and seducer awakening to spiritual truth.
- *Satan in Goray* (1935) — early masterpiece set amid a false Messiah's cult, blending chronicle and psychological terror.
- Master of the short story; *Gimpel the Fool* and *The Spinoza of Market Street* are modernist masterpieces of despair and faith.
- Emigrated to New York in 1935; his fiction bridges Eastern European shtetl and American metropolis, nostalgia and estrangement.
- Wrote exclusively in Yiddish and translated his own work to English, arguing Yiddish literature was living, not dead.
- His fiction integrates Jewish folklore, demons, spirits, and mysticism into psychological realism—blurring rational and mythic modes.
