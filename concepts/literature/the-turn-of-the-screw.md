# The Turn of the Screw

## summary

Henry James's 1898 novella is a masterwork of ambiguous horror centered on a governess who takes a position at an isolated English country house and begins to see the ghosts of two former servants who died under mysterious circumstances. The story's genius lies in its deliberate narrative unreliability: readers cannot determine whether the ghosts are real presences or hallucinations born from the governess's deteriorating psychological state, making every act of protection or violation a question of interpretation.

## you gotta know

- The governess arrives at Bly House to care for two children, Miles and Flora, and is tasked with absolute secrecy regarding her employer.
- She begins seeing the apparition of Peter Quint, a former valet, on the grounds, and later the ghost of Miss Jessel, a former governess.
- The governess believes the children are complicit with the ghosts and are being corrupted by their supernatural influence, driving her to increasingly desperate interventions.
- Miles dies at the novella's climax after the governess forces him to confront the specter of Quint, leaving ambiguous whether he has been saved or destroyed by her obsession.
- James employs an unreliable narrator technique that has generated more than a century of critical debate: are the ghosts real, or is the governess projecting her own repressed desires and paranoia?
- The novella introduced the technique of psychological horror—horror derived not from external threat but from the rupture of the narrator's sanity and moral clarity.
- It became a foundational text of modern horror fiction and literary ambiguity.
