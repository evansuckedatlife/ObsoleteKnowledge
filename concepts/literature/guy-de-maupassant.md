# Guy de Maupassant

## summary

*Guy de Maupassant* (1850–1893) was a French short-story master and novelist whose work epitomised the aesthetic and moral clarity of late-19th-century realism. A protégé of Gustave Flaubert, Maupassant brought surgical precision to tales of provincial France, military life, and the human heart—never sentimentalising, always observing. His hundreds of stories, published at a prolific rate, established the short form as a vehicle for complete psychological and social portraiture, and his influence on the genre is arguably greater than any writer's except Chekhov.

## you gotta know

### Signature Stories
- *"The Necklace"* (*"La Parure"*) — a woman borrows a "diamond" necklace, loses it, and spends a decade repaying what turns out to be costume jewellery, a meditation on class and self-deception.
- *"A Piece of String"* — a peasant picks up a string on the road; suspected of theft, his reputation is destroyed by a misunderstanding he cannot correct.
- *"The Horla"* — a psychological horror story told in diary form, descending into madness and paranoia.
- *"Balls of Fat"* (*"Boule de Suif"*) — a prostitute sacrifices her body for the survival of bourgeois passengers during the Franco-Prussian War.

### Technique & Philosophy
- Master of the *plot twist* and the ironic reversal — stories that deliver a final sentence that reframes everything before it.
- Maupassant was fascinated by the gap between appearance and reality; his characters are undone by misunderstanding, vanity, and circumstance.
- Published over 300 stories in his lifetime, each precise and complete — an industrial feat of craft.

### Legacy
- Defined the modern short story as a compressed, self-contained form with a single revelation or reversal.
- Influenced American writers (O. Henry, Guy de Maupassant imitators) and Russian modernists equally.

## context

Maupassant arrived as Flaubert's heir to French realism at precisely the moment when naturalism (Zola's scientistic materialism) was challenging romanticism. He negotiated both: his precision was Flaubertian, but his subject matter—the petit bourgeoisie, soldiers, peasants—was Zolaist. What set him apart was his fundamental pessimism and his irony: Maupassant never lets his characters off the hook with sentiment. A woman's descent into poverty, a soldier's dehumanisation, a workman's irreversible loss—these are treated with cool detachment that somehow becomes deeply moral. In quiz bowl, Maupassant is essential to questions about 19th-century European realism, the short story's formal properties, and irony as a structural principle.
