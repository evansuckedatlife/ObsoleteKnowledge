# Pope's Iliad

## summary

Alexander Pope's translation of Homer's *Iliad* (1715–1720) is an English verse rendering in heroic couplets that became the standard English Homer for over a century. Though not a literal translation, Pope's elegant, energetic couplets achieved a marriage of Augustan wit and classical grandeur that made Homer speak fluently in 18th-century English.

## you gotta know

- Published in six volumes over five years; an immediate commercial and critical success that made Pope's fortune and established him as England's preeminent poet.
- Rendered in rhymed heroic couplets with strict symmetry and closure—every couplet a unit of meaning, quite unlike the dactylic hexameter of Greek but perfectly suited to the neoclassical taste.
- Pope added hundreds of prefatory and marginal notes, scholarly apparatus that positioned him as both poet and critic, defending Homer against charges of imperfection.
- His translation is famously "improved" Homer: rounded rough speeches into elegant arguments, added symmetry and wit Homer lacked, softened brutality and made gods more refined.
- Contemporary critics praised the fluency and noted the deviations; by the 19th century scholars recognized Pope had translated Homer into Pope, not Homer into English.
- The companion *Odyssey* (1725–1726, with William Broome and Elijah Fenton) was less wholly Pope's but equally polished; together they gave English readers a complete Homeric text.
- Superseded in the 19th century by more literal translations (Chapman's *Homer* was revived as more "authentic"), though Pope's couplets remain the most quotable English Homer for certain passages.
