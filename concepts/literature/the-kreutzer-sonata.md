## summary

*The Kreutzer Sonata* is Tolstoy's dark meditation on marriage, jealousy, and sexual desire, told through a passenger's monologue confessing to his wife's murder. The narrator traces his marriage's slow poisoning by physical passion, emotional distance, and the perceived intimacy between his wife and a musician, culminating in a violent act he simultaneously condemns and justifies through philosophy.

## you gotta know

- A man on a train reveals to a stranger that he has killed his wife, then narrates the deterioration of his marriage from its passionate beginning to its tragic end.
- He describes the spiritual deadness that follows sexual consummation, the erosion of tenderness, and the resentment born from treating marriage as a duty.
- The turning point arrives when a musician begins regularly visiting to play Beethoven's *Kreutzer Sonata* with his wife, and the narrator becomes convinced of an affair.
- Tolstoy uses the Sonata as a metaphor for the dangerous power of art and sensuality to stir passion and destroy domestic stability.
- The narrator's obsession with the music, his wife's apparent intimacy with the musician, and his simmering rage create an atmosphere of mounting crisis.
- He murders his wife in a fit of jealous rage, but the novella never entirely clarifies whether the affair was real or imagined—the narrator's perspective is inherently unreliable.
- The work is simultaneously a confession, a philosophical argument against sexual relationships, and an exploration of masculine violence and paranoia.
