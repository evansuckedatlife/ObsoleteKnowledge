# Chapman's Homer

## summary

Chapman's Homer is George Chapman's English verse translation of Homer's *Iliad* (1611–1615) and *Odyssey* (1614–1616), written in rhymed heroic couplets. Though not literal, Chapman's translation brought Homer vibrantly alive for English readers and inspired Keats's famous sonnet on first reading it, standing as the most celebrated English translation until Pope's version in the 18th century.

## you gotta know

- Published in folio editions in the early 1610s, Chapman's *Iliad* became the dominant English Homer for Renaissance and Stuart readers.
- Rendered in rhymed heroic couplets, a form that sacrificed classical fidelity for fluidity and natural English speech—sometimes expanding or condensing Homer freely.
- Chapman's prefatory essays and glosses made him both translator and scholarly guide, attempting to defend Homer's moral philosophy and connect him to Christian virtue.
- Keats's 1816 sonnet "On First Looking into Chapman's Homer" immortalized the translation, describing the shock of discovery: "Then felt I like some watcher of the skies / When a new planet swims into his ken."
- Chapman filled gaps and "completed" passages where manuscripts were fragmentary, decisions that influenced how later scholars reconstructed Homer.
- His style is baroque and ornate, heavy with conceits and Elizabethan-Jacobean diction, quite unlike the simpler directness many modern readers expect from Homer.
- Superseded by Alexander Pope's *Iliad* (1715–1720), which used couplets with greater elegance and became the standard until the 19th century.
