# Euripides

## summary

**Euripides** (c. 480–406 BCE) was an ancient Greek tragedian and the third of the great Athenian dramatists, alongside Aeschylus and Sophocles. He wrote approximately 90 plays, of which 19 survive, and is renowned for his psychological insight, his questioning of traditional myths and values, and his portrayal of human passion and suffering with unprecedented complexity. Unlike his predecessors, Euripides often subverted conventional moral narratives, presenting characters (especially women) driven by irrational desire, revenge, and despair rather than heroic virtue, foreshadowing modern drama.

## you gotta know

- Euripides was born around 480 BCE in Athens during the Classical period and grew up during the city's cultural and political flourishing under Pericles, later witnessing its decline during the Peloponnesian War.
- He competed in the dramatic festivals of Athens, winning first place fewer times than Aeschylus or Sophocles, though his works were popular and he eventually became highly influential.
- *Medea* (431 BCE) is perhaps his masterpiece—a tragedy depicting a woman of supernatural power who murders her own children to exact revenge on her unfaithful husband Jason; the play sparked outrage for its sympathetic portrayal of a female murderer.
- *The Bacchae*, written near the end of his life and performed posthumously, recounts the god Dionysus's revenge on the city of Thebes for refusing to acknowledge his divinity; it explores ecstasy, madness, and the destructive force of the irrational.
- *Hippolytus* portrays the tragedy of a young man destroyed by the false accusation of a rejected woman (Phaedra), exploring sexual desire, shame, and the inadequacy of virtue alone.
- *The Trojan Women* (415 BCE) is an anti-war play depicting the suffering of women and children in the aftermath of Troy's fall, unusual for its focus on civilian rather than heroic suffering.
- Euripides' innovation was psychological realism: he presented gods and heroes not as archetypes but as beings driven by passion, fear, and contradiction; his characters often question the justice of the divine and the morality of human action.

## context

Euripides lived during Athens's golden age and its decline, a period of intellectual ferment when traditional beliefs were being questioned by sophists and philosophers. His plays reflect this upheaval—they are skeptical of received myth, interrogative of authority, and deeply concerned with the irrational and destructive aspects of human nature. While Aeschylus and Sophocles presented tragedy as a means of understanding divine justice and human dignity, Euripides presented it as a unveiling of human passions, often at odds with conventional morality. This made him controversial in his own time but profoundly influential on later drama, from Roman playwrights to modern theatre.
