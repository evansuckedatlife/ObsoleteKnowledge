# Mark Twain

## summary

*Mark Twain* (1835–1910), born Samuel Clemens in Hannibal, Missouri, was the preeminent American humorist and satirist of the 19th century. A riverboat pilot turned journalist and novelist, he transmuted frontier experience and vernacular speech into fiction of enduring power and social critique. *The Adventures of Tom Sawyer* and *The Adventures of Huckleberry Finn* are canonical American novels; his wit and skepticism of authority shaped American literary voice.

## you gotta know

- Grew up in Hannibal on the Mississippi River, which became the setting and spiritual center of his greatest fiction; worked as a riverboat pilot in his twenties.
- Took the pen name "Mark Twain" (a riverboat term for safe water depth) as a journalist in Nevada and California during the 1860s.
- *The Innocents Abroad* (1869): travel memoir satirizing American tourism and European pretension; became a bestseller and established his reputation.
- *The Adventures of Tom Sawyer* (1876): boy's adventures in Hannibal; nostalgia, humor, and moral complexity; remains widely read as a children's classic.
- *The Adventures of Huckleberry Finn* (1884): sequel narrated by illiterate Huck, who escapes slavery to help the runaway slave Jim; often considered the greatest American novel, though now debated for its use of racial slurs and its equivocal treatment of racism.
- *A Connecticut Yankee in King Arthur's Court* (1889): satire of chivalry and technological superiority; American pragmatism encounters medieval romance.
- Master of vernacular speech and first-person narration; his use of authentic American dialects and colloquialism broke with genteel literary tradition.
