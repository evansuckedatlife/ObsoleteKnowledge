## summary

The *Analects* is a collection of sayings and conversations attributed to *Confucius* and his disciples, compiled in China around the 5th century BCE. It stands as the foundational text of *Confucianism*, shaping Chinese philosophy and governance for millennia.

## you gotta know

- Written as brief aphorisms and dialogues, emphasizing virtue, propriety, and proper relationships.
- *Confucius* (551–479 BCE) lived during the Spring and Autumn period of China but did not write the text himself.
- The name *Lunyu* (論語) literally means "classified sayings" or "collected conversations" in Classical Chinese.
- Core themes include *li* (propriety/ritual), *ren* (humaneness), and self-cultivation through learning and moral discipline.
- Heavily influenced Chinese education for over 2,000 years as the primary textbook for civil service exams.
- Organized into 20 books by the Han Dynasty editors, not chronologically but by topic and context.
- Famous passage: "The Master said, 'When you know a thing, to hold that you know it; and when you do not know a thing, to allow that you do not know it—this is knowledge.'"

## connections

- [[confucius]] — the teacher whose sayings and life the text records.
- [[tao-te-ching]] — contrasts with Confucianism's emphasis on ritual and order; represents Taoist alternative philosophy.
- [[buddhism]] — later influenced by and interacted with Buddhist thought in East Asia.
