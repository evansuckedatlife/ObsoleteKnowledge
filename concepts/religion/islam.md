# Islam

## summary

Islam is a monotheistic Abrahamic religion founded in seventh-century Arabia by the Prophet Muhammad, centered on faith in the one God, Allah, and adherence to the teachings revealed in the Qur'an. With approximately 1.8 billion adherents worldwide, it is the second-largest religion globally and has profoundly shaped the cultures, sciences, and politics of three continents. Islamic tradition encompasses diverse schools of theology and jurisprudence, unified by core beliefs in prophetism, divine justice, and the ultimate resurrection and judgment.

## you gotta know

### Core beliefs and texts

- Islam affirms *monotheism* (*tawhid*) — the absolute uniqueness and transcendence of God (*Allah*) — making it fundamentally incompatible with Christianity's doctrine of the Trinity.
- The *Qur'an*, believed by Muslims to be the literal, inimitable word of God revealed to Muhammad over 23 years, is Islam's supreme authority on theology and law.
- Muhammad is revered as the *final prophet*, completing a lineage that includes Abraham, Moses, and Jesus, each of whom brought divine guidance appropriate to their time.
- The *Five Pillars of Islam* — the faith declaration (*shahada*), five daily prayers (*salat*), almsgiving (*zakat*), fasting during Ramadan (*sawm*), and the pilgrimage to Mecca (*hajj*) — form the framework of Islamic practice.

### Historical development and branches

- Islam rapidly expanded across the Mediterranean and Central Asia within a century of Muhammad's death in 632 CE, absorbing Hellenistic philosophy and Indian mathematics, and driving the *Islamic Golden Age* (8th–14th centuries).
- The religion split into *Sunni* (majority, emphasizing consensus tradition and legal schools) and *Shia* (emphasizing the spiritual authority of descendants of Muhammad) branches following a succession dispute, a schism that persists in theology and jurisprudence.
- Islamic empires — the Umayyad, Abbasid, Ottoman, and Safavid — served as centers of learning, architecture, and governance, preserving Greek and Roman texts and advancing mathematics, astronomy, and medicine.
- *Al-Andalus*, the Islamic kingdom in medieval Spain (8th–15th centuries), was a crucial intellectual crossroads where Islamic, Christian, and Jewish scholars collaborated, influencing the European Renaissance.

### Global significance

- Islam's legal and philosophical tradition shaped not only Islamic civilization but also European law and political thought through contact and translation during the Crusades and Renaissance.
- The *hajj*, the annual pilgrimage to Mecca, creates a unique annual gathering of millions of Muslims from every nation, ethnicity, and social class, reinforcing global Islamic identity and unity.

## context

Islam has been foundational to the political, cultural, and intellectual life of Africa, the Middle East, South Asia, and Southeast Asia for nearly 1,400 years. In the medieval period, Islamic scholars preserved and extended Greek philosophy, transmitted Indian numerals and mathematical systems to the West, and pioneered advances in astronomy, medicine, and architecture. The religion's emphasis on scriptural interpretation (*ijtihad*) and jurisprudence generated multiple schools of law, each adapted to different societies. In the modern era, Islam's encounter with Western colonialism, nationalism, and modernization has produced diverse theological responses, from secular nation-states to revivalist movements. Understanding Islam is essential to comprehending world history, geopolitics, and the intellectual heritage of three continents.
