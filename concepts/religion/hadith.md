# Hadith

## summary

The *Hadith* is a collection of reports documenting the sayings, deeds, and approvals of the Prophet *Muhammad*, compiled in the centuries after his death. Alongside the *Qur'an*, it forms the second pillar of Islamic law and theology (*Sunna*), guiding Muslim life and jurisprudence.

## you gotta know

- *Hadith* means "report" or "account" in Arabic; each is a chain of narrators (*isnad*) leading back to someone who witnessed or heard *Muhammad's* words.
- Early Muslims orally transmitted *hadith* for the first 100–200 years; major written collections emerged from the 9th century onward.
- Six canonical collections recognized by Sunni Islam: *Sahih al-Bukhari*, *Sahih Muslim*, *Sunan Abu Dawud*, *Jami' al-Tirmidhi*, *Sunan an-Nasai*, and *Sunan Ibn Majah*.
- Scholars developed rigorous chains of transmission (*isnad*) and verification methods to distinguish authentic reports from weak or fabricated ones.
- *Hadith* covers ethics, law, ritual, medicine, family life, and spirituality—essentially all aspects of Islamic practice not explicitly detailed in the *Qur'an*.
- Later *hadith* scholarship became its own discipline, including biographical dictionaries of narrators and detailed critiques of chain reliability.
- Different Islamic schools (*madhabs*) and theological traditions (Sunni, Shia) have varying canons and weightings of *hadith* authenticity.

## connections

- [[muhammad]] — the prophet whose life and teachings the *hadith* records.
- [[quran]] — the revealed word of God; *hadith* provides context and practical interpretation.
- [[islam]] — foundational to Islamic law (*Sharia*), theology, and daily religious practice.
