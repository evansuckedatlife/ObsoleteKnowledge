# Buddhism

## summary

**Buddhism** is a major world religion and philosophical system founded in ancient India by *Siddhartha Gautama* (the Buddha) in the 6th–5th centuries BCE. Focusing on spiritual development and the attainment of insight into the true nature of reality, it teaches that existence is characterized by suffering (*dukkha*), which arises from ignorance and craving but can be overcome through ethical conduct, meditation, and wisdom to attain *Nirvana*—the ultimate liberation. Unlike religions centered on divine beings or revelation, Buddhism is fundamentally a practical path of self-transformation grounded in rational inquiry and personal experience. Starting in India, it spread throughout Asia—adapting to local cultures while maintaining core doctrines—becoming one of the world's major religions with over 500 million adherents. Buddhism's emphasis on compassion, mental discipline, and philosophical sophistication has made it influential far beyond Asia in modern psychology, philosophy, and spiritual practice.

## you gotta know

### Core Doctrines
- The *Four Noble Truths* form the foundation: suffering exists, suffering has causes (craving and ignorance), suffering can end, and there is a path to end suffering.
- The *Noble Eightfold Path*—right view, intention, speech, action, livelihood, effort, mindfulness, and concentration—provides the practical guide to liberation, divided into wisdom, ethical conduct, and mental discipline.
- *Dukkha* (suffering/unsatisfactoriness) is not pessimism but a diagnosis: all conditioned phenomena involve some form of suffering due to impermanence and lack of enduring self.
- *Anatta* (non-self or "no-soul") doctrine rejects the Vedic concept of a permanent, eternal soul (*Atman*), teaching instead that what we call "self" is a constantly changing assemblage of five aggregates.

### Rejection of Vedic Authority
- Buddhism explicitly rejects the authority of the Vedas and the Brahmin-led ritual system, attacking the Vedic caste system as unjust.
- It offers an alternative path to liberation open to all, regardless of birth caste, offering monastic community (*sangha*) as an alternative to brahminical priesthood.

### Philosophical Sophistication
- Buddhism develops sophisticated theories of epistemology, logic, metaphysics, and ethics; later Buddhist philosophers engaged in rigorous debate with Hindu and other philosophical schools.
- The *Pali Canon* (*Tripitaka*, "Three Baskets")—Vinaya (monastic rules), Sutta (discourses), Abhidhamma (philosophical analysis)—represents an encyclopedic philosophical system.

### Major Branches
- *Theravada* (the oldest surviving school, prominent in Sri Lanka and Southeast Asia) emphasizes monastic practice and individual enlightenment.
- *Mahayana* (popular in East Asia) emphasizes the *bodhisattva* ideal—postponing one's own Nirvana to help all sentient beings achieve liberation.
- *Vajrayana* (Tibetan Buddhism) incorporates esoteric practices, tantric rituals, and the idea that enlightenment can be achieved in one lifetime.

## context

Buddhism's rise and eventual decline in India yet flourishing in Asia represents one of history's great religious transformations. In India, it directly challenged brahminical authority and the Vedic worldview, offering an alternative path to liberation grounded in reason and practice rather than ritual and caste. The patronage of the Mauryan Emperor *Ashoka the Great* (3rd century BCE), who converted to Buddhism and sent missionaries throughout Asia, was crucial to its spread. The adaptation of Buddhism to different Asian contexts—Sanskrit philosophy in Kashmir, meditation mysticism in Tibet, Pure Land devotionalism in East Asia, Zen simplicity in Japan—demonstrates its flexibility while maintaining core doctrines. In modern times, Buddhism has resonated in the West as a philosophical and spiritual discipline compatible with scientific naturalism, making it influential in psychology, cognitive science, and contemporary spirituality. ## connections

- [[siddhartha-gautama]] — the historical prince who attained enlightenment to become the Buddha and founded Buddhism.
- [[upanishads]] — the Vedic philosophical texts whose ideas of rebirth and liberation Buddhism inherited and transformed.
- [[vedas]] — the Vedic scriptures whose authority Buddhism explicitly rejected in favor of reason and personal experience.
- [[ashoka]] — the Mauryan emperor whose patronage and missionary efforts spread Buddhism throughout Asia.
- [[tao-te-ching]] — the foundational Daoist text that influenced and was influenced by Buddhism, particularly in developing Zen Buddhism in China.
- [[nirvana]] — the ultimate goal and end-state of suffering that Buddhism promises.
- [[dharma]] — the Buddhist term for cosmic and ethical law, and for the Buddha's teachings.
- [[hinduism]] — the parent religious tradition from which Buddhism arose in reaction and with which it shares many concepts.
- [[zen-buddhism]] — a major branch emphasizing meditation and direct insight, developed from Mahayana Buddhism in East Asia.
- [[tibet]] — the region where Vajrayana Buddhism developed its distinctive esoteric practices and remains the dominant tradition.
