## summary

The *Avesta* is the sacred scripture of *Zoroastrianism*, an ancient Persian religion founded by the prophet *Zoroaster* (Zarathustra). Written in the Avestan language (a now-extinct Iranian tongue), it comprises hymns, liturgies, and teachings that shaped one of history's first monotheistic faiths.

## you gotta know

- The *Avesta* survives in fragmentary form; only about one-quarter of the original text remains, mostly in liturgical portions called the *Yasna*.
- Written in a language closely related to early Sanskrit, suggesting a shared Indo-Iranian cultural heritage with Vedic peoples.
- Divided into five main sections: *Yasna* (hymns and prayers), *Yashts* (songs to divine beings), *Visperad* (hymns for ritual), *Vendidad* (law against demons), and miscellaneous fragments.
- Central to the *Yasna* are the 17 *Gathas* (songs), believed by some scholars to be *Zoroaster's* own words, though authorship remains debated.
- Core doctrines include dualism between *Ahura Mazda* (good) and *Angra Mainyu* (evil), free will, and cosmic judgment.
- The *Zend* refers to Aramaic-language commentaries added later; "Zend-Avesta" is the combined term for text and commentary.
- Profoundly influenced *Judaism*, *Christianity*, and *Islam*, particularly concepts of heaven, hell, resurrection, and apocalypse.

## connections

- [[zoroaster]] — the prophet whose teachings the *Avesta* preserves and transmits.
- [[judaism]] — influenced Jewish eschatology and concepts of afterlife during and after the Babylonian exile.
- [[islam]] — Islamic theology absorbed Zoroastrian cosmological and ethical frameworks.
