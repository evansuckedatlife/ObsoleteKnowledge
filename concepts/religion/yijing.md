# Yijing

## summary

The *Yijing* (or *I Ching*, "Book of Changes") is an ancient Chinese divination text and philosophical classic, compiled over centuries and reaching its canonical form around the 10th century BCE. It describes patterns of change in nature and human affairs through 64 hexagrams, serving as a guide to understanding transformation and balance.

## you gotta know

- Composed of the *Ten Wings*, a set of commentaries attributed to *Confucius*, and the core *Yijing* text itself, organized around 64 six-line symbols called *hexagrams*.
- Each *hexagram* consists of six broken (yin) or unbroken (yang) lines, representing complementary cosmic principles and their dynamic interplay.
- Used as a divination tool: practitioners cast yarrow stalks or coins to generate random hexagrams, then interpret the resulting pattern and associated texts.
- The *Yijing* describes 64 possible human and cosmic situations, each with commentary (*xiang*) on its meaning and line-specific wisdom.
- Philosophical foundation: the cosmos is in constant flux; change follows patterns; harmony comes from recognizing and adapting to these patterns.
- Influenced all major Chinese philosophical schools—*Confucianism*, *Taoism*, *Buddhism*, and Chinese cosmology, medicine, and martial arts.
- The Yang/Yin symbol itself derives from *Yijing* cosmology, representing dynamic balance and cyclical transformation.
- Still widely consulted for divination and self-reflection, and studied as a philosophical text in academic and esoteric contexts worldwide.
