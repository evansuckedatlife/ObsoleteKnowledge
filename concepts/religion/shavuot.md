## summary

Shavuot, the *Feast of Weeks*, is a two-day festival occurring fifty days after Passover, typically in May or June. It commemorates the *giving of the Torah at Mount Sinai*, when God revealed the law to the Israelites. The holiday also marks the spring harvest and is one of the three pilgrimage festivals in Jewish tradition.

## you gotta know

- The name *Shavuot* literally means "weeks," referring to the seven-week counting period (*Omer*) between Passover and this festival.
- The holiday celebrates the receiving of the *Torah and the Ten Commandments*, making it a festival of Jewish law, learning, and covenant with God.
- *All-night study sessions* called *Tikkun Leil Shavuot* (repairing the night of Shavuot) are traditionally held, during which Jews study texts from the entire Torah.
- *Dairy foods* — especially cheesecake and blintzes — are traditionally eaten, with various mystical and practical reasons offered for this custom.
- The *Book of Ruth* is read during Shavuot, connecting themes of conversion, loyalty, and choosing to be part of the Jewish covenant community.
- Shavuot is unique among major Jewish holidays in having no specific ritual observances mandated by biblical law — its character as a Torah holiday develops through rabbinic tradition.
- The festival originally emphasized the barley and wheat harvest, but the spiritual significance shifted to focus on the giving of the Torah.
- In Israel, Shavuot is observed for one day; in the Diaspora, it is traditionally observed for two days.

## connections

- [[torah]] — the central focus of the holiday, celebrating its revelation at Sinai.
- [[passover]] — connected through the counting of the Omer period that links the two festivals.
- [[judaism]] — central to Jewish identity as a tradition rooted in Torah study and observance.
