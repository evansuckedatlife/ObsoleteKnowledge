## summary

Pidyon ha-ben (redemption of the firstborn) is a ceremony in which the parents of a firstborn son symbolically "redeem" him from priestly service by paying a sum to a *kohen* (member of the Jewish priestly class). Held thirty days after birth, this rite commemorates the biblical exemption of the firstborn Israelites from serving in the Temple.

## you gotta know

- The ceremony occurs on the *thirty-first day* after the child's birth, or later if necessary, but never on *Shabbat* or a major holiday.
- The payment to the *kohen* traditionally consists of five silver coins (in modern times, a monetary equivalent), representing the redemption amount prescribed in Torah.
- The *kohen* (priestly descendant) receives the payment and can then keep or donate it, and formally accepts the redemption with a blessing.
- The ceremony is obligatory only for firstborn sons whose mother had not been previously married and whose father is not a *kohen* or *levi*.
- A festive meal accompanies the ceremony, often more elaborate than the brit milah, emphasizing its joyous redemptive nature.
- The father traditionally presents the child to the *kohen*, asking if he will redeem the boy for the prescribed amount; the *kohen* accepts and blesses the family.
- The rite symbolizes the historical moment when God redeemed the Israelites' firstborn sons in Egypt before the Exodus.
- In modern practice, some families hold pidyon haben even when the technical obligation is unclear, to maintain the tradition and its educational value.
