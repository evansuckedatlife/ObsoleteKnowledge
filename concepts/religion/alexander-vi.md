## summary

Reigning as pope from 1492 to 1503, **Alexander VI** (born Rodrigo Borgia) is the most notorious of the Renaissance popes, with a pontificate defined by corruption, nepotism, and political intrigue. A skilled diplomat and patron of the arts, he used the wealth of the papacy to advance the careers of his children, Cesare and Lucrezia Borgia. His reign became a symbol of the spiritual decay that eventually sparked the Protestant Reformation.

## you gotta know

- *Issued the papal bull Inter Caetera* in 1493, establishing the Line of Demarcation that divided the non-Christian world between Spain and Portugal, paving the way for the *Treaty of Tordesillas*.
- *Promoted his illegitimate children Cesare and Lucrezia Borgia*, using Cesare to militarily conquer the Romagna region and arranging marriages for Lucrezia to secure political alliances.
- *Ordered the excommunication and execution of Girolamo Savonarola* in 1498, the reformist Dominican friar who had criticized Alexander's corruption and ruled Florence.
- *Won the papacy through simony* (bribery) during the 1492 conclave, defeating his chief rival Giuliano della Rovere (the future Pope Julius II).
- Commissioned the Umbrian artist Pinturicchio to paint the elaborate frescoes of the *Borgia Apartments* in the Apostolic Palace.

## connections

- [[peter]] — the apostle and first Bishop of Rome whose seat Alexander VI won via bribery.
- [[julius-ii]] — his successor and bitter rival, who refused to live in the Borgia Apartments to avoid seeing Alexander's portrait.
- [[treaty-of-tordesillas]] — the treaty dividing the New World, which was initiated by Alexander's *Inter Caetera* bull.
- [[girolamo-savonarola]] — the reformist Florentine friar whom Alexander excommunicated and had burned at the stake.
- [[cesare-borgia]] — his son, the cardinal turned military commander who served as the primary model for Niccolò Machiavelli's *The Prince*.
