## summary

Christianity is a monotheistic Abrahamic religion centered on the life, death, and resurrection of Jesus Christ, whom believers regard as the Son of God and the Messiah prophesied in the Hebrew Bible. Emerging from first-century Judaea, it became the dominant religion of the Roman Empire and spread across the Mediterranean, Europe, Africa, Asia, and the Americas. With over two billion adherents, Christianity is the world's largest religion and has profoundly shaped Western law, philosophy, art, literature, and political institutions over two millennia.

## you gotta know

### Jesus and the Gospels

- **Jesus**, born in Bethlehem during the reign of Herod the Great, preached a message of repentance, forgiveness, and the coming *kingdom of God* throughout Judaea and Galilee before his execution.
- The *Gospels* (Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John), composed in the late 1st century, are the primary historical sources for Jesus's life, teachings, and miracles, though they differ in emphasis and detail.
- Jesus's signature teachings include the *Beatitudes*, the *Sermon on the Mount*, and numerous parables (the *Good Samaritan*, the *Prodigal Son*, the *Sower*) that convey moral and spiritual lessons.
- He selected *twelve apostles* (disciples) to accompany him and spread his message; after his death, they became the leaders of the early Christian communities.

### Death and Resurrection

- Jesus was arrested in Jerusalem, condemned by the Roman governor *Pontius Pilate*, and executed by *crucifixion* — the state punishment for sedition and blasphemy under Roman law.
- Christians believe that Jesus rose from the dead three days after his crucifixion, appearing to his disciples and commissioning them to preach the gospel to all nations.
- The *Resurrection* and the promise of eternal life through faith in Jesus form the theological core of Christianity and distinguish it from Judaism and Islam.
- *Paul*, a missionary apostle (not one of the Twelve), systematized Christian theology in his epistles, developing the concept of salvation through faith in Christ's death and resurrection.

### Development of doctrine and practice

- The *Trinity* — the concept that God is simultaneously One and Three (Father, Son, and Holy Spirit) — emerged in the early church and became orthodox Christian doctrine by the Council of Nicaea (325 CE).
- The *Virgin Mary*, mother of Jesus, became central to Christian theology and devotion; many Christian traditions hold her as an intercessor and offer her veneration.
- Christian practice centers on *baptism* (initiation) and the *Eucharist* (communion, the ritual consumption of bread and wine commemorating Jesus's final meal), though their significance and theology vary across denominations.
- The *New Testament*, composed of the four Gospels, the Acts of the Apostles, the epistles (letters) of Paul and other early leaders, and the Revelation to John, became the authoritative text of Christian doctrine alongside the Hebrew Bible (called the Old Testament by Christians).

### Church and history

- The early Church faced persecution under Roman emperors but gained favor under *Constantine I* (4th century), leading to its establishment as the official religion of the Roman Empire under Theodosius I.
- Major divisions emerged: the *Roman Catholic Church* (centered in Rome under papal authority), the *Eastern Orthodox Church* (centered in Constantinople), and the *Protestant* churches (following the Reformation of the 16th century).
- Christianity's expansion, first along trade routes and then through European colonization, made it a global religion; it became the dominant faith in Europe, the Americas, and parts of Africa and Asia.
- Christian theology and philosophy — developed by thinkers like Augustine, Thomas Aquinas, and Martin Luther — profoundly shaped Western intellectual, legal, and political traditions.

## context

Christianity has been the dominant religious and cultural force in the West for nearly two thousand years, profoundly shaping law, morality, art, science, and statecraft. The religion's central claim — that God became incarnate in a human being and was executed and raised to vindicate a new covenant with humanity — is theologically unique and historically generative of both tremendous devotion and fierce skepticism. The early Church's encounter with Hellenistic philosophy produced sophisticated theology and metaphysics in the works of the Church Fathers and medieval scholastics, integrating Aristotelian logic with revelation. The Bible became the most published, translated, and studied book in human history, shaping literature, legal codes (the Ten Commandments as foundation for law), and ethical philosophy. Christianity's internal diversity — spanning Catholic, Orthodox, and Protestant traditions, and encompassing mystical, liturgical, evangelical, and intellectual expressions — reflects its adaptability across cultures and centuries. For understanding Western history, art, politics, and philosophy, Christianity is indispensable.
