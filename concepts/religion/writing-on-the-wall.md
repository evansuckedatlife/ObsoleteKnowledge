## summary

This phrase comes from *Daniel*, where mysterious writing appears on a palace wall during a feast, and Daniel must interpret it as an omen of doom. It means an unmistakable sign that something bad is about to happen, a warning that is clear and unavoidable.

## you gotta know

- In *Daniel 5*, King Belshazzar of Babylon holds a great feast and a ghostly hand appears, writing on the wall: "Mene, mene, tekel, upharsin" (an Aramaic phrase meaning "numbered, numbered, weighed, divided").
- Daniel is brought in to interpret the writing and tells Belshazzar that God has judged his kingdom and found it wanting—it will be divided and given to the Medes and Persians.
- That very night, the prophecy comes to pass: Belshazzar is killed and his kingdom falls, validating Daniel's interpretation.
- The phrase emphasizes *inevitability*—the writing is on the wall, visible and undeniable, and what it predicts *will* happen.
- Medieval and Early Modern Christians saw this as a type of final judgment and divine accounting, adding religious weight to the phrase.
- Modern usage: "the writing is on the wall" means failure or disaster is imminent and can be clearly foreseen—it cannot be stopped or hidden.
