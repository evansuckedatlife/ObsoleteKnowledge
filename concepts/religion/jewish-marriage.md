# Jewish marriage

## summary

Jewish marriage (*kiddushin*) is a covenant between two partners sanctified by Jewish law and ritual. The groom gives the bride an object of value (traditionally a ring) while making a declaration of *kiddushin*, formally acquiring her as a wife according to Jewish law. The ceremony includes the bride's acceptance, the reading and signing of a *ketubah* (marriage contract), and the ritualistic breaking of a glass.

## you gotta know

- The core ritual is *kiddushin* (literally "sanctification"), enacted when the groom presents an object of value to the bride while declaring "Behold, you are consecrated to me."
- The *ketubah* (marriage contract) is signed by two witnesses and read aloud before the couple, spelling out each partner's rights and responsibilities.
- The bride circles the groom (or both circle each other in modern egalitarian ceremonies) under the *chuppah* (wedding canopy), symbolizing the creation of a new home.
- Seven blessings called *sheva brachot* (seven blessings) are recited during or after the main ceremony, blessing the couple and invoking divine presence.
- The groom breaks a glass at the ceremony's conclusion, traditionally interpreted as remembering the destruction of the Temple amidst joy, or symbolizing the fragility of human relationships.
- Two valid witnesses must be present throughout the *kiddushin* and *ketubah* signing; in Orthodox practice, they must be observant Jewish men.
- The ceremony is typically held in a public setting, affirming the marital bond before the community.
- A festive meal (*seudah*) follows, and the couple traditionally begins seven days of celebration during which *sheva brachot* are repeated.
