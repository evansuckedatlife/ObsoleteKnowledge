# Vedas

## summary

The *Vedas* are the oldest and most authoritative scriptures of *Hinduism*, composed in Vedic Sanskrit between approximately 1500–500 BCE. They form the foundation of Hindu philosophy, ritual, cosmology, and ethical thought, revered as *shruti* (divine revelation heard by ancient sages).

## you gotta know

- Four *Vedas* exist: the *Rigveda* (hymns of praise), *Yajurveda* (sacrificial formulas), *Samaveda* (chants), and *Atharvaveda* (spells and incantations).
- The *Rigveda*, the oldest, contains approximately 1,200 hymns composed orally and preserved through memorization (*shruti*) before written transmission.
- Each *Veda* is subdivided into four layers: *Samhita* (mantras), *Brahmana* (ritual explanations), *Aranyaka* (forest teachings), and *Upanishads* (philosophical knowledge).
- Central deities include *Indra* (storm god), *Agni* (fire), *Soma* (ritual drink), and *Varuna* (cosmic order); polytheistic but with underlying monistic philosophy.
- The *Vedas* establish concepts of *rita* (cosmic order), *karma* (action and consequence), and *atman* (eternal self), shaping Hindu worldview for millennia.
- Vedic ritual (*yajna*) was performed by *Brahmins* to maintain cosmic order; later Hindu philosophy moved toward inward meditation and knowledge as superior to external ritual.
- Considered *apaurusheya* (without human authorship) in Hindu tradition—divine truths revealed to sages rather than composed by mortals.

## connections

- [[upanishads]] — the philosophical conclusion of the Vedas, shifting focus from ritual to metaphysics.
- [[bhagavad-gita]] — applies Vedic knowledge to ethical dilemmas and spiritual practice.
- [[hinduism]] — the foundational scripture defining Hindu theology and practice.
- [[buddhism]] — early Buddhism both engaged with and critiqued Vedic authority.
