# Torah

## summary

The Torah is the central and most sacred text of Judaism, comprising the first five books of the Hebrew Bible: Genesis, Exodus, Leviticus, Numbers, and Deuteronomy. Also known as the Pentateuch or the Five Books of Moses, it contains the foundational narrative of the Jewish people, from the creation of the world to the death of Moses, as well as the 613 divine commandments (*mitzvot*) that govern Jewish law and ethics.

## you gotta know

- Its name comes from the Hebrew root meaning "to guide" or "to teach," and it is also referred to as the *Pentateuch* (Greek for "five vessels") or the *Written Law*.
- In synagogue services, the Torah is read from a *Sefer Torah*, a scroll hand-written with a quill on kosher parchment by a specially trained scribe (*sofer*).
- The scroll is stored in the *Holy Ark* (Aron Kodesh) within the synagogue, covered in ornamental velvet and silver crowns, and is traditionally pointed to using a pointer (*yad*) during reading to avoid touching the parchment.
- The reading of the Torah is divided into 54 weekly portions (*parashot*), completing the entire cycle each year on the holiday of *Simchat Torah* ("Rejoicing in the Torah").
- Traditional Judaism holds that Moses received both the Written Torah and the *Oral Torah* (interpretive traditions later recorded in the Talmud) from God at Mount Sinai.
- The Documentary Hypothesis, a major school of biblical criticism, suggests that the Torah was compiled from four distinct source texts: the Jahwist (J), Elohist (E), Deuteronomist (D), and Priestly (P) sources.
