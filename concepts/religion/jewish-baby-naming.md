## summary

Jewish baby naming refers to the ceremonies that formally welcome a newborn into the Jewish community and assign a Hebrew name. Traditionally, boys receive their names at the *brit milah* (circumcision) on the eighth day, while girls are welcomed through *simchat bat* (daughter's joy) ceremonies held shortly after birth.

## you gotta know

- A Hebrew name is essential in Jewish identity and is used for religious purposes, including aliyahs to the Torah and in ketubah documents.
- Boys typically receive their names at the *brit milah* (circumcision ceremony), while girls are welcomed through *simchat bat* ceremonies.
- *Simchat bat* ceremonies vary widely: some families hold them in synagogue during the Torah service, others organize private home celebrations called *zeved habat*.
- The Hebrew name often honors a deceased relative (in Ashkenazi tradition) or reflects the family's hopes and connection to Jewish heritage.
- In traditional practice, a child is not called by their Hebrew name until formally named in the ceremony, emphasizing the ritual's significance.
- The naming is typically announced during synagogue services, giving the broader community knowledge of the child's Jewish identity.
- Modern Jewish movements offer diverse approaches: Reform temples may hold formal naming ceremonies for any baby, while Orthodox practice follows more gender-distinct traditions.
- The festive meal (*seudah*) following the naming ceremony celebrates the child's welcome into the covenant community.
