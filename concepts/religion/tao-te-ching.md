## summary

The *Tao Te Ching* (or *Daodejing*) is a foundational text of *Taoism*, attributed to the sage *Laozi* and composed around the 4th–6th century BCE. This short, poetic work describes the *Tao* (the way), the underlying principle of all existence, and how to live in harmony with it.

## you gotta know

- Just 81 brief chapters (around 5,000 words total), making it one of the most concise major philosophical texts.
- Attributed to *Laozi* (literally "old master"), though his historical existence remains debated; some scholars view him as legendary.
- Written in Classical Chinese aphorisms and paradoxes that resist single interpretation; countless commentaries exist.
- Core concept: the *Tao* is ineffable, beyond language and logic; those who speak of it do not know it, and those who know it do not speak.
- Teaches *wu wei* ("non-action" or "action without force"), flowing with natural patterns rather than imposing will.
- Emphasizes feminine imagery (valley, water, emptiness) and critiques rigid Confucian morality and social hierarchy.
- Profoundly influenced Chinese philosophy, art, martial arts, and medicine; also shaped Zen Buddhism in East Asia.
- Famous opening: "The Tao that can be spoken of is not the eternal Tao."
