# Purim

## summary

Purim, celebrated on the 14th of Adar (February or March), commemorates the *deliverance of Jews from persecution* in ancient Persia. The holiday is based on the Book of Esther and celebrates the *thwarting of Haman's plot* to exterminate the Jewish population. It is the most festive and lighthearted of Jewish holidays, marked by costumes, feasting, and playful reversals of social norms.

## you gotta know

- The *Megillah* (Book of Esther) is read aloud twice — once in the evening and once in the morning — with the congregation booing and drowning out Haman's name each time it is mentioned.
- *Costumes and masks* are worn, making Purim visually distinctive and playful; the reversal of identities reflects Esther's hidden identity as a Jew.
- *Hamantaschen* — triangular pastries filled with poppy seeds or jam — are traditional treats, with their three-cornered shape said to represent Haman's hat.
- The *four mitzvot* (obligations) include sending gifts of food to friends, giving gifts to the poor, feasting, and reading the Megillah.
- Unlike most Jewish holidays, Purim involves alcohol and intoxication as part of the celebration, seen as a way to express joy and reverse the usual order of things.
- The story centers on *Queen Esther*, a Jewish woman who concealed her identity and became queen of Persia, then risked her life to save her people.
- Purim is observed even on different dates in different communities; in Jerusalem it is observed on the 15th of Adar (Shushan Purim).
- The holiday emphasizes *hashgacha* (divine providence) — God's presence in the story, though God's name is never mentioned in the Book of Esther.
