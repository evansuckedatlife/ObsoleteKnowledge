# Tisha b'Av

## summary

Tisha b'Av, the *Ninth of Av*, is a day of mourning and fasting observed in July or August. It commemorates *the destruction of both Jewish Temples* — the First Temple by the Babylonians in 586 BCE and the Second Temple by the Romans in 70 CE — as well as other calamities in Jewish history. The day marks the lowest point in the Jewish calendar's cycle of commemoration.

## you gotta know

- A *25-hour fast* parallels Yom Kippur in duration, though Tisha b'Av is observed as a day of mourning rather than atonement.
- The *Book of Lamentations* is read during evening services, using a mournful melody that emphasizes the sorrow and loss commemorated by the day.
- *Sitting on the floor or low stools* and wearing *black or dark clothing* are traditional signs of mourning observed during the holiday.
- The day is considered inauspicious in Jewish tradition; major calamities, including the Bar Kokhba revolt's final defeat and the expulsion from Spain, are traditionally dated to this day.
- Unlike other fast days, work and study of Torah (except mourning-related texts) are prohibited on Tisha b'Av, emphasizing its gravity.
- The *Kaddish* prayer is recited in a special form, and *kinot* (elegies) specific to the day are chanted during services.
- The Temple's destruction ended the sacrificial system and transformed Jewish practice toward prayer and study rather than Temple-centered worship.
- Paradoxically, Jewish tradition teaches that the Messiah will be born on Tisha b'Av, making the day one of potential future redemption despite present sorrow.
