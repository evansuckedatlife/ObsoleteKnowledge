# Jewish conversion

## summary

Jewish conversion (*giyur*) is the formal process by which a non-Jew becomes a member of the Jewish people, adopting Jewish faith, law, and identity. The conversion process requires study of Jewish law and practice, acceptance of the *mitzvot* (commandments), and ritual acts including immersion in a *mikveh* (ritual bath). Conversion is recognized differently across Jewish denominations, with Orthodox, Conservative, and Reform each establishing their own standards and procedures.

## you gotta know

- A *beit din* (rabbinic court) oversees the conversion process, assessing the sincere motivation and commitment of the convert.
- The convert studies Jewish law, history, theology, and practice with a rabbi or designated instructor, typically over months or years depending on depth and thoroughness.
- The convert must demonstrate commitment to observing *halakha* (Jewish law), though what constitutes acceptable observance varies across denominations.
- Ritual immersion in a *mikveh* (ritual bath) is performed with witnesses, marking the spiritual transformation and entry into the Jewish people.
- For male converts, *brit milah* (circumcision) or *hatafat dam brit* (ritual drawing of a drop of blood) is performed as part of the conversion.
- The convert receives a Hebrew name, formally establishing Jewish identity and used in all subsequent Jewish religious contexts.
- After conversion, the individual is considered fully Jewish for all purposes; their status as a Jew is equal to that of a born Jew.
- Conversion standards and recognitions differ: Orthodox conversion is not automatically recognized by Reform or Conservative movements, and vice versa, creating practical complications for some converts.
