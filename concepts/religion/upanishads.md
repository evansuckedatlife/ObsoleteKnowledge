# Upanishads

## summary

The *Upanishads* are a collection of philosophical texts forming the foundation of *Advaita Vedanta* and Hindu metaphysics, composed between roughly 1500–500 BCE. They explore the nature of *Brahman* (ultimate reality), the *Atman* (self), and the path to spiritual liberation (*moksha*).

## you gotta know

- The term *Upanishad* derives from Sanskrit roots meaning "to sit down near," reflecting the tradition of a teacher imparting secret knowledge to disciples.
- There are over 200 texts called *Upanishads*, but 13–14 are considered the principal ones (*Mukhya Upanishads*), including the *Isha*, *Kena*, *Katha*, and *Chandogya*.
- Written as philosophical dialogues and mystical teachings appended to or independent of the *Vedas*, they represent a shift from ritual to metaphysical inquiry.
- Central doctrine: *Brahman* (absolute consciousness) and *Atman* (individual self) are one and identical; the illusion of separateness (*maya*) is what binds consciousness to the cycle of rebirth (*samsara*).
- The *Upanishads* introduce concepts like *yoga*, *meditation*, *prana* (life force), and the chakras, foundational to Hindu spirituality and later *Tantric* traditions.
- *Advaita Vedanta* non-dualism (especially as developed by *Adi Shankara*) draws heavily from *Upanishadic* teaching, making them central to Hindu philosophy.
- Influenced later *Buddhism*, *Taoism*, and Western philosophy; *Schopenhauer* and *Aldous Huxley* drew on them.

## connections

- [[vedas]] — the earlier ritual texts; *Upanishads* form their philosophical conclusion (*Vedanta*).
- [[bhagavad-gita]] — presents *Upanishadic* ideas in narrative form for practical application.
- [[hinduism]] — foundational to Hindu metaphysics, yoga, and the quest for *moksha*.
- [[buddhism]] — influenced early Buddhist philosophy, particularly meditation and self-inquiry.
