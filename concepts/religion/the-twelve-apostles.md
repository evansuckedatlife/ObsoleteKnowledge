# The Twelve Apostles

<!-- /foundations -->

## summary

The Twelve Apostles were the disciples chosen by Jesus to be his closest followers and, after his resurrection, the leaders and missionaries of the early Church. Their mission to spread the gospel to all nations forms the narrative core of the Acts of the Apostles and early Christian literature. These twelve figures — drawn from diverse backgrounds, occupations, and personalities — became the foundation of Christian authority and tradition.

## you gotta know

- Jesus selected *twelve apostles* from among his broader circle of followers, symbolically evoking the twelve tribes of Israel and signifying that he was reconstituting God's covenant people.
- The apostles included **Peter** (Simon Peter), to whom Jesus promised leadership; **John** (the Beloved Disciple); **James** (John's brother); **Andrew** (Peter's brother); and eight others, including Thomas, Philip, Bartholomew, Matthew, James the Less, Judas the son of James, Simon the Zealot, and *Judas Iscariot*.
- *Judas Iscariot* betrayed Jesus to the Jewish authorities for thirty pieces of silver, leading to Jesus's arrest and crucifixion; he is often depicted as the arch-traitor in Christian art and theology.
- After Jesus's resurrection, the apostles were commissioned to preach the gospel to all nations and baptize believers, becoming the authoritative teachers and leaders of the early Church.
- **Peter** became the leader of the Jerusalem church and was recognized as the *rock* upon which Jesus said he would build his church, a claim that later became the basis for papal authority in Roman Catholicism.
- **John** wrote the Gospel of John, the three epistles of John, and (in Christian tradition) the Revelation to John, making him one of the most prolific authors in the New Testament.
