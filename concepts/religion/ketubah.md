# Ketubah

## summary

The Ketubah is a Jewish marriage contract signed before the wedding ceremony that outlines the groom's financial and personal obligations to his wife. Composed in Aramaic (though sometimes with Hebrew elements) and traditionally read aloud at the ceremony, the Ketubah serves as a legal and binding document that protects the bride's rights and financial security. It is one of the oldest continuous legal documents in Western civilization, with versions dating back over two thousand years.

## you gotta know

- The Ketubah is signed by two witnesses and the groom, and historically obligated the groom to provide his wife with food, clothing, conjugal relations, and a specified financial settlement (*ketubah money*) in case of divorce or his death.
- The text of the Ketubah is written in Aramaic, the vernacular language of Jews in the Talmudic period, though it contains legal terminology that has remained essentially unchanged for centuries.
- Before the wedding ceremony, the Ketubah is read aloud or announced, often in Hebrew, so that the couple and congregation understand the groom's obligations and the bride's protections.
- Over time, the Ketubah has evolved to include language reflecting modern values, such as mutual obligations of both partners, equal rights, and stipulations regarding divorce and financial arrangements, though the core function remains unchanged.
- Ketubahs are often artistically decorated with calligraphy, illumination, and symbolic Jewish imagery, making them valued as both legal documents and works of Jewish art.
- The signing of the Ketubah is one of the essential rituals that validates a Jewish marriage according to halakha (Jewish law).
