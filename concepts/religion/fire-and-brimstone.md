## summary

This phrase describes divine destruction and damnation, rooted in the destruction of Sodom and Gomorrah with fire and sulfur (brimstone). It evokes punishment from God, often used to warn of hell or catastrophic judgment, and carries intense moral severity.

## you gotta know

- The phrase comes from *Genesis 19*, where God destroys Sodom and Gomorrah because of their wickedness, raining fire and brimstone (burning sulfur) upon them.
- Brimstone is sulfur, which burns with a distinctive yellow flame and acrid smell—symbolizing both divine punishment and the torments of hell.
- Fire and brimstone imagery reappears throughout scripture, especially in descriptions of hell and the final judgment in *Revelation*.
- Preachers used "fire and brimstone sermons" to terrify congregations into repentance, making it synonymous with stern, apocalyptic religious warnings.
- Modern usage extends beyond religious contexts to mean any catastrophic destruction or harsh consequences—"fire and brimstone consequences" for wrongdoing.
- The phrase emphasizes the finality and severity of judgment, not mercy or redemption.
