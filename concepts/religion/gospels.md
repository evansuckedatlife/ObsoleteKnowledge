# Gospels

## summary

The Gospels are the first four books of the New Testament—Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John—which chronicle the life, ministry, death, and resurrection of Jesus. Combining historical narrative, theological interpretation, and ethical instruction, they serve as the primary source of information about Jesus and form the spiritual and scriptural core of the Christian religion.

## you gotta know

- The word "Gospel" derives from the Old English *godspell*, translating the Greek *euangelion*, meaning "good news."
- The first three books (Matthew, Mark, and Luke) are known as the *Synoptic Gospels* because they share a similar structure, sequence, and wording, contrasting with the more theological and distinct Gospel of John.
- Under the Two-Source Hypothesis, the Gospel of *Mark* is considered the earliest written, serving as a source for Matthew and Luke alongside a hypothesized collection of Jesus's sayings known as the *Q source*.
- The Gospel of *Matthew* (symbolized by a winged man) focuses on presenting Jesus as the Jewish Messiah who fulfills Old Testament prophecies.
- The Gospel of *Luke* (symbolized by a winged ox), traditionally authored by a physician companion of Paul, emphasizes compassion for the poor, women, and outcasts.
- The Gospel of *John* (symbolized by an eagle) opens with a philosophical prologue identifying Jesus as the *Logos* ("Word") made flesh, presenting his ministry through seven symbolic signs and long discourses rather than parables.
