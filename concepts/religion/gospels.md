## summary

The **Gospels** are the first four books of the New Testament—*Matthew*, *Mark*, *Luke*, and *John*—which form the primary historical and theological records of Jesus's life, ministry, death, and resurrection. Written in the decades following Jesus's crucifixion, they blend eyewitness testimony, theological interpretation, and narrative artistry to present Jesus as Messiah and Son of God. The Gospels are the foundation of Christian faith and have been the most intensely studied and debated texts in Western literature. Their four distinct portraits of Jesus—the Jewish Messiah, the suffering servant, the friend of the marginalized, and the divine Word—offer complementary yet sometimes divergent perspectives on the same figure. Together, they form a lens through which billions of Christians have understood their faith and through which scholars continue to reconstruct the historical Jesus.

## you gotta know

### Origins and Definition
- "Gospel" derives from Old English *godspell*, translating Greek *euangelion*, meaning "good news"—the announcement of salvation through Jesus.
- Written primarily in Greek in the late first century CE, decades after Jesus's death, they blend oral tradition, eyewitness memory, theological reflection, and literary artistry.
- All four were considered canonical and authoritative by the second century CE, though numerous other gospels (Thomas, Peter, Mary Magdalene) circulated in early Christian communities.

### The Synoptic Gospels
- *Matthew*, *Mark*, and *Luke* are called the *Synoptic Gospels* (from Greek "seen together") because they share remarkably similar structure, sequence, and sometimes identical wording.
- Under the *Two-Source Hypothesis*, *Mark* is the earliest, composed ca. 70 CE; *Matthew* and *Luke* independently used Mark plus a hypothesized sayings collection called *Q* (Quelle, German for "source").
- *Matthew* emphasizes Jesus as the *Jewish Messiah* fulfilling Old Testament prophecy; *Luke* emphasizes compassion for the poor, women, and Gentiles; both expand on Mark's narrative.

### The Gospel of John
- *John* is theologically distinct: it opens with a philosophical prologue declaring Jesus the *Logos* ("Word") made flesh and presenting him as divine from the outset.
- Where the Synoptics feature parables, John features long theological discourses (e.g., "I am the bread of life," "I am the way, the truth, and the life").
- John structures Jesus's ministry around seven miracles (signs) and three Passover visits to Jerusalem, emphasizing symbolism and mystical meaning over chronological narrative.

### Authorship and Symbolism
- Traditional authorship attributed to apostles (Matthew and John) and apostolic companions (Mark = Peter's companion; Luke = Paul's companion), though scholars debate these attributions.
- In medieval Christian art, the four Gospels are symbolized by winged creatures: *Matthew* (winged man), *Mark* (winged lion), *Luke* (winged ox), *John* (eagle).

## context

The Gospels occupy a unique position: they are simultaneously documents of early Christian belief, theological proclamations, and historical sources for reconstructing first-century Judaism and the life of Jesus. The differences between them are theologically productive, not embarrassing—the church canonized all four rather than harmonizing them into one. The *Jesus Seminar* and other historical-critical approaches attempt to sift the earliest layers of tradition from later theological development, but the Gospels themselves blur this line intentionally. Each evangelist shapes the material to address specific communities' concerns: Matthew to a Jewish-Christian audience, Luke to Gentile-Christian readers, John to a mystically inclined community. In quiz bowl and culture, the Gospels appear as scripture, history, literature, and theology simultaneously—the most influential texts in Western civilization.
