# Second Temple

## summary

The Second Temple Period (c. 516 BCE–70 CE) refers to the era when a reconstructed temple stood on the Temple Mount in Jerusalem following the return of Jewish exiles from Babylon. This period witnessed the development of rabbinic Judaism, the flourishing of Jewish sectarian movements (Pharisees, Sadducees, Essenes), and dramatic encounters with Hellenistic and Roman powers, culminating in the temple's destruction by Roman legions in 70 CE.

## you gotta know

- The Second Temple was constructed after the Babylonian Exile when Cyrus the Great permitted Jewish exiles to return to Judaea under *Zerubbabel* (c. 516 BCE).
- The temple was extensively renovated and expanded by King *Herod the Great* (37–4 BCE), making it one of the grandest religious structures of the ancient Mediterranean world.
- During the Second Temple Period, *Torah* study and rabbinic interpretation displaced temple sacrifices as the center of Jewish religious practice, laying the groundwork for post-temple Rabbinic Judaism.
- Jewish sectarian movements — including the *Pharisees* (those emphasizing oral law and resurrection), the *Sadducees* (priestly conservatives skeptical of resurrection and oral tradition), and the *Essenes* (ascetic communities) — emerged and competed for religious authority.
- The *Maccabean Revolt* (167–160 BCE) was sparked when the Seleucid king Antiochus IV desecrated the temple by dedicating it to Zeus; the Maccabees rededicated it, an event commemorated by *Hanukkah*.
- The temple was destroyed by Roman legions under the future emperor *Titus* in 70 CE during the First Jewish-Roman War, an event mourned annually on *Tisha B'Av*; only the Western Wall remained standing.
