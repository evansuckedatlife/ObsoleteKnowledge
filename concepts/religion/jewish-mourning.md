# Jewish mourning

## summary

Jewish mourning is a structured system of rituals and practices honoring the deceased and supporting the bereaved. The most intense period, *shiva* (seven days), involves remaining at home, avoiding work and normal activities, and reciting *Kaddish*. The mourning extends through *shloshim* (thirty days) and, for parents, through a full year of *avelut* (mourning).

## you gotta know

- *Shiva* (literally "seven") comprises the first seven days of mourning following burial, during which close relatives (*avel/aveilah*) remain at home or in one location.
- During *shiva*, mourners do not bathe, cut hair, shave, wear leather shoes, sit on low stools, and refrain from work, entertainment, and marital relations.
- A *minyan* (quorum of ten) gathers daily in the home or synagogue to recite *Kaddish*, the memorial prayer that affirms God's greatness despite loss.
- *Shloshim* (thirty days) is the next period of mourning, during which restrictions ease; mourners may resume work and normal activities but continue reciting *Kaddish*.
- For parents, *avelut* (full mourning) extends for twelve months, during which *Kaddish* is recited at services, particularly meaningful in Jewish tradition.
- *Yahrzeit* (death anniversary) is observed annually on the Hebrew calendar date of death; mourners light a memorial candle and recite *Kaddish*.
- *Yizkor* (memorial prayers) are recited publicly in synagogue four times yearly (on *Pesach*, *Shavuot*, *Sukkot*, and *Yom Kippur*), allowing the broader community to honor deceased family members.
- The mourning structure provides practical support: friends typically bring meals, and the community's presence acknowledges grief while facilitating healing.
