## summary

Guru Nanak (1469–1539) was the founding guru of *Sikhism*, a monotheistic religion emphasizing equality, community service, and devotion to God. Emerging in Punjab at a time of religious and social division, he created a tradition that bridges Hindu and Islamic elements while asserting its own distinctive spiritual path.

## you gotta know

- Guru Nanak was born in Dera Baba Nanak in what is now Pakistan, during a period of tension between Hindu and Muslim communities in the Punjab region.
- He taught *Ik Onkar* ("There is One God"), emphasizing a formless, transcendent divinity accessible to all people regardless of caste, creed, or gender—revolutionary ideas for his time.
- His core message centered on *Nam Simran* (remembrance of God's name), *Kirat Karna* (honest living), and *Vand Chhakna* (sharing with others), the foundation of Sikh ethical practice.
- He rejected the ritualism, caste hierarchy, and claims of priestly authority in both Hinduism and Islam, advocating direct personal connection to the divine.
- Guru Nanak established the *Langar* (communal kitchen), where all people—regardless of caste, religion, or social status—eat together as equals, embodying his egalitarian principles.
- He compiled the *Guru Granth Sahib*, the sacred scripture of Sikhism, which includes his own hymns and poetry alongside compositions from other saints and spiritual teachers.
- Sikhism preserves his legacy through ten successive *Gurus*, with the final Guru, *Guru Granth Sahib*, being the holy book itself that serves as the eternal guide for the faith.
- His life was devoted to spiritual practice, social reform, and traveling (*udasi*) throughout Asia preaching his message of monotheism and human equality.
