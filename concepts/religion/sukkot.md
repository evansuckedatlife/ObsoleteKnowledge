# Sukkot

## summary

Sukkot, the *Feast of Tabernacles*, is a seven-day festival beginning on the 15th of Tishrei (September or October). It commemorates the *Israelites' wandering in the desert* after the Exodus and celebrates the autumn harvest. The holiday is marked by dwelling in *temporary booths* (sukkot) and using the *four species* of plants in ritual observance.

## you gotta know

- *Sukkot* (booths) are small, temporary structures with roofs made of branches and vegetation, symbolizing the dwellings used during the desert wandering.
- The *lulav and etrog* — a bound bundle of palm, willow, and myrtle branches alongside a citron fruit — are shaken in all directions during morning prayers.
- The holiday blends commemoration of ancient wanderings with a joyful harvest festival, making it one of the most celebratory Jewish holidays.
- *Simchat Torah* (Rejoicing of the Law) occurs at the end of Sukkot and marks the conclusion and immediate restart of Torah reading for the year.
- Living in and eating in the sukkah is meant to inspire gratitude and humility, reminding Jews of reliance on God's protection and provision.
- A secondary observance, *Hoshana Rabbah* (the seventh day), features additional processionals and water libations that developed through rabbinic tradition.
- The four species are said to represent different parts of the Jewish community and the importance of unity.
- Historically, Sukkot was one of the three pilgrimage festivals when Jews would travel to the Temple in Jerusalem.

## connections

- [[passover]] — another pilgrimage festival commemorating the Exodus, with which Sukkot is paired in Jewish memory.
- [[shavuot]] — completes the triad of major pilgrimage festivals.
