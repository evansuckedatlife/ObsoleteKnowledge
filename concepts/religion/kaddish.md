# Kaddish

## summary

The Kaddish is a prayer of sanctification and praise for God recited in Aramaic during Jewish prayer services and at funerals. Most famously known as the Mourner's Kaddish, it is recited by the bereaved for eleven months after a death and on the anniversary of the death (*yahrzeit*), though it does not explicitly mention death or mourning. The prayer affirms faith in God's greatness and goodness while the community responds *Amen*, creating a communal act of faith amid grief.

## you gotta know

- The Kaddish is composed in Aramaic rather than Hebrew, reflecting the language spoken by Jews in the medieval Middle East and diaspora communities.
- It is structured as a responsive prayer: the leader recites the main text, and the congregation responds with *Yehe shlomah rabah min shemayah* ("May there be abundant peace from heaven").
- The prayer praises God, prays for peace, and affirms that God will establish peace and bring redemption, but makes no explicit mention of death or the deceased, a feature that has prompted theological interpretation and reflection.
- The *Mourner's Kaddish* is recited by the closest relatives of the deceased (children, spouses, parents, siblings) standing in the synagogue for eleven months (not the full twelve, following Jewish custom) and then on every anniversary of the death.
- Saying Kaddish for a deceased relative is considered a paramount Jewish obligation and honor, and the presence of a *minyan* (quorum of ten adult male Jews in Orthodox tradition, or ten adults regardless of gender in Conservative and Reform) is required for its recitation.
- The prayer reflects the Jewish emphasis on community, continuity, and the affirmation of faith even in grief, making it central to Jewish funeral and mourning practice.
