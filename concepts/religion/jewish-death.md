# Jewish death and burial


<!-- foundations -->
> [!note]+ Foundations — read these first
> [[judaism]]
<!-- /foundations -->

## summary

Jewish death and burial practices reflect values of dignity, simplicity, and respect for the deceased. The body is prepared by the *chevra kadisha* (holy society—a burial committee), and burial occurs quickly, typically within 24 hours. The practices emphasize the equality of all people in death and rejection of elaborate embalming or display.

## you gotta know

- The body is not left alone between death and burial; *shomer* (watchers) maintain vigil and may recite Psalms or prayers honoring the deceased.
- The *chevra kadisha* (burial society) ritually washes the body (*taharah*), a sacred and solemn act performed with specific prayers and care.
- The deceased is dressed in a simple white shroud (*tachrichin*), emphasizing equality and humble return to the earth, regardless of wealth or status.
- Embalming and viewing of the body are traditionally avoided or discouraged in Jewish practice; cremation is prohibited in Orthodox and Conservative Judaism.
- The *kesones* (professional mourners) or family members may recite *Kaddish* before and after burial, sanctifying God's name.
- The *aron* (wooden coffin) is simple and unadorned, allowing natural decomposition; metal, upholstery, and elaborate decoration are not used.
- Burial occurs in a Jewish cemetery in a grave dug specifically for the individual, symbolizing a final resting place within the Jewish community.
- The funeral typically includes eulogies (*hesped*), the recitation of *Kaddish* by immediate relatives, and the symbolical act of tearing a garment or wearing a *kriah* ribbon.
