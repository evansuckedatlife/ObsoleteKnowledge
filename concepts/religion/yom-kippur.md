# Yom Kippur

## summary

Yom Kippur, the *Day of Atonement*, is the holiest day in the Jewish calendar, falling on the tenth day of Tishrei (September or October). It concludes the *High Holy Days* period begun at Rosh Hashanah. Jews observe it through fasting, prayer, and repentance, seeking forgiveness for sins against God and reconciliation with others.

## you gotta know

- Complete *fasting* (no food or drink) lasts 25 hours, from sunset to nightfall, and is observed even by secular Jews.
- The *Kol Nidrei* prayer, chanted at the start of the evening service, is perhaps the most recognizable Jewish liturgy outside of formal worship.
- Five daily prayer services are held, more than any other Jewish holiday, emphasizing the day's spiritual intensity.
- *White garments* are traditionally worn, symbolizing purity and a break from everyday life.
- Reconciliation with others is considered essential; forgiveness from humans cannot be granted by God alone.
- The holiday originates in biblical law requiring a day of atonement for the nation, performed by the High Priest in the ancient Temple.
- *Yizkor* (memorial prayers for the dead) are recited, making it also a day to remember those who have passed.
- The shofar is sounded at the end of the holiday, marking both the conclusion and the beginning of a spiritually renewed year.
