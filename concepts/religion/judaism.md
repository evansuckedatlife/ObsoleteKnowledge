# Judaism

## summary

**Judaism** is an ancient, monotheistic Abrahamic religion, representing the collective religious, cultural, and legal tradition of the Jewish people across more than three millennia. Traced to the patriarch *Abraham* and crystallized through the covenant with *Moses* at Mount Sinai, it is the oldest surviving monotheistic faith tradition. The religion centers on the *Torah*, the sacred law, and emphasizes study, ethical conduct, and adherence to divine commandments (*mitzvot*) as the path to holiness. Judaism has evolved from temple-based sacrificial worship to rabbinic interpretation and modern denominational expression, yet maintains unbroken continuity with its ancient sources. Its influence on Christianity and Islam, and its emphasis on textual learning and ethical reasoning, makes it foundational to Western and Middle Eastern civilization.

## you gotta know

### Origins and Scripture
- The oldest of the three major *Abrahamic religions*, tracing lineage to patriarch *Abraham*, with whom God established the founding covenant.
- The *Torah* (also called the *Pentateuch*) comprises the first five books of the Hebrew Bible and is believed to have been revealed by God to *Moses* at Mount Sinai.
- Judaism holds the *Tanakh* (Hebrew Bible) as scripture; the *Talmud* is the vast rabbinical text that interprets and applies Torah law to all circumstances.
- The central prayer is the *Shema Yisrael* ("Hear, O Israel: The Lord our God, the Lord is one"), the foundational declaration of absolute monotheism.

### Historical Transformation
- It transitioned from a temple-based sacrificial cult (with the *Temple in Jerusalem* as its center) to a text- and rabbinic-focused tradition after Rome destroyed the Second Temple in 70 CE.
- Diaspora Judaism developed portable, community-centered practices centered on the synagogue and Torah study rather than temple sacrifice.

### Branches and Practice
- Modern Judaism encompasses Orthodox (Torah as immutable divine law), Conservative (law as historically evolving but binding), and Reform (emphasis on ethics and personal autonomy) branches.
- Central practices include keeping *Shabbat* (the Sabbath), observing the 613 *mitzvot*, dietary laws (*kashrut*), and major holidays like *Passover* and *Yom Kippur*.

## context

Judaism's resilience and adaptability—surviving exile, persecution, and diaspora across continents—is central to both its internal theology and its historical significance. Rabbinic Judaism, forged after the Temple's destruction, invented the structures (synagogue, prayer, textual study) that allowed the tradition to survive without a physical center, and this model profoundly influenced Christianity and Islam. The emphasis on *Talmudic debate* itself became spiritually central: argumentation and interpretation are acts of piety, not mere scholarship. In quiz bowl and culture broadly, Judaism appears as the fountainhead of ethical monotheism, the source of the Decalogue and Prophetic tradition, and through its influence on Christianity and Islam, as a shaping force in world history.
