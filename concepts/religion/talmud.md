# Talmud


## summary

The *Talmud* is a vast body of Jewish law, theology, and commentary compiled over centuries, consisting of the *Mishnah* (3rd century CE) and the *Gemara* (5th–6th centuries CE). It serves as the primary source of rabbinic Judaism and shapes Jewish ethics, law, and practice to this day.

## you gotta know

- The *Mishnah* ("repetition") is a concise legal code organized into six orders (*sedarim*) covering ritual, agriculture, family law, damages, holy things, and purity.
- The *Gemara* ("completion") is expansive rabbinic discussion and interpretation of the *Mishnah*, often including debates between named scholars across generations.
- Two versions exist: the *Palestinian Talmud* (compiled in the Land of Israel, c. 400 CE) and the *Babylonian Talmud* (compiled in Babylon, c. 500 CE); the latter is longer and more authoritative in mainstream Judaism.
- Written in a mixture of Hebrew (Mishnah) and Aramaic (*Gemara*), reflecting the linguistic reality of Jewish communities in antiquity.
- Extends and interprets the *Torah*, addressing questions the written law leaves unanswered and applying ancient principles to new circumstances.
- Famous for dialectical style: pages present conflicting opinions, often without final resolution, encouraging ongoing interpretation and debate.
- Encompasses far more than law—includes folklore, science, medicine, ethics, and mystical teachings (*Kabbalah*).
- Traditionally studied by Jewish scholars through memorization and debate (**chavruta*); study itself is considered a form of worship.

## connections

- [[torah]] — the foundation on which *Talmud* builds; the five books of *Moses*.
- [[moses]] — the lawgiver whose Torah the Talmud interprets and applies.
- [[judaism]] — central to Jewish theology, practice, and identity after the Second Temple period.
