# Bar/bat mitzvah

## summary

Bar mitzvah (son of commandment) and bat mitzvah (daughter of commandment) are coming-of-age ceremonies marking a young person's transition to Jewish adulthood and religious responsibility. A bar mitzvah traditionally occurs at age 13 for boys; a bat mitzvah at age 12 for girls (in some traditions, also at 13). The celebrant typically reads from the Torah in front of the community for the first time.

## you gotta know

- A *bar mitzvah* boy becomes religeable and responsible for observing Jewish law (*mitzvot*) at age 13; a *bat mitzvah* girl at age 12 in many traditions, though modern practice often aligns both at 13.
- The celebrant prepares extensively, typically learning to *chant* (cantillate) a portion of the Torah (*parshah*) and sometimes the Haftarah (prophetic reading).
- The first public *aliyah* (call up) to recite the blessings over the Torah reading is the core ritual moment, marking formal entry into adult religious life.
- Traditionally, only boys were called to the Torah for *aliyot*; contemporary denominations affirm girls' equal participation.
- A family celebration (*simchat bar/bat mitzvah*) with a festive meal follows the synagogue service, welcoming the young person into adulthood.
- In Orthodox communities, the celebration may be more modest; in Reform and Conservative traditions, elaborate parties and social celebrations are common.
- The young person often gives a *devar Torah* (speech on a Torah passage), demonstrating their understanding of Jewish texts and values.
- Many families donate to charity or sponsor community projects in honor of the bar/bat mitzvah, reflecting Jewish values of *tzedakah* (righteous giving).
