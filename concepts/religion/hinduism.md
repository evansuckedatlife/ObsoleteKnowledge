# Hinduism

## summary

Hinduism is the world's oldest major world religion, originating in the Indian subcontinent around 1500 BCE and encompassing a diverse array of beliefs, practices, and philosophies united by reverence for the Vedas and concepts of karma, dharma, and moksha (liberation). Home to over one billion adherents, it emphasizes cyclical time, reincarnation, and the ultimate unity of the self (*Atman*) with the supreme cosmic principle (*Brahman*). Hinduism is as much a cultural and social system as a religious one, deeply intertwined with Indian art, music, philosophy, and daily life.

## you gotta know

### Sacred texts and revelation

- The *Vedas* (c. 1500–1200 BCE), composed in Vedic Sanskrit, are Hinduism's oldest and most authoritative scriptures, comprising the *Rigveda*, *Yajurveda*, *Samaveda*, and *Atharvaveda*, containing hymns, rituals, and philosophical speculation.
- The *Upanishads*, philosophical texts appended to the Vedas, develop concepts of *Brahman* (ultimate reality) and *Atman* (individual soul), laying the foundation for Hindu metaphysics and the doctrine of non-duality (*Advaita*).
- The *Bhagavad Gita*, a dialogue between the warrior prince Arjuna and the divine Krishna embedded within the epic *Mahabharata*, addresses duty (*dharma*), renunciation, and the path to liberation, and is revered as the core spiritual manual.
- The *Ramayana* and *Mahabharata*, the two great Sanskrit epics, transmit Hindu theology, ethics, and mythology through narrative and remain central to religious instruction and cultural identity.

### Core concepts and practice

- *Karma* (action) and *dharma* (duty, righteousness) govern the cyclical process of birth, death, and rebirth (*samsara*); individuals' accumulated karma determines their station in future lives.
- *Moksha* (liberation) — the ultimate goal in Hinduism — is the release from the cycle of samsara through knowledge, devotion, or ethical conduct, achieved through different *yogas* (paths): *jnana* (knowledge), *bhakti* (devotion), *karma* (action), and *raja* (meditative discipline).
- The *Trimurti* (trinity) — **Brahma** (creator), **Vishnu** (preserver), and **Shiva** (destroyer and transformer) — represents the cosmic functions, though Hinduism encompasses countless deities as manifestations or aspects of *Brahman*.
- Ritual practices include *puja* (worship), *yajna* (sacrifice), pilgrimage, yoga, and meditation; the religion is organized into schools and philosophical systems (such as *Advaita Vedanta* and *Vaishnavism*) that interpret the texts and path to liberation differently.

### History and cultural influence

- Hinduism has continuously adapted and absorbed influences — from pre-Vedic indigenous traditions to Buddhist and Islamic encounters — while maintaining core philosophical continuity over three millennia.
- The caste system, historically justified through karma and dharma, has structured Hindu society with hereditary occupational and social divisions, though it has been legally abolished in modern India and is increasingly reformed.
- Hindu philosophy profoundly influenced Buddhism (which emerged as a reform movement in 5th-century BCE India), Jainism, and later European idealism and transcendentalism through 19th- and 20th-century encounters with the West.

## context

Hinduism stands as the world's oldest continuously practiced major religion, and its philosophical depth and diversity make it as much a framework for understanding reality as a system of worship. The religion's central metaphysical problem — the relationship between the individual self and ultimate reality — generated millennia of sophisticated theology and epistemology that parallels and preceded Western philosophy. The Vedic hymns and Upanishadic dialogues represent humanity's earliest recorded attempts at systematic metaphysics. Hinduism's ethical and social teachings, encoded in the epics and dharma-sutras, shaped Indian civilization's approach to law, governance, and interpersonal duty. The religion's emphasis on multiple paths to liberation (*yoga*) and its recognition of diverse valid perspectives (*many paths, one summit*) offer a distinctive pluralism rarely seen in world religions. For students of philosophy, history, and comparative religion, Hinduism is indispensable.

- [[brahman]] — the ultimate, transcendent reality at the heart of Hindu metaphysics.
- [[karma-and-dharma]] — the interlocking doctrines of action and duty that govern the cycle of rebirth.
- [[ramayana]] — one of the two great epics, recounting the life of the avatar Rama.

- [[trimurti]] — the trinity of Brahma, Vishnu, and Shiva representing cosmic functions.
- [[yoga]] — the philosophical and physical discipline for attaining liberation and spiritual union.
