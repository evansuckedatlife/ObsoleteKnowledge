# Rosh Hashanah

## summary

Rosh Hashanah marks the *Jewish New Year* and falls on the first and second days of Tishrei (September or October). It opens the *High Holy Days*, a ten-day period of introspection and repentance that culminates in Yom Kippur. The holiday commemorates God's creation of the world and is celebrated as a day of judgment when one's actions of the past year are weighed.

## you gotta know

- The *shofar* (ram's horn) is blown throughout the holiday to call people to spiritual awakening and repentance.
- *Apples dipped in honey* are eaten as a symbol of hope for a sweet new year.
- The *talit* (prayer shawl) is worn during morning prayers as a sign of respect and solemnity.
- It lasts two days, making it one of the longer Jewish holidays in observance.
- The New Year date itself (1 Tishrei) is not mentioned explicitly in the Torah; the tradition developed through rabbinic interpretation.
- Congregational prayers include the *Unetaneh Tokef*, a powerful liturgical poem about divine judgment.
- Traditional greeting is "L'shana tova" — "for a good year" — to wish others well in the coming year.
- The holiday is deeply connected to the concept of *teshuvah* (repentance and return), beginning a season of self-examination.
