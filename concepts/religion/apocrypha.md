## summary

The *Apocrypha* is a collection of religious texts written between the Hebrew Bible and the New Testament, primarily during the Hellenistic period (roughly 300–100 BCE). Considered *deuterocanonical* (second-canon) by Catholic and Orthodox Christianity, they are excluded from Protestant Bibles.

## you gotta know

- Includes books like *Maccabees*, *Tobit*, *Judith*, *Wisdom of Solomon*, and *Ecclesiasticus* (Ben Sira).
- The term "apocrypha" derives from Greek *apokryphos*, meaning "hidden" or "obscure," reflecting their disputed authority.
- Written primarily in Greek and Hebrew during a period when the Jewish canon was not yet fully fixed.
- Many texts address themes of martyrdom, divine wisdom, and resistance to foreign rule (especially Seleucid oppression).
- Catholic and Orthodox churches include them in their biblical canon; Jewish and Protestant traditions exclude them.
- The *Maccabees* recounts the Jewish revolt against Seleucid rule and the rededication of the Jerusalem Temple (basis for Hanukkah).
- Never considered lost or hidden in intent—early Christian writers knew and quoted them as sacred literature.

## connections

- [[torah]] — the Hebrew scriptures that form the foundation of Jewish tradition these texts support.
- [[judaism]] — crucial to understanding intertestamental Jewish thought and history.
- [[christianity]] — central to Catholic and Orthodox biblical tradition; debated in Protestant theology.
