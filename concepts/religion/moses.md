# Moses

## summary

**Moses** is the greatest prophet and lawgiver in Hebrew scripture and Jewish tradition, credited with liberating the Israelites from slavery in Egypt and mediating the divine covenant at Mount Sinai. Born as the son of Hebrew slaves but raised as an Egyptian prince, he became the liberator of his people and the foundational figure of Judaism. His leadership through forty years of wilderness wandering, his reception of the *Ten Commandments*, and his authorship of the *Torah* established the laws and spiritual identity of the Jewish nation. Moses appears across Jewish, Christian, and Islamic traditions as a towering exemplar of prophecy, faithfulness, and lawgiving. His life narrative—from vulnerability to power, from doubt to certainty, from liberator to lawgiver—has made him a prototype for redemptive leadership in Western culture.

## you gotta know

### Birth and Flight
- Born to Hebrew slave parents; his mother placed him in a basket on the Nile River to escape Pharaoh's decree that all Hebrew male infants be killed.
- Adopted and raised as an Egyptian prince by Pharaoh's daughter, he grew up with access to Egyptian royal education and power.
- At age forty, he killed an Egyptian taskmaster who was beating a Hebrew slave; fearing discovery, he fled to the wilderness of Midian.

### The Call and Plagues
- In Midian, God spoke to him from a *burning bush*, revealing the divine name *YHWH* and commanding him to return to Egypt and liberate the Israelites.
- He led the people through the ten *plagues of Egypt*, each a divine judgment against Egyptian gods and Pharaoh's resistance.
- The tenth plague, the *Passover*, killed the firstborn of Egypt while sparing Hebrew homes marked with blood on their doorposts.

### The Exodus and Law
- He parted the *Red Sea*, allowing the Israelites to escape across on dry land while Pharaoh's army pursuing them was swallowed by the returning waters.
- He received the *Ten Commandments* written on two stone tablets by the divine finger at the summit of Mount Sinai, written in the presence of thunder and fire.
- He spent forty years wandering with the Israelites in the desert, mediating between God's will and the people's complaints, delivering the laws of the *Torah*.

### Legacy and Death
- He did not enter the Promised Land himself, but was permitted to see it from Mount Nebo before his death at the age of 120 in perfect health.
- Jewish tradition holds that he wrote the Torah under divine inspiration, making him not merely a political leader but a scribe of God's word.

## context

Moses stands at the intersection of history, theology, and legend. The historical Moses (if one existed) was almost certainly an Egyptian-name Hebrew who lived during Egypt's New Kingdom, but Jewish, Christian, and Islamic traditions have transfigured him into a transcendent figure. His role as mediator between God and the people defined the prophetic tradition: the prophet is not a mystic but a speaker of God's law, a bearer of obligation and covenant. The *Ten Commandments* attributed to him became the foundation of Western legal codes; the *Exodus* narrative became the archetype of liberation in political and spiritual thought. In quiz bowl, Moses appears as prophet, lawgiver, writer, liberator, and mediator—each role resonating across religious and secular culture.
