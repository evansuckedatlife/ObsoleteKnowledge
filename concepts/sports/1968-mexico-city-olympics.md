# 1968 Summer Olympics

## summary

The *1968 Summer Olympics* in Mexico City became a watershed moment when African American sprinters *Tommie Smith* and *John Carlos* raised their fists in a Black Power salute during the medal ceremony, transforming the Games into a platform for civil rights protest and forcing the world to reckon with politics in sport.

## you gotta know

- Held in Mexico City at high altitude (2,250m), which enhanced performance in sprint events but challenged endurance athletes.
- *Tommie Smith* won gold in the 200m and *John Carlos* won bronze; both raised their black-gloved fists and bowed their heads during the national anthem—a gesture in solidarity with the Black Power movement.
- The salute provoked international controversy; the International Olympic Committee suspended both athletes and banned them from the Olympic village, framing their protest as unsportsmanlike.
- *Wyomia Tyus*, an African American woman who won gold in the 100m, also protested by turning her back during the anthem, but received less media attention.
- The Games highlighted the Olympic principle of political neutrality, which activists challenged as complicit with racism and inequality.
- Mexican student protests demanding democratic reforms occurred before the Games; the government's violent response (the Tlatelolco massacre) was largely overshadowed in international coverage by the Games themselves.
- The 1968 Olympics mark a turning point where the Games became understood as inevitably political—a platform where athletes could no longer remain silent on injustice.
