# French Open

## summary

The French Open, officially known as Roland-Garros, is one of tennis's four Grand Slam tournaments, held annually in May at the Stade Roland-Garros in Paris. Played on red clay courts, it is the second Grand Slam of the calendar year and is renowned as the most physically demanding major, where clay-court specialists and tactical players have historically flourished.

## you gotta know

- Held in May at the Stade Roland-Garros in Paris, played on red clay courts that significantly slow the ball and extend rallies.
- Named after aviator Roland-Garros, a French fighter pilot from World War I, after the facility was renamed in 1926.
- Rafael Nadal has won the tournament a record-tying 14 times, establishing himself as the greatest clay-court player of all time.
- The red clay surface heavily favors players with strong baseliners and exceptional footwork, creating a different champion profile than grass or hard courts.
- Chris Evert won seven French Open singles titles and was known as the "Queen of Clay," dominating the tournament in the 1970s and 1980s.
- The tournament was first held in 1891 as a French national championship and opened to international competitors starting in 1925.
- Roger Federer won the French Open once in 2009, his only clay-court Grand Slam, completing a career Grand Slam.
- The tournament is associated with slower matches and longer rallies than other majors, testing endurance and tactical tennis rather than pure power.

## connections
