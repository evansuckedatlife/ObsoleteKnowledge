# 1984 Summer Olympics

## summary

The *1984 Summer Olympics* in Los Angeles were boycotted by the Soviet Union and its allies in retaliation for the U.S.-led boycott of the 1980 Moscow Games. The event showcased American Olympic dominance by default and became the first Olympic Games to be profitable, setting a new model for Olympic economics and corporate sponsorship.

## you gotta know

- Held in Los Angeles; the Soviet Union, East Germany, and 13 other Eastern Bloc nations boycotted to retaliate for the 1980 Moscow boycott, mirroring Cold War symmetry.
- The U.S. and its Western allies dominated the medal count; American athletes won 83 gold medals—the most by any nation—making the Games feel like an American showcase.
- The Games were privately financed and corporately sponsored, breaking from the pattern of massive government spending; this financial model became a template for future Olympic host cities.
- Gymnast *Mary Lou Retton* became a media sensation, winning five medals and captivating American audiences; she personified the athletic excellence the Games celebrated.
- The Games operated efficiently and profitably, turning a surplus—a rarity for Olympic hosts—and demonstrated that Olympic economics could work in a market-driven approach.
- The absence of the Soviet Union meant that the most formidable athletic competition was missing, particularly in weightlifting, gymnastics, and distance running where Eastern Bloc dominance was uncontested.
- The 1984 Olympics are remembered as a commercial and patriotic success, though the absence of major rivals reduced the sporting significance of many events.
