# 1980 Winter Olympics

## summary

The *1980 Winter Olympics* in Lake Placid, New York, are remembered for the "Miracle on Ice"—when the U.S. men's ice hockey team, composed of amateurs and young professionals, defeated the heavily favored Soviet team during the Cold War. This single upset became an iconic moment in American sports history and provided a rare triumphant counter-narrative to the summer Games boycott occurring the same year.

## you gotta know

- Held in Lake Placid, New York; the Winter Games ran February 13–24, 1980, occurring just weeks before the summer Games boycott was announced.
- The U.S. hockey team, coached by *Herb Brooks* and led by captain *Mike Eruzione*, was composed largely of college players—the Soviets were seasoned veterans representing a world superpower in the sport.
- On February 22, 1980, the U.S. defeated the Soviet team 4–3 in what commentator Al Michaels called the "Miracle on Ice"—a stunning upset.
- The U.S. went on to win the gold medal by defeating Finland in the final, a result that seemed impossible before the Games began.
- The victory became a symbol of Cold War triumph for Americans and provided a morale boost during a period of national anxiety (Iran hostage crisis, Afghanistan invasion).
- Figure skater *Eric Heiden* won five gold medals in speed skating—an extraordinary individual achievement that was overshadowed by the hockey story.
- The victory remains the most famous moment in Winter Olympic history and illustrates how Olympic upsets can transcend sport to become national narratives.
