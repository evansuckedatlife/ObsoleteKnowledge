## summary

Abraham Lincoln (1809–1865) was the 16th President of the United States and the first Republican to hold the office. He led the nation through the American Civil War, preserving the Union and ending slavery, but was assassinated just days after the war's end. His leadership during America's greatest constitutional, military, and moral crisis made him a pivotal figure in establishing the United States as a democratic, multi-racial republic.

## you gotta know

### life & rise

- Born in rural Kentucky in a one-room log cabin; largely self-educated; worked as a laborer, surveyor, and lawyer before entering politics.
- Elected to the Illinois state legislature in 1834; practiced law in Springfield; represented Illinois in Congress (1847–1849) and ran unsuccessfully for Senate in 1858.
- The 1858 Senate debates with Stephen Douglas—"A house divided against itself cannot stand"—brought him national prominence, despite his loss.
- Elected president in 1860 on a platform opposing the expansion of slavery into new territories (not abolishing it where it existed, though abolitionists supported him).

### the civil war

- Southern states seceded in response to his election; he took office in March 1861 determined to preserve the Union, not destroy slavery—but the war changed the stakes.
- Issued the *Preliminary Emancipation Proclamation* (September 1862) and the *Emancipation Proclamation* (January 1863), freeing slaves in rebel states; this shifted the war from a union-preservation effort to a moral crusade.
- Delivered the *Gettysburg Address* (November 1863)—a 272-word speech reframing the war as a test of whether democracy itself could survive—which became the defining statement of the American cause.
- Re-elected in 1864 on a war platform; pushed Congress to pass the *13th Amendment* (abolishing slavery nationwide) in January 1865, five months before his death.
- Took office for his second term with the message "malice toward none, with charity for all," signaling a reconciliatory post-war approach.

### death & legacy

- Assassinated on April 14, 1865, by John Wilkes Booth, a Confederate sympathizer, just five days after Lee's surrender at Appomattox.
- Never lived to shape Reconstruction; his absence left a vacuum filled by harsher Republican policies and later Southern white backlash (lost progress persists for a century).
- Canonized in American memory as a symbol of democratic constancy, moral clarity under pressure, and martyrdom for the Union.

## context

Lincoln's assassination and the void it left are inseparable from his towering legacy. He entered office a minority president facing an existential choice between preserving the Union or abandoning it. By the time he died, he had reframed the war as a fight not just for territorial integrity but for human freedom and the viability of democratic self-government. His *Emancipation Proclamation* and the *13th Amendment* were not spontaneous moral awakenings—they were reluctant, hard-won choices driven by military necessity and political pressure from abolitionists—but they proved decisive. His *Gettysburg Address* distilled the war's meaning into prose that has echoed through American moral life ever since: the nation was "dedicated to the proposition that all men are created equal," and the war was testing whether such a nation could endure. His assassination removed the one figure who might have tempered Reconstruction and built a durable interracial republic; instead, the era that followed saw the gains of the Civil War gradually eroded.
