# Formosa → Taiwan

## summary

*Formosa* ("beautiful island" in Portuguese) was the name European traders gave to the island off the southeast coast of mainland China, beginning with Portuguese contact in the 16th century. The indigenous name *Taiwan* (meaning "terraced bay" in Austronesian languages) gradually displaced the colonial term, especially after the island became the seat of the *Republic of China* in 1949 following the Chinese Civil War. "Taiwan" is now the standard English name, reflecting both indigenous heritage and the modern nation's distinct political identity from mainland China.

## you gotta know

- Portuguese explorers named the island *Formosa* ("beautiful island") in the 16th century, and the term became standard in European trade and cartography.
- Taiwan was inhabited by indigenous Austronesian peoples long before European contact; the name *Taiwan* derives from their languages.
- The Dutch and Spanish colonized parts of the island in the 17th century, followed by the *Qing Dynasty* (1683–1895) when mainland Chinese control extended over it.
- Japan occupied Taiwan for 50 years (1895–1945) after defeating China in the First Sino-Japanese War, developing its infrastructure and agriculture.
- After Japan's defeat in 1945, the *Republic of China* government (the Nationalists/Kuomintang) fled the mainland and established their capital on Taiwan following the 1949 Communist victory.
- The name "Taiwan" became standard internationally in the late 20th century as the island's political identity diverged from mainland China.
