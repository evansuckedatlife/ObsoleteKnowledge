# Frederick Douglass

## summary

*Frederick Douglass* was a formerly enslaved person who became one of the most influential orators and intellectuals of the 19th century, whose powerful speeches and autobiographies exposed the moral bankruptcy of slavery and made him a leading voice in the abolitionist movement. His evolution from escaped slave to statesman and his cogent arguments for human equality made him a foundational figure in American civil rights discourse.

## you gotta know

- Escaped slavery in 1838 after years of brutalization in Maryland; his flight was aided by a free Black woman, Anna Murray, whom he married.
- Delivered the *"What to the Slave is the Fourth of July?"* speech in 1852, a searing indictment of American hypocrisy on freedom and slavery.
- Published three autobiographies (1845, 1855, 1881) that rank among the most powerful American narratives, recounting his enslavement, escape, and intellectual awakening.
- Became a leading *abolitionist* speaker, touring the United States and Europe and drawing crowds with his eloquence and moral authority.
- Held positions in the federal government under Presidents Lincoln, Johnson, and Garfield, including ambassador to Haiti — the highest offices held by a Black American in the 19th century.
- Advocated not only for ending slavery but for full political and civil equality, including women's suffrage (he spoke at the Seneca Falls Convention in 1848).

## connections

- [[abraham-lincoln]] — Douglass was an advisor and critic; he pushed Lincoln toward emancipation.
