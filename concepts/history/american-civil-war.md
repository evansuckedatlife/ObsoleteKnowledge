# American Civil War

## summary

The American Civil War (1861–1865) was the deadliest conflict in U.S. history, fought between the Union (Northern states) and the Confederacy (eleven Southern states that seceded over slavery and states' rights). Sparked by the election of *Abraham Lincoln*, the war cost approximately 620,000 lives and devastated the South's economy and infrastructure. Though the Union ultimately prevailed, the war's legacy—the abolition of slavery, the principle that states cannot secede, and the massive expansion of federal power—transformed the nation's constitutional structure and left deep scars on race relations that persist today.

## you gotta know

### origins and early campaigns

- The election of *Abraham Lincoln* (1860) on a platform of preventing slavery's expansion into new territories prompted eleven Southern states to secede and form the *Confederate States of America* under *Jefferson Davis*.
- The war began at *Fort Sumter* (April 1861) when Confederate forces fired on a federal garrison; *First Bull Run* (July 1861) shattered Northern hopes for a quick victory.
- The North, despite early defeats and incompetent generals, had decisive advantages: a larger population, industrial capacity, and control of the seas; the South had superior generalship but was economically isolated.

### pivotal moments and turning points

- *Antietam* (September 1862), near Sharpsburg, Maryland, was the bloodiest single day in American military history (23,000 casualties); though tactically inconclusive, it halted General *Robert E. Lee*'s first invasion of the North.
- The *Emancipation Proclamation* (September 1862, effective January 1, 1863) declared slaves in rebel states free, transforming the war from a battle for Union into a crusade against slavery and preventing European intervention on behalf of the South.
- *Gettysburg* (July 1863), fought in Pennsylvania, became the turning point: Union General *George Meade* defeated Lee's second invasion, after which the Confederacy was always on the defensive.

### total war and victory

- General *Ulysses S. Grant*, made supreme Union commander in 1864, pursued a strategy of attrition and total war, coordinating simultaneous offensives and devastating Confederate resources.
- *Sherman's March to the Sea* (1864–1865) saw Union General *William Tecumseh Sherman* burn and pillage Georgia and the Carolinas, breaking the South's will to fight.
- *Appomattox Court House* (April 1865) saw *Robert E. Lee* surrender to Grant; within weeks, the other Confederate armies surrendered, ending the war.

### aftermath and legacy

- The *13th Amendment* (1865) abolished slavery throughout the United States; the *14th Amendment* (1868) granted citizenship to all persons born in the U.S.; the *15th Amendment* (1870) forbade racial discrimination in voting.
- *Reconstruction* (1865–1877) attempted to rebuild the South and integrate formerly enslaved people as citizens, though white Southern resistance, the rise of the *Ku Klux Klan*, and Northern political weakness eventually reversed many gains.

## context

The Civil War was a test of whether a democratic republic could survive internal dissolution and whether a nation truly founded on the proposition that "all men are created equal" could abolish slavery. The war's scale—over 600,000 dead, more casualties than in any other American conflict—made it existential. The Union's victory preserved the nation and ended slavery, but it was purchased at an enormous cost. Reconstruction's failure left a century-long legacy of racial injustice, segregation, and political disenfranchisement in the South. For quiz bowl, the Civil War is the hinge of American history: before it, a federal union of semi-sovereign states; after it, a nation with concentrated federal power and the end of the institution that had defined the South's economy and culture.
