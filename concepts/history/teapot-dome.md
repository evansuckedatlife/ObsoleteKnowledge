# Teapot Dome

## summary

*Teapot Dome* is the scandal that emerged from secret leases of federal oil reserves during President *Warren G. Harding*'s administration. *Secretary of the Interior* *Albert B. Fall* granted drilling rights to private oil executives in exchange for bribes, becoming the first Cabinet member imprisoned for crimes committed in office.

## you gotta know

- In *1921*, President *Harding* secretly transferred control of naval oil reserves at *Teapot Dome, Wyoming* and *Elk Hills, California* from the Navy Department to the Interior Department.
- *Secretary of Interior* *Albert B. Fall* leased the reserves to oil magnates *Harry Sinclair* (*Mammoth Oil*) and *Edward Doheny* (*Pan American Petroleum*) without competitive bidding.
- *Fall* received bribes including a ranch loan of *$404,000*, livestock, and improvements to his property—he became visibly wealthy shortly after the secret deals.
- The scheme was exposed by *Senator Thomas J. Walsh* during 1923–24 congressional hearings; *Fall* had already left office by then.
- *Fall* was convicted of bribery and became the first U.S. Cabinet member sent to federal prison (serving nine months in *1929–1930*).
- The oil leases were voided by the Supreme Court, and the reserves were returned to government control.
- The scandal symbolized the corruption and cronyism of the *Harding* administration, contributing to public skepticism of federal government during the 1920s.

## connections

- [[warren-g-harding]] — the president under whose watch the scandal occurred.
- [[albert-b-fall]] — the Interior Secretary who orchestrated and profited from the illegal leases.
- [[american-civil-liberties]] — exposed the need for transparency in government contracting.
- [[senate-investigations]] — *Thomas J. Walsh*'s Senate hearings became a model for congressional oversight.
