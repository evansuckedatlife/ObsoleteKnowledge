# Abolitionism

## summary

Abolitionism was the social and political movement to abolish slavery in the United States, gaining substantial momentum in the early nineteenth century. Abolitionists—comprising both Black and white activists, religious leaders, and politicians—argued that slavery was a moral evil incompatible with Christianity, natural rights, and democratic principles. The movement employed various tactics: moral persuasion through speeches and published works (like William Lloyd Garrison's newspaper *The Liberator* and Frederick Douglass's autobiography), political organizing (petitions to Congress, political campaigns), and direct action (some abolitionists, like John Brown, advocated armed insurrection). Abolitionism transformed slavery from an accepted institution into a contested moral question, ultimately contributing to the Civil War and the Thirteenth Amendment's abolition of slavery.

## you gotta know

### origins and moral argument
- The abolitionist movement emerged in the late eighteenth and early nineteenth centuries, influenced by evangelical Christianity, Enlightenment ideals, and the British abolition of the slave trade (1807) and slavery (1833).
- Abolitionists argued that slavery violated the natural rights of enslaved people and Christian teachings about human dignity.
- Key figures included *William Lloyd Garrison* (editor of *The Liberator*), *Frederick Douglass* (formerly enslaved, eloquent orator and writer), *Harriet Beecher Stowe* (author of *Uncle Tom's Cabin*), and *Grimké Sisters* (Sarah and Angelina, white women from a slaveholding family).

### key tactics
- *Moral suasion*: speeches, publications, and religious appeals to change hearts and minds about slavery's evil.
- *Political organizing*: petitions to Congress (though Congress refused to receive them), political parties (the Free Soil Party, the Republican Party), and legislative campaigns.
- *Direct action*: some abolitionists, particularly *John Brown*, advocated armed resistance to slavery (Brown's raid on Harpers Ferry in 1859 aimed to spark an enslaved rebellion).

### opposition and escalation
- Abolitionists faced violent opposition from slavery supporters in the South and many in the North.
- In the 1840s-1850s, abolitionism shifted from a fringe moral position to a major political force, particularly in the Republican Party (founded 1854).
- The *Kansas-Nebraska Act* (1854), the *Dred Scott v. Sandford* decision (1857), and *John Brown's Raid* (1859) inflamed tensions and strengthened abolitionist resolve.

### impact and legacy
- Abolitionism succeeded in making slavery the central moral and political question in America, contributing to the election of Abraham Lincoln and the Civil War.
- The *Emancipation Proclamation* (1863) and the *Thirteenth Amendment* (1865) realized the abolitionist goal of ending slavery.
- Abolitionism established a template for social justice movements: moral argument combined with political organizing and, in some cases, direct action.
