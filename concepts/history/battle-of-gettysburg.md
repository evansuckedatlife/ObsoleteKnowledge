# Battle of Gettysburg

## summary

The Battle of Gettysburg (July 1–3, 1863) was the largest and most significant battle of the American Civil War, fought in and around the town of Gettysburg, Pennsylvania. The Union Army of the Potomac, commanded by Major General George Meade, defeated General Robert E. Lee's Confederate Army of Northern Virginia in a three-day engagement that marked a turning point in the war. With heavy casualties on both sides—approximately 50,000 killed, wounded, or missing—the battle effectively ended Lee's invasion of the North and shattered the myth of Southern invincibility, giving the Union decisive strategic momentum.

## you gotta know

### Background
- Lee sought to invade Pennsylvania to draw Union forces away from the South and gain a major victory on Northern soil that might sway European powers toward Confederate recognition.
- Meade assumed command of the Union forces only days before the battle and was initially unaware of Lee's exact position.
- The armies collided by accident when Confederate units searching for supplies encountered Union cavalry on July 1.

### The Battle
- Union forces held the high ground (Cemetery Ridge, Culp's Hill) and repulsed Confederate assaults on all three days despite being initially outnumbered.
- *Pickett's Charge* (July 3) was Lee's last major assault—13,000 Confederate infantry crossed open ground toward the Union center and were decimated by artillery and rifle fire; fewer than half returned to Confederate lines.
- The Union cavalry, under *Jeb Stuart*, arrived late and failed to strike Meade's rear, denying Lee a crucial advantage.

### Aftermath
- Lee retreated to Virginia on July 4, ending his invasion.
- Casualties were catastrophic: Union lost ~23,000, Confederacy ~28,000; the South could not replace these losses.
- The victory boosted Northern morale and prompted Lincoln to issue the *Emancipation Proclamation* to take effect in January 1864.

## context

Gettysburg stands as the hinge upon which the Civil War turned. Before it, the Confederacy seemed capable of winning through persistence and superior generalship; after it, the Union's numerical and industrial advantage became insurmountable. The battle's tactical lessons—entrenchment, defensive positioning, the cost of frontal assault against prepared positions—echoed through World War I. In American memory, Gettysburg became the crucible of national survival; Lincoln's address there in November 1863 reframed the war not as a constitutional conflict but as a test of whether a democratic nation could endure.
