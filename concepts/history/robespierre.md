# Robespierre

## summary

*Maximilien Robespierre* (1758–1794) was the architect of the Reign of Terror and the most infamous figure of the French Revolution, wielding extraordinary power as the dominant voice of the Committee of Public Safety (1793–1794). A provincial lawyer and idealistic radical who rose to prominence as a deputy to the Estates-General, Robespierre embodied the revolution's claim to virtue and moral purification—but increasingly saw enemies and traitors everywhere, orchestrating mass arrests, show trials, and executions that killed tens of thousands. His vision of a "republic of virtue" required total purification through terror, but when he turned on fellow radicals and the Revolution's popular base began to fear his power, even his allies voted for his arrest and execution by guillotine in July 1794. His brief, blood-soaked dominance became the defining symbol of revolutionary terror and the dangers of unchecked ideological fervor.

## you gotta know

### Rise to Power
- Rose from relative obscurity as a deputy from Arras to the Estates-General (1789) and became a leading voice in the radical Jacobin Club by the early 1790s.
- Built his power on appeals to virtue, the *General Will*, and the sovereignty of the people, positioning himself as the revolution's moral conscience against compromise and betrayal.
- Called for the trial and execution of *King Louis XVI* in December 1792, arguing that the king's survival threatened the republic; the king was executed in January 1793, a psychological turning point.

### The Reign of Terror
- Elected to the Committee of Public Safety in July 1793 and quickly became its dominant figure, expanding the secret police and accelerating mass arrests of suspected "counter-revolutionaries."
- Presided over the Reign of Terror (1793–1794), during which over 16,000 were officially executed and thousands more died in prisons or summary killings; the Terror's reach spread into the provinces through guillotines and mass drownings.
- Orchestrated the executions of major rivals: *Danton* and *Camille Desmoulins* (April 1794) for "moderation," *Hébert* and the "ultra-revolutionaries" (March 1794) for pushing terror too far, revealing his unpredictable judgment.
- Championed the *Festival of the Supreme Being* (June 1794), a quasi-religious civic ceremony reflecting his Rousseauian belief in virtue, national rebirth, and the dangers of atheism—an eerie cultural moment amid mass killing.

### Fall and Execution
- His paranoia and unpredictability eventually alarmed even his allies; he threatened further purges after June 1794, making deputies fear they might be next.
- Fell from power during the *Thermidorian Reaction* (July 27–28, 1794) when the Convention voted to arrest him; he was executed by guillotine the next day without trial, the same year he'd risen to power.
- His final hours involved a failed suicide attempt (shooting himself in the jaw) before the guillotine, a historically disputed end that has shadowed his legend.

## context

Robespierre's tragedy is that he believed absolutely in the revolution's moral mission—he saw enemies everywhere because he believed the stakes were eternal, not temporal. His idealism made him terrifying: convinced of the righteousness of his cause, he could rationalize the execution of anyone who disagreed with him as a defense of the nation's virtue. He represents the 18th century's faith in reason and virtue weaponized into totalitarianism—a cautionary tale about the dangers of revolutionary purity when divorced from democratic accountability. Yet he was also trapped: once the Terror began, stopping it seemed like betrayal, and the machinery he had built would not shut down without someone to pull the lever.
