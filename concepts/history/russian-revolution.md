# Russian Revolution

## summary

The Russian Revolution was a series of upheavals in Russia in 1917 that overthrew the Romanov dynasty's three-century rule, dismantled the Russian Empire, and established the world's first communist state. Beginning with the February Revolution that deposed Tsar *Nicholas II*, it culminated in the October Bolshevik seizure of power led by *Vladimir Lenin*, fundamentally reshaping global politics. The revolution's ideology and success inspired communist movements worldwide and defined geopolitical conflict for the remainder of the twentieth century.

## you gotta know

### prelude and february revolution

- The Russian Empire in 1917 was devastated by World War I: massive casualties, economic collapse, food shortages, and widespread discontent with *Tsar Nicholas II* and his autocratic rule.
- The *February Revolution* (March by the modern calendar) began with strikes and riots in Petrograd (now St. Petersburg) over bread rationing; mutinous soldiers refused to fire on crowds, forcing the Tsar's abdication on March 15, 1917.
- The Tsar was replaced by the liberal *Provisional Government*, which promised democratic reforms and constitutional monarchy but continued Russia's unpopular involvement in World War I.
- The *Soviets*—councils of workers, soldiers, and peasants—formed spontaneously in cities and competed with the Provisional Government for power, creating a "dual power" situation.

### bolshevik ascendancy

- *Vladimir Lenin*, leader of the radical *Bolshevik* faction of the Russian Socialist Democratic Labor Party, returned from exile in April 1917 and issued the "April Theses," calling for immediate withdrawal from the war and a seizure of power by the Soviets.
- The Bolsheviks, initially a minority, gained mass support by promising "peace, land, and bread"—an irresistible program for a war-weary, hungry populace.
- The *July Days* (a failed Bolshevik uprising) forced Lenin into hiding, but the Provisional Government's failure to resolve the crisis or end the war handed the Bolsheviks momentum.
- In October 1917, *Leon Trotsky*, now the Bolsheviks' brilliant military organizer, orchestrated the seizure of Petrograd's Winter Palace, the symbolic seat of government, in a nearly bloodless coup.

### consolidation and civil war

- The Bolsheviks rapidly dissolved the freely elected *Constituent Assembly* (which they won only a quarter of seats) and established a one-party dictatorship, claiming sole legitimacy to represent the working class.
- The revolution immediately withdrew Russia from World War I via the *Treaty of Brest-Litovsk* (March 1918), ceding vast territories to Germany but securing peace.
- A devastating *Civil War* (1918–1922) pitted the Bolsheviks' *Red Army* against the *White Armies* (anti-communist coalitions) and foreign interventionist powers, killing millions of civilians through combat, famine, and terror.
- By 1922, the Bolsheviks had won the civil war and established the *Union of Soviet Socialist Republics (USSR)*, the first communist state and the nucleus of a global ideological rivalry that would dominate the century.

## context

The Russian Revolution stands as one of history's pivotal watersheds, creating the template for the communist state and igniting global competition over which ideology would define the twentieth century. Its success demonstrated that revolutionary Marxist ideology could seize a major power and sustain itself, validating communist theory and inspiring revolutionary movements in China, Vietnam, Cuba, and dozens of other nations. The USSR's existence and Cold War antagonism with the West shaped geopolitics, military strategy, nuclear deterrence, and international relations for 74 years. The revolution's immediate consequences—terror, purges, the Gulag system under Stalin, and centralized command economy—showed that the utopian ideals of Marxism could produce authoritarian dystopia, raising enduring questions about whether the revolution was inevitable or tragically contingent on individuals like Lenin. Historians debate whether the revolution was an inevitable eruption of class conflict or whether different choices by the Provisional Government (or the Tsar in 1916) could have altered its course. Its legacy spans from the Soviet Union's collapse in 1991 to ongoing debates about communism's theoretical promise versus practical implementation.

- [[leon-trotsky]] — the brilliant military organizer of the October Revolution and architect of the Red Army's civil war victory.

- [[bolsheviks]] — the radical communist faction that transformed a general uprising into ideological seizure of state power.
- [[marxism]] — the theoretical foundation for the Bolshevik program of class revolution and proletarian dictatorship.
- [[communism]] — the ideology born from the revolution's success, defining its political practice and global influence.
- [[world-war-i]] — the war whose catastrophe in Russia precipitated the conditions enabling revolution.
