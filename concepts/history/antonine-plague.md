# Antonine Plague

## summary

The *Antonine Plague* was a pandemic that ravaged the Roman Empire between 165 and 180 CE, killing an estimated 5 to 10 million people across the Mediterranean and Near East. Likely caused by smallpox or measles, it struck during the Parthian Wars and the reign of Marcus Aurelius, profoundly affecting Roman military strength and social life.

## you gotta know

- Named after the Antonine dynasty; the plague coincided with military campaigns in Mesopotamia and is believed to have been brought back to Rome by returning troops.
- The disease, recorded by the physician Galen (who treated Emperor Marcus Aurelius), is thought to have been smallpox or possibly measles based on symptom descriptions.
- Killed an estimated 5–10 million people, with some historians suggesting mortality rates as high as 25–33% in major cities during peak years.
- Significantly depleted the Roman legions, forcing the military to recruit from Germanic tribes and weakening Rome's defensive posture on the northern frontier.
- Contemporary sources describe pustular eruptions, high fevers, and gastrointestinal symptoms; the disease spread rapidly through trade networks and military movements.
- Contributed to economic disruption, labor shortages, and psychological trauma across the empire; philosophical works of the period reflect heightened anxiety about mortality.
- Caused a demographic trough that took decades to recover from, reshaping the age structure and labor supply of Roman society.
