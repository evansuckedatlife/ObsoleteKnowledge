# Mamluk Sultanate

## summary

The *Mamluk Sultanate* (1250–1517 CE) was a unique military state ruled by *mamluks*, enslaved soldiers of Caucasian or Central Asian origin who were trained as warriors and rose to supreme power in Egypt and the Levant. Though built on the paradox of slave rule, the Mamluks became one of history's most effective and enduring military regimes, defending Islam against both the Mongols and the Crusaders while patronizing magnificent art and architecture.

## you gotta know

- Originated as the *Bahri* (River) Mamluk faction, a military caste within the Ayyubid army who seized power from the last Ayyubid sultan in 1250 CE.
- *Qutuz* and *Baybars*, early Mamluk sultans, defeated the *Mongol Ilkhanate* at the *Battle of Ain Jalut* (1260 CE), preventing Mongol conquest of Egypt and earning immense prestige across the Islamic world.
- Ruled Egypt, the *Levant*, and the *Hijaz* (including the holy cities of *Mecca* and *Medina*), making them custodians of Islam's holiest sites and spiritual authorities even when rivals claimed the caliphate.
- Operated a meritocratic military system where ambitious mamluks could rise from slavery to sultanate through martial skill and political acumen, creating competitive incentives for capable commanders.
- Patronized elaborate Islamic architecture, decorative arts, calligraphy, and urban development; the *Citadel of Cairo* and numerous mosques and madrasas from this era remain iconic.
- Maintained control for over 250 years despite persistent internal power struggles and assassinations; finally overthrown by the expanding *Ottoman Empire* in 1517 CE.
