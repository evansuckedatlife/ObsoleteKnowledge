# Lochner v. New York

## summary

Lochner v. New York (1905) is the emblematic case of the Lochner era (c. 1905–1937), when the Supreme Court struck down economic and labor regulations as violations of the Due Process Clause. Joseph Lochner, a bakery owner, was prosecuted for violating a New York statute limiting bakers' work hours to 10 per day and 60 per week. The Court, in a 5-4 decision, ruled that the law violated the *Fourteenth Amendment's* Due Process Clause by depriving Lochner and his employees of *freedom of contract*—the unenumerated right to negotiate wages and working conditions. Justice Peckham's majority opinion treated labor regulations as impermissible interference with liberty, a stance that invalidated hundreds of state and federal laws. The Lochner era is now repudiated as a cautionary tale: the Court read its own economic ideology into the Constitution, blocked democratic reform, and only reversed course under pressure during the New Deal.

## you gotta know

### The Case
- *New York's law*: In 1897, New York enacted a Maximum Hours Law restricting bakers to 10 hours per day and 60 hours per week, citing health and safety (flour dust, heat, long hours weakened workers' health).
- *Lochner's violation*: Joseph Lochner, a bakery owner, required employees to work longer and was fined $50.
- *The appeal*: Lochner argued the law violated the Fourteenth Amendment's Due Process Clause, which guarantees "liberty" and "property"—including the liberty to negotiate with employees.

### The Majority's Reasoning
- Justice Peckham wrote that the Due Process Clause protects *substantive rights*, not merely procedural regularity.
- *"The general right to make a contract in relation to his business is part of the liberty of the individual protected by the [Due Process Clause]."*
- Freedom of contract is a *natural* and *fundamental* liberty; the state cannot restrict it except to protect *legitimate* police power (health, safety, morals).
- The majority characterized the bakers' law as a *labor regulation* disguised as health protection—really just redistributing bargaining power—and found no legitimate state interest justifying it.

### The Dissent: Holmes and Harlan
- Justice Holmes's dissent is more cited today than Peckham's majority: *"The Fourteenth Amendment does not enact Mr. Herbert Spencer's Social Statics."* (No, the Constitution does not embed laissez-faire economics.)
- Holmes argued that if the legislature rationally believed the law protected health, the Court should defer to democratic judgment.
- Justice Harlan's longer dissent outlined the evidence of bakers' poor health and the legitimacy of the regulation.

### The Lochner Era: Expansion and Backlash
- From 1905 to 1937, the Court struck down some 200 state and federal laws—minimum wage laws, price controls, union regulations, workplace safety rules—as violations of freedom of contract.
- *Adair v. United States* (1908) invalidated a federal law prohibiting railroad employers from firing workers for union membership.
- *Coppage v. Kansas* (1915) struck down a state law banning "yellow-dog" contracts (agreements not to join unions).
- The Court's stance frustrated reformers and impeded the Progressive movement and New Deal legislation.

### The Reversal: The "Switch in Time"
- During the New Deal, as FDR proposed "court-packing," Justice Owen Roberts switched sides in *West Coast Hotel v. Parrish* (1937), upholding a minimum wage law.
- The Court abandoned Lochner, confirming that Congress and the states had broad power to regulate economic life under the commerce power and due process became a weak constraint.
- Lochner is now reviled as a warning: judicial activism in defense of unenumerated economic rights is illegitimate.

### Doctrinal Legacy
- Lochner is dead as precedent but haunts debates over *unenumerated rights*: Does the due process clause protect privacy? Gun ownership? All rest on Lochner-like reasoning (finding rights not in the text).
- Modern conservatives occasionally invoke Lochner-like arguments against regulations, though they disclaim the label; progressives invoke Lochner as a cautionary tale about judicial overreach.

## context

Lochner v. New York encapsulates a crucial lesson in constitutional law: the Constitution does not have a stable meaning independent of the Court's politics and the era's ideology. In 1905, a majority of Justices believed that "liberty" included freedom of contract and that the state could not redistribute bargaining power through labor laws. In 1937, a new majority (or a converted one) believed the opposite: states could regulate economic life to protect workers and consumers. Neither reading was forced by the text; the Constitution's language does not answer the question. Lochner was never overruled explicitly; it was simply abandoned. The case illustrates the danger of *substantive due process* — a doctrine that asks courts to identify and protect unenumerated rights. Progressives regard Lochner as discredited activism; conservatives note that the same doctrine protects abortion and same-sex marriage rights, raising the question: if substantive due process is illegitimate when it protects economic liberty, why is it legitimate when it protects sexual or reproductive liberty? Lochner remains unresolved in constitutional philosophy, even as its specific holdings are repudiated.
