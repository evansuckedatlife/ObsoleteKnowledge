# Tony Blair

## summary

*Tony Blair* (1953–) was the architect of New Labour and the longest-serving Labour Prime Minister in British history, transforming the party through modernization and centralism. His tenure (1997–2007) oversaw constitutional devolution, the Northern Ireland peace process, and Britain's controversial involvement in the Iraq War—a legacy of both reform and deep controversy.

## you gotta know

- Won three consecutive landslide elections (1997, 2001, 2005), giving Labour unprecedented consecutive terms in power and reshaping the party as a centrist, market-friendly force.
- Implemented devolution to Scotland and Wales in 1997, granting them their own parliaments for the first time in centuries and rebalancing the union's internal power structure.
- Played a central role in the Northern Ireland peace process, culminating in the Good Friday Agreement (1998), which established power-sharing and ended decades of conflict and violence.
- Led Britain into the 2003 Iraq War alongside the United States, claiming Saddam Hussein possessed weapons of mass destruction; the war's justification was later discredited, damaging Blair's credibility.
- Expanded the welfare state through targeted tax credits and education investment, combining market liberalism with targeted welfare spending in a synthesis he called "Third Way" politics.
- Resigned in 2007 before the Iraq War's full consequences and financial crash became apparent, departing as one of Britain's most celebrated and most controversial postwar leaders.
