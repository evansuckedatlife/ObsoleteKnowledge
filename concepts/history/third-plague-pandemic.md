# Third Plague Pandemic

## summary

The *Third Plague Pandemic* was a global resurgence of bubonic plague that began in China in 1855 and spread to every inhabited continent by the early 20th century, killing an estimated 12 to 15 million people. Unlike earlier medieval pandemics, it occurred in the age of modern science, allowing researchers to identify the plague bacillus and map transmission routes, while simultaneously affecting colonized regions disproportionately.

## you gotta know

- Began in southern China (Yunnan Province) in 1855, likely triggered by ecological disruption or expanded rat populations; spread via trade routes to Hong Kong and the world.
- Killed an estimated 12–15 million people, with heaviest mortality in Asia: India alone lost an estimated 10 million people between 1896 and 1948.
- The bacterium *Yersinia pestis* was identified by Alexandre Yersin in 1894; its flea vector was confirmed by Paul-Louis Simondt, enabling scientific understanding of transmission.
- Spread in three forms: bubonic (swollen lymph nodes), pneumonic (respiratory, highly transmissible), and septicemic (blood infection); pneumonic outbreaks caused rapid, explosive mortality.
- Colonial port cities—Hong Kong, Mumbai, San Francisco, Sydney—were hit hardest, exposing the vulnerability of global trade networks and crowded working-class districts.
- San Francisco's 1900–1910 outbreak was suppressed partly through rat extermination and quarantine but continued covertly in prairie dog populations of the American West.
- The pandemic demonstrated the power of microbiology and epidemiology to identify pathogens and transmission vectors, ushering in the modern scientific era of disease control.
