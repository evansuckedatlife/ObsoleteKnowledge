## summary

On *June 28, 1778*, the Continental Army and British forces clashed at Monmouth Courthouse, New Jersey. The battle began with American general *Charles Lee* launching an attack that faltered against British resistance; Washington arrived and rallied the troops with a stirring appearance on the battlefield. The fighting remained indecisive—neither side achieved a decisive victory—but the British withdrew and the Americans claimed a moral and strategic victory by denying Howe an easy campaign.

## you gotta know

- A rare mid-summer engagement; usually armies campaigned only in warmer months but settled in winter quarters.
- Charles Lee's initial attack was poorly coordinated and broke down; he retreated despite having superior numbers.
- Washington's arrival and personal intervention rallied the American troops, who formed a solid defensive line.
- The British, unable to break the American position, chose to withdraw toward New York rather than continue the engagement.
- American artillery, particularly under *Henry Knox*, played a crucial role in stabilizing the line and forcing British withdrawal.
- Charles Lee was later court-martialed for his mishandling of the battle and his retreat in the face of the enemy.

## connections

- [[george-washington]] — rallied the army at a critical moment; his leadership became legendary.
- [[charles-lee]] — the defeated American general; court-martialed after the battle.
- [[henry-knox]] — chief of artillery; his guns held the line when it might have broken.
- [[new-jersey]] — the battleground; part of Washington's broader New Jersey campaign.
- [[american-revolution]] — a rare tactical success, though strategically indecisive.
