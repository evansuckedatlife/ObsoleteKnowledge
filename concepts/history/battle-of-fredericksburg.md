# Battle of Fredericksburg

## summary

The Battle of Fredericksburg (December 11–15, 1862) was a major Confederate victory fought in and around the town of Fredericksburg, Virginia. Union General Ambrose E. Burnside, seeking to turn Lee's flank and capture Richmond, instead led his army into a prepared defensive position where Robert E. Lee's Army of Northern Virginia held strong ground behind the town. In repeated frontal assaults on the heights above the town—particularly Marye's Heights—Union forces suffered catastrophic losses while inflicting comparatively light casualties. The battle was a stunning demonstration of the advantage of defensive entrenchment and led to Burnside's replacement as commander.

## you gotta know

### Background
- Burnside assumed command after Antietam and immediately sought to move aggressively toward Richmond.
- Lee repositioned his army to block the Union advance, selecting a strong defensive position behind the Rappahannock River.
- Union forces required pontoons to bridge the river; Confederate artillery delayed their crossing.

### The Battle
- Union forces crossed the river on December 11 and cleared the town on December 12.
- Lee's main defensive line, anchored on *Marye's Heights*, was a stone wall at the base of an elevation where Confederate troops could fire at will.
- On December 13, Union divisions assaulted the heights in successive waves; each was repulsed with heavy loss. Fewer than 6,000 Union soldiers were casualties at the wall; more than 12,000 fell in total.

### Aftermath
- Burnside, despite urging from subordinates, pressed the attacks through December 13; the Union withdrew across the river on December 15.
- Total casualties: Union ~12,700; Confederate ~6,000. The lopsided casualty ratio vindicated Confederate defensive tactics.
- Lincoln replaced Burnside with Joseph "Fighting Joe" Hooker, seeking an aggressive commander.

## context

Fredericksburg was a triumphant moment for Lee and the Confederacy—one of the few battles where Union numerical superiority was negated by superior tactics and entrenchment. The repeated Union assaults on Marye's Heights became emblematic of the futility of frontal assault against prepared defensive positions, a lesson that would define World War I. For the Northern public, the defeat was demoralizing, but it also reinforced that the war would be decided through patience and attrition rather than brilliant maneuver. The battle accelerated the Union's recognition that victory required not just aggressive generals but ones who understood modern warfare's realities.
