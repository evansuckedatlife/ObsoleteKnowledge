# Battle of Chickamauga

## summary

The Battle of Chickamauga (September 18–20, 1863) was the second-bloodiest battle of the American Civil War, fought in northwestern Georgia near the Tennessee border. Union General William S. Rosecrans maneuvered Confederate General Braxton Bragg into a retreat, but Bragg rallied his forces, reinforced by divisions detached from other theaters, and caught Rosecrans overextended. The resulting two-day battle saw fierce combat that broke the Union right; only the steadfast defense of Union General George H. Thomas at the end of September 19 prevented complete rout. Though Confederates technically held the field, their failure to capitalize on Rosecrans' rout and their own heavy casualties prevented a decisive victory that might have altered the war's course.

## you gotta know

### Background
- Rosecrans sought to maneuver Bragg out of middle Tennessee without a direct engagement.
- Bragg initially retreated but, learning of Union supply lines extended and army dispersed, resolved to attack.
- Confederate reinforcements, including *James Longstreet's* corps from the Virginia theater, arrived to strengthen Bragg's hand.

### The Battle
- Initial skirmishing on September 18 escalated into frontal attacks on September 19 and 20.
- The Union right, commanded by General *Alexander McCook*, broke under Confederate assault, creating a rout that Rosecrans followed in retreat to Chattanooga.
- General *George H. Thomas*, with a small reserve force, held Horseshoe Ridge on September 19's evening and all of September 20, earning the epithet "Rock of Chickamauga." His stand prevented annihilation.

### Aftermath
- Bragg did not pursue Rosecrans; the Union was besieged in Chattanooga but not destroyed.
- Casualties were approximately 18,000 Union, 18,000 Confederate—a heavy toll that benefited neither side strategically.
- Union leadership remained in Chattanooga, setting the stage for the Union breakout in November at Missionary Ridge.

## context

Chickamauga was a pyrrhic Confederate victory. Bragg had briefly restored Confederate fortunes in the western theater, but his failure to destroy or capture Rosecrans' army and his subsequent passivity allowed the Union to consolidate. The arrival of reinforcements from Virginia showed the Confederacy's ability to concentrate force when necessary—yet the detachment weakened Lee elsewhere. Thomas's heroic stand exemplified individual courage's power to alter a battle's trajectory, yet courage alone could not overcome Union industrial advantage. The siege of Chattanooga that followed would become another turning point when Union forces under Ulysses S. Grant broke through.
