# Reconstruction Era

## summary

Reconstruction (1865–1877) was the period following the American Civil War during which the federal government attempted to restore the Union, secure rights for formerly enslaved people, and rebuild the shattered South. Divided into overlapping phases—*Presidential Reconstruction* under *Andrew Johnson* and *Congressional (Radical) Reconstruction* under *Republican* majorities—it saw Black Americans achieve unprecedented political and social gains: voting rights, elected office, and education. Yet Reconstruction ultimately failed. White Southern resistance, the rise of the Ku Klux Klan, Northern political fatigue, and the Supreme Court's narrowing of federal power allowed Southern whites to regain control by 1877, ushering in a century of segregation, disenfranchisement, and racial terrorism.

## you gotta know

### presidential and early reconstruction

- After the Civil War's end (April 1865), President *Andrew Johnson* pursued a lenient *Presidential Reconstruction*, quickly readmitting Southern states and granting white Southerners pardons, effectively restoring white political control.
- Southern states passed *Black Codes* (1865–1866), laws restricting freedmen's rights to owning land, testifying in court, or gathering; these laws outraged Northern Republicans and convinced them that the South was unrepentant.
- The *13th Amendment* (1865) abolished slavery; the *14th Amendment* (1868) granted citizenship and equal protection; the *15th Amendment* (1870) forbade racial discrimination in voting—revolutionary changes that defined Reconstruction's legal framework.

### radical reconstruction and black political participation

- The *Reconstruction Acts* (1867–1868), passed by *Radical Republicans* over Johnson's veto, divided the South into military districts, required new state constitutions with Black male suffrage, and mandated ratification of the 14th Amendment before readmission.
- Approximately 2,000 Black men held office during Reconstruction, including U.S. Senators *Hiram Revels* and *Blanche Bruce*, state governors, and hundreds of state legislators; Black voters outnumbered white voters in some states, creating genuine political competition.
- The *Freedmen's Bureau* (1865–1872) provided crucial services: education (establishing schools and colleges), land distribution, and legal aid, though its resources were always insufficient and its mandate was limited.

### white resistance and the rise of the klan

- White Southerners resisted Reconstruction through violence and terrorism: the *Ku Klux Klan* and similar groups attacked Black voters, Republican (especially white) politicians, and Black schools and churches.
- The *Civil Rights Act of 1875* banned racial discrimination in public accommodations, the height of federal civil rights legislation before the mid-20th century.
- *Grant*, elected president in 1868, initially enforced Reconstruction vigorously, sending troops to suppress the Klan, but public support for vigorous enforcement waned by his second term.

### collapse and redemption

- The *Compromise of 1877*, which resolved the disputed 1876 presidential election, effectively ended Reconstruction: Republicans accepted Democrat *Samuel Tilden* in exchange for Southern acceptance of *Rutherford B. Hayes* and the withdrawal of federal troops from the South.
- Once troops departed, *Redemption*—the Southern white reclamation of political power—proceeded swiftly: Black voting was suppressed through violence, fraud, and later *Jim Crow* laws; segregation was enshrined in law.
- Within a decade of Reconstruction's end, the South had re-established white supremacy and reversed nearly all gains for Black Americans; the next civil rights push would not come until the 1950s–1960s.

## context

Reconstruction was the nation's first and, until the Civil Rights era, its most radical attempt at social and political transformation from above. It showed that federal power could enforce civil rights and that Black Americans, given the franchise, would exercise it—yet it also revealed the limits of that power when public will flagged and opposition organized. Reconstruction's failure had century-long consequences: the entrenchment of segregation, the disenfranchisement of Black voters, the concentration of power in the hands of white Democrats, and the deferral of racial justice. For quiz bowl, Reconstruction is the tragic counterpart to the Civil War's triumph: the war ended slavery, but Reconstruction's collapse ensured that formal freedom would be hollowed out by discrimination and terror for another century.
