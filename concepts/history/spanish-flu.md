# Spanish Flu

## summary

The *Spanish Flu* was a catastrophic global influenza pandemic caused by the H1N1 virus that killed an estimated 50 to 100 million people between 1918 and 1920. Despite its name, it did not originate in Spain; the pandemic spread rapidly during the final months of World War I, infecting about one-third of the global population and reshaping public health policy.

## you gotta know

- An unusually virulent strain of influenza A (H1N1 subtype) that emerged in 1918; dubbed "Spanish" because Spain, neutral in WWI, openly reported cases while combatants suppressed mortality data.
- First wave (spring 1918) was relatively mild; the second wave (fall 1918) was far deadlier, with victims dying within 48 hours of infection from pneumonia and cytokine storms.
- Killed an estimated 50–100 million people globally, with the highest mortality among young, healthy adults—a pattern opposite to seasonal flu, suggesting an overactive immune response.
- Spread by respiratory droplets; crowded military barracks and transport ships during WWI created ideal conditions for transmission.
- Overwhelmed hospitals and medical systems worldwide; face masks and social distancing measures were implemented in many cities.
- The pandemic ended without a clear cause; seasonal flu variants descended from the 1918 strain continue circulating today.
- Prompted major advances in epidemiology, vaccine development, and international disease surveillance (foundations of modern public health).
