# The New Yorker

## summary

*The New Yorker*, founded in 1925 by *Harold Ross*, is an influential American magazine of commentary, criticism, fiction, and cartoons. Celebrated for its wit, sophisticated urban sensibility, and rigorous editorial standards, it has served as a launching pad for major writers and humorists while shaping literary and political discourse.

## you gotta know

- Founded in 1925 by Harold Ross with the mission to be "not edited for the old lady from Dubuque," targeting educated New York readers.
- Famous for its cartoons — iconic single-panel drawings with brief captions that became a hallmark of American humor.
- Published debut fiction and journalism from major authors including *J.D. Salinger*, *Roald Dahl*, *John Cheever*, and *James Baldwin*.
- Known for meticulous *fact-checking* and copy-editing; the magazine's rigor became an industry standard.
- Features the distinctive *"Talk of the Town"* section, offering witty commentary on New York events and culture.
- Circulation remained relatively modest (around *1 million*) compared to mass-market rivals, but its cultural influence vastly exceeded its numbers.
- Editorial stance emphasized political sophistication and cultural criticism alongside entertainment.
