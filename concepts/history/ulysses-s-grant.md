## summary

*Ulysses S. Grant* was the commanding general of the Union Army during the American Civil War's final campaigns and the 18th president of the United States (1869–1877). A career soldier of modest ambition who achieved his greatest fame in warfare, Grant earned a reputation as a relentless tactician willing to absorb casualties to win—summed up in his philosophy of *attrition*. His presidency, though marred by corruption scandals, oversaw the final phase of Reconstruction and aimed to protect the civil rights of freed slaves, though with declining congressional support.

## you gotta know

- Rose from obscurity to become commanding general of all Union armies in March 1864, after years of lesser officers had failed to decisively defeat Robert E. Lee.
- Orchestrated the strategy of simultaneous pressure on multiple fronts—while *William Tecumseh Sherman* marched toward Atlanta, Grant pressed Lee in Virginia, denying the Confederacy the ability to shift forces.
- Won the war of attrition at *Petersburg* (1864–65), surrounding Lee's army and forcing its surrender at *Appomattox* in April 1865, effectively ending the Civil War.
- Elected president in 1868 as a Republican; championed the *Reconstruction Acts*, the *Fifteenth Amendment* (guaranteeing voting rights regardless of race), and federal intervention to suppress the Ku Klux Klan.
- Suffered a damaged presidency due to scandals including the *Whiskey Ring* and corruption among his cabinet members and associates, though he himself was never personally implicated in fraud.
- Left office in 1877 with his popularity diminished; spent his final years writing his memoirs, published posthumously, which remain among the finest military autobiographies in American literature.
