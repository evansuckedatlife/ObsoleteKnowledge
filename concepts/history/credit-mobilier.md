# Crédit Mobilier

## summary

The *Crédit Mobilier* scandal involved the construction company *Crédit Mobilier of America*, created to build the Union Pacific Railroad. Executives funneled excessive profits by overcharging the railroad—and then distributed stock bribes to members of Congress and the Grant administration to suppress investigation, exposing systemic corruption during Reconstruction.

## you gotta know

- *Crédit Mobilier* was a shell construction company created by Union Pacific Railroad directors (led by *Thomas C. Durant*) to contract with the railroad at inflated prices, siphoning profits.
- Between *1865* and *1869*, the company charged the railroad over *$94 million* for work worth roughly *$50 million*—a *$40 million+* theft.
- To silence criticism, *Crédit Mobilier* distributed free or cheap stock to prominent congressmen, senators, and members of the *Grant* administration.
- The scandal broke in *1872* when *Henry C. Adams*, a *New York Sun* reporter, exposed the scheme; *Thomas W. Knox* of the same paper pursued it aggressively.
- Congressional investigations censured Representative *Oakes Ames* and Senator *James W. Patterson*, both of whom had distributed stock; *Ames* was formally censured by the House.
- The railroad was ultimately completed (*1869*) and the United States gained a transcontinental route, but the scandal undermined public confidence in corporate and government accountability.
- The affair became emblematic of *Gilded Age* corruption and helped spur later reforms in railroad regulation and congressional ethics.
