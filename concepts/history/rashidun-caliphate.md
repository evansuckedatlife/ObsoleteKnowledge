# Rashidun Caliphate

## summary

The *Rashidun Caliphate* (632–661 CE) comprises the first four Islamic caliphs—Abu Bakr, Umar, Uthman, and Ali—who were chosen from Muhammad's companions and governed the early Islamic state as it rapidly conquered the Byzantine and Persian empires. This brief period established the institutional foundation for Islamic governance, Islamic law, and the territorial base of the Islamic world, though it ended in civil conflict that would shape Islamic politics forever.

## you gotta know

- Encompassed the reigns of four caliphs (*Abu Bakr*, *Umar I*, *Uthman ibn Affan*, *Ali ibn Abi Talib*) who were elected or appointed rather than dynastic successors.
- *Umar's* reign (634–644 CE) saw the most explosive expansion—conquest of Syria, Iraq, Egypt, and parts of Persia—making the caliphate a true empire.
- Established the *shura* (consultative assembly) principle and early Islamic administrative structures that later dynasties would modify but not abandon.
- Standardized the *Qur'an* under Caliph *Uthman*, creating an official written text to prevent variant readings.
- Uthman's rule sparked the *Fitna* (civil war), as rival factions disputed whether leadership should be elective or hereditary, a schism that defined Sunni-Shiite division.
- The caliphate ended when *Ali ibn Abi Talib* was assassinated in 661 CE, followed by his rival *Mu'awiyah I* establishing the *Umayyad* dynasty.

## connections

- [[muhammad]] — the Prophet whose companions formed the caliphate after his death.
- [[umayyad-caliphate]] — succeeded the Rashidun and shifted to hereditary Arab rule.
- [[abbasid-caliphate]] — overthrew the Umayyads and claimed to restore consultative governance.
- [[islamic-law]] — the Rashidun era saw the codification of *hadith* and foundational jurisprudence.
