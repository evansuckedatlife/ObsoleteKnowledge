# Tear Down This Wall

## summary

Ronald Reagan's speech at the Brandenburg Gate on *June 12, 1987*, issued a direct challenge to Soviet leader Mikhail Gorbachev to dismantle the Berlin Wall as a test of his reform sincerity. The speech became symbolically and historically prescient — less than two years later, the wall fell — making it a signature moment of Reagan's presidency and a high point of Cold War rhetoric.

## you gotta know

- Delivered at the *Brandenburg Gate* in West Berlin on *June 12, 1987*, amid Gorbachev's glasnost and perestroika reforms.
- Reagan's challenge: *"Mr. Gorbachev, if you seek peace, if you seek prosperity for the Soviet Union and Eastern Europe, if you seek liberalization, come here to this gate. Mr. Gorbachev, open this gate. Mr. Gorbachev, tear down this wall."*
- The speech frames the wall not as a mere structure but as a symbol of Communist oppression and the division of Europe.
- Reagan locates the wall's true cause in ideology, not politics: *"We must press on"* toward freedom, implying the wall's days are numbered.
- Emphasizes the moral bankruptcy of regimes requiring walls to keep citizens in: *"What keeps the wall up are lies and the repression of the people."*
- The speech's prescience — delivered when the Cold War seemed permanent — enhanced its historical resonance after the wall fell in *November 1989*.
- Marked a shift in Reagan's tone from earlier confrontation toward a vision of Soviet reform and ultimate victory for the West.
