# Libertarian Party

## summary

The Libertarian Party, founded in 1971, is the longest-standing active third party in the United States, advocating for minimal government intervention in both economics and personal liberties. Drawing votes primarily from disaffected Republicans and fiscally conservative independents, the party has run a presidential candidate in every general election since 1972, though its electoral impact has remained limited, typically capturing 1–3% of the popular vote.

## you gotta know

- The party emerged from a coalition of libertarian intellectuals (Ludwig von Mises Institute followers) and Ron Paul supporters frustrated by both major parties' interventionism and hawkishness.
- Libertarian candidates have included Roger MacBride (1976), Ed Clark (1980), Harry Browne (1996, 2000), and Gary Johnson (2012, 2016), with Johnson's 2016 campaign attracting nearly 3.3% of the popular vote.
- The party opposes most government regulation and taxation, favors drug legalization, advocates for non-interventionist foreign policy, and champions individual liberty across social and economic dimensions.
- Despite never winning a presidential election, Libertarians have elected several candidates to state legislatures and local office, establishing a modest but persistent institutional presence.
- The party functions as a pressure valve for Republican-leaning voters frustrated with social conservatism or military spending, and for Democratic-leaning voters hostile to economic regulation.
- Internal factions dispute pragmatism (seeking state-level wins and influence) versus purism (demanding ideological consistency on property rights and non-aggression), causing recurring splits.
