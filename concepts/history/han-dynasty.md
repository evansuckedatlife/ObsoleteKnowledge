# Han Dynasty

## summary

The *Han Dynasty* (206 BCE–220 CE) was China's second imperial dynasty and one of its most influential, lasting over 400 years and establishing many cultural norms that defined Chinese civilization. Founded by *Liu Bang*, a low-born rebel leader who emerged victorious from the civil chaos following Qin's collapse, the Han synthesized Qin's administrative apparatus with a more moderate, Confucian ideology. The dynasty expanded the empire to its greatest territorial extent, established the *Silk Road* trade networks, and elevated Confucianism to state orthodoxy. The Han period itself splits into two phases: the *Western (or Former) Han* (206 BCE–9 CE), centered in Chang'an, and the *Eastern (or Later) Han* (25–220 CE), centered in Luoyang, with a brief interruption by the *Xin Dynasty*.

## you gotta know

### Foundation and Early Reign
- *Liu Bang* (Emperor Gaozu, r. 206–195 BCE), a commoner and former soldier, defeated rival warlords and established the Han Dynasty.
- Adopted a *mixed system*: retained Qin's centralized bureaucracy but allowed regional kingdoms more autonomy than Qin (though gradually reasserted central control).
- Promoted *Confucianism* over Qin's harsh Legalism, softening governance while maintaining the empire's administrative structure.

### Expansion and Military Power
- Expanded the empire to include Vietnam, Korea, Manchuria, and territories reaching into Central Asia.
- Conducted campaigns against the *Xiongnu* nomads to the north, establishing the *Silk Road* trade routes (1st century BCE onward).
- Built a professional standing army and maintained it through expanded taxation and economic growth.

### The Silk Road
- Trade networks emerged connecting Han China with Central Asia, India, and eventually Rome.
- Not merely trade; facilitated the spread of Buddhism from India into China, reshaping Chinese intellectual life.
- Brought exotic goods (glass, spices, precious metals) and ideas (astronomy, philosophy) into the Han realm.

### Intellectual and Artistic Flourishing
- *Emperor Wu* (r. 141–87 BCE) established the *Imperial University*, training bureaucrats in Confucian classics.
- Confucianism became the *state ideology*; Confucian scholars filled the imperial bureaucracy.
- Advanced *astronomy*, *mathematics*, and *medicine*; invented *paper* and *printing techniques* (precursors to woodblock printing).

### Women in Power
- *Empress Lü* (widow of Liu Bang) ruled as regent (195–180 BCE), wielding imperial power after her husband's death.
- Later dynasties saw *imperial mothers* and *empress dowagers* serve as regents, establishing a pattern of female political influence.

### Decline and Fragmentation
- *Western Han* weakened by expensive wars, nomadic pressure, and land concentration among the wealthy.
- *Xin Dynasty* (9–23 CE): *Wang Mang* usurped the throne, attempted radical reforms, and collapsed in rebellion.
- *Eastern Han* (25–220 CE) restored the dynasty but never recovered Western Han power; fragmented into regional warlordism by 220 CE.

## context

The Han Dynasty defined what it meant to be Chinese for centuries afterward. While Qin proved that centralization was possible, Han proved it was *sustainable*—and far more effective when combined with Confucian ethics and intellectual tolerance. The dynasty's embrace of Confucianism set the ideological template for imperial rule: the emperor as a moral exemplar, surrounded by educated bureaucrats selected by merit (ideally through examination, though the true civil-service examination system crystallized later). The Silk Road was not a single trade route but a network of exchanges—commercial, cultural, and intellectual—that brought Buddhism into East Asia and positioned Han China as the center of a vast Eurasian world. By the Eastern Han, the empire had begun to fragment, but the model of Han civilization proved so robust that every successor dynasty sought to reclaim it; "Han" itself became the ethnic and cultural marker for the Chinese majority.
