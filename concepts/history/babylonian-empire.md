# Babylonian Empire

## summary

Babylon was a Mesopotamian city-state that rose to imperial dominance twice: first under Hammurabi (1792–1750 BCE), who conquered and unified southern Mesopotamia and codified the famous *Code of Hammurabi*, and again under Nebuchadnezzar II (605–562 BCE), who expanded the empire to its territorial peak and rebuilt the city as a magnificent administrative and cultural center. The Old Babylonian period under Hammurabi introduced law as a tool of centralized power, while the Neo-Babylonian period under Nebuchadnezzar represented the final flourishing of Mesopotamian civilization before the arrival of the Persians. Babylon's legacy—its legal system, its scholars, and its enduring name—made it synonymous with Mesopotamian power and learning throughout antiquity.

## you gotta know

### Old Babylonian Period (Hammurabi)
- *Hammurabi* (r. 1792–1750 BCE) inherited a small city-state and conquered a regional empire in southern Mesopotamia through military campaigns and diplomacy.
- The *Code of Hammurabi*—a formal legal text of 282 laws—established the principle that rulers enforce uniform law and established social hierarchy and punishment proportional to crime (the "eye for an eye" principle).
- Under Hammurabi, Babylon became a center of mathematics and astronomy; scribes developed algebraic notation and predicted lunar cycles centuries before Greek astronomy.

### Neo-Babylonian Period (Nebuchadnezzar II)
- *Nebuchadnezzar II* (r. 605–562 BCE) conquered Syria, Phoenicia, and besieged *Jerusalem* (586 BCE), deporting the Hebrew elite and establishing Babylonian hegemony over the Levant.
- Nebuchadnezzar rebuilt Babylon into a showpiece: the *Ishtar Gate*, the *Tower of Babel*, and the *Hanging Gardens* (possibly) made it the ancient world's most magnificent city.
- The empire maintained power through a bureaucratic system of provinces, standing armies, and administrative schools—inheriting the Akkadian model and perfecting it.

### Decline
- Babylon fell to the *Persian* *Cyrus the Great* in 539 BCE without a siege; the populace welcomed the Persians as liberators from the authoritarian last Babylonian kings.
- Babylon remained a major city under Persian, Seleucid, and Parthian rule but never regained empire status.

## context

Babylon represents the maturation of Mesopotamian imperial culture. Where Akkad pioneered centralized rule through military force, Babylon added *law* as a legitimizing tool: a monarch was now accountable to public codes, not just military success. The Code of Hammurabi ensured that conquest had a *civilian face*—subjects expected justice, not just obedience. The Neo-Babylonian period showed the apotheosis of this model: magnificent architecture, advanced science, and cultural prestige made Babylon aspirational, not merely feared. In quiz bowl, Babylon tests depth: Hammurabi and his law code are accessible; Nebuchadnezzar, the Hanging Gardens, and the Ishtar Gate separate the prepared from the novice. The empire also serves as a window into a persistent historical tension—between legitimacy through law and legitimacy through power.
