# Zoot Suit

## summary

The *zoot suit* is a flashy men's outfit featuring high-waisted, wide-legged, tapered trousers and a long coat with wide lapels and exaggerated shoulders, originating in the 1930s and reaching peak popularity during World War II. Adopted primarily by young Black, Latino, and Asian American men as a symbol of style, rebellion, and cultural identity, the zoot suit became a flashpoint for racial tensions and generational conflict.

## you gotta know

- Named possibly from "suit" rhyming slang or from the Yiddish "zoot" (an ornament or fancy item); origins are debated but the style emerged in New York and Los Angeles in the 1930s.
- Characterized by exaggerated proportions: high-waisted trousers with a 20–50 inch leg opening, deep pleats, and a long coat (sometimes reaching the knees) with wide lapels and broad shoulders.
- Required significant amounts of fabric at a time of wartime rationing (1942–1945), making the zoot suit a visible symbol of extravagance and deliberate disregard for wartime austerity.
- Paired with wide-brimmed fedoras, long watch chains, and wing-tipped shoes; the complete ensemble was expensive and marked the wearer as fashionable, confident, and often working-class but upwardly mobile.
- Worn as a statement of racial and cultural pride by young men excluded from mainstream fashion and society; the suit's boldness was an act of cultural assertion.
- Became the target of violent racial attacks, most notably during the 1943 Zoot Suit Riots in Los Angeles when U.S. military personnel attacked young zoot-suited men, particularly Mexican Americans.
- Symbolized the tensions between youth culture and adult authority, wartime conformity and individualism; the suit declined after World War II as fashion normalized and racial violence made its use riskier.
