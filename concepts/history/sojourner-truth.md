# Sojourner Truth

## summary

*Sojourner Truth* (c. 1797–1883), born *Isabella Baumfree*, was an African American abolitionist and women's rights activist who escaped slavery in New York and became a powerful orator. Her speeches, especially "Ain't I a Woman?" (1851), articulated the intersecting struggles of Black women and demanded recognition of women's humanity and labor.

## you gotta know

- Born enslaved in New York; escaped to freedom in 1826 with her infant daughter, then worked as a domestic servant and preacher.
- Took the name *Sojourner Truth* in the 1840s, describing herself as a wanderer spreading the truth of God and human equality.
- Delivered her most famous speech at the Women's Rights Convention in Akron, Ohio (1851), challenging the notion that women were too delicate for equality by pointing to her own work as a slave and field laborer.
- Traveled throughout the North speaking against slavery, for women's rights, and for Black civil rights, often facing hostile audiences and violence.
- Her famous rhetorical question — "Ain't I a Woman?" — exposed the racism within the women's movement by pointing to the erasure of Black women's experiences.
- After emancipation, continued advocating for land redistribution and education for freed people, connecting liberation struggles across race and gender.

## connections

- [[slavery]] — the experience that shaped her understanding of women's and Black Americans' exploitation.
- [[abolitionism]] — her primary cause, inseparable from women's rights in her analysis.
- [[womens-rights]] — she brought a racialized, working-class perspective often missing from white middle-class suffragists.
- [[elizabeth-cady-stanton]] — contemporary women's rights leader whose movement Sojourner Truth both supported and critiqued.
