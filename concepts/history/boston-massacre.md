## summary

The **Boston Massacre** was a violent street confrontation on March 5, 1770, in colonial Boston between British soldiers and an angry crowd of American colonists. Tensions between occupying redcoats and local residents had escalated over months of colonial resistance to parliamentary taxation and trade restrictions. When soldiers fired into the crowd, killing five civilians including Crispus Attucks, the incident became a powerful symbol of British tyranny and accelerated colonial grievances toward independence. The event was weaponized by patriot propagandists, particularly through Paul Revere's engraving and the trial afterward.

## you gotta know

### The Confrontation
- Occurred in front of the Custom House on King Street (now State Street) during a tense March evening.
- A crowd of colonists threw snowballs and taunts at a small British guard unit; soldiers felt threatened.
- Captain Thomas Preston ordered his men to fire; seven shots rang out in rapid succession.

### The Victims
- Five people were killed: *Crispus Attucks* (a mixed-race laborer), *James Caldwell*, *Samuel Gray*, *Samuel Maverick*, and *Patrick Carr*.
- *Attucks* was shot first and killed instantly, often celebrated as the first casualty of the American Revolution.
- Several others were wounded but survived; Carr died later from his injuries.

### Aftermath and Trial
- The soldiers were arrested and charged with murder; defended by future President *John Adams*, who secured acquittals for all but two (who were convicted of manslaughter and branded on the hand).
- Patriots seized on the incident to fuel anti-British sentiment; Samuel Adams and the Sons of Liberty publicized it relentlessly.
- *Paul Revere*'s propagandistic engraving depicted soldiers deliberately firing on a defenseless crowd—a distortion that became the enduring image.

### Legacy
- Commemorated annually on March 5 in Boston; Attucks Street and Massacre Lane mark the site.
- Strengthened colonial unity against British rule and vindicated resistance movements ahead of the Revolution.

## context

The Boston Massacre exemplifies how a single episode of violence can be transformed by political rhetoric into a rallying cry. The event itself was chaotic and the soldiers' culpability disputed—even sympathetic witnesses testified they fired in self-defense—yet patriot organizers crafted a narrative of cold-blooded murder by occupiers on civilians. This mythology proved potent; the massacre became Exhibit A in the case for British cruelty, overshadowing the legal reality that most soldiers walked free. In American historical memory, it marks a hinge: the moment bloodshed made reconciliation with Britain seem impossible.
