# Lindbergh Kidnapping

## summary

In March 1932, the 20-month-old son of aviator *Charles Lindbergh* was abducted from his New Jersey home; despite a ransom payment, the child was found dead weeks later. The subsequent investigation and trial of suspect *Bruno Hauptmann* became the "trial of the century," dominating American media and establishing forensic techniques including ladder analysis and handwriting comparison that transformed criminal investigation.

## you gotta know

- *Charles Lindbergh* Jr., 20 months old, was taken from the Lindbergh home in Hopewell, New Jersey on March 1, 1932; a ransom note demanded $50,000.
- Lindbergh and intermediaries paid a $50,000 ransom, but the child was never returned alive; his body was discovered in a wooded area on May 12, 1932, with evidence of blunt-force trauma.
- *Bruno Hauptmann*, a German-born carpenter and paroled burglar, was arrested in September 1934 after ransom money traceable to the payment was found in his possession.
- The trial (January–February 1935) featured expert testimony on the wooden ladder used in the kidnapping; forensic comparison of wood grain and tool marks established linkage to Hauptmann's tools and lumber.
- Handwriting experts testified that Hauptmann wrote the ransom notes; evidence of his financial desperation and prior burglaries supported motive.
- Hauptmann was convicted and executed by electric chair in April 1936; he maintained his innocence throughout, and lingering questions about trial conduct and evidence reliability persist.
- The case established pathways for forensic analysis in American law and demonstrated the power of scientific testimony in high-profile trials.
