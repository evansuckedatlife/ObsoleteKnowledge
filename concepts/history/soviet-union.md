# Soviet Union

## summary

The Soviet Union was a communist state established in 1922 after the Russian Revolution, comprising a federation of Soviet socialist republics led by the Communist Party and spanning the vast territory of the former Russian Empire. Founded by *Vladimir Lenin* and built into a totalitarian superpower by *Joseph Stalin*, the USSR industrialized at breakneck speed, conducted massive social engineering through collectivization and purges, and emerged from *World War II* as one of the world's two superpowers. The regime pursued revolutionary communist ideology while maintaining absolute party control, creating a planned economy and a one-party state that served as the model for communist regimes worldwide. The Soviet Union collapsed in 1991 after decades of economic stagnation, internal contradictions, and failed reform, reshaping the post-Cold War world.

## you gotta know

### revolution and founding

- The *Russian Revolution* began with the *February Revolution* (1917), overthrowing *Tsar Nicholas II*, and concluded with the *Bolshevik seizure of power* in October 1917 under *Lenin*.
- Lenin's *Bolsheviks* won the *Russian Civil War* (1918–1922) against the *White* anti-communist forces, establishing a dictatorship of the proletariat supposedly representing workers' interests.
- The *Union of Soviet Socialist Republics* was formally created in 1922, uniting Russia, Ukraine, Byelorussia, and the Caucasian republics under a centralized communist party hierarchy.

### Leninism and early policy

- Lenin introduced the *New Economic Policy* (NEP, 1921–1928), a pragmatic retreat from war communism that allowed limited market mechanisms while maintaining party control over heavy industry and politics.
- The regime imposed *atheism* as state doctrine, persecuting the Russian Orthodox Church and other religions to eliminate competing sources of loyalty and authority.
- Lenin's death in 1924 sparked a succession struggle; *Joseph Stalin* eventually defeated rivals like *Trotsky*, consolidating power by the late 1920s.

### Stalin and totalitarian consolidation

- Stalin pursued *Five-Year Plans* (beginning 1928) to rapidly industrialize the economy and build military power through centralized state planning, creating massive factories and infrastructure.
- The *Collectivization* of agriculture (1928–1933) forcibly consolidated peasant farms into collective units (*kolkhozes*), causing widespread famine—the *Ukrainian Famine* (Holodomor) killed millions.
- *Stalin's Purges* (1936–1938) eliminated perceived enemies of the revolution, executing generals, party officials, and intellectuals on fabricated charges, creating a climate of terror and paranoia.

### World War II and emergence as superpower

- The USSR was invaded by Nazi Germany in 1941 (*Operation Barbarossa*), forcing an alliance with Britain and the United States against the Axis powers.
- Soviet forces bore the brunt of the Nazi war effort, suffering over 25 million casualties but ultimately defeating Germany in a brutal campaign that pushed Soviet armies to Berlin.
- The USSR emerged from World War II as a superpower, having liberated Eastern Europe from Nazi occupation and establishing communist satellite states under Soviet control.

### Cold War and ideological competition

- The *Cold War* (1945–1991) was a global conflict between Soviet communism and American capitalism, fought through proxy wars, nuclear arms race, space competition, and ideological propaganda.
- The USSR built a vast military apparatus, including nuclear weapons (first tested in 1949), developing an intercontinental missile capability that created the threat of *mutually assured destruction* (MAD).
- Soviet-backed communism spread to Eastern Europe, China, Vietnam, Cuba, and elsewhere, creating a global communist bloc that challenged Western liberal capitalism.

### internal contradictions and decline

- The Soviet economy was inefficient and stagnant by the 1970s–1980s: centralized planning could not match market economies' flexibility and innovation, and military spending drained resources from civilian sectors.
- *Mikhail Gorbachev* (General Secretary from 1985) attempted reform through *Glasnost* (openness) and *Perestroika* (restructuring), but these policies unleashed forces he could not control.
- The USSR dissolved in 1991 after failed hardline coup attempts, the independence of constituent republics, and Gorbachev's resignation, ending the Cold War and reshaping the global balance of power.

## context

The Soviet Union embodied the 20th century's grand ideological experiment: an attempt to create a communist utopia through centralized state power and revolutionary transformation. It pioneered totalitarianism—using ideology, surveillance, purges, and propaganda to achieve total control over society—creating a model that other communist regimes emulated and that Western scholars studied as the epitome of modern tyranny. The USSR's industrialization and military power allowed it to defeat Nazi Germany and emerge as a superpower, shaping the post-World War II order. Yet the regime's fundamental contradiction—claiming to represent workers' interests while maintaining absolute party control and creating a new bureaucratic elite—created permanent inefficiency and repression. The Soviet system generated scientific and cultural achievements (space exploration, classical ballet, literature) while exacting immense suffering through purges, collectivization famines, and ideological terror. Its collapse demonstrated the limits of centrally-planned economies and totalitarian control, though its legacy persists in Russia's political culture and authoritarian aspirations.
