# Wounded Knee

## summary

The **Wounded Knee Massacre** occurred on December 29, 1890, when the U.S. Seventh Cavalry, commanded by Colonel *James W. Forsyth*, opened fire on an encampment of Lakota Sioux near Wounded Knee Creek in South Dakota, killing approximately 150 to 300 civilians, including women and children. The incident followed the death of *Sitting Bull* and the arrest of other Lakota leaders; the Army claimed the band was armed and resisting, while eyewitness and modern accounts reveal that the killing was largely indiscriminate once gunfire began. Wounded Knee marked the symbolic end of armed Native American resistance and the final gasp of the Indian Wars; it cemented the dispossession and confinement of the Plains tribes to reservations.

## you gotta know

### The Context
- The Lakota were confined to the Pine Ridge Reservation in South Dakota, living in poverty and starvation.
- The *Ghost Dance*, a messianic religious movement spreading among Plains tribes, promised spiritual renewal and the return of bison herds; the Army viewed it as seditious.
- *Sitting Bull*, the legendary Sioux leader, was killed by reservation police on December 15 while being arrested; his death escalated tensions.

### The Encampment
- A band of Lakota, led by *Big Foot*, fled Pine Ridge toward the Stronghold; they were intercepted by the Seventh Cavalry near Wounded Knee Creek.
- The soldiers encircled the camp of roughly 350 Lakota (mostly women, children, and elderly) and demanded surrender of weapons.
- Accounts diverge on whether a scuffle over a rifle or a deliberate order triggered the volley; within moments, soldiers opened rapid fire with rifles and Hotchkiss artillery.

### The Slaughter
- Soldiers fired into the camp for several minutes; women and children were killed while attempting to flee or hide.
- An estimated 150 to 300 Lakota died; some accounts claim higher tolals; at least 25 soldiers also fell in the chaotic crossfire.
- Bodies were left exposed in freezing temperatures for three days before burial in a mass grave.

### Aftermath
- No formal court-martial was held; the soldiers' actions were ruled justified as quelling a rebellion.
- The Lakota's capacity for organized resistance effectively ended; the Ghost Dance movement collapsed.
- Wounded Knee became a symbol of American ethnic cleansing and broke the will of the remaining free tribes to resist militarily.

## context

Wounded Knee is recognized by historians as a war crime and massacre, though the U.S. Army at the time justified it as a combat operation. The event encapsulates the final stage of the Indian Wars: the Army's overwhelming technological and numerical superiority, the breakdown of tribal autonomy and subsistence, the desperation of the Lakota, and the American state's indifference to civilian casualties in pursuit of total conquest. The massacre hardened into American civic mythology as a tragic but inevitable endpoint of westward expansion—a framing that obscured its systematic character. For Native Americans, Wounded Knee became a touchstone of historical injustice and prompted a century-long reckoning with American racism.
