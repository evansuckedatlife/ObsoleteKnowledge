# Sacco and Vanzetti

## summary

In May 1920, *Nicola Sacco* and *Bartolomeo Vanzetti*, Italian immigrant anarchists, were arrested for a payroll robbery and murder in South Braintree, Massachusetts. Convicted largely on contested ballistic evidence and tried in an atmosphere of anti-radical hysteria, they were executed in 1927. The case became a symbol of injustice, judicial bias, and the persecution of political dissidents in post-war America.

## you gotta know

- A payroll robbery and murder occurred on April 15, 1920, in South Braintree, Massachusetts; $15,776 was stolen and two men were killed, including security guard *Frederick Parmenter*.
- *Sacco* (a shoemaker) and *Vanzetti* (a fish peddler), both known anarchist sympathizers, were arrested in May 1920; police suspected them based on their political affiliations and an altercation with police in a prior raid.
- The prosecution's case rested heavily on ballistic evidence: a bullet recovered from the victims allegedly matched a test-fired bullet from Sacco's pistol, analyzed by expert *Charles Van Amburgh*—matching that was later disputed.
- The trial judge, *Webster Thayer*, was openly hostile to the defendants and made disparaging remarks about "anarchistic bastards" that were later documented; the jury was composed of men with anti-anarchist bias.
- Despite weaknesses in identification evidence and ballistic analysis, Sacco and Vanzetti were convicted in July 1921; an appeal process dragged on for six years amid international outrage and demonstrations.
- Both men were executed by electric chair on August 23, 1927; Vanzetti's last words ("I am innocent") reinforced belief in their innocence among supporters and later investigators.
- Modern ballistic analysis and archival re-examination (including the 1961 identification of evidence via newer techniques) have deepened suspicion that Sacco and Vanzetti were innocent or that the evidence was fabricated.
