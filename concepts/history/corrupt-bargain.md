# Corrupt Bargain

## summary

The *Corrupt Bargain* of *1824* refers to the alleged secret deal between *John Quincy Adams* and *Henry Clay* that resulted in *Adams*' election as president despite *Andrew Jackson* winning a plurality of the popular vote. Though no evidence of an actual quid pro quo was ever proven, *Jackson* supporters' accusations of collusion poisoned *Adams*' presidency and dominated American politics for years.

## you gotta know

- In the *1824 presidential election*, four major candidates split the vote; *Andrew Jackson* won a plurality with *41 percent*, followed by *John Quincy Adams*, *William Crawford*, and *Henry Clay*.
- Since no candidate achieved a majority of electoral votes, the election went to the *House of Representatives*, where each state delegation cast one vote.
- *Henry Clay*, the *House Speaker* and a powerful figure, backed *Adams*; *Clay*'s support proved decisive, and *Adams* won in the House on the first ballot with *13* states.
- Days after *Adams* became president, he appointed *Clay* as *Secretary of State*—the office traditionally grooming a successor—fueling *Jackson* supporters' accusations of a "corrupt bargain."
- *Jackson* partisans claimed *Clay* had promised his support to *Adams* in exchange for the Cabinet post, circumventing the democratic will expressed in the popular vote.
- *Adams* and *Clay* both vehemently denied the charge; no documentary evidence of an explicit deal was ever produced, but the accusation stuck and severely damaged *Adams*' legitimacy.
- The supposed bargain energized *Jackson* supporters to organize what became the *Democratic Party*; *Jackson* defeated *Adams* decisively in *1828*, and the scandal's legacy shadowed *Adams*' entire presidency.
