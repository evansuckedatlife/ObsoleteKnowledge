## summary

The First Amendment, ratified in 1791 as part of the Bill of Rights, protects the fundamental freedoms of speech, religion, assembly, petition, and press from government restriction. It begins "Congress shall make no law respecting an establishment of religion, or prohibiting the free exercise thereof; or abridging the freedom of speech, or of the press; or the right of the people peaceably to assemble, and to petition the Government for a redress of grievances." It is the cornerstone of American civil liberties and has been litigated extensively to define the boundaries of free expression.

## context

The First Amendment is perhaps the most cited and contested part of the Constitution, central to American debates over free speech, religious liberty, and protest. Supreme Court interpretations—from *Schenck v. United States* (clear and present danger) to *Brandenburg v. Ohio* (incitement) to *Citizens United* (campaign finance)—have shaped how far First Amendment protection extends. It is foundational to civil rights movements, journalism, and dissent, and remains actively interpreted in modern cases involving social media, hate speech, and religious accommodation.

## you gotta know

### the five protections
- *Freedom of speech*: protects spoken and written expression, including political speech, art, and symbolic speech.
- *Freedom of religion*: both the Establishment Clause (no government establishment of religion) and the Free Exercise Clause (protection for individual religious practice).
- *Freedom of the press*: protects journalists and media organizations from government censorship and prior restraint.
- *Freedom of assembly*: protects the right to peaceful gathering and protest.
- *Freedom of petition*: protects the right to petition the government for redress of grievances.

### landmark cases
- *Schenck v. United States* (1919): established the *clear and present danger* test for speech advocating unlawful action.
- *Brandenburg v. Ohio* (1969): replaced the clear and present danger test with the *incitement test*, requiring speech to be directed to imminent lawless action.
- *New York Times Co. v. Sullivan* (1964): established that libel suits by public figures require proof of *actual malice* (knowing or reckless falsity).
- *Tinker v. Des Moines* (1969): protected student armband protest as symbolic speech unless it caused material disruption.
- *Engel v. Vitale* (1962): struck down state-mandated school prayer as an establishment of religion.

### scope and limits
- The First Amendment applies only to *government* action, not private action (the state action doctrine).
- Some categories of speech receive no First Amendment protection: obscenity, child sexual abuse material, true threats, and incitement to imminent lawless action.
- *Prior restraint* (government censorship before publication) faces the highest constitutional scrutiny and is rarely justified.
