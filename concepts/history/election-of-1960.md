# Election of 1960

## summary

John F. Kennedy's razor-thin victory over Vice President Richard Nixon in 1960 marked a generational shift in American politics and proved the power of media imagery in the television age. Kennedy won by 118,550 votes (0.17 percent of the popular vote) and 303 electoral votes, winning key industrial states while also capturing strong Catholic support. The election's decisive moment was widely believed to be the first televised presidential debate, where Kennedy's youth and composure on camera outshone Nixon's pallid appearance and defensive posture.

## you gotta know

### The Candidates and the Matchup
- *John F. Kennedy*, a young Catholic senator from Massachusetts, faced *Richard Nixon*, the two-term vice president and embodiment of Eisenhower-era establishment politics.
- Kennedy positioned himself as an agent of change and vigor; Nixon campaigned as experienced and steady, warning that Kennedy was reckless and unprepared.
- The religious issue was paramount: Kennedy was the first major-party Catholic nominee since 1928, and many Protestant voters feared Vatican influence.

### The Televised Debate and Its Impact
- The first of four televised debates, held September 26, 1960, showed Kennedy composed and telegenic; Nixon looked haggard and defensive (he was recovering from illness).
- Polls showed viewers who watched on television favored Kennedy; radio listeners favored Nixon—demonstrating the emerging power of visual media.
- Kennedy's image of youth and vigor contrasted with Eisenhower's aging administration and Cold War stalemate.

### The Outcome and Realignment
- Kennedy won narrowly but decisively in the electoral college, capturing Illinois and Texas on razor-thin margins.
- His victory symbolized a generational changing-of-the-guard and energized younger, more liberal Democrats.
- The civil rights issue loomed; Kennedy's support from Black voters exceeded Democratic norms, anticipating the civil rights agenda that would dominate his presidency.

## context

The 1960 election was a watershed for American political communication. Television's emergence as the dominant medium meant charisma, appearance, and camera presence now mattered as much as policy positions. Kennedy's photogenic quality—his Massachusetts accent, his composed demeanor, his relative youth at 43—translated into electoral advantage. The election also marked the beginning of the New Deal Coalition's slow dissolution: Kennedy's narrow margin was a sign that the Democratic lock on working-class voters was weakening. Yet his victory proved that the coalition, though strained, could still produce a victory. The civil rights crisis that would define the next four years was already visible: Kennedy's support from Black voters, mobilized by his intervention with Coretta Scott King after her husband's arrest, previewed the Democratic Party's reorientation toward civil rights that would cost it the white South.
