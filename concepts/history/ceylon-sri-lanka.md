# Ceylon → Sri Lanka

## summary

*Ceylon* was the island nation's name during British colonial rule, derived from Portuguese and Dutch corruption of Sanskrit *Ceilao*. Upon gaining independence in 1948, the nation gradually transitioned to *Sri Lanka* (meaning "blessed island" or "resplendent island" in Sinhalese and Sanskrit), formally adopting the name in 1972 as part of a new republican constitution. The change represented a return to the island's indigenous heritage and a break from colonial nomenclature.

## you gotta know

- The name *Ceylon* derives from 16th-century Portuguese transliteration of indigenous names, perpetuated through Dutch and British rule.
- Britain colonized Ceylon in 1796 after ousting the Dutch, making it a crown jewel of the Raj for their tea and rubber plantations.
- *Sri Lanka* was adopted officially in 1972 when the nation became a republic, replacing the colonial name and the title "Dominion of Ceylon."
- The island's Sinhalese majority comprises about 75% of the population, with Tamil minorities concentrated in the north; the name change elevated Sinhalese cultural identity.
- Ceylon was a major center of Theravada Buddhism and home to the sacred *Temple of the Tooth* (Dalada Maligawa) in Kandy.
- The transition from Ceylon to Sri Lanka symbolized postcolonial assertion, though the nation faced civil conflict between Sinhalese and Tamil communities (1983–2009).

## connections

- [[british-raj]] — the colonial framework that ruled Ceylon as a strategic crown possession for nearly 150 years.
- [[sinhalese-people]] — the ethnic majority whose language (Sinhala) and cultural identity anchor the name "Sri Lanka."
- [[tamil-minorities-in-sri-lanka]] — the significant northern population with historical ties to South India, whose political marginalization defined postcolonial conflict.
- [[colombo]] — the colonial-era capital and modern economic hub, exemplifying Ceylon/Sri Lanka's urban heritage.
- [[theravada-buddhism]] — the island's dominant religious tradition, central to Sinhalese identity.
