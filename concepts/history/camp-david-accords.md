# Camp David Accords

## summary

The *Camp David Accords* (1978) were two frameworks negotiated at the U.S. presidential retreat in Maryland between *Israel*, *Egypt*, and the *United States* (mediated by President *Jimmy Carter*). The accords established a path to peace between *Israel* and *Egypt*—the two powers that had fought four wars since 1948—and outlined principles for broader Middle Eastern peace. The treaty was revolutionary for recognising Israel as a legitimate state within the Arab world, though it ultimately deepened Israel-Arab divisions and did not resolve the Palestinian question.

## you gotta know

### Negotiation & Context
- Signed on *September 17, 1978*, after thirteen days of intensive negotiation at Camp David between *Anwar Sadat* (Egypt), *Menachem Begin* (Israel), and President *Jimmy Carter*.
- *Sadat*, isolated after military defeat to Israel and seeking economic assistance, offered peace; *Begin* sought legitimacy and security; *Carter* brokered the deal to advance Middle Eastern stability.
- The accords were codified in a formal treaty, the *Egyptian-Israeli Peace Treaty* (1979).

### Framework One: Egyptian-Israeli Peace Treaty
- Israel agreed to withdraw from the *Sinai Peninsula*, occupied since the 1967 war, fully returned by 1982.
- Egypt formally recognised Israel's right to exist and agreed to diplomatic relations and trade.
- A demilitarised zone in Sinai was established; the *Suez Canal* remained open to Israeli shipping (previously blockaded).
- First formal peace between Israel and an Arab state; *Sadat* accepted Israel's existence as a sovereign nation.

### Framework Two: Autonomy for Palestinians
- The accords outlined a framework for Palestinian self-government in the *West Bank* and *Gaza Strip* (occupied by Israel).
- A five-year transition period for negotiations was established, but no binding Palestinian state framework emerged.
- Israel's control over settlements, water, and security in the territories remained unresolved.
- The Palestinian Liberation Organisation (PLO) was not a signatory; the framework proved toothless.

### Consequences & Regional Fallout
- *Sadat* was assassinated in 1981 by Islamist extremists who viewed his peace with Israel as betrayal.
- The broader Arab world condemned Egypt and imposed sanctions; the Arab League expelled Egypt for a decade.
- Palestinian hopes for self-determination were deferred; decades of occupation and conflict ensued.
- Israel gained legitimacy in the Middle East and forged strategic ties with Egypt; Arab unity fractured.

## context

The Camp David Accords represent both a triumph and a failure of Middle Eastern diplomacy. Carter's mediation produced the first Arab-Israeli peace, a stunning achievement that won both *Sadat* and *Begin* the Nobel Peace Prize. Yet the accords were a "separate peace"—*Egypt* gained land and aid but made peace alone, leaving the Palestinian question unresolved and the broader Arab-Israeli conflict intact. *Sadat's* decision to accept Israel's existence and make peace, while pragmatic and economically advantageous to Egypt, was seen as a betrayal across the Arab world. His assassination showed the cost of peacemaking in a region where nationalist and religious ideologies rejected compromise. The Palestinian framework proved empty; decades of negotiation (Oslo, Camp David 2000, etc.) have failed to produce Palestinian statehood. The accords thus illustrate the paradox of peace in asymmetric conflicts: a separate agreement may be rational for individual parties but deepens overall divisions and fails to address core grievances. The accords stabilised the Israel-Egypt border but left the Israeli-Palestinian conflict unresolved, a division that defines the region still.
