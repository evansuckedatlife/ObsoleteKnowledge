# United States Constitution

## summary

The United States Constitution, ratified in 1788, is the supreme law of the United States, establishing the framework of the federal government and defining the rights and powers of the states and citizens. It replaced the Articles of Confederation and created a system of separation of powers across three branches (executive, legislative, and judicial). The Constitution is the world's oldest written national constitution still in use and has been amended 27 times since its inception.

## context

The Constitution is the foundational legal and political document that shaped American democracy and continues to frame every major constitutional debate. Its interpretation—whether strict originalism, living constitutionalism, or textualism—drives Supreme Court decisions on civil rights, federalism, executive power, and criminal procedure. It is the bedrock of civic knowledge in the United States and the origin point for understanding American law, rights, and governance.

## you gotta know

### structure
- The Constitution is divided into a *Preamble*, seven *Articles*, and 27 *Amendments*; the first ten amendments are collectively the *Bill of Rights*.
- *Article I* establishes the *Congress* (legislative branch: Senate and House of Representatives).
- *Article II* establishes the presidency and executive branch.
- *Article III* establishes the federal judiciary, including the Supreme Court.
- *Article V* prescribes the amendment process, requiring a two-thirds majority in both houses of Congress (or a constitutional convention called by two-thirds of state legislatures) and ratification by three-fourths of the states.

### creation and ratification
- It was drafted at the *Constitutional Convention* in Philadelphia in the summer of 1787, presided over by George Washington.
- The *Federalist Papers*, essays written by *Alexander Hamilton*, *James Madison*, and *John Jay*, argued for ratification and remain a canonical source for constitutional interpretation.
- The Constitution was ratified in 1788 and went into effect in 1789, replacing the *Articles of Confederation*.

### core principles
- The doctrine of *separation of powers* divides authority among executive, legislative, and judicial branches.
- *Checks and balances* allow each branch to limit the power of the others (e.g., the Senate confirms judges and cabinet members, the President can veto legislation, the Court can strike down laws as unconstitutional).
- The Constitution enshrines *federalism*: power is shared between the national and state governments.
- The Tenth Amendment reserves all powers not delegated to the federal government to the states or to the people.
