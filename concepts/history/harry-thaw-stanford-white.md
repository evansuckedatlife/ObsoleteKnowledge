# Harry Thaw Murder of Stanford White

## summary

In June 1906, wealthy rail heir *Harry Thaw* shot and killed renowned architect *Stanford White* in a public venue over the affections of actress *Evelyn Nesbit*, with whom both men had been involved. Thaw's subsequent trials explored insanity, moral worth, and the boundaries of American justice; though acquitted on grounds of temporary insanity, he remained controversial, and White's death marked a high point in Gilded Age scandal and transgressive behavior.

## you gotta know

- *Stanford White*, celebrated architect and co-founder of McKim, Mead & White, was shot three times at point-blank range by *Harry K. Thaw* on June 25, 1906, atop Madison Square Garden's roof garden in New York.
- *Harry Thaw*, a wealthy railroad heir (Pittston & Lake Superior heir) and known wastrel, had married *Evelyn Nesbit*, a young actress and model, in 1905; she had previously been intimate with White.
- Thaw was consumed with jealous rage, believing White had "ruined" Nesbit before their marriage; Thaw had obsessed over Nesbit's prior relationship with the prominent architect for months.
- The trial (1907) was sensational: Nesbit testified about her intimate history with White; psychiatric experts debated whether Thaw was sane or temporarily insane at the moment of killing.
- Thaw's defense successfully argued "temporary insanity" (inability to control impulse)—a novel legal strategy—and he was acquitted in April 1907; this verdict troubled many who believed him guilty but privileged.
- Thaw was committed to an asylum; he was released and re-committed multiple times over subsequent decades, complicating the case's legal closure and raising questions about justice and wealth.
- The case epitomized Gilded Age sexual scandal, architectural patronage, and the power of wealth to evade full accountability for crime.
