## summary

*Franklin Delano Roosevelt* was the 32nd president of the United States, serving from 1933 until his death in 1945—the longest tenure in American history. A distant cousin of Theodore Roosevelt, FDR led the nation through two monumental crises: the Great Depression and World War II. His expansive vision of federal power, embodied in the *New Deal*, reshaped the role of government in American life. Despite being partially paralyzed by polio, he became a symbol of resilience and transformed presidential leadership into a platform for moral persuasion and national unity.

## you gotta know

### Life and Early Career

- Born in 1882 to a wealthy Hudson Valley family; educated at Harvard and Columbia Law School; served as a New York state senator before his 1910 election.
- Contracted polio in 1921 at age 39, leaving him paralyzed from the waist down; kept his disability largely hidden from the public and continued his political career with determination.
- Served as Assistant Secretary of the Navy under President Woodrow Wilson (1913–1920), where he helped oversee naval operations during World War I.
- Elected governor of New York in 1928; ran a moderate reform administration and gained national prominence by responding to the Depression.

### The Presidency and the Great Depression (1933–1939)

- Took office during the banking crisis of March 1933; declared a bank holiday and rallied Americans with his famous first inaugural address: "the only thing we have to fear is fear itself."
- Enacted the *New Deal*, a comprehensive program of federal relief, recovery, and reform—including the Works Progress Administration (WPA), Social Security, the Civilian Conservation Corps (CCC), and the National Labor Relations Board.
- Shifted American political philosophy by arguing the federal government had a responsibility to ensure the welfare of its citizens; his policies expanded executive and legislative power dramatically.
- Faced fierce opposition from conservatives and the Supreme Court (which declared several early New Deal laws unconstitutional); responded with the "court-packing" plan of 1937, which failed but was a watershed moment in constitutional power.

### Foreign Policy and World War II (1939–1945)

- Navigated American isolationism while supporting Britain and China against Nazi Germany and Imperial Japan through *Lend-Lease* (1941) and other aid programs.
- Met with British Prime Minister *Winston Churchill* twice—at the *Atlantic Conference* (1941) and in *Casablanca* (1943)—to coordinate war strategy and establish the *Atlantic Charter*.
- Brought America into World War II after the attack on Pearl Harbor (December 7, 1941); directed the *unconditional surrender* doctrine and the *strategic bombing campaign* against Axis powers.
- Coordinated with Soviet Premier *Joseph Stalin* at the *Yalta Conference* (February 1945), where decisions were made about the postwar division of Europe and the creation of the United Nations.
- Died in office on April 12, 1945, just weeks before Germany's surrender and the end of the war in Europe; Vice President *Harry Truman* assumed the presidency.

## context

Franklin Roosevelt stands as one of the transformative figures in American history—a bridge between the limited-government tradition of the 19th century and the expansive welfare-state liberalism of the 20th. His presidency established the precedent that the federal government would actively intervene in the economy, provide a social safety net, and take a commanding role in global affairs. The New Deal created institutions (Social Security, the NLRB, the SEC) that survived his death and structured American capitalism for decades. Equally, his leadership during World War II demonstrated the capacity of democratic institutions to mobilize for total war and established America's role as a superpower in the postwar order. He appears constantly in quiz bowl—as a touchstone for Depression-era policy, as the architect of the modern executive branch, and as a defining figure of the 20th century's geopolitical reordering.

## connections

- [[theodore-roosevelt]] — distant cousin; also president; FDR admired his expansionism and progressive reformism.
- [[eleanor-roosevelt]] — his wife and political partner; she became a powerful advocate for civil rights and humanitarian causes.
- [[harry-truman]] — his vice president in his fourth term; succeeded him and made the decision to drop atomic bombs on Japan.
- [[winston-churchill]] — British Prime Minister; closest wartime ally; together they shaped the Allied strategy against Nazi Germany.
- [[joseph-stalin]] — Soviet Premier; FDR cooperated with him to defeat Hitler but their postwar vision conflicted, setting the stage for the Cold War.
- [[woodrow-wilson]] — predecessor whose League of Nations concept inspired FDR's United Nations vision.
- [[the-great-depression]] — the economic crisis whose severity defined FDR's first term and justified his New Deal programs.
- [[world-war-ii]] — the global conflict that consumed FDR's second, third, and fourth terms and defined his foreign policy legacy.
- [[the-new-deal]] — the suite of federal programs that became synonymous with his name and reshaped American governance.
