# Simone de Beauvoir

## summary

*Simone de Beauvoir* (1908–1986) was a French existentialist philosopher whose *The Second Sex* (1949) became the canonical text of second-wave feminism. She argued that women are not born but *made* — that femininity is a social construct imposed on women by patriarchy — and that authentic freedom required women's economic and sexual independence.

## you gotta know

- Wrote *The Second Sex*, a 1,000-page philosophical and historical analysis of women's oppression, arguing that women are treated as the "Other" to man's universal subject.
- Coined the phrase "One is not born, but rather becomes, a woman," establishing that gender is constructed, not essential — foundational to modern feminist theory.
- Lived in a non-marital partnership with philosopher *Jean-Paul Sartre*, modeling intellectual equality and personal freedom outside traditional marriage.
- Argued that women's liberation required access to work, education, contraception, and abortion—not moral superiority or separate spheres.
- Wrote novels, memoirs, and essays extending her analysis to race, colonialism, and aging, positioning women's liberation as connected to universal human freedom.
- Remained politically active in the French left throughout her life, supporting civil rights, decolonization, and reproductive rights.
