# Leopold and Loeb

## summary

In 1924, two wealthy Chicago teenagers, *Nathan Leopold* and *Richard Loeb*, murdered 14-year-old *Bobby Franks* in what they conceived as a "perfect crime" motivated by intellectual thrill-seeking and a twisted philosophical belief in their own superiority. The case captivated the nation, and their trial—defended by renowned attorney *Clarence Darrow*—became a watershed moment in debates over juvenile justice, determinism, and the death penalty.

## you gotta know

- *Nathan Leopold* (19) and *Richard Loeb* (18), both sons of wealthy Chicago families and University of Chicago students, kidnapped and murdered 14-year-old *Bobby Franks* on May 21, 1924.
- The crime was self-conceived as an intellectual exercise—a "perfect crime" motivated by thrill-seeking and a quasi-Nietzschean philosophy that they, as superior minds, were above morality.
- Loeb was the nephew of the prominent lawyer *Albert Loeb*; Leopold was a polyglot ornithologist and law student. Their intellectual credentials were emphasized in media coverage.
- Police recovered a pair of eyeglasses at the crime scene; the unusual lens prescription led detectives to Leopold, who confessed along with Loeb within days.
- Clarence Darrow, one of America's most famous trial attorneys, defended them; rather than mount a not-guilty plea, he sought to spare them execution through a mitigation hearing.
- Darrow's closing argument—12 hours long—appealed to the judge to consider their youth, psychological makeup, and the evolving rejection of capital punishment; the judge sentenced them to life imprisonment instead of death.
- Leopold served 63 years in prison, paroled in 1958; Loeb was killed by a fellow inmate in 1936. The case reshaped American discourse on juvenile crime and capital punishment.
