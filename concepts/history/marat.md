## summary

*Jean-Paul Marat* (1743–1793) was a radical journalist and physician whose inflammatory publications and fiery speeches drove the French Revolution's most violent phase. Lionized as a champion of the poor and a martyr to the cause, his assassination by a royalist sympathizer made him a revolutionary hero and a rallying symbol for escalating terror.

## you gotta know

- Founded and edited *L'Ami du Peuple* (The Friend of the People), a radical newspaper that called for mass executions of suspected enemies and royalist sympathizers.
- Served as a deputy to the National Convention and was a powerful voice on the Mountain (the radical faction) demanding purges and violence.
- Suffered from a chronic skin disease (likely dermatitis) that confined him to a medicinal bath; worked from his tub even during political meetings, reinforcing his image as a martyr to the cause.
- Accused the Girondins (moderate republicans) of betraying the revolution and pushed for their arrest and execution in June 1793.
- Assassinated on July 13, 1793, by *Charlotte Corday*, a royalist sympathizer who stabbed him in his bath; his death made him a revolutionary icon.
- His funeral was a massive public ceremony; his image was immortalized in paintings and revolutionary propaganda as the "Friend of the People."
- His radicalism and cult of personality accelerated the revolution's turn toward systematic terror.
