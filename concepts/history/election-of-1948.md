# Election of 1948

## summary

Harry Truman's shocking upset victory over heavily favored Thomas Dewey in 1948 defied virtually every poll and political prediction of the era. Running against a do-nothing Republican Congress and a fractured Democratic Party split by southern segregationists (Thurmond's States' Rights Democrats) and Henry Wallace's Progressive Party, Truman won 49.6 percent of the popular vote and 303 electoral votes. His comeback from near-certain defeat became a defining symbol of American democratic unpredictability and vindicated his aggressive anti-communist foreign policy.

## you gotta know

### The Four-Way Contest
- *Thomas Dewey*, the heavily favored Republican governor, was so confident he took few campaign risks; polls showed him winning by double digits.
- *Harry Truman*, the incumbent Democrat, campaigned as a scrappy underdog, attacking the Republican "do-nothing Congress" with fiery rhetoric.
- *Strom Thurmond's* States' Rights Democrats captured the white South's segregationist vote; *Henry Wallace's* Progressive Party split the liberal left.
- The Democratic base seemed irretrievably fractured; Truman appeared finished.

### The Upset Victory
- Truman won the popular vote narrowly and the electoral college decisively, carrying key industrial states (Ohio, Illinois, Pennsylvania).
- His famous "whistle-stop" campaign tour—traveling by train and speaking from the rear car—energized working-class voters and attacked Dewey's elitism.
- The Chicago Tribune's premature "Dewey Defeats Truman" headline became an iconic symbol of prediction failure and democratic surprise.

### The Realignment
- The States' Rights defection proved temporary; southern Democrats returned by 1952 but grew increasingly alienated by the party's civil rights stance.
- Truman's victory validated his foreign policy: aggressive containment of communism, support for Israel, and the Marshall Plan.

## context

The 1948 election was a vindication of popular will over elite consensus. The press, pollsters, and professional politicians had written Truman off; the voters had not. The election also crystallized the emerging Cold War alignment: Truman's anti-communist credentials, burnished by his recent Berlin Airlift (1948–49), appealed to working-class and ethnic voters who feared Soviet expansion. The Democratic Party's fracture into three wings—mainstream, southern segregationist, and progressive—previewed the civil rights crisis that would rip the party apart in the 1960s. Yet Truman's victory proved the New Deal Coalition was still viable, if strained. The election also demonstrated that polling, while sophisticated, was fallible—a lesson that would echo in later upset victories.
