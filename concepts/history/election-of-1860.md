# Election of 1860

## summary

Abraham Lincoln's 1860 election victory without a single southern electoral vote precipitated the secession crisis that exploded into the American Civil War. Running on a platform of free soil—opposing slavery's expansion into new territories—Lincoln faced a fractured opposition: the Democratic Party split into Northern and Southern wings, while a fourth-party Constitutional Union candidate attempted to paper over sectional divisions. Lincoln's victory convinced southern slaveholders that their political dominance was irreversible and that secession was the only recourse.

## you gotta know

### The Candidates and the Fracture
- *Abraham Lincoln* (Republican) won with 40 percent of the popular vote and 180 electoral votes, carrying all free states.
- *Stephen Douglas* (Northern Democrat) competed for the center but was hemmed in by *John Breckinridge* (Southern Democrat), who openly advocated slavery's expansion.
- *John Bell* (Constitutional Union) ran a fusionist ticket, calling for national reconciliation, but drew only Tennessee, Kentucky, and Virginia.

### Lincoln's Platform and Southern Reaction
- Lincoln opposed slavery's *expansion* into new territories; he did not campaign on abolition, but the South saw his presidency as existential.
- Slavery's political power had been declining as free states gained population; Lincoln's victory signaled the end of pro-slavery dominance.
- South Carolina seceded in December 1860; by April 1861, the Confederacy had formed and Fort Sumter had fallen.

### Electoral and Constitutional Significance
- Lincoln became the first Republican president, representing a new sectional coalition: free-state voters and former Free Soil advocates.
- The 1860 election exposed the electoral system's regional polarization: Lincoln won zero southern electors; Breckinridge won zero northern ones.

## context

The 1860 election crystallized the nation's three-decade struggle over slavery's future. Southern slaveholders had built a political machine—the "Slave Power"—that controlled the presidency and Senate through the three-fifths clause and the electoral college's rural bias. Lincoln's victory meant that machine was permanently broken: no pro-slavery candidate could win the presidency with northern opposition. Faced with the prospect of a Republican-dominated government hostile to slavery's expansion and potentially its survival, the South chose secession. The election thus marks the point where the electoral system itself became the mechanism for the Union's dissolution—the demos spoke, and eleven states rejected that verdict by leaving the Union.
