# Rhodesia → Zimbabwe

## summary

*Rhodesia* was the name of the British colony in south-central Africa, named after the imperialist *Cecil Rhodes*, who chartered the *British South Africa Company* to colonize the region in the 1890s. After a brutal fourteen-year guerrilla war (1966–1980), the nation achieved independence and adopted the name *Zimbabwe*, derived from the *Shona* word for "house of stone," referencing the Great Zimbabwe ruins that symbolized indigenous African civilization. The renaming was a powerful decolonial assertion of African identity and sovereignty.

## you gotta know

- *Rhodesia* was named after *Cecil Rhodes*, the mining magnate and imperial adventurer whose company seized the territory under a fraudulent charter.
- Britain declared Rhodesia a self-governing colony in 1923, but refused majority-rule independence, leading to decades of escalating racial tensions.
- The *Zimbabwean Bush War* (1966–1980) saw nationalist guerrillas led by *Robert Mugabe* and *Joshua Nkomo* fight the white-minority Rhodesian government.
- The nation was renamed *Zimbabwe* upon independence in 1980, honoring the *Great Zimbabwe*, a 13th–15th century Shona civilization whose stone ruins dominated the landscape.
- The name *Zimbabwe* reclaims indigenous heritage; "Zimbabwe" derives from Shona *dzimbabwe*, meaning "house of stone" or "great house."
- Rhodesian independence was delayed until black nationalist pressure became overwhelming; the 1980 transition marked a turning point for sub-Saharan African decolonization.
