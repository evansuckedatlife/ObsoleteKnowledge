# Flapper Dress

## summary

The *flapper dress* is a loose, straight, knee-length or shorter dress worn primarily by young women in the 1920s, embodying a radical break from Victorian and Edwardian silhouettes and representing the liberation, modernity, and youth culture of the Jazz Age. Paired with bobbed hair, dropped waistlines, and minimal ornamentation, the flapper dress became the visual symbol of the "New Woman"—independent, active, and unencumbered by restrictive garments.

## you gotta know

- Characterized by a straight silhouette with no waist definition, allowing natural body movement—a deliberate rejection of the corseted, bustled aesthetic of previous generations.
- Typically knee-length or just below (scandalously short for the era), allowing freedom of movement for dancing, driving, and active sports; hemlines shocked older generations and sparked moral panic.
- Featured dropped waistlines at the hip rather than the anatomical waist, creating a youthful, boyish silhouette that rejected feminine curves as the beauty ideal.
- Made from simple fabrics like crepe, cotton, or silk; minimal ornamentation and construction meant flappers could be affordable, appealing to working-class and middle-class young women.
- Associated with youth rebellion, sexual liberation, and women's newfound independence following World War I and women's suffrage movements.
- Paired with accessories marking modernity: bobbed hair, long pearl strands, headbands, T-strap heels, and cosmetics—creating a total aesthetic that shocked conservative society.
- Symbolized the broader social and cultural transformation of the 1920s: urbanization, jazz music, automobile culture, and young women's refusal to be confined by Victorian expectations.
- Faded somewhat in the 1930s Great Depression as fashion became more conservative, though the flapper aesthetic influenced fashion away from extreme corseted silhouettes permanently.

## connections

- [[corset]] — garment the flapper dress explicitly rejected, representing the break between old and new femininity.
