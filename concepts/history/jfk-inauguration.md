# JFK's Inauguration

## summary

*John F. Kennedy's* inaugural address on January 20, 1961, galvanized a generation with its vision of American idealism and sacrifice. At 43, Kennedy became the youngest elected president and the first Catholic to take office, delivering a speech that emphasized civic duty and international engagement during the height of Cold War tensions.

## you gotta know

- Took place January 20, 1961—established the precedent (via the 20th Amendment) for all subsequent presidential inaugurations.
- Kennedy famously urged Americans: "Ask not what your country can do for you—ask what you can do for your country."
- Called for global cooperation against tyranny and outlined the Peace Corps, a civilian volunteer initiative.
- Addressed the Soviet Union directly, proposing negotiation but promising American strength: "Let us never negotiate out of fear. But let us never fear to negotiate."
- Inspired massive civic participation and optimism, particularly among young people.
- Robert Frost, the poet, was invited to participate; he read "The Gift Outright," adding cultural gravitas.
- The address emphasized American idealism while confronting nuclear-era realities.
