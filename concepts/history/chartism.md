# Chartism

## summary

**Chartism** was a mass working-class political movement in Britain from the 1830s to 1850s demanding democratic reforms codified in the *People's Charter* of 1838. The movement sought universal male suffrage, equal electoral districts, secret ballots, and annual parliaments—radical demands for its era—and represented the first modern working-class political organization in the world. Though ultimately unsuccessful in achieving its immediate goals, Chartism demonstrated the political power of the industrial working class and laid the foundation for later labor and democratic reforms.

## you gotta know

- The *People's Charter* (1838) outlined six demands: universal male suffrage, equal electoral districts, secret ballots, annual parliaments, abolition of property qualifications for MPs, and payment of MPs.
- Led by figures including *William Lovett*, a skilled craftsman and co-founder of the London Working Men's Association, and *Feargus O'Connor*, an Irish radical politician who mobilized working-class support.
- Organized through a network of local clubs and reading rooms; the movement distributed the Charter through mass meetings, petitions, and newspapers like the *Northern Star* (edited by O'Connor).
- Three major petition campaigns (1839, 1842, 1848) presented massive numbers of signatures to Parliament, each time rejected; the largest, in 1848, claimed over 5 million signatures.
- Supporters ranged from skilled artisans threatened by mechanization to agricultural laborers and miners; women participated actively, though excluded from formal membership in most Chartist organizations.
- The movement fractured into factions—physical force Chartists who advocated armed rebellion versus moral force Chartists who insisted on peaceful persuasion—and declined after the failed 1848 petition and a series of government prosecutions.
