# Green Party

## summary

The Green Party of the United States, founded in 1991 and formally chartered in 2001, advocates for environmental protection, social justice, and anti-corporate politics. Running consumer advocate and engineer Ralph Nader as its presidential candidate in 2000, the party achieved its strongest popular showing (2.7%) but is often blamed for splitting the left-liberal vote and inadvertently helping George W. Bush defeat Al Gore in a razor-close Florida recount.

## you gotta know

- The Green Party grew out of grassroots environmentalist, anti-war, and antiglobalization movements, formally organizing in the 1990s to contest national elections.
- Ralph Nader's 2000 presidential campaign gained traction among antiestablishment left-wing voters, capturing over 2.8 million votes (2.7% nationally) and nearly 100,000 votes in Florida.
- Critics argue Nader's Florida votes exceeded Bush's margin of victory (537 votes after the recount), costing Gore Florida's electoral votes and the presidency; Nader maintained his campaign was a legitimate expression of progressive politics and that Gore should have competed harder in Florida.
- The party's platform centers on environmentalism (addressing climate change, halting fossil-fuel subsidies), anti-militarism, labor rights, and opposition to corporate power and free-trade agreements.
- Subsequent Green presidential candidates (2004, 2008, 2016, 2020) achieved lower vote shares, though the party retained institutional presence and elected local officials, particularly in New England.
- The 2000 election and the Iraq War energized Greens but also constrained their electoral ceiling, as Democratic voters feared spoiler effects on major issues (climate action, healthcare).
