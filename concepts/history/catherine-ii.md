# Catherine II

## summary

*Catherine II* (1729–1796), known as *Catherine the Great*, was the longest-reigning Russian empress and one of history's most effective autocrats. A German princess who married into the Romanov dynasty, she seized power in 1762 after her husband Peter III's assassination. She expanded the empire to its greatest territorial extent, embraced Enlightenment philosophy while ruling absolutely, and transformed Russia into a rival of Prussia and the dominant power in Eastern Europe.

## you gotta know

### Rise to Power
- Born *Sophie of Anhalt-Zerbst*, she married the future *Peter III* in 1745 as part of a diplomatic alliance.
- Deposed her incompetent and unpopular husband in 1762 in a bloodless coup with the aid of the Grigory Orlov and the imperial guard.
- Ruled as regent, then declared herself empress; legitimacy came through the love of the nobility and military.

### Territorial Expansion & Foreign Policy
- Conducted two Russo-Turkish Wars (1768–1774, 1787–1792), annexing the Crimea, parts of the Caucasus, and access to the Black Sea.
- Partitioned Poland three times (1772, 1793, 1795) with Prussia and Austria, absorbing vast Western territories and millions of Polish and Lithuanian subjects.
- Fought brief conflict with Sweden (1788–1790), securing control of the Baltic.
- Expanded the empire eastward into Siberia and southward to the borders of Ottoman Turkey and Persia.

### Enlightened Absolutism & Cultural Flowering
- Corresponded with Voltaire and Diderot, positioning herself as an enlightened monarch who implemented rational reforms.
- Established schools and legal codes based on Enlightenment principles, though she refused to abolish serfdom—the paradox of her reign.
- Patronized the arts, literature, and architecture; St. Petersburg became a center of European culture, rivaling Paris and Vienna.
- Created a meritocratic nobility via the *Guberniia Reform* (1775), centralizing provincial administration.

### Domestic Stability & Limits
- Consolidated nobility support through generous grants of land and serfs, binding the aristocracy to throne loyalty.
- Faced the massive peasant rebellion of Emelyan Pugachev (1773–1775), which terrified her and hardened her against peasant reform.
- Strengthened the institution of serfdom, binding peasants more tightly to the land to ensure noble satisfaction.

## context

Catherine the Great represents enlightened absolutism at its apex: she spoke the language of reason and progress, yet wielded power with ruthless pragmatism. She is the tsar who made Russia unambiguously European and unambiguously dominant in Eastern Europe—the Crimea, the western borderlands, the cultural prestige of St. Petersburg all date to her reign. Yet her rule also crystallized the contradiction at Russia's heart: a state that professed Enlightenment values while systematizing feudal servitude. Her refusal to reform serfdom, despite her philosophical commitment to rational governance, ensured that modernization would remain confined to the aristocracy and urban centers, a fissure that would split open a century later under her descendants.
