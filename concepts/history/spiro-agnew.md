# Spiro Agnew

## summary

**Spiro Agnew** was the thirty-ninth Vice President under Richard Nixon (1969–1973) and a controversial figure who embodied Nixon's appeal to social conservatism and working-class resentment. He resigned in disgrace in 1973 amid bribery and tax evasion charges, the first Vice President to leave office under criminal investigation.

## you gotta know

- Resigned the vice presidency in October 1973 after pleading no contest to tax evasion; he had accepted bribes throughout his political career as Maryland governor and county executive.
- Known for aggressive rhetorical attacks on the press, antiwar protesters, and intellectuals, coining phrases like "nattering nabobs of negativism" and "effete corps of impudent snobs."
- Served as Governor of Maryland (1967–1969) and built a reputation as a tough-on-crime conservative, appealing to Nixon's "silent majority."
- His resignation occurred while Watergate was unfolding, and it occurred *before* Nixon's, robbing him of whatever claim to the presidency he might have had.
- Became a symbol of both Republican ruthlessness and the corruption of the Nixon era, his fall mirroring and preceding the president's own downfall.
- Returned to private law practice and business after his resignation, living quietly until his death in 1996, largely removed from public life.
- Remains the only Vice President to resign due to criminal charges (though Andrew Johnson faced impeachment later in his presidency).
