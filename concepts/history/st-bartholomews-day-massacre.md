# St. Bartholomew's Day Massacre

## summary

The **St. Bartholomew's Day Massacre** was a multi-day spree of Catholic violence against French Protestant Huguenots beginning August 24, 1572, in Paris and spreading to provincial towns across France. The trigger was a failed assassination attempt on Admiral *Gaspard de Coligny*, a Huguenot leader, which sparked hysteria among Catholic mobs and the royal court that conspirators were plotting to seize power. Organized ostensibly under the authority of King *Charles IX* and enabled by his mother *Catherine de Medici*, thousands of Huguenots were slaughtered in their homes, streets, and churches. The massacre became a defining atrocity of the French Wars of Religion and shattered the fragile peace that had held since 1570.

## you gotta know

### The Trigger
- An assassination attempt on *Gaspard de Coligny* on August 22 failed; the Admiral was wounded but survived.
- Huguenot princes demanded the arrest and trial of the would-be assassins; Catherine de Medici and the Catholic faction saw this as a threat to royal authority.
- King *Charles IX* and the court panicked, fearing a Huguenot insurrection, and ordered the massacre to preempt it.

### The Violence
- The killing began in the early morning of August 24 (the feast day of St. Bartholomew); Catholic mobs rampaged through Paris for three days.
- An estimated 2,000 to 3,000 Huguenots were killed in Paris alone; some estimates place the total death toll across France at 10,000 or more.
- Entire families were murdered in their beds; churches where Huguenots sought refuge were set alight; bodies were mutilated and dragged through streets.

### Key Victims
- *Gaspard de Coligny* was dragged from his sickbed and murdered; his body was desecrated and hanged from a gibbet.
- Other prominent Huguenot leaders were killed; their wives and children perished alongside them.
- The violence was indiscriminate—common folk and nobility alike were targeted based on religion.

### Aftermath
- The massacre turned the Huguenots from a potentially loyal minority into an entrenched opposition force.
- It hardened religious divisions in France and vindicated Huguenot fears of Catholic persecution; subsequent peace treaties were more fragile.
- *Henry of Navarre* (the future *Henry IV*), a Huguenot prince, survived by abjuring his faith temporarily; he later returned to Protestantism and eventually granted the Edict of Nantes (1598).

## context

St. Bartholomew's Day stands as one of early modern Europe's worst atrocities of religious violence, rivaling in brutality the Spanish Inquisition's persecutions. Its significance lies not merely in the body count but in its reverberations: the massacre convinced European Protestants that Catholics could never be trusted, validated Huguenot military resistance, and accelerated the shift from religious coexistence toward open sectarian warfare. The event was instantly mythologized—Catholics called it a justified defense of the Crown, Protestants called it a premeditated genocide—and the competing narratives became foundational to how each side understood the conflict. For France, it delayed by two decades any durable settlement and cost the nation untold wealth and stability.
