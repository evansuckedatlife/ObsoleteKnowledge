# Daniel Webster

## summary

*Daniel Webster* was a dominant figure of 19th-century American politics, serving as Secretary of State under President William Henry Harrison and John Tyler (1841–1843) and again under Millard Fillmore (1850–1852). A gifted orator and skilled diplomat, Webster defended the Union's integrity during the sectional crises of the antebellum era while advancing American interests in North America and the Pacific.

## you gotta know

- Won fame as a Supreme Court advocate, defending federal power in *McCulloch v. Maryland* (1819) and the Constitution in *Dartmouth College v. Woodward* (1819).
- Negotiated the *Webster-Ashburton Treaty* (1842), resolving the Maine–New Brunswick boundary dispute with Britain and preventing war on the Canadian frontier.
- A pillar of the Whig Party, whose philosophy favored a strong federal government and internal improvements over states' rights.
- Opposed the *Nullification Crisis* (1832–1833), defending federal authority against South Carolina's threat to void federal tariffs; delivered the famous Senate speech "Liberty and Union, now and forever, one and inseparable."
- Served in the *Compromise of 1850*, though his support for the Fugitive Slave Act damaged his northern antislavery credentials.
- Championed American expansion in the Pacific and commercial treaties with Asian powers.

## connections

- [[henry-clay]] — his great Whig rival and ally; together they shaped antebellum politics.
- [[john-c-calhoun]] — his intellectual opponent in debates over nullification and federal authority.
- [[william-henry-harrison]] — the president under whom Webster first served as Secretary of State.
- [[compromise-of-1850]] — a capstone to his long career, though it came at political cost.
