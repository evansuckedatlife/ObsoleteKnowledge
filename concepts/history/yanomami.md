# Yanomami

## summary

The *Yanomami* are an indigenous people of the Amazon rainforest inhabiting territories across the Brazil-Venezuela border, numbering approximately 35,000. They practice small-scale horticulture combined with hunting and gathering and maintain fierce independence and cultural distinctiveness; their territories and traditional lifeways face ongoing threats from illegal mining, disease, and encroachment.

## you gotta know

- Inhabit the *Amazon rainforest* along the Brazil-Venezuela border, particularly the *Orinoco* and *Amazon* river basins; their territory encompasses roughly 9.6 million hectares.
- Speak *Yanomami* languages; distinct villages often speak mutually intelligible but distinct dialects; language is intimately tied to local identity and kinship systems.
- Practice *shifting cultivation*, clearing small forest plots for yams, plantains, and other crops, then allowing forest regeneration; combine agriculture with extensive hunting and gathering of rainforest resources.
- Organize into autonomous villages (*shabonos*, roughly 40–150 people) with minimal centralized authority; decisions are made through consensus discussion, particularly among senior men.
- Practice elaborate *reciprocal exchange* systems (*daximi*) with other villages, trading goods, intermarrying, and creating alliances; these networks prevent excessive warfare and strengthen collective security.
- Maintain spiritual traditions centered on *shamanism*; shamans (*xapiri*) communicate with forest spirits and perform healing and protective rituals central to community well-being.
- Face severe threats from illegal gold mining (since the 1980s), which has introduced disease, contaminated water with mercury, and triggered violent conflicts; malaria, measles, and other diseases have devastated populations.
