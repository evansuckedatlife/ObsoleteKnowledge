# Peruke

## summary

The *peruke* (or *periwig*), a full-bottomed wig made of human or animal hair and often dusted with white or colored powder, became the dominant masculine fashion accessory from the 1660s through the 1800s. Originating in France under Louis XIV, the peruke symbolized wealth, status, and gentility, transforming every well-dressed man into a walking advertisement of his social position and fashion sensibility.

## you gotta know

- Originally adopted to cover baldness and male pattern hair loss but quickly became fashionable among men with full hair; wearing a peruke signaled membership in the upper classes.
- The full-bottomed peruke featured long curls cascading down the back and shoulders, requiring significant amounts of expensive human hair and requiring tailors and wigmakers to achieve the style.
- Powdered with white, gray, or even tinted powders (wheat flour mixed with scent and color)—the powder was expensive and marked the wearer as wealthy enough to discard it daily.
- Maintenance was elaborate: perukes were dressed on wooden blocks, curled with irons, powdered, and stored carefully; a gentleman might own several for different occasions.
- Symbolized the age of absolute monarchy and aristocratic refinement; French court fashion made the peruke an international status symbol across Europe.
- Gradually simplified from massive full-bottomed versions to smaller, neater styles as the 18th century progressed; by 1800, wigs had become less fashionable for younger men.
- Powdered wigs persisted longest in formal dress, legal professions, and military uniforms, where they survive in limited use into the present day (British judges and barristers still wear them).

## connections

- [[louis-xiv]] — French king whose court established the peruke as the essential mark of gentlemanly status and fashion authority.
- [[george-iii]] — English king during the peak of peruke fashion in Britain.
- [[enlightenment]] — era associated with rational dress reform that began questioning the peruke's necessity.
