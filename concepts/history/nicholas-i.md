# Nicholas I

## summary

*Nicholas I* (1796–1855) was the apotheosis of Russian autocratic reaction, ruling with militant conservatism and Orthodox orthodoxy. He became tsar after the failed Decembrist Revolt of 1825, which permanently radicalized him against liberalism. His thirty-year reign was marked by rigid centralization, suppression of dissent, military expansion, and the development of an official state ideology (*Official Nationality*) designed to shore up autocracy. He died amid the humiliations of the Crimean War, leaving Russia weakened and reform-minded successors.

## you gotta know

### Rise to Power & The Decembrist Threat
- Became tsar unexpectedly after the death of his brother *Alexander I*; initially hesitant about claims to the throne.
- Faced the *Decembrist Revolt* (December 1825), an uprising of liberal military officers demanding a constitution; he crushed it ruthlessly, executing five conspirators.
- The event convinced him that liberalism, education, and Western ideas were existential threats to the state; he turned viciously reactionary.

### Official Nationality & Ideological Consolidation
- Developed the doctrine of *Uvarov's Official Nationality* (1833): *Autocracy, Orthodoxy, Nationality*—the three pillars of the Russian state.
- Used the secret police and censorship to suppress any hint of liberalism, Westernism, or reform sentiment.
- Restricted education, particularly university curricula, to prevent the spread of dangerous ideas.
- Promoted Russian Orthodoxy and Slavic nationalism as official ideology, suppressing minority religions and ethnic identities.

### Military Expansion & Foreign Policy
- Continued expansion in the Caucasus, though met fierce resistance from the *Caucasian War* (1817–1864), led against the warrior *Imam Shamil*.
- Intervened repeatedly in Europe to suppress revolutions (suppressed Polish rebellion in 1830–1831, Austrian request for aid in 1848–1849).
- Fought the *Russo-Turkish War* (1828–1829), securing Turkish territories and expanding influence in the Balkans.
- Died in 1855 during the *Crimean War*, a humiliating conflict that exposed Russian military weakness against the West.

### Serfdom & Social Control
- Strengthened serfdom and peasant bondage as the foundation of social stability; resisted abolition despite evidence of economic stagnation.
- Attempted modest reforms to serfdom (e.g., *inventories* of peasant obligations), but these proved insufficient and unpopular.
- The peasantry, increasingly restless, smoldered under his reign; peasant rebellions grew more frequent and desperate.

## context

Nicholas I perfected Russian autocratic reaction in the 19th century, building a state where the secret police, Orthodox Church, and military apparatus fused into an instrument of total control. His doctrine of *Official Nationality* was the intellectual scaffolding of a regime that feared change of any kind. Yet his rigidity became Russia's weakness: while the West industrialized and reformed, Nicholas locked Russia into feudalism and militarism. The Crimean War, fought with cavalry against artillery and lost to a coalition of Western powers, exposed the bankruptcy of his system. He died a failure, leaving his successor *Alexander II* with an empire in crisis and a population demanding change. His reign represents the apogee of Russian autocracy—and its brittleness.
