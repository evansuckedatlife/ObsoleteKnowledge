# Alexander the Great

## summary

*Alexander III of Macedon*, known as *Alexander the Great*, was a military genius who created the largest contiguous empire of the ancient world before his death at 32. By conquering the Persian Empire and extending Macedonian rule from Greece to Egypt to the borders of India, he fundamentally reshaped the political and cultural landscape of three continents. His conquests inaugurated the *Hellenistic* era—centuries of Greek cultural dominance in Asia and the Near East—and made him history's archetypal conqueror, studied by military strategists for over two thousand years.

## you gotta know

### early life and succession

- Born in 356 BCE in Pella, the capital of Macedon, the son of King *Philip II*, one of ancient Greece's great military reformers.
- Tutored by the philosopher *Aristotle*, who shaped his intellectual outlook and instilled in him a love of Greek culture and learning, which he spread throughout his conquered territories.
- Became king at 20 after his father's assassination (336 BCE), acting with brutal decisiveness to secure his throne: executing potential rivals and re-subjugating Greek city-states that immediately rebelled.
- Inherited the Macedonian phalanx—a revolutionary infantry formation perfected by his father—which became the instrument of his conquests.

### conquest of the Persian Empire

- Crossed into Asia Minor in 334 BCE with a relatively small force (around 35,000 men) and destroyed the Persian satrap's army at the *Granicus River*, establishing himself as a serious threat to Persian rule.
- Defeated the Persian king *Darius III* at the *Battle of Issus* (333 BCE) in southern Anatolia, a stunning tactical victory where Alexander's cavalry broke the Persian center and forced Darius to flee the battlefield.
- Conquered *Tyre* (332 BCE), a heavily fortified island city, through siege and engineering mastery, demonstrating his versatility beyond pitched battles.
- Entered Egypt where he was crowned pharaoh and proclaimed the son of the god *Amun*, lending divine sanction to his rule in the eyes of his Egyptian subjects.
- Met and decisively defeated *Darius III* again at *Gaugamela* (331 BCE), near modern-day Iraq, effectively ending Persian imperial power and making Alexander master of the Near East.
- Pursued the conquest eastward: took Babylon, Susa, and Persepolis (burning it in celebration), seized the legendary wealth of the Persian treasury, and pushed into Central Asia and India.

### the limits of conquest

- Reached the *Indus Valley* and won a major victory at the *Hydaspes River* (326 BCE) against King *Porus* and his elephant army—a battle his own soldiers regarded as nearly his greatest challenge.
- Wished to continue east to the Ganges, but his exhausted and homesick army mutinied and refused to advance further, forcing Alexander to halt his conquests.
- Died in *Babylon* in 323 BCE, likely of fever (possibly malaria or typhoid), at age 32, leaving an empire he had ruled for only thirteen years.

### military genius and legacy

- Never lost a pitched battle despite frequently facing numerically superior forces; his victories turned on his ability to read the enemy's weak point and deliver a cavalry strike with perfect timing.
- Pioneered a new kind of warfare combining heavy cavalry, light cavalry, infantry, and siege craft in a coordinated system; his tactics remained the model for armies for centuries.
- Founded over 70 cities throughout his empire, many named *Alexandria* (most famously *Alexandria* in Egypt), which became centers of Greek culture and trade.
- Married *Roxana*, a Bactrian princess, and encouraged his troops to marry local women, integrating Greek and Persian cultures and creating the mixed Greco-Oriental *Hellenistic* civilization.

## context

Alexander stands at the threshold between the ancient world and the medieval. His conquests did not establish a durable state—his empire fragmented immediately after his death into the kingdoms of the *Diadochi* (his generals)—but they accomplished something more lasting: the Hellenization of Asia and the Near East for centuries. Greek language, art, and ideas became the prestigious cultural medium of the entire eastern Mediterranean and western Asia; the resulting *Hellenistic* era saw the greatest flowering of Greek science, mathematics, and philosophy (Alexandria in Egypt became the intellectual hub of the world). His legend became the gold standard for the ambitious young warrior-king; he was revered by the Romans, studied obsessively by Renaissance military theorists, and invoked as the model for everything from Napoleon to modern military academies. His teacher Aristotle's methods of classification and observation made their way into Islamic and medieval European science through the Hellenistic libraries Alexander's cities created. Few individuals have shaped history so directly and on such a grand scale; fewer still achieved it at such a young age.

- [[aristotle]] — his tutor, whose philosophy of learning and curiosity Alexander carried into every conquered land.
- [[darius-iii]] — the Persian king who stood against Alexander; their three encounters defined the conquest of Asia.
- [[roxana]] — his Bactrian wife, symbol of his fusion of Greek and Persian cultures.

- [[battle-of-gaugamela]] — the final shattering defeat of Darius, making Alexander master of the Persian Empire.
- [[alexandria]] — the city bearing his name that became antiquity's greatest intellectual center.
- [[macedonian-empire]] — the state he inherited and transformed into history's largest ancient empire.
