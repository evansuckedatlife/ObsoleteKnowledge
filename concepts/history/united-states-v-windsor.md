# United States v. Windsor

## summary

*United States v. Windsor* (2013) is the Supreme Court decision that struck down the federal portion of the Defense of Marriage Act (DOMA), which had denied federal benefits to same-sex married couples. The 5–4 ruling, written by Justice Kennedy, established that DOMA violated equal protection and represented a major step toward full marriage equality.

## you gotta know

- Decided June 26, 2013 by a 5–4 margin, with Justice Kennedy writing the majority opinion.
- Invalidated Section 3 of the Defense of Marriage Act, which defined marriage as a union between a man and a woman for federal purposes.
- Struck down DOMA's denial of federal tax benefits, Social Security, and other spousal protections to legally married same-sex couples.
- The plaintiff, Edith Windsor, sought a federal estate tax refund after her wife Thea Spyer died, revealing how DOMA harmed surviving same-sex spouses.
- Kennedy's majority held that DOMA violated the equal protection component of the Fifth Amendment's Due Process Clause and expressed an improper disapproval of same-sex marriage.
- Left in place state-level DOMA definitions, setting the stage for *Obergefell* two years later to address marriage bans themselves.
- Justice Scalia's dissent warned that the ruling would inexorably lead to nationwide same-sex marriage (which proved accurate).
