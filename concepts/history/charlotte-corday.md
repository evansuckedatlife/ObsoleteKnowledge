# Charlotte Corday

## summary

*Charlotte Corday* (1768–1793), a noblewoman and royalist sympathizer, assassinated the radical journalist *Jean-Paul Marat* in July 1793 in an attempt to save the revolution from extremism. Her murder of the "Friend of the People" was intended as an act of political heroism, but instead made Marat a martyr and accelerated the Reign of Terror.

## you gotta know

- Came from the Corday d'Armont family, minor Norman nobility with Girondin sympathies; idealized the moderate phase of the revolution before its radicalization.
- Traveled to Paris in July 1793 with the explicit intent to kill Marat, viewing him as the embodiment of radical violence and the architect of the Girondins' purge.
- Gained entry to Marat's home by posing as a petitioner with information about royalist conspiracies; stabbed him in his medicinal bath on July 13, 1793.
- Made no attempt to escape; was arrested immediately and tried for assassination within days of her act.
- Maintained her composure and conviction during trial, arguing that she had killed a tyrant and a threat to true liberty, earning admiration even from some revolutionaries.
- Executed by guillotine on July 17, 1793, just four days after her assassination of Marat.
- Her act backfired politically: Marat became a revolutionary icon and martyr, and her assassination was used to justify intensified persecution of suspected royalists and counter-revolutionaries.
