## summary

The Emancipation Proclamation was an executive order issued by President *Abraham Lincoln* on January 1, 1863, during the American Civil War, declaring all slaves in Confederate states "forever free." The order applied only to slaves in the rebelling Southern states, not to slaves in the Union-loyal border states or those under Union occupation. While the proclamation did not immediately free all enslaved people—enforcement depended on Union military victory—it transformed the Civil War from a war for the Union into a war for emancipation and paved the way for the Thirteenth Amendment, which abolished slavery nationwide.

## you gotta know

- Lincoln issued the *Preliminary Emancipation Proclamation* on September 22, 1862, after the Union's victory at the *Battle of Antietam*, giving the Confederacy 100 days to rejoin the Union or lose enslaved workers.
- The final *Emancipation Proclamation* was issued on January 1, 1863, and declared all slaves in Confederate states "are, and henceforward shall be free."
- It applied only to the Confederate states (South Carolina, Georgia, Florida, Alabama, Mississippi, Louisiana, Texas, Virginia, Arkansas, and North Carolina), not to slavery in Union-loyal border states (Maryland, Delaware, Kentucky, Missouri).
- Lincoln justified the proclamation as a *military necessity*, a war measure to weaken the Confederate economy and bolster Union military strength.
- The proclamation encouraged enslaved people to escape to Union lines and authorized the recruitment of formerly enslaved people into the Union Army (the U.S. Colored Troops).
- Approximately 180,000 African American soldiers served in the Union Army and Navy during the war, many of them formerly enslaved.
- The proclamation did not legally free all enslaved people; it was a war measure that would be superseded by the Thirteenth Amendment (ratified in 1865).
