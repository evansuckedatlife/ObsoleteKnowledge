# Marquis de Lafayette

## summary

*The Marquis de Lafayette* (1757–1834), born Gilbert du Motier, was a noble reformer and military hero who championed both the American and French Revolutions. His idealism about constitutional liberty and his early military successes made him a bridge figure between the two revolutions, though he was ultimately sidelined by the radical phase and forced to flee France.

## you gotta know

- A hero of the American Revolution, serving without pay as a general in George Washington's army and lobbying French support for the cause.
- Returned to France in 1785 and played a key role in summoning the Estates-General, hoping to institute constitutional monarchy and protect rights.
- Commanded the National Guard during the early revolution and defended the royal family during the Women's March on Versailles (October 1789), trying to preserve both order and reform.
- Authored the Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen (1789), one of the revolution's founding documents, reflecting his faith in universal rights and law.
- Clashed with radicals over his moderate vision; his support for the constitutional monarchy alienated both royalists and republicans.
- Fled France in 1792 as the radical phase began, fearing arrest; was captured by Austrians and imprisoned for five years.
- His early idealism and ultimate marginalization made him a symbol of the revolution's transformation from liberal reform to ideological terror.
