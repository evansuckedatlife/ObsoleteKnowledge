# Smallpox in the Americas

## summary

*Smallpox in the Americas* refers to a catastrophic wave of epidemics triggered by the introduction of Old World pathogens—primarily smallpox—following Columbus's arrival in 1492. The disease killed an estimated 90% of the Indigenous population of the Americas over the course of the 16th and 17th centuries, fundamentally reshaping the continents and enabling European conquest of civilizations that would have been militarily formidable.

## you gotta know

- Smallpox, endemic in Eurasia and Africa for millennia, was entirely absent from the Americas before 1492; Indigenous peoples had no acquired immunity.
- First documented smallpox outbreak in Hispaniola occurred in 1518–1519, killing an estimated 30–50% of the Taíno population within a few years.
- The Aztec capital Tenochtitlan was ravaged by smallpox in 1520 during Cortés's siege; the epidemic killed the Aztec emperor Cuitláhuac and weakened military resistance.
- Death tolls were catastrophic: an estimated 90% of the pre-Columbian Indigenous population (56–100 million people, by various estimates) died from European diseases over 130 years.
- Other pathogens followed smallpox: measles, typhus, typhoid, and influenza created waves of repeated epidemics that prevented population recovery.
- The epidemics were not intentional bioweapons in most cases; diseases spread unknowingly through trade and military contact, though some Spanish soldiers allegedly used infected blankets against Indigenous peoples.
- Ecological collapse followed demographic collapse: abandoned Indigenous settlements allowed forests to regrow, reshaping the continental environment; agricultural societies fragmented.
