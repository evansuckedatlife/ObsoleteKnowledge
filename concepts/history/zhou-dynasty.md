# Zhou Dynasty

## summary

The *Zhou Dynasty* (1046–256 BCE) was China's longest-lasting dynasty, spanning nearly 800 years and encompassing some of its most intellectually fertile and politically fragmented periods. Founded by the Duke of Zhou after overthrowing the Shang, the Zhou introduced the *Mandate of Heaven*—a justification for dynastic rule that would echo through Chinese politics for millennia. The dynasty experienced two broad phases: the Western Zhou (1046–771 BCE), marked by centralized feudal control, and the Eastern Zhou (771–256 BCE), during which royal authority fragmented and competing states gave rise to intense philosophical flourishing (the age of *Confucius* and *Laozi*).

## you gotta know

### Foundation and Legitimacy
- Founded circa 1046 BCE by *King Wu*, who overthrew the last Shang king, *Di Xin*.
- The *Duke of Zhou* served as regent and effectively governed; celebrated as a sage-statesman in Confucian tradition.
- Introduced the concept of *Mandate of Heaven*: the king's right to rule derived from moral virtue, not birthright—allowing overthrow of corrupt dynasties.

### Western Zhou (1046–771 BCE)
- Established a *feudal system*: the king granted fiefs to regional lords (many his relatives) in exchange for military and tributary loyalty.
- Capital at Hao (near modern Xi'an); ruled from a position of relative strength.
- Culture marked by bronze vessels inscribed with *bronze inscriptions*, recording grants and royal pronouncements.

### Eastern Zhou and Political Fragmentation (771–256 BCE)
- *Sack of Hao* (771 BCE) by barbarian invaders and internal rebellion weakened central authority; court relocated eastward to Luoyang, marking the Eastern Zhou.
- Kingdom fractured into competing *vassal states*; Zhou kings became ceremonial figureheads.

### Spring and Autumn Period (771–476 BCE)
- Named after the historical chronicle *Spring and Autumn Annals*, which documents this era of constant warfare among feudal lords.
- Hegemons (*ba*) like Duke Huan of Qi temporarily dominated, but no single power consolidated rule.

### Warring States Period (476–221 BCE)
- Intensive interstate warfare; seven major powers competed for supremacy: *Chu, Qi, Yan, Zhao, Wei, Han, Qin*.
- Ironically, constant conflict spurred military innovation, philosophical debate, and state consolidation—setting the stage for Qin's eventual unification.

### Intellectual Flourishing
- Age of *Confucius*, *Laozi*, *Zhuangzi*, and the *Hundred Schools of Thought*.
- Confucianism, Daoism, and Legalism emerged as competing worldviews, each offering solutions to political chaos.

## context

The Zhou Dynasty paradoxically peaked intellectually during its political decline. The Eastern Zhou's fragmentation paradoxically enabled the greatest flowering of Chinese philosophy—thinkers competed to answer the urgent question: *how should a state be governed?* This age produced foundational works like the *Analects* (Confucius) and the *Daodejing* (Laozi), texts that shaped Chinese thought for 2,000 years. The Mandate of Heaven concept, born from Zhou's conquest of Shang, became the legitimizing narrative for every subsequent dynasty and profoundly influenced how Chinese rulers understood their authority. The Warring States period ended not with Zhou restoration, but with Qin's ruthless consolidation—yet Qin's own rapid collapse validated the Mandate of Heaven framework: a dynasty without virtue could not endure.
