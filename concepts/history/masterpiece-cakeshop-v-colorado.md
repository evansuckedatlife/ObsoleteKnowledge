# Masterpiece Cakeshop v. Colorado

## summary

*Masterpiece Cakeshop v. Colorado* (2018) is the Supreme Court decision that ruled Colorado's civil rights agency violated the First Amendment by displaying hostility toward religion when it sanctioned a baker who refused to design a wedding cake for a same-sex couple. The 7–2 ruling, written by Justice Kennedy, narrowly decided the case without setting broad precedent for religious exemptions from public accommodation laws.

## you gotta know

- Decided June 4, 2018 by a 7–2 margin, with Justice Kennedy writing the majority opinion.
- The baker, Jack Phillips, refused to create a custom cake for Craig and Mullins' same-sex wedding, invoking religious beliefs about marriage.
- Colorado's Civil Rights Commission had ordered him to make such cakes, but the Supreme Court found the Commission had shown bias against his religious views during proceedings.
- Kennedy's majority emphasized the Commission's comments denigrating religion, particularly a commissioner's statement that religious objections to same-sex marriage echoed pro-slavery and pro-segregation arguments.
- The decision expressly declined to resolve whether a business can refuse services based on religious objections, leaving that broader question open.
- Dissenters (Justices Ginsburg and Sotomayor) argued the case was straightforward application of public accommodation laws to prevent discrimination.
- Set up later test cases like *303 Creative v. Elenis* to directly address religious exemptions.
