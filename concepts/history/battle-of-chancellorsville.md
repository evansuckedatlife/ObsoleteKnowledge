# Battle of Chancellorsville

## summary

The Battle of Chancellorsville (April 30–May 6, 1863) was a Confederate tactical masterpiece in which Robert E. Lee, despite being outnumbered two-to-one, defeated Union General Joseph "Fighting Joe" Hooker's Army of the Potomac in the dense Virginia woods known as "the Wilderness." Lee boldly split his forces, sending Thomas J. Jackson on a daring flanking march that surprised and routed the Union right. Though the Confederacy achieved its greatest tactical victory, the battle cost the life of Jackson—Lee's most trusted lieutenant—and Lee's failure to exploit the victory prevented the capture of the Union army. The battle exemplified Lee's aggressive generalship while demonstrating the tactical limitations of Confederate strategy.

## you gotta know

### Background
- Hooker commanded the Union army after Burnside and sought to exploit Lee's smaller force by rapid maneuvering.
- Hooker planned to send cavalry around Lee's left while his main army crossed the Rappahannock to flank the Confederate position.
- Lee, though informed of Union movements, correctly divined Hooker's intent and took aggressive counteraction.

### The Battle
- Lee divided his force, leaving 10,000 troops under *Jubal Early* to face the Union frontal attack at Fredricksburg.
- Jackson marched ~28,000 troops on a 12-mile route around the Union right, arriving on May 2 to attack the exposed right flank.
- Union forces were routed; Hooker, despite numerical superiority, lost confidence and began withdrawing toward his starting position.

### Aftermath
- Jackson was wounded by friendly fire on the evening of May 2 and died on May 10. Lee said, "I have lost my right arm."
- Lee pursued and attacked on May 3–4 but could not force a decisive victory; Hooker withdrew across the Rappahannock.
- Union casualties were ~17,000; Confederate ~13,000. Though smaller, Confederate losses were less replaceable.

## context

Chancellorsville was the apex of Lee's offensive genius—a maneuver of almost reckless audacity that worked precisely because Lee understood his enemy and his subordinates' capabilities. Yet the loss of Jackson exposed a crucial vulnerability in Confederate strategy: the Confederacy lacked trained officers capable of replacing Lee's vision. Without Jackson, Lee's subsequent campaigns—including Gettysburg—would lack the coordinated execution they required. The battle demonstrated that Confederate tactical superiority, while brilliant, could not overcome Union numerical and industrial advantage. For the North, the defeat stung, but it accelerated the development of more methodical, less romantic commanders.
