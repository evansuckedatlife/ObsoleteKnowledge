# Horatio Gates

## summary

*Horatio Gates* was a British-born officer who became a major American general and won the pivotal victory at *Saratoga* in 1777, convincing France to enter the war. However, his later command was marked by setbacks and political intrigue, and he is remembered as a capable tactician whose star dimmed after his early success.

## you gotta know

- Rose to prominence as commander of the *Northern Department*, winning the crucial victory at *Saratoga* (September–October 1777) when his forces trapped and forced the surrender of British General *Burgoyne* and his army.
- The Saratoga victory proved that the Continental Army could defeat a professional British force in pitched battle, convincing France to formally ally with the Americans—a turning point in the war.
- Owed much of his success to subordinate officers like *John Stark* and *Benedict Arnold*, who handled tactical matters on the battlefield while Gates managed supplies and strategy.
- Later transferred to the southern theater, where he suffered a catastrophic defeat at *Camden* (August 1780), discredited by his hasty retreat and poor reconnaissance.
- Known for political ambition and careful cultivation of his image; his public popularity far exceeded his actual military ability, fueling jealousy and friction with *George Washington*.
- Faded from command after Camden, though he remained a general without significant field responsibility until the war's end.

## connections

- [[george-washington]] — Gates's nominal superior, whom Gates both admired and competed with for authority and credit.
- [[nathanael-greene]] — succeeded Gates in the South and retrieved the situation through Greene's superior strategy.
- [[saratoga-campaign]] — Gates's crowning achievement and the hinge on which French entry turned.
- [[benedict-arnold]] — his brilliant but volatile subordinate at Saratoga, whose tactical aggressiveness offset Gates's caution.
- [[john-stark]] — the "Old Man of the Mountain" who fought at Saratoga and embodied New England militia resistance.
