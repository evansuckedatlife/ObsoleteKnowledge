## summary

Fort Sumter, a federal garrison in Charleston Harbor, South Carolina, was the site of the opening engagement of the American Civil War (April 12–13, 1861). The Confederate government, freshly independent, demanded the Union's evacuation of the fort; when the Union refused to relinquish it, Confederate artillery bombarded it for 34 hours. The federal garrison, commanded by Major Robert Anderson, surrendered after the fort's magazines were exhausted. Though there were no combat deaths, the attack on the fort electrified both North and South, transformed political conflict into armed warfare, and solidified Union public opinion behind Lincoln's call for volunteers to suppress the rebellion.

## you gotta know

### Background
- South Carolina seceded in December 1860; the state inherited federal forts and arsenals within its borders.
- Fort Sumter remained a Union garrison, becoming a symbol of federal authority in the seceded states.
- Lincoln, newly inaugurated, decided to resupply the isolated garrison with provisions.

### The Attack
- Confederate General Pierre Beauregard issued an ultimatum to evacuate; Major Anderson refused.
- Confederate forces encircling the harbor—approximately 6,000 troops—opened fire at dawn on April 12 with artillery from Fort Moultrie and other batteries.
- After 34 hours of bombardment, Anderson's small garrison (about 80 soldiers) returned fire but could not resist. Anderson surrendered on April 13.

### Aftermath
- The attack killed no one in combat but two Confederate soldiers died in accidents during the bombardment.
- Lincoln called for 75,000 volunteers to suppress the rebellion; this prompted further secessions and mobilization.
- The event is marked as the official beginning of the Civil War, though secession was political—Sumter was the military rupture.

## context

Fort Sumter transformed a constitutional crisis into a shooting war. The fort's isolation and symbolic importance made it the inevitable flashpoint; both Lincoln and Jefferson Davis understood that whoever "fired first" would bear the political cost of initiating violence. Lincoln masterfully positioned himself to respond defensively, not aggressively, to the Confederate attack. The rapid mobilization following Sumter demonstrated both sides' willingness to fight and their confidence in quick victory—a confidence that would prove tragically wrong. The bombardment remains the symbolic beginning of American fratricide.
