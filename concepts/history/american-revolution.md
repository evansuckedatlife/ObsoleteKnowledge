# American Revolution

## summary

The American Revolution (1775–1783) was the colonial uprising against British rule that created the United States of America. Ignited by grievances over taxation without representation and British military occupation, the thirteen colonies unified under the Continental Congress and Continental Army to declare independence in 1776 and secure it through eight years of armed conflict. The Revolution succeeded against overwhelming odds, establishing a republic founded on Enlightenment principles of individual rights and popular sovereignty that transformed the Western political imagination.

## you gotta know

### sparks and causes

- Tensions exploded after the *Stamp Act* (1765) and *Townshend Acts* (1767), which imposed taxes on colonial goods without colonial representation in Parliament, sparking the rallying cry *"No taxation without representation."*
- The *Boston Massacre* (1770) and *Boston Tea Party* (1773) crystallized colonial opposition: British soldiers fired on protesters; colonists destroyed a shipment of tea to protest the Tea Act.
- *Lexington and Concord* (April 1775) marked the first military engagements, when British regulars attempted to seize colonial arms and were repelled by local militiamen.

### war and independence

- The *Declaration of Independence* (July 4, 1776), drafted by *Thomas Jefferson*, articulated the philosophical case for independence and listed grievances against King *George III*.
- *George Washington* led the *Continental Army* through eight years of warfare, winning crucial victories at *Trenton* (1776) and *Yorktown* (1781) while enduring catastrophic winters at *Valley Forge*.
- The *French*, seeking to weaken Britain, entered the war in 1778, providing naval power and troops that proved decisive at Yorktown; *Benjamin Franklin* secured the alliance through diplomacy in Paris.

### legacy

- The *Treaty of Paris* (1783) formally recognized American independence and established the United States as a sovereign nation.
- The Revolution inspired republican and democratic movements worldwide, especially the French Revolution; its ideas shaped the *U.S. Constitution* and the *Bill of Rights*.

## context

The American Revolution stands as the first successful colonial independence movement against a European imperial power and the birth of the first modern democratic republic. Its emphasis on natural rights, popular sovereignty, and written constitutional limits on government became the template for democratic revolutions globally. The Revolution's success proved that an armed citizenry could defeat professional armies, that colonial subjects could govern themselves, and that Enlightenment philosophy could be translated into law. Its vocabulary—liberty, inalienable rights, consent of the governed—entered the permanent lexicon of modern politics. For quiz bowl, it is the pivot point where abstract Enlightenment thought becomes concrete political reality.
