# New York Times Co. v. Sullivan

## summary

*New York Times Co. v. Sullivan* (1964) established that public officials suing for libel must prove *actual malice*—that a statement was made with knowledge of its falsity or with reckless disregard for the truth. The landmark ruling sharply restricted libel liability and expansively protected press freedom.

## you gotta know

- L.B. Sullivan, a Montgomery, Alabama police commissioner, sued *The New York Times* for a 1960 advertisement criticizing his department's response to civil rights protests.
- Alabama's libel law allowed recovery for defamatory falsehoods; the *Times* would have been liable even for minor factual errors.
- Justice *William Brennan* held that the First Amendment requires public officials to prove *actual malice*—knowledge or reckless disregard of falsity—to recover damages.
- The decision applied to criticism of official conduct; the public's right to criticize government outweighs reputation interests.
- Shielded newspapers and media from crippling libel suits, enabling fearless reporting on government and public figures.
- Extended in *Curtis Publishing Co. v. Butts* (1967) to "public figures" more broadly, not just officials.

## connections

- [[first-amendment]] — press freedom and protection for robust public debate.
- [[libel-law]] — fundamentally transformed legal standards for defamation.
- [[civil-rights-movement]] — the advertisement at issue protested segregation and police conduct.
- [[united-states-constitution]] — the scope of free speech and press rights expanded.
- [[journalism]] — courts recognized the press's role as a check on power.
