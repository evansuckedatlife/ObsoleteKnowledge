## summary

*John Lewis* (1940–2020) was a towering figure of the civil rights movement who transitioned from Freedom Rider and march organizer to a Georgia congressman serving 34 years. His quiet dignity, moral consistency, and legislative longevity made him a living bridge between the movement and post-civil-rights America.

## you gotta know

- Elected to the *House of Representatives* from Georgia in 1986; served until his death in 2020, holding the seat for 34 consecutive terms.
- A founder and leader of the *Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee* (SNCC), organizing *sit-ins* and voting-rights campaigns in the Deep South in the early 1960s.
- Participated in the 1961 *Freedom Rides*, buses that integrated interstate travel; was arrested and beaten multiple times by segregationists.
- Marched across the Edmund Pettus Bridge in Selma, Alabama (1965), suffering a skull fracture from state troopers in what became known as *Bloody Sunday*.
- Authored *Walking with the Wind* (1998), a defining memoir of the movement; became known for his quiet moral authority in the House, rarely raising his voice but commanding respect.
- In his final years, emerged as a moral voice on democracy, voting rights, and conscience, especially during the Trump presidency.

## connections

- [[civil-rights-movement]] — he was a pivotal organizer and speaker; his legislative career extended its moral force into the electoral sphere.
- [[sncc]] — he rose through its ranks, serving as national chairman 1963–1966.
- [[selma-montgomery-march]] — his near-death experience at Selma became an icon of movement sacrifice and commitment.
- [[georgia]] — the state that elected him and that he represented for over three decades.
