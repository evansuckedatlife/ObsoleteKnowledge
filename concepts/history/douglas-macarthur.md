# Douglas MacArthur

## summary

*Douglas MacArthur* was the principal American commander in the Pacific theater of World War II and the first military administrator of occupied Japan, shaping the post-war order in East Asia. An officer of towering ambition and supreme confidence, he engineered spectacular comebacks—the *Inchon landing* in Korea—and left an indelible mark on occupied Japan's democratization. Yet his dismissal by President Truman during the Korean War, stemming from their irreconcilable disagreement over strategy and civilian control of the military, made him a controversial figure epitomizing the tension between military genius and political authority.

## you gotta know

- Born in 1880 into a military family; graduated first in his West Point class and served in multiple campaigns (Mexico, World War I) before rising to prominence in the 1930s as a senior commander.
- Commanded American forces in the Pacific during World War II, implementing the *island-hopping strategy* that bypassed heavily fortified Japanese positions and struck at isolated targets, bringing the war to Japan's home islands.
- Directed the Aleutian campaign, the Papua New Guinea campaign, and the liberation of the Philippines, fighting back from near-catastrophic Japanese victories to establish American dominance.
- Accepted Japan's surrender in 1945 and served as Supreme Commander of the Allied Powers in occupied Japan (1945–1951), presiding over Japan's transformation from militaristic empire to democratic nation: he oversaw land reform, dissolution of the zaibatsu (industrial conglomerates), and drafting of Japan's *Peace Constitution*.
- Launched the *Inchon landing* (September 1950) in the Korean War, an amphibious assault at the Inchon port that caught Communist forces off-guard and turned the tide of the war, a brilliant tactical stroke in an otherwise stalemate conflict.
- Clashed with President *Harry S. Truman* over the conduct of the Korean War, particularly over whether to expand the war into China; Truman sacked him in April 1951, a stunning moment of civilian authority overruling a military commander.
- After leaving office, became a hero to the Republican right, returning to testify before Congress and remaining a fixture of American politics until his death in 1964.
