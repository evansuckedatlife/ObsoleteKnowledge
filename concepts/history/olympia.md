# Olympia

## summary

*Olympia*, a sacred sanctuary in the western Peloponnese dedicated to *Zeus*, was the religious and athletic center of the Greek world for over a thousand years. The birthplace of the *Olympic Games*, the greatest athletic competition in antiquity, Olympia embodied the Greek ideals of competition, physical excellence, and religious devotion.

## you gotta know

- Hosted the *Olympic Games* every four years without interruption for nearly 1,200 years (776 BCE to 393 CE), attracting athletes and spectators from across the Greek world.
- Built as a sanctuary to *Zeus*, featuring the Temple of Zeus and the *Statue of Zeus*, a gold-and-ivory masterpiece by *Phidias* counted among the Seven Wonders.
- The *Phidias* sculpture of Zeus stood over 40 feet tall and seated within the temple; it was so magnificent that visitors wept upon seeing it.
- Housed the *Altis* (sacred grove), an open-air sanctuary containing temples, treasuries, and votive monuments built by city-states to honor victories.
- The Olympic truce (*Ekecheiria*) suspended all warfare during games; even enemies agreed to peace so athletes could compete safely.
- Crowned victors with olive wreaths, not gold—victory itself was the supreme prize, bringing honor and prestige to the champion's home city.
- Remains of the sanctuary, buried by earthquakes and floods, were discovered by archaeologists in the 19th century and extensively excavated.
