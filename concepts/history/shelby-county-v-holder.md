# Shelby County v. Holder

## summary

*Shelby County v. Holder* (2013) gutted the *Voting Rights Act of 1965*, striking down Section 5's requirement that jurisdictions with a history of racial discrimination obtain federal preclearance before changing voting rules. Chief Justice *John Roberts*, writing for a 5–4 majority, held that the formula determining which jurisdictions needed approval was outdated; the decision removed a crucial tool for preventing voter suppression and sparked a wave of restrictive voting laws.

## you gotta know

- Shelby County, Alabama, a jurisdiction with a long history of discrimination, challenged the constitutionality of Section 5 of the *Voting Rights Act*.
- Chief Justice *Roberts* argued that conditions had changed sufficiently that the pre-clearance regime was no longer justified under the *15th Amendment*.
- The decision did not overturn Section 5 entirely but invalidated the formula Congress used to identify which jurisdictions required federal approval, effectively halting preclearance.
- Within months, states previously covered began enacting strict voter ID laws, reducing early voting, purging voter rolls, and closing polling places in minority districts.
- Voting rights advocates argue the decision unleashed voter suppression; defenders contend the *VRA* had succeeded and become outdated.
- Remains one of the most consequential civil rights decisions in decades, reshaped American voting rights and electoral politics.
