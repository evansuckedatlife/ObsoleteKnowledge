# Athens

## summary

*Athens* is the birthplace of Western democracy and a dominant force in ancient Greece, celebrated for its innovations in philosophy, drama, and governance. Located in Attica on the mainland, it became the cultural and intellectual heart of the Greek world during the 5th and 4th centuries BCE, producing Socrates, Plato, Sophocles, and Aeschylus.

## you gotta know

- Founded according to legend by *Athena*, the goddess of wisdom, whose olive tree competed with *Poseidon's* gift of a salt spring for the city's patronage.
- Adopted radical *democracy* around 500 BCE, where free male citizens voted directly on laws and elected leaders—unique among Greek city-states.
- Built the *Parthenon* and other marble temples during the *Golden Age* under *Pericles* (460–429 BCE), establishing architectural standards that defined Classical Greece.
- Led the Greek alliance against the invading Persian Empire in the *Greco-Persian Wars*, defeating *Xerxes* at *Marathon*, *Salamis*, and *Plataea*.
- Rivals with *Sparta* over dominance; their conflict erupted in the *Peloponnesian War* (431–404 BCE), which Athens ultimately lost.
- Home to the *Agora* (marketplace), the *Academy* where *Plato* taught, and the *Lyceum* where *Aristotle* lectured.
- Produced unparalleled tragic dramatists (*Sophocles*, *Aeschylus*, *Euripides*) whose plays won prizes at the annual *City Dionysia* festival.
