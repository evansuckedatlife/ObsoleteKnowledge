# Battle of Wagram

## summary

The Battle of Wagram on July 5–6, 1809, was Napoleon's hard-fought victory over the Austrian army under Archduke Charles near Vienna during the War of the Fifth Coalition. Though less decisive than many Napoleonic victories and achieved only after a full two-day battle of maneuver and attrition, it demonstrated that Austrian military reforms had made them a formidable opponent; the subsequent Treaty of Schönbrunn reduced but did not eliminate Austrian power.

## you gotta know

- Fought on July 5–6, 1809, between approximately 177,000 French troops (and German allies) and 145,000 Austrian forces.
- The battle was a close contest; the Austrian army under Archduke Charles fought well and nearly broke the French center multiple times through disciplined counterattacks.
- Both days saw intense fighting across the Marchfeld plain; the French gradually wore down the Austrians through superior coordination and reserves, finally breaking their right flank on the second day.
- Austrian casualties were approximately 40,000; French losses approximately 34,000—a much closer engagement than Napoleon's previous victories.
- Austria signed the Treaty of Schönbrunn, ceding Salzburg, parts of Poland, and Galicia to France and its allies, and paying a large indemnity.
- Unlike after other defeats, Austria retained enough strength and territory to remain a great power; within a few years, it would join the Coalition against Napoleon.
- The battle demonstrated that Archduke Charles had successfully reformed and modernized the Austrian army, making it a genuine threat to French dominance.
