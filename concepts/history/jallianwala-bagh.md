## summary

The **Jallianwala Bagh Massacre** took place on April 13, 1919, in Amritsar, Punjab, when British Brigadier General *Reginald Dyer* ordered troops to fire without warning into a peaceful gathering of Indian civilians in a walled garden (bagh) called Jallianwala Bagh, killing between 379 and 1,200 people and wounding over 1,000 more. The massacre occurred amid widespread protests against the *Rowlatt Acts*, repressive wartime legislation that authorized detention without trial, and marked a decisive rupture between British colonial rule and the Indian independence movement. The slaughter of unarmed civilians, including women and children, crystallized Indian nationalist sentiment and made armed revolution seem inevitable.

## you gotta know

### The Background
- The British had enacted the *Rowlatt Acts* in March 1919, extending wartime emergency powers and allowing indefinite detention without trial.
- Indian nationalist leaders and the press denounced the laws as draconian; widespread *satyagraha* (nonviolent civil disobedience) protests erupted across Punjab.
- Agitation in Amritsar intensified; British authorities banned public gatherings and arrested prominent nationalist leaders.

### The Gathering
- On April 13, Baisakhi (the Sikh and Hindu spring harvest festival), thousands of Indians—men, women, and children—gathered in Jallianwala Bagh, a walled garden with few exits.
- The meeting was ostensibly to discuss the arrests and the Rowlatt Acts; speakers included nationalist figures and religious leaders.
- Most attendees were unaware that *Dyer* had issued a ban on all public assemblies; the gathering was not explicitly defiant but rather coincided with the festival.

### The Shooting
- *Brigadier General Reginald Dyer* arrived with approximately 90 Gurkha and Sikh soldiers; upon seeing the crowd, he immediately ordered them to fire without warning or demand for dispersal.
- Soldiers fired 1,650 rounds into the densely packed, panicked crowd for 10 minutes; people were crushed in the stampede toward the single exit.
- Dyer left without tending the wounded; bodies lay in the garden overnight; official British estimates placed deaths at 379, but Indian sources and modern historians claim closer to 1,000.

### Immediate Aftermath
- Martial law was imposed across Punjab; further protests were violently suppressed.
- Dyer was initially hailed by the British community in India; he became a symbol of colonial intransigence.
- The Indian press and nationalists erupted with outrage; the massacre unified previously divided factions of the independence movement.

### Long-term Consequences
- An official inquiry (the *Hunter Commission*, 1920) concluded Dyer had acted unjustifiably but without malice; he was relieved of command but not court-martialed.
- Dyer was later denounced by Britain itself as having acted without authority; he became a pariah in official circles but remained celebrated by right-wing British imperialists.
- The massacre vindicated *Mohandas Gandhi's* earlier warnings that British rule rested on force, not consent, and accelerated support for his *Non-Cooperation Movement* and later independence struggle.

## context

Jallianwala Bagh stands as a watershed moment in the history of decolonization. The massacre demonstrated that the British Raj, despite its claim to civilized governance, would resort to mass killing of unarmed civilians to suppress dissent. The event destroyed the fiction of a benevolent British "civilizing mission" and united Indian nationalists—Hindu, Muslim, Sikh, and secular—in the conviction that independence was not negotiable but inevitable. For Britain, it exposed the moral bankruptcy of imperial rule; for India, it transformed nonviolent resistance from one tactic among many into a moral imperative and strategic necessity. The massacre's infamy endured for a century and remained a touchstone of colonial atrocity in postcolonial memory.
