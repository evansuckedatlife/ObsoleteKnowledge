# Andrew Johnson

## summary

Andrew Johnson (1808–1875) was the 17th President of the United States, ascending to office following Abraham Lincoln's assassination in 1865. A Tennessee tailor and senator with limited education, he faced immediate crisis managing Reconstruction—the reintegration of the defeated Southern states and the status of freed slaves. His conflict with Republican Congress over Reconstruction policy led to his impeachment, making him a polarizing figure at a crucial historical moment.

## you gotta know

- Self-taught tailor from Tennessee; became a senator and remained in the Senate after the secession of other Southern states, the only senator from a seceded state to remain loyal to the Union.
- Lincoln appointed him Military Governor of Tennessee (1862–1865) as a symbol of Union loyalty in a border state; chose him as running mate in 1864.
- Became president in April 1865, weeks after Lee's surrender, facing the colossal task of Reconstruction without Lincoln's political skill or moral authority.
- Adopted a lenient Reconstruction policy, rapidly readmitting Southern states with minimal conditions; pardoned most Confederate leaders and allowed them to regain political power.
- Opposed the *Freedmen's Bureau* and the *14th Amendment*, which extended civil rights to freed slaves and restricted Southern political participation; his opposition to civil rights for Black Americans was explicit.
- Clashed with the Republican-controlled Congress, which sought harsher Reconstruction through the *Reconstruction Acts* (1867), dividing the South into military districts and requiring states to guarantee voting rights to freed slaves.
- *Impeached* by the House (1868) for violating the Tenure of Office Act by dismissing Secretary of War Edwin Stanton; acquitted by the Senate by one vote, narrowly avoiding removal.
- Served out his term (1865–1869) as a lame-duck, irrelevant to subsequent Reconstruction policy; his legacy became synonymous with Reconstruction's failure to establish durable civil rights for Black Americans.
