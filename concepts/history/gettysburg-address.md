# Gettysburg Address

## summary

Abraham Lincoln's brief remarks delivered at the dedication of the Soldiers' National Cemetery in Gettysburg, Pennsylvania on *November 19, 1863*, reframe the Civil War not as a contest for union but as a struggle for the proposition that *"all men are created equal."* Though only 272 words, it became one of history's most consequential political statements, reshaping American identity through its vision of democratic renewal.

## you gotta know

- Spoken at the dedication of the battlefield cemetery on *November 19, 1863*, four months after the Battle of Gettysburg.
- Opens with the famous line: *"Four score and seven years ago our fathers brought forth on this continent, a new nation, conceived in Liberty, and dedicated to the proposition that all men are created equal."*
- Redefines the war's purpose from preserving the Union to *"a new birth of freedom"* — a moral reckoning with slavery.
- Employs birth and renewal metaphors: the nation is not decades old but *"fourscore and seven years,"* and must be reborn through sacrifice.
- Lincoln's actual delivery was brief; the main oration (by Edward Everett, lasting two hours) was forgotten while Lincoln's 272-word address endured as the canonical statement.
- The phrase *"the last full measure of devotion"* (describing the soldiers' sacrifice) became a shorthand for ultimate civic commitment.
- Delivered amid ongoing Civil War tragedy; the speech resolved the catastrophe into moral purpose.
