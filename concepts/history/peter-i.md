# Peter I

## summary

*Peter I* (1672–1725), known as *Peter the Great*, transformed Russia from a semi-feudal Eurasian power into a modern European empire. Through forced modernization, military innovation, military conquest, and the founding of St. Petersburg, he aligned Russian culture and statecraft with Western models. His reign marked the effective beginning of Russia as a great power in European affairs, cementing the Romanov dynasty.

## you gotta know

### Westernization & Cultural Transformation
- Undertook the *Grand Embassy* (1697–1698), traveling incognito to Western Europe to study shipbuilding, engineering, and military science.
- Mandated "westernization" edicts: nobles were required to shave beards and adopt Western dress, a radical break with Orthodox tradition.
- Reformed the church, subordinating the patriarch to a secular governing board (the Holy Synod), making the Russian church an instrument of state.

### Military Reforms & The Great Northern War
- Modernized the Russian army along Western lines, replacing feudal levies with a standing conscripted force.
- Fought the Great Northern War (1700–1721) against Sweden's *Charles XII*, struggling at first but eventually defeating Sweden and establishing Russia as the dominant Baltic power.
- Victory at the Battle of Poltava (1709) was the turning point; Sweden's decline cleared the way for Russian hegemony in Eastern Europe.

### St. Petersburg & Maritime Expansion
- Founded *St. Petersburg* (1703) as a "window to the West," building it on captured Swedish territory to secure Baltic access.
- Created a modern Russian navy, transitioning from inland river power to seagoing strength.
- Moved the capital from Moscow to St. Petersburg, symbolizing the turn toward Europe.

### Absolute Governance
- Centralized power ruthlessly, establishing the Table of Ranks (1722), a hierarchy tying nobility status to state service rather than birth.
- Conducted military campaigns in the Caucasus and Caspian region, expanding the empire southward.
- His succession was fraught; he had his own son Alexei executed (1718) on suspicion of plotting against him.

## context

Peter the Great embodies the *enlightened absolutist* archetype: he willed Russia into modernity through state coercion, accepting the suffering of his own people as the price of imperial greatness. His methods were brutal—forced conscription, religious reform, execution of rivals—yet they worked: he took a landlocked, culturally isolated state and made it a player in European power politics. St. Petersburg became one of Europe's great cities; Russian culture began to absorb French and Italian influences; the fleet defeated Sweden. This template of top-down modernization, state terror in service of progress, and naked will-to-power became the blueprint for later Russian leaders, from Catherine the Great to Stalin. He is the hinge on which Russia turns from East to West.
