# Populist Party

## summary

The Populist Party, also known as the People's Party, emerged in the 1890s as a radical agrarian and labor movement responding to rural hardship, farm debt, and industrial monopolies. Running on a platform of free silver coinage and regulation of big business, the Populists achieved significant electoral success in the 1890s but were absorbed into the Democratic Party by 1896, when Democratic nominee William Jennings Bryan adopted their core silver-coinage plank.

## you gotta know

- The party arose from the Farmers' Alliance movement, championing debt-ridden farmers in the South and Great Plains who blamed railroads, banks, and urban elites for their plight.
- In the 1892 presidential election, Populist candidate James Weaver won over 8% of the popular vote and 22 electoral votes—the strongest showing for a third party since the GOP's founding in 1856.
- The party demanded unlimited coinage of silver at a 16-to-1 ratio with gold (free silver), believing inflation would ease farm debt and aid debtors over creditors.
- Populist platforms also included government ownership of railroads and telegraph lines, an eight-hour workday, and restrictions on land ownership by foreign investors.
- William Jennings Bryan's capture of the 1896 Democratic nomination with his "Cross of Gold" speech effectively ended the Populist Party as an independent force, though some called themselves Populists into the early 1900s.
- The party's political energy was absorbed into Progressivism; many of its ideas—railroad regulation, antitrust action—became mainstream Democratic policy.
