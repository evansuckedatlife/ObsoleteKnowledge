# Sand Creek

## summary

The **Sand Creek Massacre** occurred on November 29, 1864, when Colorado cavalry volunteers under Colonel *John Milton Chivington* attacked a peaceful encampment of *Cheyenne* and *Arapaho* tribes near Sand Creek in southeastern Colorado, killing between 150 and 200 civilians, mostly women, children, and elderly. The tribes, under Chief *Black Kettle*, believed they were under U.S. military protection after negotiating a truce; the massacre was shocking precisely because it violated that fragile trust and demonstrated the ruthlessness of frontier militias toward Native Americans. The atrocity became a turning point that united the Great Plains tribes in armed resistance and exposed the moral bankruptcy of American Indian policy.

## you gotta know

### The Encampment
- The Cheyenne and Arapaho band, led by *Black Kettle*, had agreed to a cease-fire with the U.S. Army following months of conflict over settlers encroaching on their lands.
- *Black Kettle* believed an American flag flown above the camp signaled the tribe's protected status; he had explicitly been told the camp was safe.
- The encampment contained roughly 500 to 600 people, approximately 100 to 150 of them warriors; the rest were women, children, and elderly.

### The Attack
- Chivington and approximately 700 cavalry volunteers attacked at dawn without warning; Chivington explicitly ordered his men to take no prisoners and kill all they could.
- Soldiers fired artillery and rifles into the camp; survivors fled, pursued across the plains for miles.
- Women were sexually assaulted and mutilated; scalps and body parts were collected as trophies; bodies were left where they fell.

### Casualties and Aftermath
- Between 150 and 200 Cheyenne and Arapaho were killed, the majority noncombatants; American casualties were minimal (9 killed, 38 wounded).
- Survivors scattered; the trauma and rage among the tribes accelerated armed resistance across the Great Plains.
- Chivington initially reported a great victory; he was celebrated in Colorado newspapers and recruited for an election campaign.

### Investigation and Vindication
- Congressional investigation (the *Sand Creek Massacre Committee*, 1865) concluded the attack was unprovoked and unjustifiable; Chivington was denounced but never court-martialed.
- The atrocity shocked the American public and foreign observers; it became a defining example of American frontier violence.
- *Black Kettle*, despite the massacre's horrors, attempted to maintain peace with the U.S. for several more years until killed in a similar attack at Washita River (1868).

### Consequences
- The Cheyenne and Arapaho united with the *Sioux* and other tribes in coordinated resistance; the Great Plains War of 1865–1868 followed.
- Sand Creek vindicated warrior factions within the tribes who had opposed accommodation with the Americans; it hardened the conviction that peace treaties were worthless.

## context

Sand Creek was not a random act but part of a systematic campaign to clear the Great Plains of Native Americans and open the land to settler colonization. Chivington and the Colorado militia were motivated by gold rushes, settlement expansion, and a prevailing ideology of racial supremacy that viewed Indigenous people as obstacles to civilization. The massacre revealed that official protections and treaties were worthless when frontier militias faced no real consequences for violation. Sand Creek radicalized Native American resistance; it transformed the Great Plains conflict from an irregular frontier skirmish into a full-scale war, and it demonstrated that American promises of peace and protection were hollow. The event became a touchstone of Indigenous memory and a harbinger of the Indian Wars' final phase.
