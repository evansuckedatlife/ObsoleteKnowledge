## summary

The Battle of Normandy (June–August 1944), codenamed *Operation Overlord*, was the largest amphibious invasion in history: over 150,000 Allied troops stormed the beaches of Normandy, France, to establish a foothold in Nazi-occupied Western Europe. Led by American General *Dwight D. Eisenhower*, the assault broke through German defensive lines and opened a decisive western front. The battle's success—and the subsequent breakout into France—turned the tide of the war in Europe and began the liberation of occupied Western Europe. Normandy is remembered for the scale of coordination, the courage of the troops, and the pivotal shift it represented in the war.

## you gotta know

### Background
- Planned as the primary Allied assault to break German control of Western Europe; Hitler's Atlantic Wall fortifications defended the Normandy coast.
- Supreme Commander *Dwight D. Eisenhower* coordinated American, British, and Canadian forces; Field Marshal *Bernard Montgomery* commanded ground forces.
- German General *Erwin Rommel* had reinforced Normandy's defenses, but Hitler refused to move panzer reserves without direct orders, paralyzing German response.

### The Invasion (June 6–30, 1944)
- D-Day, June 6, 1944: 5,000 ships and over 150,000 troops landed on five beach sectors (*Utah, Omaha, Gold, Juno, Sword*) across a 50-mile front.
- American forces faced the fiercest resistance at *Omaha Beach*; British and Canadian landings were more successful.
- Airborne drops behind German lines (American 101st and 82nd Airborne, British 6th Airborne) disrupted German reinforcements and secured critical objectives.

### Breakthrough (July–August 1944)
- The beachhead expanded slowly through hedgerow combat in the Norman bocage (dense, tangled countryside); breakout was delayed by German resistance and poor weather.
- American forces captured *Saint-Lô* and *Cherbourg*; British and Canadian forces pushed toward *Caen*, delayed by German panzer counterattacks.
- The *Falaise Pocket* (mid-August) trapped and destroyed a German army group, breaking German resistance; the Allies liberated Paris and advanced toward Germany.

## context

Normandy stands as the Allies' defining offensive of World War II—the moment industrial-scale amphibious assault proved feasible and strategic surprise and coordination could overcome a prepared enemy. The scale of logistics, the necessity of air superiority, and the human cost (over 200,000 total casualties across all sides) exemplify modern warfare's complexity. Normandy also marks the opening of the long-delayed second front in Europe, relieving pressure on the Soviet Union and initiating the West's race to Central Europe before Soviet armies arrived. In quiz bowl, Normandy is inseparable from Eisenhower, Montgomery, the invasion plan, individual beaches (especially Omaha), and the liberation narrative.
