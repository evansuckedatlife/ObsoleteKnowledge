# Christopher Columbus

## summary

*Christopher Columbus* (1451–1506) was a Genoese navigator whose four transatlantic voyages (1492–1502) under the Spanish flag initiated sustained European contact with the Americas and ushered in the Age of Exploration. Though not the first European to reach the New World—Norse seafarers preceded him by five centuries—Columbus's achievement was far more consequential: his voyages triggered the wholesale colonization, conquest, and demographic catastrophe that would define the next four centuries. Columbus was driven by a mixture of ambition, greed, and religious zeal; he believed (incorrectly) that he had reached Asia and never grasped the magnitude of his "discovery." His personal legacy as a colonial administrator was one of brutality and mismanagement, yet his voyage became mythologized as the beginning of European modernity.

## you gotta know

### The Voyages
- Sailed under the Spanish flag commanding three ships (*Niña*, *Pinta*, *Santa María*) on his first transatlantic voyage (1492), which lasted 70 days and reached the Caribbean.
- Believed he could reach *Asia* by sailing west across the Atlantic, underestimating the Earth's circumference and completely unaware of the existence of the Americas.
- Landed in the *Bahamas* in October 1492 and subsequently explored islands in what he called the *West Indies*, convinced he had reached the outer islands of Asia.
- Made four Atlantic voyages between 1492 and 1502, returning to Spain between expeditions and always maintaining that he had reached the Asian coast, despite mounting geographical evidence to the contrary.

### Colonial Administration
- Established the colony of *La Isabela* on *Hispaniola* in 1494, the first permanent European settlement in the Caribbean, which served as the base for further exploration and conquest.
- His tenure as governor was marked by extreme brutality: he demanded tribute in gold from indigenous peoples, imposed cruel punishments on colonists who defied him, and oversaw the enslavement of thousands of natives.
- Enslaved indigenous people and sent them back to Spain as human cargo; when indigenous uprisings threatened Spanish control, he responded with military force and systematic violence.
- Arrested and returned to Spain in chains in 1500 after reports of his violent and chaotic governance reached the Crown; he spent his final years petitioning for recognition and financial recompense, dying in relative obscurity.

### Historical Impact
- His voyages triggered the Age of Exploration and opened the door to European colonial expansion, conquest, and the transatlantic trade systems (including the slave trade) that would define the following centuries.
- Initiated the first sustained contact between Eurasia and the Americas, leading to the Columbian Exchange—the biological, cultural, and epidemiological transformation of both hemispheres.
- The indigenous populations of the Caribbean and Americas experienced demographic catastrophe: diseases, warfare, and enslavement killed an estimated 90% of native populations within a century.

## context

Columbus is a figure around whom myth and historical reality clash sharply. In popular imagination, he is celebrated as the courageous discoverer who proved the Earth was round and opened a new world to European civilization—a narrative that omits his cruelty, his geographical errors (he never reached Asia and didn't realize it), and the catastrophic consequences of his voyage for indigenous peoples. Modern scholarship has reframed him as the initiator of European colonialism and genocide, a man whose ambitions and methods were entirely typical of his era but whose consequence was historically singular. The question of how to remember Columbus—as hero or harbinger of conquest—remains deeply contested.
