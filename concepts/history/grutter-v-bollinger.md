# Grutter v. Bollinger

## summary

*Grutter v. Bollinger* (2003) upheld affirmative action in higher education, ruling 5–4 that universities have a compelling interest in achieving a diverse student body and may use race-conscious admissions policies to achieve it. Barbara Grutter, a white applicant rejected from University of Michigan Law School, sued; Justice *Sandra Day O'Connor*'s majority opinion reaffirmed that race-conscious admissions are constitutional when narrowly tailored and that diversity itself is a worthy educational goal.

## you gotta know

- *Barbara Grutter* was rejected from Michigan Law School after her application was reviewed in a process that considered race.
- Justice *O'Connor* wrote that student body diversity is a compelling state interest and that race-conscious admissions satisfy strict scrutiny if used flexibly.
- The decision reaffirmed *Bakke*'s holding that quotas are forbidden but race-conscious holistic review is permissible.
- O'Connor suggested that race-conscious admissions should phase out in 25 years as inequalities diminish—a sunset clause that motivated recent litigation.
- Universities nationwide adopted the decision as license for race-conscious programs; it was the law for two decades.
- Overruled by *Students for Fair Admissions v. Harvard* (2023), which held that race-conscious admissions violate the *14th Amendment*.
