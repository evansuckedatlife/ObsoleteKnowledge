# The Civil Rights Cases (1883)

## summary

*The Civil Rights Cases* (1883) struck down the *Civil Rights Act of 1875*, which had prohibited racial discrimination by private businesses in public accommodations, ruling that the *14th Amendment* constrains only state action, not private conduct. Justice *Joseph P. Bradley*'s 8–1 majority held that Congress lacked the power to regulate private discrimination, gutting the last major civil rights legislation of Reconstruction and opening the door to Jim Crow statutes that could evade constitutional challenge.

## you gotta know

- The cases consolidated five separate actions challenging the 1875 act's ban on discrimination by hotels, railroads, theaters, and similar private businesses.
- Justice *Bradley* wrote that the *14th Amendment* applies only to state action; Congress cannot regulate purely private discrimination through the *Enforcement Clause*.
- The decision severed Congress's power to protect freedmen from private violence and discrimination, leaving federal civil rights authority in tatters.
- Effectively repealed the 1875 act and handed victory to those seeking to roll back Reconstruction gains.
- Stood as law for nearly 90 years until *Heart of Atlanta Motel v. United States* (1964) found a different constitutional hook—the *Commerce Clause*—for federal civil rights enforcement.
- Justice *John Marshall Harlan* dissented, warning the decision would facilitate widespread racial oppression.
