# Battle of Leipzig

## summary

The Battle of Leipzig, fought October 16–19, 1813, was called the "Battle of the Nations" and is considered the largest land battle of the Napoleonic Wars, with over 500,000 troops engaged. The combined Prussian, Russian, Austrian, and Swedish armies defeated Napoleon in a four-day struggle in Saxony; the French were forced to retreat westward, and the Coalition invasion of France became inevitable.

## you gotta know

- Fought October 16–19, 1813, between approximately 190,000 French troops (including Saxon and Polish contingents) and 320,000 Allied troops.
- The battle turned on the fourth day when Saxon forces switched sides mid-engagement, collapsing the French left flank and forcing a general retreat.
- Combined casualties were estimated at over 100,000, making it one of the bloodiest battles of the era.
- Napoleon was unable to achieve numerical superiority on any part of the field despite tactical maneuvering; the Coalition's coordinated assault from multiple directions overwhelmed him.
- The retreat from Leipzig was disorganized; only about 60,000 French troops remained combat-effective, a loss of roughly 130,000 from the Grande Armée.
- The defeat effectively ended French control of Germany and triggered an Allied invasion of France itself in early 1814.
- Leipzig marked the psychological turning point—both sides recognized that Napoleon's military dominance had ended.
