## summary

The Battle of El Alamein (October–November 1942) was a desert warfare engagement in Egypt between the British Eighth Army and the Axis forces (German-Italian) for control of a strategic point on the North African coast. British General *Bernard Montgomery* halted German General *Erwin Rommel*'s advance toward the Suez Canal and Cairo, inflicting his first major defeat. The victory allowed the British to counterattack and begin the liberation of North Africa, marking the Allies' first clear triumph over the Wehrmacht in sustained combat.

## you gotta know

### Background
- Fought near the village of *El Alamein* on Egypt's Mediterranean coast, 66 miles west of Alexandria.
- *Erwin Rommel*'s Afrika Korps had pushed east across Libya and threatened Egypt; British forces, low on supplies and confidence, fell back to defend the Nile Delta.
- British General *Bernard Montgomery* assumed command of the Eighth Army in August 1942 and prepared a defensive stand at El Alamein.

### The Battle
- The British established strong defensive positions with minefields, artillery, and anti-tank guns; Montgomery avoided the mobility warfare Rommel preferred.
- Fighting raged for 11 days (October 23 – November 4) with grinding, attritional combat; the British had numerical superiority in men and materiel.
- Rommel, outnumbered and short of fuel and ammunition, ordered a retreat; the Axis forces withdrew westward into Libya.

### Aftermath
- The British pursued Rommel westward; combined with the American *Operation Torch* landings in Morocco and Algeria (November 1942), Axis forces were squeezed from both directions.
- El Alamein ended the myth of Rommel's invincibility in North Africa and restored British prestige after defeats at Tobruk and Gazala.
- The Allies secured control of the Mediterranean and prepared to invade Sicily and mainland Europe.

## context

El Alamein was the Allies' watershed in North Africa—the moment the initiative shifted from Axis to Allies and proved that German forces could be defeated decisively. Unlike Stalingrad's savage urban destruction, El Alamein was a textbook defensive victory: strong position, attrition, and maneuver. The battle rehabilitated British credibility after a string of Mediterranean losses and showcased *Bernard Montgomery*'s methodical, defensive approach (in sharp contrast to Rommel's daring). In quiz bowl, El Alamein anchors discussions of the North Africa campaign, Montgomery's career, and the Allies' first major success.
