# Corset

## summary

The *corset* is a rigid undergarment designed to shape the torso, particularly to create a pronounced waistline and support the bust. Worn primarily by women from the 16th century through the early 20th century, the corset became synonymous with Victorian femininity and ideal beauty standards, though it was also frequently criticized for restricting movement and potentially damaging organs.

## you gotta know

- Constructed from linen, cotton, or silk with internal boning (originally whalebone, later steel) to maintain rigid structure and shape.
- The Victorian corset cinched the waist dramatically, often to 18–20 inches for fashionable women, creating the distinctive hourglass silhouette.
- Stayed in fashion for over 400 years, evolving from simple body wraps in the Renaissance to elaborate engineered undergarments by the 1800s.
- Medical debate surrounded corsets: physicians argued they compressed organs and restricted breathing, while wearers claimed comfort and necessity for fashion.
- The corset's decline accelerated in the 1920s as the flapper dress and modern fashion movements rejected restrictive silhouettes and emphasized comfort.
- Early corsets laced at the back; later designs incorporated front closures and eventually hook-and-eye fasteners for easier dressing.
- Symbol of both feminine power and female constraint—feminists critiqued it as patriarchal oppression, while fashionable women wore it as essential beauty armor.

## connections

- [[farthingale]] — companion understructure that worked with the corset to define Renaissance and Elizabethan silhouettes.
- [[bustle]] — successor garment that redirected the corset's emphasis from waist to rear projection in the Victorian era.
- [[crinoline]] — alternative volume-creating undergarment that competed with and eventually replaced rigid corsetry.
- [[flapper-dress]] — modern design that rejected corsetry in favor of loose, straight silhouettes.
- [[victoria]] — British queen whose era codified the ultra-tight Victorian corset as the beauty standard.
