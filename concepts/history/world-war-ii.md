# World War II

## summary

World War II was fought from 1939 to 1945 between the *Axis Powers* (*Germany*, *Italy*, *Japan*) and the *Allied Powers* (*Britain*, *Soviet Union*, *United States*, and numerous others), killing an estimated 70–85 million people and reshaping global politics, technology, and morality. It was the deadliest and most destructive war in human history, encompassing genocide, total warfare, nuclear weapons, and the emergence of two superpowers that would dominate the Cold War era.

## you gotta know

### origins and escalation

- *Adolf Hitler* and the *Nazi Party* exploited Germany's postwar resentment and economic collapse, exploiting the *Treaty of Versailles* to justify territorial expansion and the pursuit of *Lebensraum* (living space) for the *Aryan* race.
- Germany's invasion of *Poland* on 1 September 1939 triggered Britain and France to declare war; Germany's subsequent blitzkrieg conquered *France*, the *Netherlands*, *Belgium*, and threatened *Britain* in the *Battle of Britain* (1940–41).
- Japan's militarism in Asia led to the invasion of *Manchuria* (1931), *China* (1937), and eventually French Indochina; Italy under *Benito Mussolini* expanded in *Ethiopia* and the *Mediterranean*; the three Axis powers coordinated but rarely achieved perfect synchronization.

### the war in Europe

- Germany's surprise invasion of the *Soviet Union* in June 1941 (*Operation Barbarossa*) opened a titanic two-front struggle; the *Eastern Front* became the war's bloodiest theater, with the *Siege of Stalingrad* (1942–43) and *Battle of Kursk* (1943) among history's deadliest battles.
- The Allies invaded *Normandy* on *D-Day* (6 June 1944), establishing the *Western Front* and beginning the liberation of Western Europe from Nazi occupation.
- The *Holocaust*—Nazi Germany's systematic genocide of six million Jews, along with millions of Roma, disabled people, Poles, Soviet prisoners, and others—represents history's deadliest act of mass murder and the nadir of industrial-scale atrocity.

### the war in Asia and the Pacific

- Japan's attack on *Pearl Harbor* (7 December 1941) brought the *United States* into the war; the subsequent island-hopping campaign by the U.S. moved steadily toward Japan's homeland.
- Germany surrendered unconditionally on 8 May 1945 after Hitler's suicide; Japan fought until the U.S. dropped *atomic bombs* on *Hiroshima* (6 August) and *Nagasaki* (9 August) 1945, killing hundreds of thousands and prompting Japan's surrender on 2 September.
- The war cost approximately *70–85 million lives*, with the Soviet Union alone losing over 27 million; civilian deaths exceeded military, and entire cities and industries were reduced to rubble.

### aftermath and redrawing

- The *Yalta Conference* (February 1945) and *Potsdam Conference* (July 1945) divided postwar Europe into *Allied occupation zones*, partitioning Germany and establishing Soviet hegemony over Eastern Europe—the seeds of the *Cold War*.
- The *United Nations* was founded (April 1945) to prevent future global wars; the *Universal Declaration of Human Rights* (1948) responded to the Holocaust.
- Germany and Japan were demilitarized and rebuilt under U.S. and Soviet influence; the world emerged bipolar, with the U.S. and Soviet Union as superpowers, initiating nuclear-armed standoff that would define the next 45 years.

## context

World War II crystallized the horrors of total war and industrial genocide, shattered the Eurocentric world order, and thrust nuclear weapons into human hands. The war spawned existentialism as a response to meaninglessness (Sartre, Camus); it traumatized a generation and gave rise to psychoanalytic critiques of fascism and authoritarianism. The Holocaust remains the paradigm case of systematic dehumanization. The war's technology—radar, jets, rockets, nuclear fission—rocketed humanity into the atomic age. Its territorial redrawing (the division of Korea, the partition of India, the birth of Israel) set the stage for late-20th-century conflicts. The war's moral accounting—Nuremberg trials, war crimes definitions, human rights law—fundamentally changed how civilization reckons with atrocity.
