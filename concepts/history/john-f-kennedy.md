# John F. Kennedy

## summary

John F. Kennedy (1917–1963) was the 35th President of the United States and the youngest to assume office at 43. His presidency, from 1961 to his assassination in 1963, was defined by Cold War crises—the Bay of Pigs, the Cuban Missile Crisis—civil rights advocacy, and the space race. His rhetoric of idealistic American purpose and his tragic death at the height of his power made him an enduring symbol of American aspiration and loss.

## you gotta know

### early life & rise

- Born to a wealthy, prominent Irish-American family in Boston; his father was an ambassador and patriarch of the political Kennedy clan.
- Served in the Navy during World War II; his patrol torpedo boat (*PT-109*) was sunk by a Japanese destroyer, and he was rescued after swimming for help.
- Elected to the House (1947–1953) and Senate (1953–1960) from Massachusetts; suffered from chronic health issues (Addison's disease, chronic back pain) throughout his life, unknown to the public.
- Defeated Richard Nixon in the 1960 presidential election in a close, televised debate that marked the emergence of television as a decisive political medium.
- Delivered his famous *inauguration speech* ("Ask not what your country can do for you—ask what you can do for your country") in January 1961, setting a tone of idealistic public service.

### foreign policy crises

- Authorized the disastrous *Bay of Pigs* invasion (April 1961), an attempt by CIA-backed Cuban exiles to topple Fidel Castro; the invasion failed, damaging American credibility internationally.
- Confronted the *Cuban Missile Crisis* (October 1962)—a 13-day standoff in which the Soviet Union placed nuclear missiles in Cuba, bringing the world to the brink of nuclear war. Kennedy's "quarantine" (blockade) and backroom negotiations with Nikita Khrushchev achieved Soviet withdrawal without military conflict, widely seen as his greatest foreign policy success.
- Escalated American military involvement in Vietnam, increasing the number of military "advisors" from ~900 to ~16,000 by his death.
- Supported the space race aggressively; committed the nation to landing a man on the moon by decade's end (achieved in 1969).

### civil rights & domestic

- Faced pressure from the civil rights movement; while initially reluctant, he deployed federal troops to enforce desegregation at the University of Mississippi (1962) and University of Alabama (1963).
- In June 1963, after the Birmingham campaign, he delivered a televised address embracing civil rights as a "moral issue" and submitted legislation that became the *Civil Rights Act of 1964* (passed after his death).
- Signed the *Nuclear Test Ban Treaty* (1963), a Cold War thaw that reduced atmospheric radiation from nuclear testing.

### assassination

- Assassinated in Dallas, Texas, on November 22, 1963, by Lee Harvey Oswald (officially; conspiracy theories persist).
- His death shocked the nation and the world; the images—the motorcade, Jackie Kennedy cradling his head—became indelible in American memory.

## context

Kennedy inherited the Cold War and the civil rights crisis; he did not originate either, but his decisions shaped their trajectories. The Bay of Pigs was a humbling early failure, but the Cuban Missile Crisis—his response to a genuine nuclear threat—vindicated his caution and diplomatic skill. His reluctant embrace of civil rights near the end of his presidency suggested he was evolving, though his assassination prevents any assessment of what his full commitment might have been. He was a creature of his time—glamorous, eloquent, beneficiary of wealth and family power—but he also presided over genuine achievements: the space program, the test ban, the beginnings of a legal framework for civil rights. His youth and energy contrasted with his predecessors and set a new standard for presidential vigor (a standard his chronic illness belied). His death elevated him to tragic icon; Kennedy became shorthand for lost American idealism, a president cut down before his full agenda could unfold.
