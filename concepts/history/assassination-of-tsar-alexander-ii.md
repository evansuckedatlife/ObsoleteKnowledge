# Assassination of Tsar Alexander II

## summary

On March 13, 1881, *Tsar Alexander II* of Russia was mortally wounded by a bomb thrown by members of the revolutionary group *Narodnaya Volya* (People's Will) in *Saint Petersburg*. The assassination of the "Tsar Liberator"—who had freed the serfs and initiated modest reforms—was shocking because it demonstrated that even cautious reform could not appease radical revolutionaries. The event accelerated Russia's retreat into authoritarianism and became a symbol of both the power and the terrorism of revolutionary idealism.

## you gotta know

### The Assassination
- Occurred March 13, 1881 (March 1 by the Julian calendar then used in Russia), in Saint Petersburg.
- Perpetrators: *Narodnaya Volya*, a revolutionary terrorist organization dedicated to overthrowing the Romanov dynasty.
- The assassination used a bomb placed in the street; Alexander's carriage was destroyed.
- Alexander was mortally wounded and died within hours at the Winter Palace from the blast injuries.

### Revolutionary Context
- Narodnaya Volya had attempted to assassinate Alexander II multiple times; the 1881 bombing succeeded after years of failed attempts.
- The group's ideology blended anarchism, socialism, and Russian nihilism; they believed terrorism was justified as a tool against autocracy.
- Alexander II, despite his "Liberator" reputation for freeing the serfs in 1861, had become increasingly conservative and repressive by the 1870s.
- The revolutionaries rejected his reforms as inadequate and demanded the overthrow of the entire system.

### Political Aftermath
- Alexander's son, *Alexander III*, became tsar and immediately adopted a policy of strict reaction and repression.
- The executive of Narodnaya Volya, including the female terrorist *Sophia Perovskaya*, were arrested and executed.
- Alexander III blamed liberalism itself for the assassination and reversed his father's reforms, leading to decades of autocratic rule.
- The event discredited radical terrorism in Russian intellectual circles for a generation but also radicalised the next wave of revolutionaries.

## context

The assassination of Alexander II exemplifies the paradox of revolutionary violence: by killing a reformer, the terrorists provoked a crackdown far harsher than anything they opposed. The event marks a critical moment in Russian history—the point at which the Romanov autocracy chose reaction over evolution, a choice that would contribute to the instability leading to revolution in 1917. Alexander II's death also stands as a watershed in the history of anarchist and socialist ideology: was terrorism a justified weapon against autocracy, or did it discredit the cause? Russian intellectuals debated this question for decades.
