# Ich bin ein Berliner

## summary

President John F. Kennedy's speech delivered in West Berlin on *June 26, 1963*, expressed American solidarity with the divided city and symbolically reaffirmed the West's commitment to resisting Soviet expansion. The phrase *"Ich bin ein Berliner"* ("I am a Berliner") — spoken in halting German — became an iconic gesture of unity across ideological divide, spoken mere months before his assassination.

## you gotta know

- Delivered at the *Rudolph Wilde Platz* in West Berlin on *June 26, 1963*, at the height of Cold War tensions.
- Kennedy's declaration *"Ich bin ein Berliner"* (in German) electrified the crowd by claiming solidarity with the trapped city.
- Comes sixteen months after the construction of the *Berlin Wall* (August 1961), which had physically divided the city.
- Kennedy acknowledges *"Lasst mich sagen, im Westen, wo ich bin, sitze ich euch"* — situating American power as a protector of freedom.
- The speech reiterates American values — *"freedom has many difficulties and democracy is not perfect"* — yet insists these are preferable to tyranny.
- Kennedy notes that dictatorships boast of accomplishments; free people tell of their failures and learn from them.
- The crowd of approximately 450,000 roared approval; the speech became an enduring symbol of the West's ideological commitment.
