## summary

Franklin D. Roosevelt's address to Congress on *January 6, 1941*, articulated four freedoms — of speech, of worship, from want, and from fear — as the universal aspirations at stake in the looming global conflict. Delivered before American entry into World War II, the speech reframed the war as a contest for fundamental human rights and became foundational to the postwar liberal world order.

## you gotta know

- Delivered to Congress on *January 6, 1941*, nine months before Pearl Harbor, during the Lend-Lease debate.
- Enumerates the *Four Freedoms*: freedom of speech and expression, freedom of worship, freedom from want, and freedom from fear.
- Roosevelt frames these freedoms not as American luxuries but as *"universal values"* — *"things for which all peoples in all lands are fighting."*
- The speech anticipates American military mobilization: *"As Americans, we are not afraid of this future."*
- FDR emphasizes that the freedoms include protection from *"want"* — suggesting economic security as a human right, not mere charity.
- The Four Freedoms became iconographic — Norman Rockwell created four paintings illustrating them, further embedding them in American culture.
- The speech helped justify Lend-Lease aid to Britain and Soviet Union, framing economic/military support as defense of universal principles.
