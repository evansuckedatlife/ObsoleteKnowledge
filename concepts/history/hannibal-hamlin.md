## summary

**Hannibal Hamlin** was the first Vice President under Abraham Lincoln (1861–1865) and a Maine political stalwart who represented the radical Republican faction's antislavery principles. Though he was not renominated in 1864—replaced by Andrew Johnson—Hamlin remained an influential figure in Republican politics during and after the Civil War.

## you gotta know

- Vice President during the outbreak of the American Civil War and Lincoln's critical first term, serving as a link to the radical Republicans who demanded aggressive prosecution of the war.
- Not renominated for a second term in 1864 when Lincoln, seeking broader coalition, selected Tennessee War Democrat Andrew Johnson as his running mate instead.
- Served in the U.S. Senate from Maine before and after his vice presidency, becoming a leading voice for Reconstruction policy and equal rights for freed slaves.
- Supported the Radical Republican agenda of aggressive Reconstruction, placing him in conflict with President Andrew Johnson's more lenient approach to the South.
- Previously served in the House of Representatives and as Governor of Maine, establishing himself as a stalwart of Maine Republican politics.
- Was a newspaper editor and abolitionist whose passionate antislavery convictions aligned him with the party's core ideology.
- Served as a customs house official in Maine late in his career and remained a respected elder statesman until his death in 1891.

## connections

- [[abraham-lincoln]] — nominated him as Vice President to unite the party's radical wing.
- [[andrew-johnson]] — replaced him on the 1864 ticket, shifting the party toward reunion rather than radical reconstruction.
- [[the-american-civil-war]] — Vice President during the conflict that defined his era.
- [[radical-republicans]] — ideological ally in the fight for emancipation and equal rights.
