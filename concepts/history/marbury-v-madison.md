# Marbury v. Madison

## summary

Marbury v. Madison (1803) is the foundational case establishing that the U.S. Supreme Court possesses the power of *judicial review* — the authority to strike down laws and executive actions that violate the Constitution. William Marbury, denied a federal judgeship commission in the transition from John Adams to Thomas Jefferson, sued Secretary of State James Madison. Chief Justice John Marshall's opinion ruled that while Marbury had a right to the commission, the statute granting the Court jurisdiction to issue writs of mandamus was unconstitutional. The ruling's elegance lay in denying Marbury relief while cementing the Court's supremacy as the final arbiter of constitutionality.

## you gotta know

### The Background
- *Judicial vacuum*: President Adams, leaving office, issued last-minute commissions (including Marbury's) that Jefferson's incoming administration refused to deliver.
- *Marbury's frustration*: William Marbury, a D.C. justice of the peace appointee, sued Secretary of State Madison directly in the Supreme Court for a writ of mandamus ordering delivery of his commission.
- *Marshall's dilemma*: Chief Justice John Marshall wanted to rebuke Jefferson's overreach but knew the administration would ignore a direct order to Jefferson.

### The Holding and Reasoning
- Marshall ruled that the Judiciary Act of 1789, which granted the Supreme Court *original jurisdiction* over mandamus cases, exceeded the scope of the Constitution.
- The Constitution lists the Supreme Court's original jurisdiction (cases involving ambassadors, cases where a state is a party); all other cases must arrive as appeals.
- *Marbury's legal right*: Marshall conceded that Marbury had a right to the commission and that Jefferson had violated the law by withholding it—a significant moral victory for Marbury.
- *But no remedy available*: Because the statute was unconstitutional, the Court lacked power to issue the writ, so Marbury lost the case despite being in the right.

### The Doctrine: Judicial Review
- *"It is emphatically the province and duty of the judicial department to say what the law is."* Marshall declared that courts must interpret the Constitution and invalidate any law that conflicts with it.
- Judicial review was not explicitly named in the Constitution but derived from the logic of constitutionalism: a written constitution is supreme law; statutes must conform to it; courts apply law to cases.
- The decision was *strategic*: Marshall got the principle without triggering Jefferson's defiance (no order to disobey), and Jefferson's hand was forced to accept judicial supremacy.

### Legacy and Limits
- Marbury established that the Supreme Court, not Congress or the President, is the final arbiter of constitutionality—a claim without precedent in 1803.
- *Long hiatus*: The Court did not strike down another federal statute on constitutional grounds for 54 years (Dred Scott, 1857).
- Judicial review remains foundational to American law but highly contested—scholars and justices debate whether judges have the right to overrule the elected branches on constitutional meaning.

## context

Marbury v. Madison is the hinge of American constitutional law. Without judicial review, the Constitution would be merely the preamble to whatever Congress and the President choose—a dead letter binding no one. Marshall's opinion grounded the Court's legitimacy not in the Constitution's text (which says almost nothing about this power) but in the *logic* of constitutionalism: if a constitution is supreme and unchangeable by ordinary law, someone must be empowered to enforce it, and that someone must be a neutral arbiter. This reasoning has been accepted for over two centuries, despite ongoing debate about its scope. The case embodies a paradox: the Court's most powerful assertion of authority is disguised as judicial restraint (the Court struck down a statute, but in doing so, denied itself jurisdiction and ruled against a petitioner asking for relief). Every subsequent constitutional check—from striking down laws to overturning executive orders—flows from Marshall's principle.
