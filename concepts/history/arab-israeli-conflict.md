# Arab–Israeli Conflict

## summary

The *Arab–Israeli Conflict* is a prolonged geopolitical and territorial dispute between the state of *Israel* and various Arab nations and Palestinian groups, centered on claims to the land of *Palestine*. Beginning in the late nineteenth century with Jewish immigration into Ottoman-controlled Palestine and intensifying after Israel's establishment in 1948, the conflict encompasses competing national aspirations, refugee crises, and repeated wars. It remains one of the world's most intractable and consequential disputes, involving regional powers, global superpowers, and fundamental questions of national identity, refugee rights, and territorial sovereignty.

## you gotta know

- The *Balfour Declaration* (1917) promised British support for a Jewish homeland in Palestine while protecting the rights of the existing non-Jewish population, setting up future conflict between Jewish and Arab national aspirations.
- *Zionism*, the movement for a Jewish national state in Palestine, accelerated Jewish immigration to Palestine during the 1920s and 1930s, establishing settlements and communities in a land predominantly inhabited by Palestinian Arabs.
- The *United Nations Partition Plan* (1947) proposed dividing Palestine into Jewish and Arab states, which Jewish leaders accepted but Arab leaders rejected, leading to the 1948 Arab–Israeli War.
- *Israel* declared independence on May 14, 1948, prompting immediate invasion by armies from Egypt, Syria, Transjordan, Lebanon, and Iraq; the Israeli victory in this war displaced hundreds of thousands of Palestinian Arabs, creating the *refugee problem* that has persisted for decades.
- The *Suez Crisis* (1956) erupted when Egypt's *Nasser* nationalized the Suez Canal; Israel invaded Egypt in coordination with Britain and France, revealing regional tensions over control of the Middle East and Israel's strategic vulnerability.
- The *Six-Day War* (1967) saw Israel's overwhelming victory over Egypt, Syria, and Jordan, resulting in Israeli occupation of the *West Bank*, *Gaza Strip*, and *Golan Heights*—territories central to subsequent peace negotiations.
- The *Yom Kippur War* (1973) resulted from Egypt and Syria's surprise attack on Israel during the Jewish holiday; though Israel prevailed militarily, the war shifted regional dynamics and led to peace negotiations, culminating in the *Camp David Accords* (1978).
- *Palestinian uprisings*—the *First Intifada* (1987–1993) and *Second Intifada* (2000–2005)—involved Palestinian resistance to Israeli occupation through both armed and nonviolent means, resulting in thousands of casualties on both sides.
- Multiple attempts at peace—the *Oslo Accords* (1993), the *Road Map for Peace* (2003), and numerous bilateral negotiations—have failed to achieve a permanent settlement, leaving the status of Palestinian statehood and Israeli security permanently contested.

## connections

- [[palestine]] — the historical region and the displaced Palestinian people whose national aspirations compete with Israeli claims.
- [[zionism]] — the movement for a Jewish nation-state that motivated Jewish settlement and drove Israeli state-building.
- [[middle-east]] — the broader regional context encompassing Arab states, Iran, and international powers invested in the outcome.
- [[suez-crisis]] — a major regional war arising from the conflict and control of Middle Eastern strategic resources.
- [[six-day-war]] — the swift Israeli victory that reshaped regional geography and deepened Palestinian displacement.
- [[yom-kippur-war]] — the 1973 war that demonstrated Arab military capability and led to eventual peace negotiations.
- [[world-war-ii]] — the Holocaust and Jewish displacement that precipitated the Zionist movement and international support for an Israeli state.
- [[united-nations]] — the body that proposed the partition plan and has remained central to discussions of peace and sovereignty.
