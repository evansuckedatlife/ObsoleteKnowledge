## summary

*Pinckney Benton Stewart Pinchback* (1837–1921) was Louisiana's most prominent Black Reconstruction politician, serving as acting governor in 1872 and as a U.S. senator and congressman. A riverboat captain and political organizer of extraordinary ambition, he navigated the treacherous rapids of Reconstruction politics with agility and resolve.

## you gotta know

- Acting *Governor of Louisiana* (1872–1873), the only Black governor of the Reconstruction era, though his legitimacy was contested and his tenure cut short.
- Elected to the U.S. Senate in 1873, though his seat was challenged and never formally confirmed due to Democratic obstruction and recounts.
- Served in the U.S. House of Representatives (1875–1876), representing a Louisiana district under Republican Reconstruction until Democratic Redemption ended his seat.
- A riverboat captain before the war who accumulated wealth and political networks rapidly during Reconstruction, becoming Louisiana's Republican Party boss.
- Known for pragmatic deal-making and coalition-building across racial lines, though he remained unyielding on Black suffrage and civil rights.
- His tenure in high office was brief but audacious; he remained politically active after Reconstruction and served as U.S. Marshal for the *Eastern District of Louisiana*.

## connections

- [[reconstruction-era]] — the brief window during which Black politicians could rise to the highest executive and legislative offices in the South.
- [[louisiana]] — the state whose politics he dominated during Reconstruction and whose elite circles resisted his power afterward.
- [[governor]] — his acting governorship was the singular achievement of Black executive power in the postwar South, making him historically unique.
- [[robert-smalls]] — fellow Reconstruction congressman and freedman who rose from bondage to office across the same era.
