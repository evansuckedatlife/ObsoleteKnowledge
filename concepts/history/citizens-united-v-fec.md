## summary

*Citizens United v. Federal Election Commission* (2010) held that restrictions on independent campaign spending by corporations and unions violate the First Amendment. The decision struck down key provisions of the Bipartisan Campaign Reform Act and enabled unlimited "dark money" in politics.

## you gotta know

- Citizens United, a nonprofit corporation, sought to air a film criticizing Hillary Clinton during the 2008 primary and was blocked by *McCain-Feingold* campaign finance restrictions.
- The organization challenged the law's ban on corporate and union independent expenditures as a violation of free speech.
- Justice *John Roberts*' majority held that political speech by corporations is protected and cannot be limited based on the speaker's corporate status.
- The decision treated corporate spending as a form of political speech entitled to First Amendment protection, equating money with speech.
- Struck down decades of campaign finance regulation; corporations and unions could now spend unlimited sums independently in elections.
- Overturned portions of *McCain-Feingold* and earlier precedent (*Austin v. Michigan Chamber of Commerce*), unleashing record campaign spending.

## connections

- [[first-amendment]] — free speech protection extended to corporate campaign spending.
- [[campaign-finance]] — fundamentally transformed American election funding and regulation.
- [[corporate-personhood]] — the controversial doctrine that corporations possess constitutional rights.
- [[united-states-constitution]] — the scope of First Amendment protection dramatically reinterpreted.
- [[mccain-feingold]] — the campaign finance law partially invalidated by the decision.
