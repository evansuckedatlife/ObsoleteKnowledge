## summary

*Barbara Jordan* (1936–1996) was the first Black woman elected to Congress from the South, representing Texas from 1973 to 1979. A commanding orator and constitutional scholar, she rose to national prominence during the Watergate hearings with moral gravity and rhetorical precision.

## you gotta know

- First Black woman representative from the South, elected to the House from Houston in 1972; took office in 1973.
- Gained national fame during the *Watergate* impeachment inquiry (1974) when she delivered a principled speech on constitutional duty, declaring *"My faith in the Constitution is whole."*
- Served on the *House Judiciary Committee*, the first Black woman to do so, where she grilled President Nixon's lawyers during the televised hearings.
- A lawyer and former Texas state legislator, she brought judicial precision and moral authority to her legislative voice.
- Advocated for civil rights, voting rights enforcement, and environmental protection; retired from Congress at age 42, citing health reasons (she had multiple sclerosis).
- Became a revered teacher and public intellectual, lecturing at the University of Texas and remaining a symbol of integrity and eloquence in American public life.

## connections
