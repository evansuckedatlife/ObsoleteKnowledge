# Gibbons v. Ogden

## summary

Gibbons v. Ogden (1824) consolidated federal dominion over interstate commerce and struck down a New York state monopoly on steamboat navigation. Thomas Gibbons, operating a licensed federal steamboat service between New York and New Jersey, was sued by Aaron Ogden, who claimed exclusive rights under a New York monopoly granted to steamship pioneer Robert Fulton. Chief Justice John Marshall's opinion for a unanimous Court held that Congress's commerce power extends to *all* commerce that "affects" interstate relations, not merely commerce explicitly crossing state borders, and that the federal license preempted New York's monopoly. The decision obliterated state barriers to interstate trade and established the framework for federal economic regulation that persists today.

## you gotta know

### The Monopoly War
- *Fulton's legacy*: Robert Fulton secured an exclusive monopoly from New York to operate steamboats in state waters; Ogden purchased operating rights from Fulton's heirs.
- *Gibbons's challenge*: Thomas Gibbons obtained a federal coastal license and launched a competing service between New York and New Jersey, undercutting Ogden's prices.
- *The legal clash*: Ogden sued in New York courts; New York's highest court upheld the monopoly, reasoning that states could regulate internal commerce within their borders.

### The Constitutional Question
- Does Congress's power to "regulate commerce ... among the several states" permit federal interference with a state-granted monopoly on routes partly within a state's borders?
- New York argued that *internal* commerce (all steamboat travel within state waters) was the state's to regulate; only *interstate* commerce crossing state boundaries belonged to Congress.
- Gibbons countered that his federal license preempted state law and that congressional commerce power extended to navigation anywhere it touched interstate trade.

### Marshall's Expansive Definition
- Marshall defined *commerce* broadly: not just the exchange of goods, but also *navigation* and all commercial intercourse.
- Interstate commerce includes any commerce that "affects" states other than the origin—a far wider net than purely cross-border trade.
- *"Commerce among the states cannot stop at the external boundary-line of each state, but may be introduced into the interior"* — states cannot bar commerce that passes through them, even if some legs are intrastate.
- Congress's commerce power is sovereign within its sphere and is not limited by the state police power or state-granted monopolies.

### The Preemption Doctrine
- Federal law (Gibbons's coastal license) supersedes conflicting state law (Ogden's monopoly).
- A state cannot use its police power (regulating internal affairs) to obstruct interstate commerce or interfere with federal regulatory choices.
- This establishes the *conflict preemption* doctrine: when state and federal law clash, federal law wins.

### Immediate and Long-Term Impact
- New York's steamboat monopoly evaporated; competition flourished; prices fell; steamship transport boomed.
- The decision opened the door for federal regulation of railroads, telegraphs, and eventually the internet—anything touching interstate commerce.
- Over the 20th century, Congress used the commerce power to regulate labor (Fair Labor Standards Act), civil rights (Civil Rights Act of 1964), and the environment.

## context

Gibbons v. Ogden is the Constitutional greenlight for the federal economic state. Marshall's broad reading of "affects interstate commerce" has proven to be almost unlimited in scope—by the mid-20th century, courts upheld federal regulation of local manufacturing, agriculture, and even non-commercial activity (such as a private restaurant's refusal to serve Black patrons) on the grounds that it "substantially affects" interstate commerce. The case's genius is that it appears to constrain federal power (Congress regulates "commerce among the states," a categorical limit) while actually unleashing it (because almost all economic activity can be said to "affect" interstate relations). Without Gibbons, states could have erected tariff barriers and monopolies to protect local industries, balkanizing the U.S. economy. The case made the U.S. a genuine common market and enabled the regulatory apparatus that followed the New Deal and Civil Rights Act. Conversely, modern critics argue that the breadth of Gibbons has erased meaningful limits on federal power.
