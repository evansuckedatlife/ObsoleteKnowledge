## summary

*Plessy v. Ferguson* (1896) upheld the constitutionality of racial segregation under the "separate but equal" doctrine, allowing states to mandate segregated facilities for Black and white citizens. Homer Plessy, who was one-eighth Black, challenged a Louisiana law requiring segregated railroad cars; the Court's 7–1 decision sanctified Jim Crow legislation for nearly 60 years, becoming the legal foundation for systematic racial apartheid in the South.

## you gotta know

- *Homer Plessy* boarded a whites-only railroad car in New Orleans to challenge the constitutionality of Louisiana's segregation law.
- Justice *Henry Brown* wrote that segregation was constitutional if facilities were "separate but equal"—a standard almost never actually met in practice.
- The Court reasoned that the *14th Amendment* guaranteed political equality but not social equality, allowing states wide latitude to classify citizens by race.
- Justice *John Harlan* dissented memorably, arguing the Constitution is "color-blind" and that the ruling would embolden discrimination.
- Provided legal cover for widespread segregation across schools, transit, public accommodations, housing, and voting—the foundation of Jim Crow.
- Reigned until overturned by *Brown v. Board of Education* (1954), which declared "separate educational facilities are inherently unequal."
