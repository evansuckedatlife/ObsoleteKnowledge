## summary

*Baker v. Carr* (1962) held that federal courts could review legislative apportionment and reapportionment cases, overturning the "political question doctrine" that had barred judicial intervention. The decision enabled challenges to malapportionment and set the stage for the "one person, one vote" principle.

## you gotta know

- Tennessee had not reapportioned state legislative districts since 1901, despite massive population shifts; urban voters were vastly underrepresented.
- Charles Baker challenged the apportionment as unconstitutional, but courts initially dismissed the case as a nonjusticiable political question.
- Justice *William Brennan* wrote that apportionment disputes are justiciable under the *Equal Protection Clause*, not barred as political questions.
- The decision removed the doctrine that courts cannot intervene in disputes over how legislatures are structured—a revolutionary shift.
- Opened the floodgates to reapportionment litigation; within a year, federal courts were ordering redistricting in multiple states.
- Led directly to *Reynolds v. Sims* (1964), which established the "one person, one vote" standard for state legislative apportionment.
