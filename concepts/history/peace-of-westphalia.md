# Peace of Westphalia

## summary

The *Peace of Westphalia* (1648) consisted of two treaties that ended the devastating *Thirty Years' War* in Europe, signed in the German cities of Münster and Osnabrück. The treaties fundamentally reshaped European politics by establishing the principle of *national sovereignty* and religious tolerance—each ruler could choose the religion of their state without external interference. Westphalia is credited with inaugurating the modern state system and ending centuries of overlapping feudal claims and religious warfare.

## you gotta know

### War & Context
- The *Thirty Years' War* (1618–1648) was fought primarily on German soil, devastating the region and claiming roughly 1/3 of the population.
- Conflict blended religious (Protestant vs. Catholic) and political (empire vs. princes) dimensions; by the end, secular power politics dominated.
- *Sweden* emerged as a major power; *France* gained territory and prestige.

### Sovereignty & State System
- Introduced the principle of *cuius regio, eius religio* (whose realm, his religion): rulers determined their state's faith; subjects could emigrate if unwilling.
- Formally recognised the *Holy Roman Empire* as a loose confederation, not a centralised state, breaking imperial dominance.
- Established the notion of *sovereign equality*—states recognised each other as juridically equal, not hierarchically ordered under pope or emperor.

### Territorial Realignment
- *France* gained *Alsace* and territory in the Pyrenees; *Sweden* acquired *Pomerania* and Baltic lands, becoming a major Baltic power.
- The *Dutch Republic* was formally recognised as independent, ending Spanish overlordship.
- German princes gained greater autonomy within the Empire, fragmenting German political power for two centuries.

### Religious & Diplomatic Precedent
- Replaced religious uniformity with *tolerance and coexistence*—a revolutionary concept for 1648.
- Established international diplomacy and treaty law as the mechanism for settling disputes, not just conquest.
- Created the concept of the *balance of power*: multi-state diplomacy to prevent any single power from dominating.

## context

The Peace of Westphalia marks the death of medieval Christendom and the birth of the modern interstate system. Before Westphalia, legitimacy flowed from universal claims—the Holy Roman Emperor, the Catholic Church—and religious uniformity was an enforced ideal. After it, legitimacy rested on secular territorial control and mutual recognition; religious diversity was tolerated in the name of stability. This shift was radical: it desacralised sovereignty and replaced religious war with diplomatic negotiation. The treaties are cited as the foundation of modern international law, and the principle of sovereign equality (codified centuries later in the UN Charter) traces directly to Westphalia. The concept took centuries to stabilise—religious violence persisted—but the Westphalian framework provided the intellectual architecture for peaceful coexistence of rival faiths and powers.
