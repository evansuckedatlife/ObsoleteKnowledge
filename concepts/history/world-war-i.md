# World War I

## summary

World War I was a global conflict fought from 1914 to 1918, triggered by the assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand and drawing in the major European powers in a brutal stalemate of industrial warfare. The war reshaped the map of Europe, ended four empires (German, Austro-Hungarian, Ottoman, Russian), and killed an estimated 20 million people. It fundamentally transformed warfare, culture, and politics, ushering in the modern age through mechanized slaughter and ideological upheaval.

## you gotta know

### spark and alliances

- The war began on 28 June 1914 when *Gavrilo Princip*, a Bosnian Serb nationalist, assassinated *Archduke Franz Ferdinand* of Austria-Hungary in Sarajevo, triggering a cascade of alliance obligations that dragged most of Europe into conflict within weeks.
- The conflict pitted the *Central Powers* (*Germany*, *Austria-Hungary*, *Ottoman Empire*) against the *Allied Powers* (*France*, *Russia*, *Britain*, and later the *United States*), with dozens of smaller nations choosing sides based on imperial interests and security treaties.
- *Kaiser Wilhelm II* led Germany with aggressive expansionism and a strategy of *Schlieffen Plan*—a preemptive strike through Belgium to knock France out quickly—but the plan's failure led to years of stalemate.

### the war itself

- The Western Front became synonymous with static, grinding trench warfare: soldiers huddled in muddy, rat-infested trenches separated by a deadly no-man's-land, with battles like the *Somme* (1916) killing hundreds of thousands for minimal territorial gain.
- The war pioneered or perfected *tanks*, *poison gas*, *poison gas masks*, *poison gas attacks* (especially *chlorine* and *mustard gas*), *machine guns*, and *artillery bombardment*, making it the first truly industrial and mechanized war.
- *Russia's withdrawal* in 1917 following the *Bolshevik Revolution* left Germany briefly able to focus on the West, but *American entry* in April 1917 swung the numerical advantage decisively to the Allies.
- The *German Spring Offensive* of 1918 was the last major Central Powers push; its failure broke German morale, and by November 1918, an armistice ended the fighting.

### aftermath and legacy

- The *Treaty of Versailles* (1919) imposed harsh reparations and territorial losses on Germany, creating economic hardship and nationalist humiliation that would fuel the rise of fascism in the 1930s.
- The war killed approximately *20 million people* (military and civilian combined) and left an entire generation traumatized, giving rise to the *Lost Generation* of writers and artists who questioned meaning, progress, and authority.
- The conflict reshaped the artistic and intellectual landscape: the irrationality and horror of the war discredited 19th-century optimism, spawning *modernism*, *Dada*, *surrealism*, and existentialist philosophy as responses to the void.
- New nations emerged from the wreckage: Poland, Czechoslovakia, Yugoslavia, and the Irish Free State; the Middle East was carved into *British* and *French* mandates, sowing seeds for future conflict.

## context

World War I stands as the fulcrum of modern history. The war industrialized mass death, shattered faith in progress, and created the political and economic conditions from which World War II and the Cold War would rise. Its poetry—from *Wilfred Owen* to *Siegfried Sassoon*—made the sacrifice viscerally real; its painters (Otto Dix, George Grosz) documented its dehumanizing machinery; its composers (Schoenberg's atonal break) reflected its discord. The treaty's vindictiveness, the economic devastation, and the territorial redrawing planted the seeds for fascism and another, even deadlier, global conflict just two decades later.
