# Youngstown Sheet & Tube Co. v. Sawyer

## summary

*Youngstown Sheet & Tube Co. v. Sawyer* (1952), the "Steel Seizure Case," held that President Truman lacked constitutional authority to seize steel mills threatened by a labor strike during the Korean War without explicit Congressional authorization. The decision reaffirmed that the presidency is not an unlimited executive office.

## you gotta know

- During the Korean War, steelworkers threatened to strike; Truman ordered Secretary of Commerce Sawyer to seize and operate the mills to maintain war production.
- The steel companies sued, arguing the President had no constitutional power to seize private property without Congressional action.
- Justice *Robert Jackson*'s concurrence articulated a three-tier framework for presidential power: greatest when acting with Congress, uncertain in a gray zone, and at its nadir when acting against Congress.
- The majority held that only Congress has the power of the purse and can authorize extraordinary economic measures; the President cannot unilaterally seize property.
- Established that inherent executive power does not justify overriding property rights or substituting for Congressional authority.
- Became the leading case defining the limits of presidential power during emergencies and crises.
