# Mirabeau

## summary

*Honoré-Gabriel Riqueti, Comte de Mirabeau* (1749–1791) was the French Revolution's most brilliant and charismatic orator in its early phase. Despite his noble origins, he championed popular sovereignty and constitutional limits on royal power, becoming a bridge figure between the old order and revolutionary reform—though his hidden royalist sympathies and sudden death left questions about where his true allegiances lay.

## you gotta know

- Elected as a deputy to the Estates-General in 1789 despite being a noble, and emerged as the Third Estate's most persuasive voice for reform and popular rights.
- Delivered the famous riposte to the Baron de Breteuil's ultimatum: "Go tell those who sent you that we are here by the will of the people and shall not leave except by the force of bayonets."
- Played a central role in the Tennis Court Oath (June 20, 1789), the National Assembly's defiant vow to write a constitution.
- Negotiated secretly with the royal court, hoping to preserve the monarchy through a constitutional settlement that would satisfy both reform-minded nobles and popular demands.
- Chaired the National Assembly and used his oratory to guide early revolutionary legislation, maintaining a vision of moderate, law-based change.
- Died under mysterious circumstances on April 2, 1791, possibly from a chronic illness exacerbated by debauchery; his private papers later revealed his royalist dealings.
- His death removed a potential moderate voice from the assembly just as the revolution was shifting toward radicalism.
