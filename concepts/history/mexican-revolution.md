# Mexican Revolution

## summary

The Mexican Revolution (1910–1920) was a prolonged civil conflict and social upheaval that transformed Mexico's political and economic structure. Sparked by resentment of *Porfirio Díaz*'s authoritarian 30-year rule and the concentration of land in the hands of elites and foreign corporations, the Revolution began as a movement for political reform and evolved into a struggle for agrarian justice, indigenous rights, and national sovereignty. Though the Revolution itself ended around 1920, its institutional aftermath—a one-party state, land redistribution, and anti-clerical nationalism—defined Mexico for the rest of the 20th century and made it Latin America's first major social revolution.

## you gotta know

### origins and initial phase

- Decades of rule by *Porfirio Díaz* (1876–1911) brought economic modernization but massive inequality: peasants were landless, foreign corporations controlled mines and oil, and political opposition was suppressed.
- *Francisco Madero*, a reformist who called for *Tierra y Libertad* (Land and Liberty), ran against Díaz in 1910; when Díaz fraudulently reelected himself, Madero's call to arms sparked the Revolution.
- Early phases saw *Emilio Zapata* lead southern peasants demanding land reform, and *Pancho Villa* command northern armies; Díaz was forced to resign and Madero became president (1911–1913).

### radicalization and civil war

- *Victoriano Huerta*, a conservative general, overthrew Madero in 1913 and reinstated authoritarian rule, triggering a renewed uprising by Zapata, Villa, and other factions.
- The Revolution fragmented into multiple armies pursuing different goals: agrarian reform, political democracy, indigenous autonomy, and national sovereignty from foreign (especially U.S.) intervention.
- Battles devastated the countryside; armies conscripted peasants; cities changed hands repeatedly; an estimated one million people died in violence, disease, and famine.

### constitution and reform

- The *Constitution of 1917*, drafted by revolutionary delegates, incorporated radical provisions: ejido land collective ownership, limits on foreign landownership, restrictions on the Catholic Church, and strong executive power.
- *Emilio Zapata* continued guerrilla warfare in southern Mexico until his assassination in 1919, fighting for agrarian reform to be implemented; *Pancho Villa* surrendered in 1920.
- The Revolution's military phase ended by 1920, though banditry and sporadic uprisings continued; the victors consolidated power into the *Institutional Revolutionary Party* (PRI), which would dominate Mexico for 71 years.

### legacy and international significance

- Land was redistributed to peasant communities (*ejidos*); the Church's political power was curtailed; indigenous communities gained nominal recognition (though often exploited in practice).
- The Revolution made Mexico the first Latin American nation to implement large-scale land reform and assert sovereignty over natural resources; it inspired later revolutions, notably in Cuba.
- The Revolution's artistic legacy—muralism by *Diego Rivera*, *David Alfaro Siqueiros*, and others—became the 20th century's most recognizable artistic form celebrating indigenous culture and revolutionary ideals.

## context

The Mexican Revolution was Latin America's first major social upheaval, preceding Cuba's by over 40 years. Unlike the French or Russian revolutions, it was less ideologically coherent: it blended agrarian reform, indigenous nationalism, anti-clericalism, and anti-imperialism into a distinctly Mexican program. The Revolution failed to achieve most of Zapata's vision—land reform remained incomplete, indigenous populations remained marginalized—yet it permanently altered the nation's trajectory. The Revolution's suppression of the Church, its emphasis on indigenous culture and mestizaje (racial mixture), and its assertion of economic sovereignty became permanent features of Mexican nationalism. For quiz bowl, it is the template for 20th-century Latin American revolution: indigenous and peasant in character, nationalist and anti-imperialist in ideology, and producing a one-party state claiming revolutionary legitimacy long after revolutionary fervor faded.
