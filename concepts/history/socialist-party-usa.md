# Socialist Party USA

## summary

The Socialist Party of America, founded in 1901 and formally established as the Socialist Party USA in 1973 (successor to the earlier Socialist Workers Party tradition), advocated for democratic socialism, workers' rights, and labor self-management. Through Eugene V. Debs and later candidates, the party attracted workers, intellectuals, and reformers, reaching peak influence in the 1910s–1920s before declining through internal schisms, McCarthyism, and the absorption of much of its agenda into Democratic New Deal policies.

## you gotta know

- The party emerged from merger of the Socialist Labor Party and the Social Democratic Party, consolidating radical labor activists, Marxist intellectuals, and immigrant workers—particularly German and Eastern European socialists.
- Eugene Debs, a railroad labor organizer and five-time presidential candidate (1900–1920), became the party's most prominent figure; his 1912 campaign netted 6% of the popular vote, the highest socialist showing in U.S. history.
- The party advocated for workers' ownership of the means of production, abolition of the wage system, massive redistribution of wealth, and workers' democratic control of industry.
- Socialists elected several mayors (Milwaukee's Emil Seidel, 1910) and state legislators in the early 20th century, establishing footholds of institutional power before declining.
- Internal division between democratic socialists and communists (especially after the 1917 Russian Revolution) weakened the party; many revolutionary members departed for the Communist Party by the 1920s.
- The party's decline accelerated after World War II, as Cold War anticommunism suppressed radical politics and the New Deal incorporated labor protections, unemployment insurance, and welfare measures the Socialists had championed.
