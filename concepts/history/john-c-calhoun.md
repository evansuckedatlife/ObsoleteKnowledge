# John C. Calhoun

## summary

**John C. Calhoun** was the seventh Vice President of the United States, serving under John Quincy Adams (1825–1829) and Andrew Jackson (1829–1832), before becoming one of the leading political and intellectual voices of the antebellum South. A prominent nationalist turned fierce sectionalist, he shaped the debate over slavery and states' rights that led toward the Civil War.

## you gotta know

- Served as Vice President to two presidents but left office to pursue a Senate career where he wielded greater influence.
- Authored the *South Carolina Exposition and Protest* (1828), the theoretical foundation for the doctrine of nullification—the idea that states could reject federal laws they deemed unconstitutional.
- Led the Nullification Crisis (1832–1833) when South Carolina threatened to secede over the Tariff of Abominations, nearly precipitating civil war a generation before Fort Sumter.
- Pivoted from an early nationalist stance (supporting internal improvements and a national bank) to become the South's most eloquent defender of slavery and states' rights.
- Served as Secretary of War under President James Monroe, overseeing military expansion and the acquisition of Spanish Florida.
- Known for his piercing intellect and oratorical power; his speeches on slavery and constitutional theory profoundly influenced Southern political thought.
- Served in the Senate for 17 years, making him one of the most powerful congressional figures of his era.
