# Luddites

## summary

The **Luddites** were textile workers in the Midlands and North of England who, from 1811 to 1816, engaged in organized machine-breaking as a tactic against factory owners introducing new textile machinery that displaced skilled workers and depressed wages. Guided by a mythical leader called "General Ludd," they attacked factories and mills at night, smashing power looms and frames to preserve their livelihoods and craft integrity. Though widely portrayed as mindless vandals, Luddites were in fact sophisticated workers defending their interests against technological displacement and social degradation; their actions represented an early form of industrial resistance that influenced later labor movements.

## you gotta know

- Luddism emerged during the Napoleonic Wars (1811–16) when factory owners faced labor shortages but chose to use new machinery to reduce wages rather than employ skilled workers at higher rates.
- The movement was strongest among framework knitters in Nottinghamshire and hand-loom weavers in Lancashire and Yorkshire—trades with established guild traditions and strong apprenticeship systems.
- "General Ludd" was a symbolic figurehead; the actual organization was decentralized, coordinated through local meetings and pledges; members wore disguises and operated at night to avoid identification.
- Luddites used violence strategically and selectively: they attacked specific factories and machines, not indiscriminately; they issued manifestos explaining their grievances and demands for wage restoration.
- The government deployed the military against Luddites, treating machine-breaking as sedition; trials resulted in executions, transportation, and imprisonment; in 1813, 17 Luddites were executed in York.
- The movement declined after 1816 as the economy recovered and government repression intensified; skilled workers turned to trade unions and friendly societies as alternative forms of organization.

## connections

- [[industrial-revolution]] — Luddism was a reaction against the displacement and deskilling that mechanization caused.
- [[chartism]] — both movements mobilized working-class discontent; some Chartists remembered Luddism as an earlier form of class resistance.
- [[factory-acts-movement]] — parallel effort to regulate industrial working conditions and protect workers from technological and wage displacement.
- [[peterloo]] — the 1819 massacre occurred in a region with residual Luddite sentiment and anti-factory-owner grievances.
- [[british-abolitionism]] — both movements invoked moral language about workers' rights and human dignity in response to dehumanizing economic systems.
