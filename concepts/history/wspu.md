# Women's Social and Political Union (Suffragettes)

## summary

The **Women's Social and Political Union** (WSPU), founded by *Emmeline Pankhurst* in Manchester in 1903, became the most militant and visible suffrage organization in Britain. Its members, known as **Suffragettes**, adopted confrontational tactics—arson, window-smashing, and public disruption—to demand women's voting rights, arguing that polite petitioning had failed and that dramatic action was necessary to force the government's attention. Though they remained a minority among women's suffrage advocates, the Suffragettes captured public imagination and transformed the debate over women's political citizenship; their efforts contributed to women's enfranchisement (partially in 1918, fully in 1928).

## you gotta know

- *Emmeline Pankhurst* founded the WSPU in 1903 with her daughter *Christabel*; the motto "Deeds Not Words" captured their embrace of militant tactics over peaceful petition.
- From 1905 onward, Suffragettes disrupted political meetings, chained themselves to railings, set fires to empty buildings and property, and slashed paintings in museums to draw press attention.
- *Emily Davison*, a Suffragette martyr, threw herself beneath the King's horse at the 1913 Epsom Derby; her death from injuries became a symbol of women's sacrifice for the cause.
- The government imprisoned hundreds of Suffragettes; many underwent force-feeding when they went on hunger strike; the resulting brutality generated sympathy for their cause.
- The movement spanned class lines, attracting middle-class women, actresses, and teachers, though working-class women had less access to the organization's resources and media platform.
- World War I suspended militant action; Pankhurst redirected energies to wartime, arguing that women's war work proved their fitness for citizenship. Women (aged 30+ with property) gained the vote in 1918; full equality came in 1928.

## connections

- [[emmeline-pankhurst]] — the founder and leader of the WSPU; her tactical militancy defined the suffragettes.
- [[elizabeth-cady-stanton]] — American suffragist whose strategies and writings influenced British women's rights advocates.
- [[susan-b-anthony]] — American suffragist whose life and struggle paralleled the Suffragettes' battle for voting rights.
- [[chartism]] — the nineteenth-century working-class movement that, despite its radicalism, excluded women from formal participation.
- [[british-abolitionism]] — earlier reform movement in which women played crucial roles but were denied leadership and formal membership.
