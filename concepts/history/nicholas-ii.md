## summary

*Nicholas II* (1868–1918) was the last tsar of Russia, presiding over a doomed empire during the final age of autocracy. Weak-willed, indecisive, and devoted to the principle of absolute power, he refused meaningful reform despite military defeat, economic crisis, and revolutionary ferment. His incompetence during the Russo-Japanese War triggered the 1905 revolution; his failure to deliver on promised reforms radicalized the population. In 1917, the Russian Revolution overthrew him; he and his entire family were executed by the Bolsheviks in 1918, ending three centuries of Romanov rule.

## you gotta know

### Early Reign & Conservative Inheritance
- Ascended in 1894 after his father *Alexander III's* death; initially well-intentioned but fatally indecisive.
- Inherited his father's reactionary policies and commitment to absolute autocracy, despite the empire's need for reform.
- Married *Alexandra of Hesse-Darmstadt*, a German princess devoted to Orthodoxy and autocracy; she reinforced his conservative instincts.
- Appointed ineffectual and reactionary advisors, blocking progressive voices in the government.

### The Russo-Japanese War & 1905 Revolution
- Waged the *Russo-Japanese War* (1904–1905), a military disaster; Russia was defeated by a non-European power for the first time, exposing imperial weakness.
- Military defeat triggered the *Revolution of 1905*: workers' strikes, peasant unrest, and military mutiny shook the empire.
- Faced the *October Manifesto* crisis (1905): he reluctantly issued a manifesto promising civil liberties and a representative assembly (the Duma), then slowly undermined it.
- Allowed Prime Minister *Pyotr Stolypin* to implement controversial reforms (1906–1911), but resisted constitutional limitations on his power.

### The Duma Years & Growing Instability
- The *Duma* (parliament) functioned as a powerless advisory body; Nicholas repeatedly dissolved it when it opposed his will.
- Political factions in the Duma—liberals, socialists, conservatives—battled while the executive remained immobile and autocratic.
- Stolypin's assassination (1911) removed the one capable reformer; Nicholas sank into passivity and religious mysticism.
- Court intrigues intensified around *Grigory Rasputin*, a self-proclaimed holy man who gained influence over the empress and the sickly heir.

### World War I & Final Collapse
- Entered *World War I* in 1914, confident in Russian strength; the war exposed the empire's military, economic, and logistical weakness.
- Took personal command of the army (1915), leaving government to the empress and Rasputin; administrative collapse followed.
- Military losses mounted: by 1916–1917, millions of soldiers were dead or wounded; food shortages and inflation devastated the home front.
- Resigned under pressure in March 1917; the *Bolsheviks* seized power in October.
- Executed along with his entire family (wife, five children) by the Bolsheviks on July 17, 1918, in Yekaterinburg, ending the Romanov dynasty.

## context

Nicholas II is a tragic figure—not evil, but fatally unsuited to power during a time of revolutionary change. He inherited a system of absolute autocracy when the world was moving toward democracy and his own empire was riven by social conflict. His refusal to share power, his indecision, his reliance on reactionary advisors and mystical cures (Rasputin) made him the last tsar—not because he was uniquely terrible, but because he represented a system that could no longer sustain itself. The 1905 revolution offered a chance for constitutional reform; Nicholas's half-measures and backtracking radicalized the opposition and made revolution inevitable. World War I accelerated the empire's collapse. He presided over the deaths of millions and the end of the Romanov dynasty, a loss the Russian exiles would lament throughout the 20th century. He embodies the failure of autocracy in the modern age.
