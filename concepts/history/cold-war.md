# Cold War

## summary

The Cold War was a geopolitical and ideological struggle between the *Soviet Union* and the *United States* (and their respective allies) lasting roughly from 1947 to 1991, characterized by nuclear-armed tension, proxy wars, espionage, and competition for global influence without direct military confrontation between the superpowers themselves. It shaped international relations, divided the world into blocs, spawned the nuclear arms race, and ended only with the Soviet Union's collapse.

## you gotta know

### origins and the Iron Curtain

- The Cold War emerged from the ashes of World War II as the U.S. and Soviet Union, having defeated Nazi Germany together, revealed incompatible visions for the postwar order: American capitalism and democracy versus Soviet communism and authoritarian control.
- *Winston Churchill*'s 1946 "Iron Curtain" speech in Fulton, Missouri crystallized the ideological divide; the Soviets consolidated control over Eastern Europe, imposing communist puppet regimes in Poland, Czechoslovakia, Hungary, Romania, and East Germany.
- The *Truman Doctrine* (1947) committed the U.S. to "containment"—the strategy of checking Soviet expansion wherever it threatened; the *Marshall Plan* (1948–52) rebuilt Western Europe economically to prevent communist takeover.

### flashpoints and crises

- The *Berlin Blockade* (1948–49) saw Stalin attempt to starve West Berlin into submission; the U.S. responded with the *Berlin Airlift*, a defiant supply operation that humiliated the Soviets and established American resolve.
- The *Korean War* (1950–53) was the Cold War's first hot proxy war, with the U.S. and UN-backed South Korea fighting Soviet/Chinese-backed North Korea, ending in armistice and continued division.
- The *Cuban Missile Crisis* (October 1962) brought the world closest to nuclear war: Soviet missiles in Cuba prompted U.S. blockade, high-stakes Kennedy-Khrushchev negotiations, and a standoff resolved by Soviet withdrawal and secret U.S. concessions.
- The *Vietnam War* (1955–75) became the Cold War's bloodiest proxy conflict, with the U.S. fighting North Vietnam (backed by the Soviet Union and China) in a futile effort to prevent communist unification that killed millions and shattered American consensus.

### ideology, culture, and the arms race

- The two blocs developed competing ideologies, economies, and cultural productions: *existentialism* and *beat literature* in the West; *socialist realism* in the Soviet sphere; Hollywood and American jazz versus Soviet ballet and propaganda art.
- The *nuclear arms race* escalated exponentially: the Soviets detonated their first atomic bomb in 1949 (shocking Americans); both sides developed hydrogen bombs, ICBMs, and submarine-based deterrence, achieving *mutually assured destruction (MAD)*.
- *McCarthyism* in America (early 1950s) reflected Cold War paranoia, targeting suspected communists in government, entertainment, and academia; Soviet *purges* and *show trials* mirrored totalitarian paranoia on the other side.

### space race and détente

- The *Space Race* became the defining peacetime competition: the Soviets launched *Sputnik* (1957) and *Yuri Gagarin* (1961); the U.S. responded with the *Apollo program*, culminating in *Neil Armstrong*'s moon landing (1969) and a symbolic American victory.
- The 1960s and 1970s saw periods of *détente*: the *Nuclear Test Ban Treaty* (1963), *SALT I talks* (1972), and the *Helsinki Accords* (1975) eased tensions and acknowledged mutual vulnerability.
- The Soviet *invasion of Afghanistan* (1979–89) reignited tensions, provoking U.S. support for *Mujahideen* rebels; the U.S. boycotted the 1980 *Moscow Olympics* and increased military spending under *Ronald Reagan*.

### collapse and legacy

- The Soviet Union, economically exhausted by military competition and bogged down in Afghanistan, collapsed in 1991 under *Mikhail Gorbachev*'s reforms (*glasnost* and *perestroika*), ending the Cold War abruptly.
- The Cold War left a legacy of nuclear weapons, ideological scars, divided nations (Korea, Vietnam, Germany—though Germany reunified in 1990), proxy conflicts whose consequences persisted, and a U.S. positioned as the sole superpower.

## context

The Cold War defined the second half of the 20th century: it shaped politics, art, literature, science, and philosophy. The existentialist movement grappled with atomic anxiety and ideological absurdity; the *beat generation* rebelled against conformity and Cold War orthodoxy; American film noir reflected nuclear dread and paranoia. The Space Race inspired science fiction and symbolized technological prowess; the arms race drove nuclear physics and weapons engineering into dominant academic and industrial roles. The war's proxy conflicts—Korea, Vietnam, countless CIA-backed coups—killed millions in the Global South and structured post-colonial politics. The Cold War's end was surprising—most analysts expected it to persist—and its abruptness left geopolitical voids that persist today.
