## summary

The Battle of Stalingrad (August 1942 – February 1943) was a sprawling urban combat between Nazi Germany and the Soviet Union for control of a major city on the Volga River. It became the bloodiest battle in history, with combined casualties exceeding 2 million. The German Sixth Army's failure and surrender marked a psychological and military turning point: the first major Axis defeat and the beginning of Soviet momentum that would push Germany back toward Berlin. Stalingrad shattered the myth of German invincibility and galvanized Soviet resolve for the long war ahead.

## you gotta know

### Background
- Fought in and around the industrial city of *Stalingrad* (modern Volgograd) on the Volga River in southern Russia, a strategic hub for Soviet oil supplies.
- German Army Group B under General *Friedrich Paulus* attacked the city as part of *Operation Barbarossa*'s drive toward Soviet oil fields in the Caucasus.
- Soviet forces, under General *Aleksandr Rodimtsev* and *Georgy Zhukov*, were ordered by Stalin to defend the city at any cost ("Not one step back").

### The Battle
- Fighting began in August 1942 with intense house-to-house urban combat; the city was largely reduced to rubble.
- German forces encircled the city by November 1942, but Soviet counteroffensive *Operation Uranus* (November–December) encircled the German Sixth Army instead.
- Over 330,000 Axis troops (mostly German) were trapped in a shrinking pocket as winter set in; supply lines were cut and starvation set in.

### Aftermath
- Paulus and the remnants of the Sixth Army surrendered on February 2, 1943; fewer than 6,000 German prisoners survived Soviet captivity.
- German morale collapsed and Soviet morale soared; the Axis lost the initiative on the Eastern Front permanently.
- Stalingrad became a symbol of Soviet sacrifice and resilience; its defense inspired propaganda and historical memory on both sides.

## context

Stalingrad represents the moment the Second World War's trajectory fundamentally shifted. Before Stalingrad, the German Wehrmacht seemed unstoppable; after, the Soviet Union was clearly ascendant. The battle's ferocity—block-by-block devastation, starving soldiers, sub-zero conditions—embodied the total war the Eastern Front had become. In quiz bowl, Stalingrad is shorthand for the breaking point of the Blitzkrieg myth, the scale of Eastern Front suffering, and Soviet determination. It looms in discussions of World War II turning points, WWII casualties, and the Holocaust's context (Stalingrad was also a region of mass murder).
