# Lizzie Borden

## summary

In August 1892, two people were killed with an axe in their Fall River, Massachusetts home: Andrew Borden and his wife Abby. Their daughter *Lizzie Borden* was arrested and tried for the murders, captivating the nation with a high-profile defense that hinged on social propriety and circumstantial evidence. Though acquitted, the case remains unsolved and has become an American cultural touchstone.

## you gotta know

- The victims were *Andrew Borden*, a wealthy merchant, and his wife *Abby Gray Borden*, discovered dead in the Borden family home on August 4, 1892.
- *Lizzie Borden*, Andrew's 32-year-old unmarried daughter, was the primary suspect; she gave contradictory accounts of her whereabouts during the murders.
- The murders were committed with a hatchet or axe; *Abby* was struck repeatedly while resting; *Andrew* was killed later in the morning as he slept.
- The trial (June 1893) hinged on propriety: the defense argued a respectable woman of her social standing would not commit such violence; contemporaneous forensic evidence was limited.
- Lizzie was acquitted on June 20, 1893, despite circumstantial evidence and her evasive testimony—a verdict reflecting jury skepticism rather than forensic proof.
- The case galvanized public interest in forensic investigation and criminal procedure; the phrase "Lizzie Borden took an axe" entered American folklore.
- No one was ever convicted; Andrew's insurance and will became motives scrutinized by later historians and crime writers.
