# Election of 1824

## summary

The 1824 election was thrown into the House of Representatives when no candidate won an Electoral College majority, resulting in the controversial selection of John Quincy Adams over the popular-vote leader Andrew Jackson. The so-called "Corrupt Bargain"—alleged collusion between Adams and Henry Clay to hand the presidency to Adams—sparked outrage and fractured the Democratic-Republican Party. The election exposed the inadequacy of the electoral system and became the rallying cry for Jacksonian democracy.

## you gotta know

### The Four-Way Contest
- *Andrew Jackson* led with 99 electoral votes and 42 percent of the popular vote, but fell short of a majority.
- *John Quincy Adams*, *William Crawford*, and *Henry Clay* followed; only the top three could advance to the House vote under the 12th Amendment.
- Clay's elimination—despite his formidable stature—left him free to influence the House, a position he capitalized on brilliantly.

### The House Decision and the "Corrupt Bargain"
- The House chose *Adams* over Jackson in one ballot, with Clay's crucial support from the Kentucky delegation.
- Jackson supporters claimed a backroom deal: Adams would win the presidency; Clay would become Secretary of State—and indeed Clay accepted that post.
- The "Bargain" became the defining scandal of Adams's term, poisoning his presidency before it began.

### Aftermath and Realignment
- Jackson's partisans mobilized for 1828, vowing to overturn the "stolen" election and launching the Democratic Party as a distinct faction.
- Adams's one term was marked by legislative paralysis; he could accomplish little against a hostile, Jackson-allied Congress.

## context

The 1824 election crystallized the democratic demand for direct popular will—Jackson won the most votes and electoral strength, yet lost in the House due to procedural rules and backroom dealing. This violation of democratic expectation was explosive: it shattered the broad Democratic-Republican consensus and split the party into Jacksonian Democrats (populist, nationalist) and National Republicans (later Whigs). The election also demonstrated the Electoral College's fundamental problem: when no candidate achieves a majority, the system defaults to institutional players—Clay in 1824, the House in general—rather than the demos. The "Corrupt Bargain" became shorthand for elite manipulation and galvanized a new democratic movement.
