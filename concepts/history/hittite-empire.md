# Hittite Empire

## summary

The Hittite Empire (1650–1180 BCE) was the ancient Near East's dominant power during the *Bronze Age*, centered on *Anatolia* (modern Turkey) and extending into *Syria* and *Mesopotamia* at its height. The Hittites pioneered *iron-working* technology (which gave them military advantage over bronze-using rivals), developed a sophisticated legal code, and engaged in formal diplomatic treaties—the *Treaty of Kadesh* (1259 BCE) between Hittite king *Mursili II* and Egyptian pharaoh *Ramesses II* is the world's oldest recorded peace agreement. The empire was organized as a feudal system where the great king held absolute authority, supported by vassal rulers of conquered territories, and maintained power through military campaigns, religious authority, and administrative control. The empire collapsed suddenly around 1180 BCE—the cause remains debated (invasion, climate change, internal conflict)—but its influence persisted in later Levantine cultures and its diplomatic and legal innovations shaped Near Eastern statecraft.

## you gotta know

### Foundations and Early Expansion
- The Hittites, an Indo-European people, migrated to Anatolia around 1700 BCE and gradually consolidated power, establishing their capital at *Hattusa* (modern Boğazköy, Turkey).
- The *Old Hittite Kingdom* (1650–1450 BCE) saw repeated expansion into Syria and Mesopotamia, with kings like *Mursili I* (r. 1620–1590 BCE) reaching as far as *Babylon*, though gains were rarely held.
- The *New Hittite Kingdom* (1450–1180 BCE) under *Telepinu* and later *Mursili II* established a more stable, feudal system of vassal states that could be held and taxed more effectively.

### Military Innovation and Iron Technology
- The Hittites pioneered *iron smelting* (around 1500 BCE) and incorporated iron into weaponry, giving them technological superiority over bronze-using rivals like Egypt and Assyria.
- The Hittite chariot corps, armed with iron-tipped spears and arrows, became a dominant force; the *Chariot Manual*, written by a Hittite named *Kikkuli*, is the oldest surviving treatise on military training.
- Hittite armies used coordinated formations and siege tactics that influenced later Near Eastern warfare and may have influenced *David's* military innovations in biblical accounts.

### Law, Diplomacy, and Administration
- The *Code of Hammurabi* heavily influenced Hittite law, but the Hittites developed a more sophisticated legal code emphasizing proportional justice, restitution over vengeance, and distinctions between classes of people.
- The *Treaty of Kadesh* (1259 BCE), negotiated after the inconclusive *Battle of Kadesh* (1274 BCE) between Mursili II and Ramesses II, established a boundary between Hittite and Egyptian spheres and ended a century of warfare—it is the oldest surviving international treaty and defined the model for diplomatic agreements.
- The Hittites maintained vassal states through a feudal system: subordinate rulers swore oaths of loyalty in exchange for protection and the right to govern locally; rebellion was met with swift military punishment.

### Religion and Culture
- The Hittites adopted the gods of their Anatolian predecessors and incorporated deities from conquered territories, creating a syncretic pantheon that reflected their multi-ethnic empire.
- The royal family maintained religious authority, with the king serving as the intermediary between gods and subjects, justified through elaborate rituals and written edicts.
- The Hittites used *cuneiform* (borrowed from Mesopotamia) and developed their own *Hittite hieroglyphics* for ceremonial inscriptions.

### Collapse and Legacy
- The *New Hittite Kingdom* collapsed around 1180 BCE in a period called the *Bronze Age Collapse*—cities were destroyed and populations fled, possibly due to invasion by the *Sea Peoples*, famine, or internal conflict. The cause remains unclear.
- The Hittite empire fell, but Hittite culture persisted in *Neo-Hittite* city-states in northern Syria that survived until the *Assyrian* conquest in the 8th century BCE.

## context

The Hittites occupy a crucial but often-overlooked position in ancient history as the bridge between the Akkadian-Babylonian-Assyrian world of Mesopotamia and the later Greek and Mediterranean civilizations. Their iron technology fundamentally shifted the military balance of power; their legal innovations (proportional justice, restitution) influenced later Mediterranean legal traditions; and their diplomatic treaties established the precedent for formal international law that Rome would codify. The *Treaty of Kadesh* is particularly significant because it shows that by the 13th century BCE, the ancient Near Eastern powers had begun to regulate competition through formal agreement rather than endless warfare. The Hittites also demonstrate the fragility of empire: a sudden catastrophe (invasion, climate disaster, civil strife) can obliterate even a wealthy, technologically advanced state in a generation. For quiz bowl, the Hittites test knowledge of the Bronze Age, iron technology, the Treaty of Kadesh, and the distinction between the Old and New Hittite kingdoms.
