# Petticoat Affair

## summary

The *Petticoat Affair* (also called the *Eaton Affair*) was a social scandal during President *Andrew Jackson*'s administration centered on *Margaret "Peggy" Timberlake Eaton*, wife of *Secretary of War* *John Henry Eaton*. Washington society ostracized *Peggy Eaton* over rumors of her moral character; *Jackson*'s defense of her fractured his Cabinet and deepened partisan divides over propriety, women's honor, and executive authority.

## you gotta know

- *Peggy Timberlake* was the widow of a purser in the U.S. Navy; she married *Senator John Henry Eaton* of *Tennessee* in *1829*, shortly after *Jackson* appointed *Eaton* Secretary of War.
- Washington society, led by the wives of Cabinet members (notably *Floride Calhoun*, wife of *Vice President John C. Calhoun*), shunned *Peggy Eaton*, insinuating she had been sexually immoral before her marriage.
- President *Jackson* defended *Peggy Eaton*'s virtue and demanded that Cabinet families receive her socially; his refusal to tolerate slander allegedly derived from his late wife *Rachel*'s experience with similar gossip.
- The controversy split the Cabinet. *Secretary of State* *Martin Van Buren*, a widower, sided with *Jackson* and cultivated *Peggy Eaton*'s friendship, enhancing his standing with the President.
- *Vice President* *John C. Calhoun* and his allies used the scandal to challenge *Jackson* and engineer the resignation of anti-*Jackson* Cabinet members, particularly *Secretary of the Treasury John Ingham*.
- *Eaton* eventually resigned as Secretary of War in *1831*, and the scandal contributed to the Cabinet's reorganization and the estrangement between *Jackson* and *Calhoun*.
- The affair exposed tensions over women's social authority, the boundaries of presidential power in private society, and the brittle nature of early-19th-century political coalitions.
