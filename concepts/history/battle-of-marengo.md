# Battle of Marengo

## summary

The Battle of Marengo on June 14, 1800, was one of Napoleon's most celebrated early victories, fought in the Piedmont region of northern Italy against the Austrian army. Though the French nearly lost the battle in the afternoon, a timely flanking attack secured victory and restored French control of Italy after a period of Austrian gains; it became a cornerstone of Napoleonic legend.

## you gotta know

- Fought on June 14, 1800, between approximately 28,000 French troops and 35,000 Austrian forces near the village of Marengo in Piedmont.
- The Austrian general Michael von Melas attacked aggressively in the morning and initially drove the French back; by midday, Napoleon was nearly routed and contemplated retreat.
- A powerful flanking counterattack by French general Desaix, killed during the assault, shattered the Austrian formation and turned the battle decisively.
- Austrian losses were approximately 14,000 casualties and prisoners; French losses around 6,000 but inflicted strategically decisive damage on the enemy.
- The Treaty of Lunéville followed, confirming French control of Italy and the left bank of the Rhine, establishing French hegemony in western Europe.
- Marengo boosted Napoleon's political authority at home and solidified his position as First Consul; it became his personal favorite among his victories.
- The battle demonstrated the value of tactical flexibility and the ability to recover from tactical adversity through bold maneuver.
