# Black Dahlia

## summary

In January 1947, the mutilated body of 22-year-old *Elizabeth Short*, an aspiring actress in Los Angeles, was discovered in a vacant lot—surgically bisected at the waist and drained of blood. The murder captivated the nation through sensational press coverage, spawned thousands of false confessions, and remains unsolved. The case epitomizes Hollywood's darker side and the public obsession with beautiful, doomed women.

## you gotta know

- *Elizabeth Short*, known as the "Black Dahlia" for her striking appearance and preference for black clothing, was found dead on January 15, 1947, in a Los Angeles field.
- The body showed signs of sophisticated surgical mutilation: a precise bisection at the waist, drainage of blood, and careful placement of the remains—suggesting medical knowledge or experience.
- No suspects were ever definitively identified; the Los Angeles Police Department interviewed over 150 people and investigated multiple false leads, but the case went cold.
- The investigation was hampered by sensationalist media coverage, which published details that encouraged over a hundred false confessions and vigilante theories.
- Short's background—a transient, chronically broke actress mixed with military men and small-time criminals—suggested an exposure to dangerous circles, but her movements in her final days remained obscure.
- Decades later, DNA technology, genealogical databases, and renewed cold-case investigations have not produced definitive closure, though investigators have circled several suspects.
- The case became a fixture of American true-crime culture, inspiring books, films, and documentaries that transformed Elizabeth Short into a cultural icon of victimhood.
