# Brown v. Board of Education

## summary

*Brown v. Board of Education* (1954) unanimously declared that "separate educational facilities are inherently unequal" and unconstitutional, overturning *Plessy v. Ferguson*'s 58-year-old "separate but equal" doctrine. Chief Justice *Earl Warren*'s landmark decision held that segregation in public schools violates the *14th Amendment*, striking at the heart of Jim Crow and becoming a watershed moment for the modern civil rights movement.

## you gotta know

- Linda Brown, a Black third-grader in Topeka, Kansas, was denied admission to an all-white school; her parents sued, making her case the lead consolidation.
- Chief Justice *Earl Warren* wrote that education is "perhaps the most important function of state and local governments" and segregation inherently stigmatizes Black children.
- The Court overruled its own precedent in *Plessy*, recognizing that psychological research showed separation caused harm and inferiority.
- *Brown II* (1955) ordered desegregation "with all deliberate speed"—language many Southern districts used to delay compliance.
- Southern states mounted massive resistance, including closing public schools and funding private segregationist academies, stretching desegregation for decades.
- Sparked both the modern civil rights movement and intense backlash; remains the most symbolically powerful Supreme Court ruling on race.
