## summary

*Robert Smalls* (1839–1915) was an enslaved maritime pilot who became a Civil War hero, Reconstruction congressman, and collector of customs for Charleston. His journey from bondage to political prominence embodied the radical democratization of the postwar South and the era's fragile promise of Black citizenship.

## you gotta know

- Born enslaved in Charleston; during the *Civil War* commandeered the Confederate steamship *Planter* and sailed it to Union lines, becoming a symbol of enslaved heroism.
- Elected to the *South Carolina* House of Representatives (1868) and then to Congress (1875–1879), serving during the height of Reconstruction.
- Purchased his former master's plantation and served as mayor and port collector of Charleston, accumulating wealth and influence in the postwar South.
- Advocated for voting rights, education, and economic opportunity for freedmen; his legislative work focused on securing Black land ownership and suffrage.
- Witnessed the Democratic restoration (*Redemption*) that ended Reconstruction and disenfranchised Black voters; remained politically active but with diminishing power.
- Authored *My Life on the Water* (1895), a memoir recounting his escape and rise, establishing himself as a living bridge between enslavement and freedom.

## connections

- [[reconstruction-era]] — the period when his election and service exemplified the brief window of Black political power.
- [[south-carolina]] — the state where he was born enslaved, fought for Union victory, and later served in office and commerce.
- [[civil-war]] — his escape on the *Planter* was a defining moment of enslaved resistance and Union-allied heroism.
