# House of Stuart

## summary

The **House of Stuart** ruled Scotland from 1371 and inherited the English and Irish thrones in 1603, reigning until 1714. The Stuarts navigated the treacherous waters of religious upheaval, civil war, and constitutional revolution, but their conflicts with Parliament over divine right and religious authority left deep scars on British political culture and eventually cost them their thrones.

## you gotta know

- *James VI* of Scotland became *James I* of England in 1603, uniting the two kingdoms under one monarch; first Stuart king of England.
- Believed in the *divine right of kings*—a doctrine that generated fierce conflict with an increasingly assertive Parliament.
- *Charles I* (r. 1625–1649), James's son, provoked the *English Civil War* through his religious policies and tyrannical rule; captured, tried for treason, and executed in 1649.
- The *Interregnum* (1649–1660), under *Oliver Cromwell's* Commonwealth, saw the exile of the Stuart line, but *Charles II* was restored to the throne in 1660.
- *James II* (r. 1685–1688), a Catholic convert, sparked the *Glorious Revolution* (1688); his Protestant daughter *Mary II* and her husband *William III* of Orange took the throne instead.
- The *Bill of Rights* (1689) established parliamentary supremacy over the monarchy, transforming British government.
- The Stuart line ended with Queen *Anne* (r. 1702–1714); the *House of Hanover* inherited Britain's thrones.
