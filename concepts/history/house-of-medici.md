# House of Medici

## summary

The **House of Medici** rose from merchant banking to become the de facto rulers of Florence and one of Renaissance Europe's most influential families. Though they rarely held an official title, the Medici wielded immense political power through wealth and patronage, cultivating some of the greatest artists, scholars, and thinkers of the Italian Renaissance—including *Michelangelo* and *Leonardo da Vinci*—and fundamentally shaping Western culture.

## you gotta know

- Began as wealthy merchant bankers in Florence; *Cosimo de Medici* (1389–1464) became the city's most powerful man, though technically a private citizen.
- *Lorenzo the Magnificent* (1449–1492), Cosimo's great-grandson, perfected the art of power-brokering without formal title; a diplomat, patron, and poet who made Florence a cultural epicenter.
- Wielded power through accumulated wealth from banking, cloth trade, and political alliances with popes and foreign rulers; married into European nobility and papacy.
- Supreme patrons of the *Italian Renaissance*—commissioned *Michelangelo's* David and Sistine Chapel, *Botticelli's* works, and *Leonardo da Vinci's* innovations.
- *Cosimo I* (r. 1537–1574) became the first Grand Duke of Tuscany, elevating the family to formal princely rank; his successors ruled Tuscany until 1737.
- Produced two queens of France—*Catherine de Medici* (married *Henry II*) and *Marie de Medici* (married *Henry IV*).
- Used culture as statecraft; their patronage was calculated to enhance prestige, legitimize rule, and project power across Italy and beyond.
