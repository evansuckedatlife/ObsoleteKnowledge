# Assassination of Yitzhak Rabin

## summary

On November 4, 1995, Israeli Prime Minister *Yitzhak Rabin* was shot and killed by *Yigal Amir*, a right-wing Israeli extremist opposed to the *Oslo Accords* peace process with the Palestinians. The assassination was shocking because it represented the first time an Israeli leader had been killed by a fellow Israeli for political reasons. The event exposed deep rifts within Israeli society and became a watershed moment in the history of the Israeli-Palestinian conflict, marking the beginning of the end for the Oslo peace process.

## you gotta know

### The Assassination
- Shot on November 4, 1995, in *Tel Aviv, Israel*, at the conclusion of a peace rally attended by over 100,000 supporters of the Oslo process.
- Assassin: *Yigal Amir*, a right-wing law student and member of the Kahanist movement, opposed to territorial concessions to Palestinians.
- Amir fired at point-blank range; Rabin died at *Ichilov Hospital* within hours.
- The assassination shocked Israel; memorial sites filled with mourners; the event was termed *sikat Rabin* ("the Rabin assassination").

### Political Context & Motivation
- Rabin and Foreign Minister *Shimon Peres* had negotiated the *Oslo Accords* in 1993, establishing the foundation for Palestinian self-government and a two-state solution.
- Right-wing Israeli opponents viewed the Oslo process as a betrayal of Jewish territorial claims in the West Bank and Gaza.
- Rabin had received death threats from extremists; some Israeli political figures had used inflammatory rhetoric depicting him as a traitor.
- Amir belonged to the Kahanist movement, an extremist group advocating for territorial maximalism and opposing concessions to Palestinians.

### Aftermath & Impact
- Amir was arrested, tried, and sentenced to life imprisonment; he remains incarcerated.
- Rabin's death was mourned internationally as the loss of a statesman willing to compromise; *Bill Clinton* eulogized him.
- The assassination demoralized the Israeli left and emboldened the right; subsequent elections brought right-wing government back to power.
- The Oslo peace process stalled and eventually collapsed; no two-state solution has been achieved in the decades since Rabin's death.

## context

Rabin's assassination stands as a singular moment in Middle Eastern history: a leader making a historic compromise for peace was killed by his own countrymen for that very compromise. The event shattered the mythology of Israeli unity and exposed the depth of political division over the question of territory and coexistence. Unlike many assassinations driven by external forces, Amir's act was rooted entirely in internal Israeli politics—in the struggle between two competing visions of the Israeli state. The assassination did not prevent the peace process immediately, but it marked its symbolic end and became the hinge point after which the Israeli-Palestinian conflict entered a new, more violent phase. In global memory, Rabin's death is inseparable from the failure of the Oslo Accords and the continuation of occupation and conflict to the present day.
