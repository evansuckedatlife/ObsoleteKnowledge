## summary

*William Howe* was the commander-in-chief of British forces during the pivotal years of the American Revolution (1775–1778), winning every major battle in conventional warfare but failing to crush the Continental Army or achieve political reconciliation. His cautious strategy ultimately allowed *George Washington* to survive and rebuild, and his replacement marked Britain's shift toward a less winnable strategy.

## you gotta know

- Took command of British forces in 1775 with orders to suppress the rebellion and restore royal authority; he was experienced, professional, and backed by superior manpower and naval resources.
- Defeated Washington decisively at the *Battle of Long Island* (1776), forcing the Americans into a chaotic retreat that nearly destroyed the Continental Army.
- Captured *New York City* in 1776 and held it as a base throughout the war, establishing Britain's military dominance in the North during the early years.
- Executed a skillful *Philadelphia Campaign* (1777), defeating Washington at *Brandywine* and occupying the American capital—but his occupation did not break the rebellion or sway loyalist sentiment as expected.
- Chose not to pursue destroyed American armies aggressively, allowing Washington to regroup and survive; his critics argue he should have ended the war by annihilating the Continental Army when he had the chance.
- Was replaced in 1778 as the war stalled and British strategy shifted; his departure marked the end of the conventional warfare phase and the beginning of a more attritional conflict.
