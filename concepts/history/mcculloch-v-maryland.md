# McCulloch v. Maryland

## summary

McCulloch v. Maryland (1819) stands as the landmark case establishing that Congress possesses *implied powers* beyond those explicitly enumerated in the Constitution and that states cannot tax federal institutions. The case arose when Maryland attempted to tax a branch of the Second Bank of the United States; James McCulloch, the bank's cashier, refused to pay. Chief Justice John Marshall's sweeping opinion upheld Congress's right to charter the bank (not mentioned in the Constitution) under the *necessary and proper clause* and declared that Maryland's tax violated the principle of federal supremacy. The ruling tilted the balance decisively toward federal power and away from strict constitutionalism.

## you gotta know

### The Dispute
- *Maryland's tax*: In 1818, Maryland imposed a tax on banknotes issued by any bank not chartered in the state—a thinly veiled assault on the Second Bank of the United States.
- *The Bank's origins*: Congress chartered the Second Bank in 1816 as a quasi-public institution to manage federal finances and stabilize the currency, even though the Constitution does not explicitly authorize Congress to create banks.
- *McCulloch's defiance*: The bank's cashier refused to pay the tax, arguing it was unconstitutional; Maryland sued to enforce collection.

### Marshall's Broad Construction of Federal Power
- Marshall upheld Congress's power to charter the bank under the *necessary and proper clause* ("Congress may make all laws which shall be necessary and proper for carrying into execution" its enumerated powers).
- The clause grants Congress latitude to choose means to achieve constitutional ends, not limited to those strictly indispensable.
- *"Let the end be legitimate, let it be within the scope of the constitution, and all means which are appropriate, which are plainly adapted to that end, which are not prohibited, but consist with the letter and spirit of the constitution, are constitutional."*

### The Supremacy Doctrine
- Marshall ruled that Maryland's tax was unconstitutional because *the power to tax is the power to destroy*—a state tax on a federal instrument could throttle federal power.
- No state may tax an instrumentality of the federal government; conversely, the federal government cannot tax state-chartered institutions (implied).
- *"The constitution and the laws made in pursuance thereof are supreme; they control the constitution and laws of the respective states, and cannot be controlled by them."*

### The Shift in Constitutional Interpretation
- McCulloch moved from *strict* construction (powers must be explicitly stated) to *broad* construction (Congress has discretion to choose means to legitimate ends).
- The decision effectively read the necessary and proper clause as a broad grant of legislative flexibility rather than a narrow tie-breaker.
- Federalists cheered; strict-constructionists (including James Madison, ironically, who had earlier opposed the Bank) and state-rights advocates howled in protest.

### Lasting Consequences
- Grounded the expansion of federal regulatory power that accelerated through the 20th century (interstate commerce, tax, welfare).
- Transformed the Bank question from constitutional dead-letter to practical settled law—the federal government could and should manage money and markets.
- Remains the most-cited precedent for implying congressional powers and for the doctrine of federal supremacy over states.

## context

McCulloch v. Madison is the constitutional charter of federal dominance. In one stroke, Marshall solved two problems that plagued the early republic: the Bank's legitimacy and the proper relation of state and federal power. The opinion is brilliant rhetoric disguised as law—Marshall's "necessary and proper" reading is plausible but not inevitable; a strict constructionist could have required an explicit constitutional grant. Yet the breadth of his language ("let the end be legitimate") became the template for centuries of federal expansion. Without McCulloch, Congress would be confined to enumerated powers and would struggle to regulate interstate commerce, impose income taxes, or manage crises. The case exemplifies how a single Supreme Court decision can reshape the Constitution's balance without changing a word of its text. It also marks the origin of the "living Constitution" school: if the Constitution's meaning is not fixed but evolves with each generation's reading of clauses like "necessary and proper," then judges, not the text, are the locus of constitutional authority.
