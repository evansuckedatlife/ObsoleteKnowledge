## summary

The Battle of Kursk (July–August 1943) was the largest tank engagement in history, fought in southern Russia over a bulging Soviet salient (the Kursk Bulge). German forces under *Erich von Manstein* and *Walter Model* launched *Operation Citadel* to pinch off and destroy the salient, but Soviet forces under *Georgy Zhukov* had anticipated the offensive and prepared multiple defensive lines with thousands of tanks and guns. The Soviets repulsed the German attacks and counterattacked, pushing German forces west and sealing Germany's fate on the Eastern Front. After Kursk, Germany would never again mount a major offensive in the East.

## you gotta know

### Background
- The Kursk Salient was a Soviet bulge in the front line south of Moscow; German commanders believed destroying it would regain momentum lost at Stalingrad.
- *Operation Citadel* was to be a double pincer: *Army Group South* under *Erich von Manstein* attacking from the south, *Army Group Center* under *Walter Model* from the north.
- Soviets learned of the offensive plan in advance (via the *Lucy* spy ring) and fortified the salient with seven defensive belts, thousands of anti-tank guns, and massed reserves.

### The Battle (July 5–12, 1943)
- German forces attacked on July 5; Model's northern offensive gained little ground against Soviet defenses; Manstein's southern pincer advanced 25 miles but was blunted by Soviet reserves.
- Soviet Fifth Guards Tank Army under *Pavel Rotmistrov* delivered massive counterattacks; fierce tank and anti-tank duels engulfed the salient (notably at *Prokhorovka*, July 12).
- By July 12, German forces called off the offensive; losses were crippling and German resources could not be replenished, whereas Soviet production was surging.

### Aftermath and Significance
- The Soviets launched their own offensives, pushing German forces west; by August, the Kursk Salient was eliminated in Soviet favor, and Soviet armies advanced hundreds of miles.
- German losses in men, tanks, and aircraft were catastrophic and irreplaceable; Germany abandoned hopes of regaining the initiative in the East.
- Kursk marked the final strategic shift: the Soviet Union would now dictate the pace of war in Eastern Europe, pushing Germany toward Berlin and liberation of Soviet territory.

## context

Kursk was the Eastern Front's climactic tank battle—the moment Germany's offensive capability was permanently exhausted and the Soviet Union's ascendancy became unstoppable. The scale—over 8 million men deployed, 5,000 tanks, 5,000 aircraft—made it a colossal industrial and human clash. Germany's defeat at Kursk was not tactical mismanagement but the logical outcome of economic exhaustion: Germany could not replace its losses, while the Soviet Union could. After Kursk, the war became a race eastward, with Soviet armies liberating Ukraine, Poland, and Eastern Europe. In quiz bowl, Kursk is discussed for its tank numbers, the Soviet intelligence success, and as the threshold after which the Soviet Union was permanently on the offensive.
