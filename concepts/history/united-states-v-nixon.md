# United States v. Nixon

## summary

*United States v. Nixon* (1974) held that while the President possesses a qualified executive privilege, that privilege is not absolute and may be overridden by the needs of criminal justice. The decision ordered Nixon to surrender tape recordings sought for prosecution of Watergate conspirators, hastening his resignation.

## you gotta know

- The Watergate scandal and investigations into Nixon's role prompted a federal grand jury to subpoena nine tape recordings from the Oval Office.
- Nixon's lawyers claimed executive privilege shielded the tapes from disclosure; they argued the President cannot be forced to disclose confidential communications.
- Chief Justice *Warren Burger* held that executive privilege exists but is not absolute—it yields to the needs of criminal justice and judicial proceedings.
- The Court balanced the President's need for confidential advisors against the judiciary's need for evidence in criminal prosecutions.
- Nixon refused to comply initially, then released the tapes; they revealed his involvement in the cover-up and led to his resignation days later.
- Established that the President is subject to judicial process and that no absolute immunity protects presidential communications from courts.
