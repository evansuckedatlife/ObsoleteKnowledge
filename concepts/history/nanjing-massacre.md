# Nanjing Massacre

## summary

The **Nanjing Massacre** was a six-week campaign of mass killing, rape, and pillage perpetrated by the Japanese Imperial Army against the civilian population of Nanjing, China, from December 1937 to February 1938. After capturing the capital of the Nationalist Chinese government, Japanese forces executed an estimated 200,000 to 300,000 civilians and prisoners of war—though some estimates exceed 400,000—making it one of the deadliest atrocities of the twentieth century. The violence was characterized by systematic sexual assault, torture, summary executions, and deliberate starvation; it shocked the international community and presaged the industrial-scale war crimes that would define the Pacific War and the Holocaust.

## you gotta know

### The Capture
- The Japanese captured Nanjing on December 13, 1937, after a series of defeats inflicted on Nationalist Chinese forces during the Second Sino-Japanese War.
- The city was the capital of the Nationalist government; its seizure represented a symbolic victory but also left the Japanese with a city of over 1 million civilians.
- Japanese military discipline had already deteriorated during the campaign, and plans for civilian governance were rudimentary.

### The Atrocities
- Japanese soldiers systematized killings, dividing captives into categories: suspected soldiers, laborers, and civilians. Executions occurred in makeshift firing squads, ditches, and temples.
- Sexual violence was endemic; an estimated 20,000 to 80,000 women (Chinese, Korean, and other nationalities) were raped; many were imprisoned in "comfort stations" or murdered afterward.
- Soldiers looted and burned entire neighborhoods; starvation was weaponized as soldiers confiscated food supplies.

### Documented Crimes
- Prisoners of war and disarmed soldiers were executed en masse after the fighting ended, violating international law.
- The "Killing Contests" (a dubious historical claim, but widely reported at the time): two officers allegedly bet on who could kill 100 Chinese first, using swords.
- Bodies were dumped in mass graves, rivers, and burned; the Yangtze River allegedly ran red with blood.

### International Response
- Foreign nationals (German businessmen, American missionaries, Swedish diplomats) established a "Safety Zone" that sheltered approximately 250,000 civilians; without it, the death toll would likely have been far higher.
- Eyewitness accounts and photographs were smuggled out by journalists and diplomats; the atrocities were reported in foreign press, horrifying international opinion.
- The Japanese military suppressed reports and initially denied the scale of killings; the Chinese Nationalist government documented the atrocities as war crimes.

### Aftermath
- After Japan's surrender in 1945, the International Military Tribunal for the Far East indicted key Japanese commanders for crimes against humanity.
- Estimates of casualties ranged widely; the Chinese government claimed 300,000; Japanese revisionists minimized the death toll to 40,000 or fewer (claims disputed by scholars).
- The massacre remains contested in Japanese, Chinese, and Korean historical memory, shaping tensions over regional reconciliation.

## context

The Nanjing Massacre exemplifies the dehumanization that occurs in industrialized modern warfare, particularly when imperial ideologies and racial hierarchies go unchecked. The Japanese military had absorbed a cult of emperor worship, glorified death and obedience, and viewed Chinese people as racially inferior and expendable; the result was organized violence with almost no restraint. Nanjing shocked the world not because massacres were new—they had occurred throughout history—but because it revealed how modern armies with modern logistics could systematize and scale atrocity into genocide. The event foreshadowed the Holocaust and the carpet bombing of cities; it remains the deadliest single atrocity in the Asia-Pacific war and a permanent stain on the Imperial Japanese military record.
