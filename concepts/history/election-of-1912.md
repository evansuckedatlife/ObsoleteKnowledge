# Election of 1912

## summary

The 1912 election split the Republican Party and ushered in an era of progressive reform under Woodrow Wilson. Theodore Roosevelt, estranged from his successor William Howard Taft, launched a third-party Bull Moose campaign that captured more popular votes than Taft but gifted the presidency to Wilson. The election demonstrated the power of an insurgent progressive movement, both within parties and outside them, and realigned American politics around industrial regulation and social justice.

## you gotta know

### The Split and the Candidates
- *Theodore Roosevelt*, ex-president, ran as the *Progressive (Bull Moose) Party* candidate, winning 27 percent of the popular vote and 88 electoral votes.
- *William Howard Taft*, the incumbent Republican president, came third in popular votes (23 percent) but retained the Republican machinery and 8 electoral votes.
- *Woodrow Wilson*, the Democrat, won with just 42 percent of the popular vote but 435 electoral votes, benefiting from the Republican split.

### The Progressive Insurgency
- Roosevelt's platform—the most radical ever run by a major candidate—included women's suffrage, child labor laws, and expanded antitrust action.
- Eugene Debs, the Socialist candidate, received 6 percent of the popular vote, the Socialist Party's peak; Wilson's progressivism appealed to many potential Debs voters.
- The Bull Moose ticket split traditionally Republican voters, enabling Wilson to win nearly every state.

### The Realignment
- The GOP fractured, with progressives defecting to Roosevelt and moderates rallying to Taft; the party's center of gravity shifted rightward.
- Wilson took office with a mandate for reform, implementing tariff reduction, antitrust action, and Federal Reserve creation.

## context

The 1912 election was a referendum on the pace of progressivism. Taft, though conservative by later standards, had pursued trust-busting more vigorously than Roosevelt, but was seen as insufficiently aggressive and too deferential to conservatives. Roosevelt's Bull Moose challenge represented the spirit of the Progressive Era—faith in expert management, corporate regulation, and social reform. The election's outcome—Wilson's victory on a minority vote—was possible only because the opposition split, but it demonstrated that progressivism had captured both major parties' bases. The split proved permanent: the Republicans never reunited; by the 1920s, the conservatives had reclaimed the party's leadership, and the progressives were permanently realigned as Democrats or independent voters.
