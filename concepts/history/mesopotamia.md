# Mesopotamia

## summary

Mesopotamia, the region between the Tigris and Euphrates rivers in modern-day Iraq and Syria, was the cradle of Western civilization, where humanity developed writing, complex government, cities, agriculture, and law. From approximately 3500 BCE to the rise of the Persian Empire in the 6th century BCE, a succession of civilizations—*Sumer*, *Akkad*, *Babylon*, *Assyria*, and others—flourished in this fertile region, each advancing human knowledge and political organization. Mesopotamian innovations in irrigation, mathematics, astronomy, literature, and law fundamentally shaped the world: the *Code of Hammurabi* established the principle of written law, *cuneiform* was the world's first writing system, the Sumerians invented the wheel and the sailboat, and the Epic of Gilgamesh is one of humanity's oldest literary masterpieces. Mesopotamia's significance lies not only in these specific achievements but in the very concept of civilization itself—the idea that humans could organize into complex societies with written laws, specialized labor, and monumental architecture.

## you gotta know

### geographic and agricultural foundations

- Mesopotamia's name means "between rivers"—the region was bounded by the Tigris (east) and Euphrates (west), providing water for *irrigation agriculture* in an otherwise arid climate.
- The annual flooding of the Tigris and Euphrates deposited rich silt, creating an extraordinarily fertile floodplain that allowed large agricultural surpluses, supporting dense populations.
- Because Mesopotamia lacked natural stone and timber, civilizations developed *mud brick* architecture and *trade networks* extending to Anatolia, Egypt, the Indus Valley, and beyond.

### Sumer and the invention of civilization

- *Sumer*, in southern Mesopotamia (modern southern Iraq), saw the emergence of the world's first *city-states* (independent cities ruling surrounding territory) around 3500 BCE: *Uruk*, *Ur*, *Lagash*, *Nippur*.
- The Sumerians invented *cuneiform* (c. 3200 BCE), the world's first writing system, initially developed for accounting but later used to record literature, law, and history.
- Sumerians invented numerous technologies and practices: the *wheel* (c. 3500 BCE), the *sailboat*, *bronze metallurgy*, *irrigation systems*, and the 60-minute hour (the basis of modern time-keeping).

### Akkad and empire

- The *Akkadians*, a Semitic-speaking people, conquered Sumer around 2350 BCE under *Sargon of Akkad*, establishing the world's first empire—a centralized state ruling diverse territories and peoples.
- The Akkadian Empire lasted only about 200 years before collapsing, but established the template for later Mesopotamian and world empire: bureaucracy, military organization, and the idea that one ruler could govern vast territories.
- *Akkadian*, a Semitic language, gradually replaced Sumerian, though Sumerian continued as a sacred and scholarly language, similar to Latin in medieval Europe.

### the Babylonian golden age

- *Babylon*, a small city-state on the Euphrates, rose to dominance under *Hammurabi* (r. 1792–1750 BCE), who conquered Mesopotamia and created a centralized kingdom.
- Hammurabi issued the *Code of Hammurabi* (c. 1754 BCE), one of the earliest and most comprehensive written legal codes, establishing principles like "an eye for an eye" and demonstrating that laws should be publicly known and uniformly applied.
- Babylon became a center of learning: Babylonian mathematics advanced to solving quadratic equations, Babylonian astronomers tracked the planets and invented the zodiac, and Babylonian literature included the *Epic of Gilgamesh*, a masterpiece of human literature.

### Assyrian militarism

- *Assyria*, in northern Mesopotamia (modern northern Iraq), gradually expanded and eventually conquered all of Mesopotamia and surrounding territories, creating an even larger empire.
- The *Assyrian Empire*, especially under *Sargon II*, *Sennacherib*, and *Ashurbanipal* (7th century BCE), was known for military brutality, mass deportation of conquered peoples, and elaborate palaces.
- Ashurbanipal's library at *Nineveh* preserved thousands of cuneiform texts, including the Epic of Gilgamesh, which would otherwise have been lost to history.

### intellectual and cultural achievements

- Mesopotamian mathematics developed *algebra*, *geometry*, and the *base-60* number system (the foundation of modern trigonometry and time measurement).
- *Astronomy* was advanced: Mesopotamian astronomers named the constellations and planets, tracked lunar eclipses, and developed the zodiac used in astrology.
- *Literature* flourished: besides the Epic of Gilgamesh, Mesopotamian literature included hymns, lamentations, and the flood narrative (paralleled in the Biblical *Book of Genesis*).

### decline and succession

- The *Mesopotamian civilizations* gradually declined as the Persian Empire under *Cyrus the Great* (6th century BCE) conquered Mesopotamia, ending the period of Mesopotamian political independence.
- After Persian conquest, Mesopotamia continued as an important cultural and economic region but never again dominated the Near East as it had during the Sumerian, Babylonian, and Assyrian periods.
- The invention of the *Aramaic alphabet* (simpler than cuneiform) gradually displaced cuneiform writing, and by the Common Era, cuneiform was entirely extinct, though Mesopotamian learning survived in Greek and Arabic sources.

## context

Mesopotamia was where human civilization itself began: where writing was invented, where the first cities were built, where law was codified, and where humans first organized into complex states with specialized labor, bureaucracy, and cultural institutions. Every aspect of Mesopotamian civilization—writing, law, mathematics, astronomy, irrigation, monumental architecture—would be copied, improved upon, and transmitted to later civilizations: Egypt, Greece, Rome, and the Islamic world. The Code of Hammurabi established the principle that law should be written, public, and uniform, a revolutionary idea that underpins modern legal systems. Mesopotamian mathematics and astronomy were so advanced that their systems (base-60, the zodiac) persist in modern society. The Epic of Gilgamesh, dating to around 2100 BCE, is one of humanity's oldest and greatest works of literature, addressing themes of friendship, mortality, and the human condition that remain relevant today. Mesopotamia's legacy is so profound that understanding the region's civilizations is essential to understanding how human civilization itself developed and what makes complex society possible.
