# Assassination of Abraham Lincoln

## summary

On April 14, 1865, President *Abraham Lincoln* was shot by actor *John Wilkes Booth* at Ford's Theatre in Washington, D.C., dying the following morning. The assassination occurred just days after the end of the Civil War and marked a turning point in American history, transforming Lincoln into a martyr and reshaping the nation's Reconstruction. The act was motivated by Booth's Southern sympathies and opposition to Lincoln's abolition policies.

## you gotta know

### The Event
- Shot on April 14, 1865, at *Ford's Theatre* in Washington, D.C., during a performance of *Our American Cousin*.
- Assassin: *John Wilkes Booth*, a prominent actor and Confederate sympathizer.
- Booth entered the president's box unchallenged and fired a .44-caliber derringer at close range.
- Lincoln died the next morning, April 15, at the *Petersen House* across the street, never regaining consciousness.

### Context & Aftermath
- Assassination occurred just 5 days after General *Robert E. Lee* surrendered at *Appomattox*, effectively ending the Civil War.
- Booth escaped the theatre but was tracked to a barn in *Virginia* and shot by federal troops on April 26.
- Vice President *Andrew Johnson* assumed the presidency and proved less merciful toward the South than Lincoln would have been.
- Lincoln became the first U.S. president to be assassinated, sparking a national trauma that unified North and South in grief.

## context

Lincoln's assassination stands as a watershed moment in American memory and political mythology. The death of a wartime leader just as the nation faces reunification created a profound "what if"—how might Reconstruction have unfolded under Lincoln's stated moderation versus Johnson's vindictiveness? The event inaugurated a genre: the presidential assassination as both intimate tragedy and national catastrophe. It also codified the Secret Service's modern protective role. In quiz bowl and public discourse, Lincoln's death recurs as the hinge between Civil War and Reconstruction, and as America's first reckoning with political violence at the highest office.
