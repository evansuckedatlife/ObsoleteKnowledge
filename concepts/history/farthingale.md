# Farthingale

## summary

The *farthingale* is a hooped or structured petticoat that creates a distinctive bell-shaped or cone-shaped silhouette in women's skirts, worn predominantly in the 16th and early 17th centuries. Originating in Spain and spreading across Europe, the farthingale became essential to Elizabethan and Renaissance fashion, transforming women's bodies into architectural monuments of fabric and frame.

## you gotta know

- Constructed from linen with horizontal hoops of wood, bone, or whale baleen that flare outward from the waist—the Spanish farthingale created a cone, while the English version produced a bell shape.
- The Spanish farthingale could extend as wide as 48 inches at the hem, making doorways a genuine hazard and requiring women to turn sideways to pass through.
- Required multiple layers of skirts and petticoats over the hoop structure; the weight and bulk made movement difficult and sitting nearly impossible in narrow chairs.
- Farthingales rose and fell in width according to fashion cycles—the most extreme versions required wearers to sit on cushions rather than chairs.
- Became intertwined with Elizabethan court culture under Queen Elizabeth I, who wore progressively more extravagant versions as a display of royal authority and fashion leadership.
- Associated with moral controversy; some religious authorities condemned the farthingale as a device of vanity and deception, distorting the natural female form.
- Gradually superseded by the crinoline in the 19th century, which offered similar silhouette with simpler construction.

## connections

- [[corset]] — rigid undergarment worn simultaneously to shape the torso while the farthingale shaped the skirt.
- [[elizabeth-i]] — English queen who made the extravagant farthingale a symbol of Elizabethan court power and fashion innovation.
- [[ruff]] — elaborate neckwear that paired with farthingales as part of high Renaissance formal dress.
- [[crinoline]] — 19th-century successor technology that achieved similar silhouettes with hooped petticoats.
