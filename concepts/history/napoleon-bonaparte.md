## summary

*Napoleon Bonaparte* (1769–1821) rose from Corsican obscurity to become the French Revolution's greatest military hero and ultimately its usurper, consolidating revolutionary gains into a personal empire that would reshape Europe. A brilliant general whose campaigns from Italy to Egypt built an unstoppable reputation, he engineered the Coup of 18 Brumaire (1799) to end the revolution's political chaos, then gradually transformed himself from First Consul to *Emperor* (1804). Though he crowned himself as a monarch, Napoleon paradoxically spread revolutionary ideals across Europe: the Napoleonic Code established legal equality, feudalism was abolished in conquered territories, and meritocratic administration replaced aristocratic privilege. His ambition ultimately overreached when he invaded Russia, and his final defeat at Waterloo (1815) left him a prisoner, though his legacy profoundly shaped 19th-century nationalism and legal systems.

## you gotta know

### Rise to Power
- Rose to prominence as a military officer during the revolution's wars against European monarchies, building a reputation for tactical brilliance and innovative strategy.
- Suppressed the royalist Insurrection of 13 Vendémiaire (October 1795), earning the government's trust and establishing himself as the revolution's indispensable military hero.
- Led dazzling campaigns in Italy (1796–1797), defeating Austrian armies and expanding French control while building his personal legend across Europe.
- Conducted the Egyptian campaign (1798–1799), attempting to threaten British interests in India; though ultimately unsuccessful militarily, it enhanced his mythic status.
- Engineered the Coup of 18 Brumaire (November 1799), overthrowing the ineffective Directory government and installing himself as First Consul—the revolution's strongman.

### The Napoleonic System
- Consolidated revolutionary gains (legal equality, abolition of feudalism, meritocratic administration) while systematically eliminating revolutionary democracy and restoring order through autocracy.
- Crowned himself *Emperor of the French* in December 1804, a symbolic transformation from revolutionary general to Continental monarch that scandalized and inspired Europe.
- Established the *Napoleonic Code* (civil law), which enshrined legal equality and property rights and became the model for legal systems across Europe and Latin America.
- Declared himself Emperor of Italy, King of the Netherlands, and Protector of the Confederation of the Rhine, redrawing the political map through conquest and alliance.

### Military Ambition and Downfall
- Defeated Austria repeatedly but faced mounting coalitions of European powers determined to contain French expansion—Britain remained undefeated and bankrolled his enemies.
- Invaded Russia in 1812 with the Grande Armée (over 600,000 soldiers), expecting a quick victory; instead faced catastrophic losses in the harsh winter and lengthy supply lines.
- Abdicated and was exiled to Elba (1814), but escaped and returned for the Hundred Days (March–June 1815), only to be defeated definitively at *Waterloo* and exiled to remote Saint Helena.

## context

Napoleon is history's supreme example of the revolutionary who becomes the tyrant he fought against—and yet his paradox is that he genuinely transformed the institutions and ideologies of Europe. The Code Napoléon, the abolition of feudalism in conquered territories, and the meritocratic principle that anyone could rise by talent (including himself) represented a revolutionary rupture with the feudal past that no restoration could fully erase. European nationalism and liberal legal reform both trace lineage to Napoleon's impact, making him simultaneously a betrayer of the revolution and its most successful propagandist.

## connections

- [[french-revolution]] — he was the revolution's greatest product and its usurper, ending its chaos while preserving its ideals.
- [[robespierre]] — opposed the Terror but benefited from the revolutionary impulse that created it; Napoleon inherited the order Robespierre sought.
- [[louis-xvi]] — his rise occurred in the shadow of the king's execution; he replaced monarchy with empire.
- [[danton]] — both represented different visions of revolutionary consolidation; Napoleon's synthesis succeeded where Danton's was guillotined.
- [[enlightenment]] — the philosophical tradition of reason and natural law that underlay his Code.
- [[british-empire]] — his nemesis and the only major power never defeated by him; British naval supremacy prevented his continental dominance from becoming global.
- [[congress-of-vienna]] — the post-Napoleonic settlement that attempted to erase his redrawing of Europe.
- [[nationalism]] — his career and example sparked nationalist movements across Europe and Latin America in the 19th century.
