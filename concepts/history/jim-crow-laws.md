# Jim Crow Laws

## summary

Jim Crow laws were state and local statutes enacted primarily in the American South from the 1870s through the 1960s that enforced racial segregation and discriminated against African Americans. Named after a racist minstrel show character, these laws mandated the separation of Black and white people in schools, public facilities, transportation, housing, and other areas of life under the doctrine of "separate but equal" (upheld in *Plessy v. Ferguson*, 1896). Jim Crow laws, enforced by both legal punishment and extrajudicial violence, systematized white supremacy and disenfranchised Black voters through literacy tests, poll taxes, and grandfather clauses.

## you gotta know

### origins and scope
- Jim Crow laws proliferated after Reconstruction ended in 1877, reversing gains African Americans had made during Reconstruction.
- They enforced segregation not through explicit racial language (after the Fourteenth Amendment) but through facially neutral statutes and regulations that applied only where race was involved (e.g., "no intermarriage").
- They governed schools, transportation, hotels, restaurants, parks, water fountains, cemeteries, and even blood banks.

### voter suppression mechanisms
- *Literacy tests*: required voters to read and explain complex passages, administered with racist discretion.
- *Poll taxes*: levied a tax on voting; exemptions for voters whose grandfather had voted (grandfather clause) allowed poor white men to vote while excluding formerly enslaved Black men.
- *White primaries*: excluded Black voters from Democratic primary elections, effectively excluding them from the only competitive elections in the one-party South.

### legal justification: "Separate but Equal"
- *Plessy v. Ferguson* (1896) upheld the doctrine that segregation did not violate equal protection if facilities were "separate but equal."
- In practice, facilities provided to Black people were vastly inferior to those for white people.
- *Plessy* remained the law for 58 years until overturned in *Brown v. Board of Education* (1954).

### mechanisms of enforcement
- Legal penalties (fines, imprisonment) for violating segregation statutes.
- Extrajudicial violence by the *Ku Klux Klan* and white mobs.
- Economic coercion: loss of employment, housing, credit for Black people who challenged segregation.

### impact and legacy
- Jim Crow laws created a rigid caste system denying African Americans equal access to education, economic opportunity, political power, and basic dignity.
- They were the legal foundation for wealth inequality between Black and white Americans that persists today.
- The *Civil Rights Act of 1964* prohibited discrimination in public accommodations; the *Voting Rights Act of 1965* prohibited voter suppression.
