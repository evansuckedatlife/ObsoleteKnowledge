# COVID-19 Pandemic

## summary

The *COVID-19 pandemic* began in late 2019 in Wuhan, China, when a novel coronavirus (SARS-CoV-2) emerged and spread globally, killing an estimated 7 to 20 million people by 2024. The pandemic prompted unprecedented international lockdowns, accelerated vaccine development, disrupted supply chains, and exposed deep inequities in health access and pandemic preparedness across nations.

## you gotta know

- Caused by the virus SARS-CoV-2, likely originating from animal-to-human transmission (zoonosis) in late 2019; the exact initial spillover route remains debated.
- Spread through respiratory droplets and aerosol transmission; severity ranged from asymptomatic to severe pneumonia and multi-organ failure, particularly in elderly and immunocompromised populations.
- Killed an estimated 7–20 million people officially reported or excess deaths estimated through 2024; actual toll likely far higher due to underreporting in many countries.
- Prompted global lockdowns, border closures, school shutdowns, and business closures—disrupting economies and creating secondary health crises in mental health and routine medical care.
- Vaccines developed in under a year using mRNA technology (Pfizer, Moderna) and viral-vector approaches; vaccination programs began late 2020 and saved millions of lives, though rollout was inequitable globally.
- The pandemic exposed weak global health governance, supply chain fragility, and stark disparities: wealthy nations hoarded vaccines while poor nations faced shortages.
- Continued as endemic circulation; new variants (Delta, Omicron) emerged and surged; disease burden shifted toward respiratory symptoms in vaccinated populations and became less lethal but remained transmissible.

## connections

- [[virology]] — SARS-CoV-2 is a coronavirus; study of its evolution and mutation informed pandemic forecasting.
- [[vaccine-development]] — mRNA and viral-vector vaccines developed at unprecedented speed; reshaped confidence in future vaccine timelines.
- [[pandemic-response]] — exposed the gaps in international disease surveillance, equity mechanisms, and preparedness funding.
- [[epidemiology]] — real-time genomic surveillance, phylogenetic tracking, and global sequencing networks scaled rapidly.
- [[mental-health-crisis]] — lockdowns, isolation, and economic collapse created secondary mental health and substance-use epidemics.
