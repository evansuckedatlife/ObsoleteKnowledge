# Heart of Atlanta Motel v. United States

## summary

*Heart of Atlanta Motel v. United States* (1964) unanimously upheld the constitutionality of the *Civil Rights Act of 1964*'s prohibition on racial discrimination in public accommodations, establishing that Congress could regulate discrimination through the *Commerce Clause*. The Heart of Atlanta Motel in Atlanta, Georgia, which refused to rent to Black customers, challenged the law; the Supreme Court's swift, decisive ruling gave the landmark civil rights statute firm constitutional footing.

## you gotta know

- The *Heart of Atlanta Motel* refused to serve Black customers; Congress had recently outlawed such discrimination in the *Civil Rights Act of 1964*.
- Chief Justice *Earl Warren* wrote that Congress could regulate discrimination in interstate commerce, since hotels serve interstate travelers.
- The Court rejected arguments that the law violated due process or property rights; the *Commerce Clause* gave Congress ample power.
- Decided the same day as *Katzenbach v. McClung*, which upheld the act's ban on discrimination in restaurants affecting interstate commerce.
- Provided constitutional validation for the broadest civil rights legislation since Reconstruction, enabling federal enforcement of public accommodations equality.
- Established that the *Commerce Clause* is a powerful tool for civil rights enforcement, not merely for regulating trade.
