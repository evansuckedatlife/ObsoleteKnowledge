# Great Reform Act of 1832

## summary

The **Great Reform Act of 1832** fundamentally restructured British electoral representation, abolishing rotten boroughs (sparsely populated constituencies with outsized parliamentary power), redistributing seats to industrial cities, and expanding the franchise to include the middle class. Though it stopped short of democratic suffrage—still excluding the working class—the Reform Act of 1832 marked a watershed in British political history, breaking the aristocratic monopoly on power and establishing the principle that electoral representation should reflect population and economic capacity. Its passage came only after years of mass agitation, popular pressure, and two parliamentary rejections.

## you gotta know

- The unreformed Parliament was dominated by rotten boroughs where a handful of voters or even a single landlord controlled representation; industrial cities like Manchester and Birmingham sent no MPs despite massive populations.
- The *Whig* Prime Minister *Earl Grey* introduced the bill in 1831; it was rejected twice before passing in 1832 after the King created new peers to break the Tory resistance in the House of Lords.
- The Act eliminated 56 rotten boroughs and drastically reduced representation from another 30, redistributing seats to industrial and urban constituencies.
- Franchise expanded from roughly 3% to roughly 7% of the adult male population; property qualifications were standardized but still excluded workers and all women.
- *Robert Peel* and the Conservative Party initially opposed reform but eventually accepted it as necessary to avert revolution; the violence of [[peterloo]] and subsequent reform agitation made reform inevitable.
- The 1832 Act set a precedent for franchise expansion; later reforms in 1867, 1884, and 1918 progressively enfranchised more of the working class and eventually women.

## connections

- [[peterloo]] — the 1819 massacre and subsequent reform agitation created the political climate for the 1832 Act.
- [[chartism]] — the movement's core demand was genuine democracy; frustrated by the limited 1832 franchise, Chartists demanded universal male suffrage.
- [[william-ewart-gladstone]] — dominated mid-Victorian politics as a Liberal reformer, extending the franchise further in 1867.
- [[robert-peel]] — Conservative leader who accepted electoral reform as a political necessity.
- [[british-abolitionism]] — parallel moral and political movement that also mobilized middle-class voters empowered by the 1832 Act.
