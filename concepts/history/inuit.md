# Inuit

## summary

The *Inuit* are the indigenous circumpolar peoples of the Arctic, inhabiting regions across Alaska, Canada, Greenland, and Russia's Chukotka Peninsula. They developed sophisticated maritime and terrestrial hunting cultures adapted to extreme Arctic conditions, including iconic technologies like kayaks and dog sleds, and maintained complex societies with distinct regional languages and traditions.

## you gotta know

- Inhabit the *Arctic* regions across Alaska, Northern Canada, Greenland, and eastern Russia; their territories span one of Earth's most extreme environments.
- Speak *Inuit-Yupik* languages, an Eskimo-Aleut family with multiple distinct languages and dialects; *Inuktitut*, *Inupiatun*, and *Kalaallisut* (Greenlandic) are major varieties.
- Developed sophisticated marine hunting traditions targeting seals, whales, and fish using specialized equipment including *kayaks* (originally *qajaq*), harpoons, and nets.
- Practice dog sledding (*dog teams*) for winter transportation across frozen landscapes; historically central to mobility and trade; still culturally significant in some communities.
- Created intricate art forms including *tupilak* carvings (spiritual figures), ivory carving, and drum-making; oral traditions preserved narratives and ecological knowledge.
- Historically lived in seasonal camps and settlements adapted to animal migrations and seasonal changes; developed efficient housing including *igloo* snow houses in certain regions.
- Today number approximately 180,000 across the Arctic; face ongoing challenges of climate change, cultural preservation, and self-determination; increasingly advocating for indigenous rights and environmental protection.
