# Black Death

## summary

The *Black Death* was a catastrophic pandemic of bubonic plague that swept across Eurasia and North Africa between 1347 and 1353, killing an estimated 75 to 200 million people. It arrived in Europe through trade routes, decimating populations by up to 60% in some regions and profoundly reshaping medieval society, labor markets, and religious life.

## you gotta know

- Caused by the bacterium *Yersinia pestis*, spread primarily by fleas on infected rats; also transmitted directly between humans in the pneumonic form.
- Began in Central Asia and reached Europe via Genoese traders fleeing the Mongol siege of Kaffa (1347), where some accounts report catapulted plague-infected corpses.
- Killed an estimated 75–200 million people across Eurasia; in Europe alone, populations declined by 30–60% in major cities and trade centers.
- Characterized by painful swollen lymph nodes (buboes), blackened tissue from gangrene, and rapid death—most victims died within days of infection.
- Prompted mass migration of survivors, creation of flagellant movements seeking divine atonement, and violent pogroms against Jewish communities falsely blamed for the disease.
- Labor shortages after the pandemic raised wages for surviving workers and contributed to the decline of feudalism across Europe.
- Contemporary chronicles like those of Jean de Venette and Giovanni Boccaccio (*Decameron*) recorded the social chaos and moral breakdown during peak mortality.
