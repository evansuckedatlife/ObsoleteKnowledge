# Watergate

## summary

*Watergate* is the political scandal arising from the 1972 break-in at the Democratic National Committee headquarters and the Nixon administration's subsequent cover-up. The scandal revealed abuse of presidential power, obstruction of justice, and political espionage, ultimately forcing President *Richard Nixon* to resign in 1974 and fundamentally altering public trust in government.

## you gotta know

- Five burglars were arrested breaking into the Democratic National Committee offices at the Watergate complex in *Washington, D.C.* on *June 17, 1972*, during the presidential campaign.
- The break-in was directly connected to *CREEP* (the *Committee to Re-elect the President*), which orchestrated the burglary and other illegal campaign activities.
- President *Nixon* and his advisors engaged in a cover-up, destroying evidence and attempting to obstruct the FBI investigation—crimes that ultimately exceeded the initial break-in's gravity.
- *Bob Woodward* and *Carl Bernstein* of the *Washington Post* broke the story; their source *Deep Throat* (later revealed to be *Mark Felt*, the FBI's Deputy Director) provided crucial leaks.
- The *House Judiciary Committee* voted to impeach *Nixon* on three counts: obstruction of justice, abuse of power, and contempt of Congress.
- Faced with certain conviction in a Senate trial, *Nixon* resigned on *August 9, 1974*—the only U.S. president to do so. *Gerald Ford* assumed the presidency and later pardoned him.
- The scandal triggered the *War Powers Act*, campaign finance reforms (*Federal Election Campaign Act*), and the creation of the independent counsel statute.
