## summary

The **My Lai Massacre** was the systematic slaughter of between 347 and 504 South Vietnamese civilians, including women, children, and elderly, by U.S. Army soldiers of Charlie Company under the command of *Lieutenant William Calley* on March 16, 1968, in the hamlet of My Lai in Quang Ngai Province. Operating under orders to clear the village of Viet Cong suspects, soldiers conducted house-to-house killings, sexual assaults, and mutilations; the violence was essentially unopposed by armed combatants. The massacre remained largely concealed for over a year until journalist *Seymour Hersh* exposed it, sparking outrage in America and internationally, eroding public support for the Vietnam War and prompting trials for war crimes.

## you gotta know

### The Mission
- Charlie Company was part of *Task Force Barker*, assigned to search and destroy suspected Viet Cong positions in the hamlet of My Lai during *Operation Wheeler Wallowa*.
- Briefing materials instructed soldiers to eliminate all civilians in the area, claiming they were either Viet Cong or sympathizers; the company had experienced casualties from booby traps and received orders to take no prisoners.
- The operation was ostensibly a response to enemy activity, though My Lai had no significant military presence.

### The Killings
- Soldiers entered the hamlet and began killing indiscriminately: in homes, rice paddies, and gathering points; they shot children, elderly, and infants at close range.
- Women were raped before being murdered; some soldiers mutilated bodies and committed other atrocities.
- A small group of soldiers, led by *Warrant Officer Hugh Thompson Jr.*, attempted to stop the violence; Thompson evacuated some civilians and threatened to shoot soldiers if the killing continued, effectively halting the massacre.

### Aftermath and Cover-up
- Army commanders filed false reports claiming 128 "Viet Cong" had been killed; the true death toll and civilian character of the victims were concealed.
- Survivors and soldiers' accounts eventually reached journalist *Seymour Hersh*, who broke the story in November 1969.
- Public outrage erupted; anti-war protests intensified; the Army launched an investigation and court-martial proceedings.

### Trials and Convictions
- *William Calley* was court-martialed and convicted of murder; he was sentenced to life imprisonment, though later pardoned.
- Thirteen other soldiers were initially charged; most were acquitted or their charges were dismissed; only *Calley* served significant time.
- *Hugh Thompson Jr.* and his crew received death threats from the public and military; they were vindicated only decades later as moral exemplars.

### Consequences
- My Lai became synonymous with American military atrocities and war crimes; it irreversibly damaged U.S. credibility on the world stage.
- The exposure accelerated the anti-war movement and contributed to American withdrawal from Vietnam.
- The massacre prompted international discussions of war crimes, the laws of war, and military accountability.

## context

My Lai crystallized American doubts about the Vietnam War. The massacre itself, though horrific, was not unique in warfare; what was unique was its exposure in a democratic society with a critical press. The photographs and testimony showed that American soldiers—ordinary conscripts—were capable of systematic atrocity when dehumanizing ideology, poor discipline, and unclear rules of engagement aligned. My Lai demolished the myth of American moral superiority and turned the war from an abstract geopolitical conflict into a visceral moral catastrophe. For many Americans, the massacre represented the corruption of military purpose and the futility of an unjust war; it remains a touchstone of military ethics education and a cautionary tale about how institutional failure can license individual atrocity.
