# Great Plague of London

## summary

The *Great Plague of London* was a major outbreak of bubonic plague in 1665–1666 that killed an estimated 70,000 to 100,000 people in London, roughly 15–20% of the city's population. It was the last major plague pandemic to strike England and occurred just before the Great Fire of London, which many believed had cleansed the city of the disease.

## you gotta know

- The final major bubonic plague epidemic in England, occurring during the reign of King Charles II when much of the court and nobility had fled the city.
- Spread primarily through flea-infested rats in crowded tenements and trade districts; mortality was highest among the poor who could not flee or isolate.
- Killed an estimated 70,000–100,000 people (some sources suggest higher figures), making it one of the deadliest disasters in English history relative to population.
- Peak death toll in September 1665 exceeded 7,000 per week according to bills of mortality kept by parish clerks—likely an undercount of true deaths.
- Prompted the adoption of quarantine measures: houses of infected persons were marked with a red cross and sealed; entire families were locked inside and guards stationed outside.
- Samuel Pepys documented the plague in his famous diary, recording the social breakdown, the stench of mass burial pits, and the courage and desperation of those who remained.
- The Great Fire of London in September 1666 is sometimes credited with ending the plague by destroying rat habitats, though disease had already begun declining.
