# Assassination of Julius Caesar

## summary

On March 15, 44 BCE (the Ides of March), *Julius Caesar* was stabbed to death in *Rome* by a group of senators including his trusted ally *Marcus Brutus*. The assassination was intended to preserve the *Roman Republic* from Caesar's apparent drift toward monarchy, but instead it triggered civil war and hastened the republic's collapse, leading directly to the *Roman Empire* under Caesar's heir *Octavian*.

## you gotta know

### The Murder
- Committed on March 15, 44 BCE, in the Theatre of Pompey in Rome during a meeting of the Senate.
- Perpetrators: a conspiracy of roughly 60 senators, most famously including *Marcus Brutus*, whom Caesar trusted as a friend.
- Caesar was struck approximately 23 times by daggers wielded by the conspirators.
- Caesar's last words, traditionally rendered as *"Et tu, Brute?"* ("And you, Brutus?"), capture his shock at Brutus's betrayal.

### Political Motivation
- The conspirators feared Caesar's accumulation of power and his apparent moves toward kingship, which violated republican ideals.
- Caesar had recently been declared dictator for life, an unprecedented honor that alarmed the senatorial class.
- Brutus, despite his personal affection for Caesar, was persuaded by the conspiracy's leader, *Gaius Cassius*, to join the plot.
- The conspirators believed themselves to be saving the Republic from tyranny.

### Consequences
- Contrary to the conspirators' hopes, Caesar's death did not restore the Republic but triggered the Second Civil War.
- *Mark Antony* and Caesar's adopted heir *Octavian* (later *Augustus*) joined forces to hunt down Caesar's assassins.
- Brutus was defeated and committed suicide; most of the conspirators were killed or exiled.
- Within a decade, Octavian became Rome's sole ruler and declared himself *Augustus*, founding the *Roman Empire*.

## context

The assassination of Caesar is the archetypal political murder: noble-minded men destroying the very man they admired to save an ideal (the Republic) that they believed he threatened. The event embodies a tragic paradox: the remedy became worse than the disease. By killing Caesar to prevent monarchy, the conspirators ensured the collapse of the Republic and the rise of the Empire—the very thing they feared. The phrase "Ides of March" and the image of the betrayed leader stabbed by friends have resonated through Western literature, drama (Shakespeare's *Julius Caesar*), and political rhetoric ever since. For quiz bowl, it is inseparable from Roman history, republicanism versus monarchy, and the question of whether violence in defense of principle is justified.
