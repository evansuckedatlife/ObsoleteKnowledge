# Alexander I

## summary

*Alexander I* (1777–1825) ascended to the throne after his father Paul I's assassination and became Russia's preeminent statesman during the Napoleonic Wars. Initially an idealist influenced by Enlightenment thought, he shifted from reform-minded liberal rule to conservative consolidation after defeating Napoleon. His leadership at the Congress of Vienna (1815) shaped the post-Napoleonic order; he emerged as the arbiter of European politics and founder of the reactionary Holy Alliance.

## you gotta know

### Early Reign & Domestic Reform
- Became tsar after the overthrow of his erratic father *Paul I* in 1801; possibly aware of the conspiracy, enhancing claims of complicity.
- Appointed progressive advisors including *Mikhail Speransky*, implementing administrative reforms, legal codes, and university expansion.
- Flirted with constitutionalism and liberal governance, granting autonomy to Poland and Finland as semi-independent kingdoms within the empire.
- Gradually abandoned reforms after 1810, turning toward conservatism and Orthodox mysticism.

### The Patriotic War of 1812 & Defeat of Napoleon
- When Napoleon invaded Russia in 1812 with the Grande Armée, Alexander refused capitulation despite initial military defeats.
- The Russian strategy of retreat, scorched earth, and winter attrition devastated the French; the Grande Armée was destroyed; Napoleon was forced to retreat.
- Alexander pursued Napoleon westward, leading the coalition armies into Europe; he was instrumental in forcing Napoleon's abdication (1814).
- Re-entered Russia to become the "savior of Europe" and the continent's most influential voice in the post-war settlement.

### The Congress of Vienna & Conservative Reaction
- At the Congress of Vienna (1815), Alexander played a leading role in redrawing European boundaries and installing conservative monarchs.
- Founded the *Holy Alliance* (1815), a loose conservative coalition of European monarchs pledging to uphold Christian principles and monarchical legitimacy—effectively an anti-revolutionary alliance.
- Used Russian military power to suppress revolutions in Europe (e.g., intervention in Spain, Greece); the gendarme of the old order.
- Increasingly isolated and melancholic, he withdrew from public life in his later years, reportedly haunted by the destruction he had witnessed.

### Territorial Gains & Expansion
- Annexed Georgia (1801) and other Caucasian territories from the Ottoman and Persian empires.
- Acquired significant Polish territory through the Congress; Poland became a Russian satellite.
- Expanded influence in the Mediterranean and Near East at the expense of the Ottoman Empire.

## context

Alexander I is the paradox of progressive idealism curdled by power and trauma. He began as perhaps the most enlightened European monarch of his generation, yet ended as the architect of continental conservatism. The Patriotic War of 1812 marked the pivot: having sacrificed millions to defeat Napoleon, he lost faith in reform and embraced authoritarianism in the name of stability. The Holy Alliance, founded in idealistic language about Christian fraternity, became the instrument of reactionary suppression. His reign established Russia as the dominant European power, yet his turn toward autocracy, religious mysticism, and conservative repression deferred internal reform and sowed the seeds of later revolutionary upheaval. He represents the moment Russian progressivism caved to the weight of empire.
