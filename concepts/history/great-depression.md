# Great Depression

## summary

The Great Depression (1929–1939) was the worst economic catastrophe of the 20th century, triggered by the *stock market crash* of October 1929 and deepened by bank failures, agricultural collapse, and deflationary spirals. Unemployment reached 25% in the U.S.; production plummeted; millions lost homes and savings. The Depression ravaged the developed world, destabilized democracies, fueled fascism, and fundamentally transformed how governments understood their role in economic management.

## you gotta know

### origins and the crash

- The 1920s saw speculative euphoria in the stock market; Americans bought stocks on *margin* (borrowing to invest), inflating prices detached from underlying corporate value; the market peaked in August 1929 at unprecedented valuations.
- On *Black Tuesday* (29 October 1929), the stock market crashed catastrophically; stocks lost 40% of their value within weeks, wiping out fortunes, destroying credit, and triggering a cascade of defaults and bankruptcies.
- The crash exposed deeper weaknesses: unequal income distribution, overproduction of agricultural goods, international debt from World War I, and the *Smoot-Hawley Tariff* (1930), which raised U.S. import duties and provoked retaliation, collapsing international trade.

### the economic collapse

- *Bank failures* cascaded across America: as depositors panicked and withdrew savings, thousands of banks failed because they had loaned out more than they held in reserves; no *FDIC insurance* existed yet, so depositors lost everything.
- *Agricultural collapse* devastated farmers: commodity prices plummeted; the *Dust Bowl* (1930s) compounded agricultural misery with severe drought and soil erosion across the Great Plains, driving tenant farmers and sharecroppers westward to California.
- Industrial production fell by 50%; unemployment in the U.S. reached 25% (and higher in industrial cities); millions of families lost homes to foreclosure; *bread lines* and *Hoovervilles* (shanty towns) became symbols of American destitution.

### social and political consequences

- The Depression destabilized democracies worldwide: *Hitler* rose to power partly by promising to restore German economic vitality; *militarism* in Japan was fueled by economic desperation; authoritarian regimes offered certainty in chaos.
- In America, *Hoover*'s belief in limited government prevented aggressive federal intervention; he vetoed relief bills, believing the crisis would self-correct; voters rejected him in 1932, electing *Franklin D. Roosevelt*, who promised active government intervention.
- Roosevelt's *New Deal* (1933–39) fundamentally expanded federal power: *Work Progress Administration (WPA)* employed millions in public works; *Civilian Conservation Corps (CCC)* restored forests and built infrastructure; *Social Security* (1935) created a safety net for the elderly and unemployed.

### cultural and intellectual responses

- The Depression inspired a generation of artists, writers, and photographers to document American suffering: *Walker Evans* and *Dorothea Lange* photographed migrant workers and displaced families; *John Steinbeck*'s *The Grapes of Wrath* (1939) became the Depression's literary epitaph.
- *Woody Guthrie* and folk music emerged as the voice of working-class America; the song *"Brother, Can You Spare a Dime?"* (1931) crystallized the pathos of the breadline.
- Economists reckoned with the Depression's causes: *John Maynard Keynes* published *The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money* (1936), arguing that government spending could stimulate demand and end recessions—a revolutionary idea that transformed economic policy.

### recovery and legacy

- The Depression began lifting in the mid-1930s (though with setbacks in 1937), partly from New Deal programs and public works, and more decisively from *World War II*'s rearmament and industrial mobilization in the early 1940s.
- The Depression's political legacy was profound: it discredited laissez-faire economics in policymakers' eyes and established the expectation that government must intervene in recessions to prevent mass suffering.
- The Depression remained a benchmark of economic catastrophe; its specter haunted policymakers during the *2008 financial crisis*, prompting aggressive government intervention to prevent a repeat.

## context

The Great Depression was a watershed in modern history. It demonstrated that markets, left unregulated, could collapse catastrophically; it established that government intervention was legitimate and necessary; it gave birth to *Keynesian economics*, which dominated policy for decades. The Depression spawned the welfare state and safety-net programs that persist today. Its cultural mark was indelible: American literature, music, and photography of the era captured human resilience amid destitution. The Depression's lessons—especially about preventing bank panics and maintaining demand—shaped every major economic intervention thereafter. It also exposed democracy's fragility: the same desperation that could be channeled into New Deal support could, elsewhere, feed fascism. The Depression remains the paradigm of economic collapse and the template for crisis response.
