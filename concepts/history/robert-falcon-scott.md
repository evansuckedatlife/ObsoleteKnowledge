# Robert Falcon Scott

## summary

*Robert Falcon Scott* was a British Royal Navy officer and polar explorer whose fatal expedition to the South Pole became one of history's most haunting tales of courage, failure, and human endurance in the face of nature's extremity. Leading the Terra Nova Expedition (1910–1913), he reached the South Pole only to discover that the Norwegian Roald Amundsen had arrived first, a blow compounded by the tragedy that claimed Scott and his final companions' lives during the return journey. His journals and the romantic Victorian narrative surrounding his expedition transformed defeat into a profound myth of British fortitude and sacrifice.

## you gotta know

- Born in 1868 into the English gentry; joined the Royal Navy and made a reputation as a competent officer with experience in surveying and torpedo work before turning to polar exploration.
- Led the *Discovery Expedition* (1901–1904) to Antarctica, reaching a latitude farther south than any previous expedition and establishing a scientific base; this success made him the natural choice to lead the assault on the Pole.
- Planned the *Terra Nova Expedition* (1910–1913) to beat Amundsen to the South Pole, employing both sledges pulled by Siberian ponies and man-hauling—a strategy inferior to Amundsen's dog teams.
- Reached the South Pole in January 1912, only to find Amundsen's flag already planted and a letter confirming the Norwegian's victory, a devastating psychological blow to Scott's expedition.
- Died with his final companions (Edward Wilson and Henry Bowers) in their tent approximately eleven miles south of One Ton Depot during the desperate return journey in March 1912, the result of starvation, exhaustion, and frostbite.
- Left journals documenting the expedition's decline, including his final entries, which were found with his body and published posthumously, making him one of history's most eloquent witnesses to human limitation and dignity under duress.
- Became a symbol of British imperial courage, celebrated not for his defeat but for his steadfastness in facing it—a narrative shift that transformed polar exploration into a story of character rather than conquest.
