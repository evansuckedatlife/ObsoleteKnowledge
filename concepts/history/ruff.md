# Ruff

## summary

The *ruff* is an elaborate circular or standing collar made of stiffened linen, worn from the late 16th through early 17th centuries, most famously during the Elizabethan era. Created using starch and intricate pleating, the ruff transformed the humble neckline into the Renaissance's most visible and expensive display of status, skill, and fashion authority.

## you gotta know

- Constructed from fine linen pleated into precise geometric folds and stiffened with starch—originally imported expensive starch made the ruff a luxury item accessible only to nobility.
- Sizes varied dramatically: modest ruffs were 2–3 inches wide, while extreme versions reached 12 inches or more in diameter, requiring wire or bone supports to maintain shape.
- The standing ruff stood vertically around the neck; the falling ruff draped downward—both styles framed the face and dominated the wearer's appearance.
- Time-intensive to create: pleating and starching a ruff could take hours, and wearing one required careful positioning and frequent adjustments to prevent sagging or wrinkling.
- Symbolized wealth and status—the finest ruffs were elaborately blackwork-embroidered or trimmed with expensive lace, and a ruff's condition signaled its wearer's social rank.
- Associated with Spanish court fashion before spreading throughout Europe; became the quintessential element of Elizabethan portrait formality.
- Declined as fashion shifted toward more natural necklines in the 1620s–1630s, eventually replaced by the falling band collar.

## connections

- [[elizabeth-i]] — English queen whose portraits prominently featured elaborate ruffs as symbols of royal authority.
- [[farthingale]] — complementary garment worn simultaneously in formal Elizabethan dress.
- [[codpiece]] — contemporary masculine statement piece that shared the Renaissance ethos of ostentatious display.
- [[starch]] — essential material whose introduction to Europe made ruffs economically possible.
