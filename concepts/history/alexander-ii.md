# Alexander II

## summary

*Alexander II* (1818–1881), known as the *Tsar Liberator*, inherited a humbled empire from his father Nicholas I and embarked on the greatest period of Russian reform since Peter the Great. The Emancipation Edict of 1861 freed the serfs, transforming rural Russian society; he followed with sweeping reforms of the judiciary, military, and administration. His reign was progressive but unstable: the reforms energized both optimists and radicals, culminating in his assassination by revolutionary terrorists in 1881, which ended the reform era.

## you gotta know

### The Emancipation Edict of 1861
- Issued the *Emancipation Edict* (1861), freeing approximately 23 million serfs—the single most consequential act of his reign.
- Serfs received land and modest compensation schemes; however, redemption payments were onerous, and the transition was chaotic.
- The edict delegitimized the landed gentry but failed to fully integrate freed peasants into the economy, creating a new class of impoverished rural laborers.
- Inspired reform movements across Europe; he was celebrated as a progressive monarch.

### The Great Reforms of the 1860s
- Instituted legal reforms (1864): introduced an independent judiciary, trial by jury, and secular law codes—revolutionary for autocratic Russia.
- Reformed the military (1874): replaced feudal conscription with universal conscription and modernized officer training.
- Decentralized provincial administration through the establishment of *zemstva* (local councils), introducing limited self-governance at the provincial level.
- Reformed education: expanded university access and eased censorship, creating intellectual ferment and student activism.

### Foreign Policy & Territorial Expansion
- Lost the Crimean War (1853–1856) but recovered diplomatically and militarily under his rule.
- Fought the *Russo-Turkish War* (1877–1878), defeating the Ottomans and supporting Balkan independence movements.
- Gained territory in Central Asia and the Caucasus, expanding the empire's footprint.
- Sold *Alaska* to the United States (1867), ceding North American territory but gaining funds for domestic development.

### The Radical Turn & Assassination
- Reforms unleashed radical movements: nihilists, socialists, and anarchists proliferated, unsatisfied with gradual change.
- Faced numerous assassination attempts, including plots by the revolutionary organization *Narodnaya Volya* (People's Will).
- Attempted limited counter-reforms in the 1870s (the *Reaktsiya*, or Reaction), which provoked further radicalism.
- Assassinated by Narodnaya Volya terrorists on March 13, 1881, in St. Petersburg; his death marked the end of the reform era.

## context

Alexander II is the tragic reformer: a monarch who attempted to reconcile autocracy with modernization and paid with his life. The Emancipation Edict freed the serfs but failed to create a stable, prosperous peasantry; the Great Reforms of the 1860s introduced quasi-democratic institutions but threatened the autocratic system itself. He embodied the contradiction at Russia's heart: the empire needed reform to survive the modern world, yet reform threatened the very basis of absolute power. His assassination in 1881 shattered elite confidence in gradual change; his successor *Alexander III* responded with reactionary consolidation, closing the window that had briefly opened. Had he lived and continued reform, Russian history might have unfolded differently; instead, his death inaugurated three decades of stagnation that led to revolution. He remains Russia's most progressive tsar—yet his progressivism was incomplete and fragile.
