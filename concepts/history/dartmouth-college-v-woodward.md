# Dartmouth College v. Woodward

## summary

*Dartmouth College v. Woodward* (1819) is a landmark Supreme Court decision establishing that contracts, including corporate charters, are protected from state interference by the Constitution's Contract Clause. Chief Justice John Marshall's ruling shielded private corporations from state legislative retaliation and became foundational to American corporate law.

## you gotta know

- Arose when New Hampshire's legislature attempted to convert Dartmouth College from a private to a public institution by amending its charter without consent.
- Chief Justice *John Marshall* wrote the majority opinion, holding that a corporate charter is a contract protected by the *Contract Clause* of the Constitution.
- Marshall's reasoning: once a charter is granted, the state cannot impair it through subsequent legislation, even for seemingly public purposes.
- The decision applied constitutional protection to private corporations, not just individuals and governments.
- Established the principle that corporations possess legal personhood and constitutional rights.
- Became a bulwark for business against state regulatory authority in the 19th century, limiting democratic control over corporations.
