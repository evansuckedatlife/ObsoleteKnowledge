# Regents v. Bakke

## summary

*Regents of the University of California v. Bakke* (1978) addressed affirmative action in university admissions for the first time, ruling 5–4 that while race-conscious admissions programs are permissible to remedy past discrimination and achieve diversity, rigid numerical quotas violate the *14th Amendment*'s equal protection guarantee. Allan Bakke, a white applicant rejected from UC Davis Medical School, challenged an admissions program that reserved 16 of 100 seats for minority students; the fragmented decision allowed race-conscious review but condemned quotas.

## you gotta know

- *Allan Bakke*, a white applicant, was rejected twice by UC Davis; he alleged he would have been admitted without the school's minority admissions program.
- Justice *Lewis Powell* cast the deciding vote and wrote a plurality opinion holding that schools may consider race as one factor among many, but not as a rigid quota.
- The Court ruled quotas unconstitutional but endorsed the *Harvard College* model of holistic admissions where race could be weighed in context.
- Bakke was ordered admitted; the decision fractured the Court—no single rationale commanded a majority.
- For decades, universities used the decision to justify race-conscious admissions while avoiding formal quotas.
- Opened 40 years of litigation over affirmative action, culminating in *Students for Fair Admissions v. Harvard* (2023), which restricted race-conscious admissions further.
