# Toga

## summary

The *toga* is a draped woolen garment worn by male Roman citizens as the formal dress of state, consisting of a large piece of cloth (often 6 yards or more) wrapped around the body in a distinctive asymmetrical configuration. More than mere clothing, the toga represented citizenship, legal status, and civic authority, making it one of ancient Rome's most politically significant garments.

## you gotta know

- Worn exclusively by male citizens; enslaved people, non-citizens, and non-Romans were legally prohibited from wearing togas, making it a marker of political status and belonging.
- Required a specific wrapping technique that took time and skill—most men employed a slave to drape the toga correctly, as the many folds had to be arranged precisely.
- The *toga pratica* (plain white toga) was standard civic dress; the *toga candida* (bright white) was worn by political candidates; the *toga picta* (embroidered purple) was reserved for generals and emperors.
- Worn over a *tunica* (tunic), and the arrangement could indicate social rank, military status, or religious office through variations in color, trim, and draping pattern.
- Impractical for labor or warfare: the flowing cloth restricted movement, which is why working-class Romans and soldiers preferred simpler tunics and why wearing a toga signaled freedom from manual work.
- The toga represented Roman identity so strongly that Romans referred to themselves as *togati* (the toga-wearing people) in contrast to barbarian neighbors who wore pants and shorter garments.
- Declined in the 4th–5th centuries CE as practical tunics and barbarian-influenced clothing replaced the toga; the garment disappeared from common use by the early medieval period.
