# Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo

## summary

The *Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo* (1848) ended the *Mexican-American War*, transferring roughly half of Mexico's territory to the *United States* in exchange for a payment of $15 million. The treaty redrew the North American map, extending U.S. borders to the Pacific Ocean and making the U.S. a continental power. For Mexico, the treaty was a national catastrophe that ceded *California*, *Nevada*, *Utah*, most of *Arizona* and *New Mexico*, and confirmed U.S. control of *Texas*, fundamentally altering its future.

## you gotta know

### War & Context
- The *Mexican-American War* (1846–1848) was fought over territorial disputes and U.S. expansionism; American forces invaded Mexico and occupied Mexico City.
- Mexico, militarily defeated and politically fractured, was forced to negotiate; the U.S. imposed harsh terms.
- Signed on *February 2, 1848*, in the small village of Guadalupe Hidalgo outside Mexico City.

### Territorial Transfers
- Mexico ceded approximately 525,000 square miles—*California*, *Nevada*, *Utah*, most of *Arizona* and *New Mexico*—roughly 55% of its territory.
- The *Rio Grande* became the boundary between Texas and Mexico; the border defined in the treaty remains largely unchanged today.
- The U.S. acquired vast gold deposits (California Gold Rush, 1849) and strategic Pacific coast territory.

### Payment & Indemnity
- The U.S. paid Mexico $15 million (roughly $500 million in modern dollars) as compensation, a token payment for such vast lands.
- The U.S. assumed $3.25 million in claims by American citizens against Mexico, further reducing net payment.
- The payment did not offset Mexico's loss; Mexico was financially crippled for decades.

### Rights of Mexicans & Manifest Destiny
- Roughly 80,000 Mexicans living in the ceded territories were given a choice: relocate to Mexico or become U.S. citizens.
- Most remained, becoming a subordinate ethnic minority in the newly American Southwest; their property rights were often violated.
- The treaty exemplified U.S. *Manifest Destiny*: expansion across the continent, regardless of indigenous and Mexican claims.

## context

The Treaty of Guadalupe Hidalgo is a defining moment in U.S. continental expansion and Mexican national trauma. For the U.S., the treaty represented the fulfilment of Manifest Destiny—territorial expansion from Atlantic to Pacific, justifying westward conquest in the name of civilisational progress. The acquisition of the Southwest and California, with their mineral wealth and strategic value, made the U.S. a transcontinental power and set the stage for 20th-century dominance. For Mexico, the treaty was catastrophic: the loss of half its territory, the humiliation of military defeat, and a legacy of grievance and asymmetry that haunts U.S.-Mexican relations today. The ceded territories, rich with resources and later economic potential, became the American Southwest; the wealth extracted from those lands (gold, cattle, oil) flowed to the U.S., not Mexico. Mexican resentment at the treaty persisted for generations; it became a touchstone of nationalist feeling and a reminder of American imperialism. The treaty also precipitated the displacement and marginalisation of Native Americans in the West, as the U.S. subsequently seized tribal lands. Today, the border defined by Guadalupe Hidalgo remains the front line of U.S.-Mexican relations, shaped entirely by the treaty's outcome.
