# Germaine Greer

## summary

*Germaine Greer* (born 1939) is an Australian-British writer and cultural critic whose *The Female Eunuch* (1970) became a manifesto of second-wave feminism. Known for her sharp wit and provocative analysis of female sexuality, marriage, and motherhood, she brought irreverent humor and frank talk about women's bodies and desire to feminist discourse.

## you gotta know

- Wrote *The Female Eunuch*, arguing that marriage, domesticity, and patriarchal sexuality have left women emotionally and sexually dead, and that women must reclaim their bodies and desires.
- Challenged the idea that marriage and motherhood are women's natural or necessary destiny; advocated for women's independence and pleasure.
- Appeared regularly on television and in popular media, making feminism visible and accessible to mainstream audiences with her intelligence and charisma.
- Wrote extensively on female sexuality, sexuality, and the cultural representation of women's bodies, always with attention to power and authenticity.
- Later work addressed aging, ecology, and global women's struggles, expanding beyond individual liberation to broader political analysis.
- Remained controversial throughout her career, known for iconoclasm and willingness to challenge taboos and even progressive orthodoxies.

## connections

- [[the-female-eunuch]] — her landmark text attacking the subjugation of female sexuality and desire.
- [[second-wave-feminism]] — she shaped the movement's public voice and cultural visibility.
- [[sexuality]] — she centered women's sexual autonomy and pleasure as central to liberation.
- [[womens-liberation]] — she framed it as requiring personal and cultural transformation, not just legal reform.
