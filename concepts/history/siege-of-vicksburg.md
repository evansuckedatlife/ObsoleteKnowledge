## summary

The Siege of Vicksburg (May 22–July 4, 1863) was a prolonged Union campaign to capture the fortified city of Vicksburg, Mississippi, a Confederate stronghold controlling the middle Mississippi River. General Ulysses S. Grant, employing bold maneuvers and encirclement, cut off the city and besieged it for 47 days while Confederate General John C. Pemberton held out. After weeks of starvation and artillery bombardment, Pemberton surrendered on July 4, 1863, granting the Union complete control of the Mississippi River. Combined with the Battle of Gettysburg (concluded the same day), Vicksburg marked the symbolic and strategic turning point in the war.

## you gotta know

### Background
- Vicksburg was the last major Confederate stronghold on the Mississippi River; Union control would split the Confederacy in two.
- Grant attempted multiple approaches before executing a bold plan: crossing the river south of the city, defeating Confederate forces between the city and Jackson, then surrounding Vicksburg.
- Pemberton was ordered to hold the city at all costs, despite being outnumbered and cut off from reinforcement.

### The Siege
- Grant positioned his army in a semicircle around Vicksburg and interdicted all supply lines.
- Union artillery bombarded the city relentlessly; civilians and soldiers endured hunger, disease, and constant shelling.
- Pemberton attempted sorties and appeals for relief; all failed. Confederate forces under Joseph E. Johnston could not break the Union siege.

### Aftermath
- Pemberton surrendered unconditionally on July 4; Grant paroled the soldiers rather than establish a prisoner camp.
- Union forces secured approximately 30,000 prisoners and substantial supplies and artillery.
- Lincoln declared the Mississippi River "unveiled," completing the waterway's control by the North.

## context

Vicksburg broke the Confederacy's back not just militarily but logistically. The South's western territories were severed from the east; supplies, reinforcements, and coordinated strategy became impossible. Grant's success at Vicksburg—his aggressive maneuvering, his logistical daring, his willingness to live off the land—marked him as the strategic innovator the Union had been seeking. The dual victories of Gettysburg and Vicksburg on the same day became a rallying point for Northern morale and a moment when Confederate defeat became mathematically inevitable.
