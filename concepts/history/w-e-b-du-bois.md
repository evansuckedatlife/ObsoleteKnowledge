# W.E.B. Du Bois

## summary

*W.E.B. Du Bois* was an intellectual giant of the 20th century — a sociologist, historian, civil rights activist, and founding member of the *NAACP* whose concept of "double consciousness" defined the African-American experience. His prolific writing and organizing work made him a towering presence in Black thought and activism, from the early 1900s until his death in Ghana in 1963.

## you gotta know

- Founded the *Niagara Movement* (1905), the first major Black-led civil rights organization, challenging Booker T. Washington's accommodationist approach.
- Helped found the *NAACP* in 1909 and served as editor of its magazine *The Crisis* for 24 years, using it to reach and mobilize the Black community.
- Wrote *The Souls of Black Folk* (1903), a seminal collection of essays introducing the concept of "double consciousness" — the Black American's dual identity as both Black and American.
- Coined the term "the Talented Tenth" to describe the educated Black elite he believed would lead racial advancement.
- Spent decades as a sociology professor and produced groundbreaking empirical work on Black communities, institutions, and social conditions.
- Moved to the political left over his lifetime, becoming increasingly critical of capitalism and aligned with socialist thought in his later years.
- Emigrated to Ghana in 1961 at age 93, rejecting American racism, and died there in 1963.

## connections

- [[booker-t-washington]] — ideological opponent on strategy for Black advancement (accommodation vs. agitation).
- [[marcus-garvey]] — competitor for influence; Du Bois was skeptical of Garvey's separatism.
- [[malcolm-x]] — intellectual successor to Du Bois's pan-African and separatist thinking.
- [[martin-luther-king-jr]] — inheritor of Du Bois's institutional civil rights legacy through the NAACP.
