# James Madison

## summary

James Madison (1751–1836) was the fourth President of the United States and the primary architect of the U.S. Constitution, earning the title "Father of the Constitution." A political philosopher and legislator, he shaped the foundational documents of American democracy—the Constitution and the *Bill of Rights*—and stewarded the republic through the *War of 1812*. His intellectual rigor and political pragmatism made him a pivotal bridge between the revolutionary generation and the early republic.

## you gotta know

- Virginia statesman and theorist of republican government; studied political philosophy and advocated for a federal system balancing state and national power.
- Orchestrated the *Constitutional Convention* (1787), authoring the *Virginia Plan* which became the template for the Constitution; his *Federalist Papers* (with Hamilton and Jay) were the definitive arguments for ratification.
- Served in Congress and drafted the *Bill of Rights* (1789), the first ten amendments to the Constitution protecting individual liberties; essential to securing state ratification.
- Served as Secretary of State under Thomas Jefferson (1801–1809); helped negotiate the *Louisiana Purchase* (1803), doubling the nation's territory.
- Elected president in 1808; his presidency (1809–1817) was dominated by the *War of 1812*, a conflict with Britain over impressment of American sailors, trade restrictions, and frontier tensions.
- The War of 1812 ended in a stalemate; the peace treaty restored pre-war boundaries without resolving core disputes, but Americans celebrated it as a victory that vindicated independence.
- Advocated for strict separation of church and state in Virginia; his *Virginia Statute for Religious Freedom* (1786) became a model for First Amendment protections.
- Wrote extensively on the origins of factions and the dangers of tyrannical majorities; his *Federalist No. 10* remains the foundational essay on controlling factionalism in democracies.
