# Ain't I a Woman?

## summary

Sojourner Truth's extemporaneous speech delivered at an 1851 women's rights convention in Akron, Ohio, fused abolitionist and feminist rhetoric by testifying to her own strength and labor as an enslaved woman, challenging both racism and sexism with searing directness. The speech, recalled by Frances Dana Gage and published under the title *"Ain't I a Woman?"*, became a foundational text of Black feminism and remains one of the most powerful statements of intersectional identity in American history.

## you gotta know

- Delivered at the *Akron Women's Rights Convention* in Ohio in *May 1851*.
- Truth's signature refrain: *"Ain't I a woman?"* — asserting her humanity against the erasure of Black women from both movements.
- She catalogs her own labor: *"I have plowed and planted, and gathered into barns, and no man could head me!"* — proving physical capability deemed male.
- Truth challenges the paternalism of the cult of true womanhood: *"And ain't I a woman?"*
- The speech was reconstructed (not verbatim) by Frances Dana Gage and published with dialect speech that may not reflect Truth's actual diction.
- Truth addresses both white women (questioning their understanding of Black women's experience) and white men (their justifications for oppression).
- The speech became central to 20th-century Black feminist theory, particularly after scholars critiqued the "Gage version" and sought to recover Truth's likely original words.
