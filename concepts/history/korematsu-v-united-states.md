# Korematsu v. United States

## summary

Korematsu v. United States (1944) upheld the constitutionality of Japanese American internment during World War II, a decision now widely condemned as one of the Supreme Court's greatest failures. Fred Korematsu, a Japanese American citizen, violated an exclusion order forcing persons of Japanese descent to leave the West Coast and report to internment camps. He was convicted and appealed, claiming the government's racial classification violated the Fifth Amendment's due process protections. The Court, in a 6-3 decision, upheld the internment, applying *strict scrutiny* (its highest level of review for governmental classifications) yet still deferring to military necessity during wartime. The decision stands as a monument to the dangers of allowing executive power to trump civil liberties in the name of security; decades later, the government's own documents revealed that military officers had falsified the evidence of danger that justified internment.

## you gotta know

### The Orders and Internment
- *Executive Order 9066* (February 1942): President Roosevelt authorized military commanders to exclude persons of Japanese ancestry from the West Coast.
- *Mandatory exclusion and internment*: Japanese Americans (including U.S. citizens) were ordered to leave their homes, sell property at distressed prices, and report to internment camps in remote locations (Wyoming, Utah, Colorado, etc.).
- *No individual assessment*: All persons of Japanese descent were treated as threats, without regard to citizenship, loyalty, or individual conduct; no criminal charges or trials were required.

### Korematsu's Challenge
- Fred Korematsu, a U.S. citizen and Nisei (second-generation), defied the exclusion order and remained in the San Francisco Bay Area.
- He was arrested, tried, and convicted of violating the exclusion order.
- He appealed on constitutional grounds: the exclusion and internment violated the Fifth Amendment's Due Process Clause because they were based solely on race.

### The Court's Holding
- Justice Hugo Black wrote for the majority: the exclusion order was constitutional because it was justified by *military necessity* during wartime.
- The Court applied *strict scrutiny* (its highest standard of review for race-based classifications) but upheld the classification.
- *"Pressing public necessity may sometimes justify the existence of such restrictions; racial antagonism never can."* — Yet the decision treated the "military judgment" that Japanese Americans posed a security threat as sufficient to override civil liberties.
- The opinion did not address whether the internment camps themselves (as opposed to exclusion) were constitutional; that distinction was murky and proved significant later.

### The Dissents
- Justice Owen Roberts: the exclusion order and internment camps were inseparable; internment was not justified even by military necessity; the order was racial discrimination pure and simple.
- Justice Frank Murphy: *"Individuals must not be left impoverished of their constitutional rights on a plea of military necessity that has neither substance nor support."*
- Justice Jackson: once courts defer to military judgment on constitutional matters, there is no check on executive power; better to admit the emergency is beyond law.

### Evidence of Government Misconduct
- *Yasui v. United States* and *Hirabayashi v. United States*, decided the same day as Korematsu, upheld the exclusion on narrower grounds (avoiding the internment question).
- Decades later (1980s–1990s), historians and legal scholars discovered that the government's own military intelligence indicated no credible threat from Japanese Americans; the War Department had suppressed dissenting memos.
- In 1983, a federal court granted Korematsu a new trial based on government misconduct, and he was exonerated.

### Doctrinal Legacy and Repudiation
- Korematsu remains formally on the books as binding precedent, but the Court, Congress, and the American conscience have repudiated it.
- In 2018, the Court explicitly overruled Korematsu (in *Trump v. Hawaii*, upholding a travel ban targeting Muslim-majority countries), announcing that Korematsu was wrong.
- The case is taught as a cautionary tale: even *strict scrutiny* is not an absolute bar to racial discrimination when courts defer to executive claims of security necessity.

## context

Korematsu v. United States is the Supreme Court's greatest moral failure in the modern era. At the time, the decision was controversial; dissenting Justices and civil rights advocates argued that military necessity cannot justify wholesale racial discrimination. But the majority, deferring to executive judgment during wartime, upheld internment. Only after the war, when documents revealed that military leaders had deliberately misrepresented the evidence of threat, did the full extent of the injustice become clear. The case embodies three crucial lessons: (1) wartime claims of necessity are dangerous to civil liberties and should be scrutinized, not deferred to; (2) even "strict scrutiny" of racial classifications can crumble if courts abdicate review; (3) historical distance and evidence can expose injustices that seemed justified at the time. Korematsu has become so infamous that the government itself disowned it, making it unique in constitutional law—a precedent no one defends. The case is cited not as authority but as a warning: when does protecting national security justify suspending civil rights? Korematsu shows that the answer is not "never," but that the bar for doing so should be nearly insurmountable, and that courts must scrutinize executive claims of necessity, not accept them on faith.
