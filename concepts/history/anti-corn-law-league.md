# Anti-Corn Law League

## summary

The **Anti-Corn Law League** (1839–1846) was a mass political movement that successfully campaigned for the repeal of Britain's *Corn Laws*—tariffs on imported grain that artificially raised bread prices and protected landowners' interests at the expense of workers and industrial employers. Founded by Manchester merchants *Richard Cobden* and *John Bright*, the League became the nineteenth century's most effective pressure group, employing mass petitions, public lectures, and electoral campaigns to mobilize middle-class opinion and eventually split the Conservative Party. The victory of free trade principle and the repeal of the Corn Laws in 1846 marked a triumph of industrial and commercial interests over agricultural privilege.

## you gotta know

- The *Corn Laws*, enacted after the Napoleonic Wars to protect British grain farmers from foreign competition, kept bread prices artificially high and made it unaffordable for workers; repeal was a working-class demand since the 1820s.
- *Richard Cobden* and *John Bright*, radical manufacturers from Manchester, founded the League in 1839; they argued that free trade would benefit all classes, lower prices, and increase prosperity.
- The League organized on an unprecedented scale: public meetings, petition drives (the 1845 petition gathered over 3 million signatures), and a sophisticated electoral machine that fielded candidates.
- *Robert Peel*, initially a protectionist, was convinced by arguments of economic necessity and the Irish Famine (1845–46) that the Corn Laws had to be repealed; he brought repeal to Parliament in 1846.
- Peel's decision split the Conservative Party; protectionist Tories, led by *Benjamin Disraeli*, denounced him as a traitor; the party fracture left the Liberals dominant for decades.
- The repeal of the Corn Laws in 1846 ushered in an era of British free trade and commercial expansion, aligning policy with industrial rather than agricultural interests.

## connections

- [[chartism]] — overlapping working-class grievances and supporters; Chartists viewed grain prices as an economic justice issue.
- [[robert-peel]] — Conservative Prime Minister who, despite initial opposition, repealed the Corn Laws in response to pressure and the Irish Famine.
- [[great-reform-act-of-1832]] — the expanded middle-class franchise created by the Act provided the electoral base for the League.
- [[british-abolitionism]] — parallel moral and political movement; both mobilized middle-class activism and public opinion.
- [[free-trade]] — the League was the vanguard of the free-trade movement in Britain, establishing it as the dominant economic philosophy.
