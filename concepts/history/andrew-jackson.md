# Andrew Jackson

## summary

Andrew Jackson (1767–1845) was the 7th President of the United States and the dominant political figure of the 1820s–1830s. A military hero and populist outsider, he reshaped the presidency and the Democratic Party by championing the "common man" against the elite. His presidency was marked by the expansion of executive power, the forced removal of Native Americans, and the deepening sectional crisis over slavery and states' rights.

## you gotta know

- Military fame rested on his victory at the Battle of New Orleans (1815) during the War of 1812; became a national hero and the symbol of American frontier individualism.
- Elected president in 1828 after the controversial *election of 1824*, in which he lost to John Quincy Adams despite a plurality of votes; Adams's appointment as secretary of state to Henry Clay sparked the "Corrupt Bargain" narrative that fueled Jackson's populist challenge.
- Championed *Jacksonian Democracy*—the belief that power should flow to ordinary citizens and that the federal government's elites were corrupt; mass participation in politics surged during his era.
- Signed the *Indian Removal Act* (1830), which forced thousands of Native Americans, particularly the Cherokees, Creeks, and Seminoles, westward on the *Trail of Tears*; one of the darkest chapters in American history, resulting in thousands of deaths.
- Clashed with South Carolina over the *Nullification Crisis* (1832–1833)—the state's refusal to enforce federal tariff laws, claiming states had the right to nullify federal laws; Jackson's firm response asserted federal supremacy but heightened sectional tensions.
- Dismantled the *Second Bank of the United States* by vetoing its recharter (1832) and removing federal deposits; populist anti-bank sentiment drove this, though the move destabilized the economy.
- Pioneered the *spoils system* (rewarding political supporters with government jobs), corrupting the civil service but cementing party loyalty as a political principle.
