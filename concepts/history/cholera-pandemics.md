# Cholera Pandemics

## summary

*Cholera pandemics* refer to seven major waves of cholera epidemic that spread globally from 1817 onward, killing millions and fundamentally transforming public health and epidemiology. The disease, caused by the bacterium *Vibrio cholerae*, spread through contaminated water and killed by rapid dehydration; the pandemics were engines of the modern public health movement.

## you gotta know

- Caused by the bacterium *Vibrio cholerae*, transmitted through contaminated water supplies; kills through rapid secretory diarrhea and dehydration.
- The first pandemic (1817–1824) began in the Ganges Delta and spread across Asia, killing millions; subsequent waves in 1829, 1852, 1863, 1881, 1899, and 1961–1975 propagated globally.
- The 1831–1832 European pandemic killed an estimated 100,000–200,000 people across the continent and sparked panic-driven social upheaval and riots.
- John Snow's investigation of the 1854 London outbreak—tracing deaths to the Broad Street pump—pioneered epidemiological detective work and proved the waterborne transmission route.
- Unlike plague, cholera was associated with poverty and poor sanitation; urban centers with inadequate sewage systems suffered catastrophic mortality.
- Prompted the creation of modern water treatment systems, sewage infrastructure, and international disease reporting (foundations of epidemiology and public health).
- The seventh pandemic (1961–1975 and ongoing) is caused by the *El Tor* biotype, which is less virulent but more transmissible than earlier strains.

## connections

- [[john-snow]] — his epidemiological breakthrough during the 1854 outbreak established the scientific method in disease investigation.
- [[epidemiology]] — cholera pandemics were the crucible in which modern epidemiology was forged.
- [[public-health-history]] — waterborne disease surveillance, sanitation reform, and international health coordination all trace to cholera responses.
- [[microbiology]] — cholera research led to germ theory acceptance and culture-based diagnostics.
- [[industrialization-and-disease]] — urbanization and crowded tenements created conditions for cholera's spread.
