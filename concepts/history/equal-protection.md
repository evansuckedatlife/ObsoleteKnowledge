# Equal Protection

## summary

The Equal Protection Clause, part of *Section 1* of the Fourteenth Amendment (1868), prohibits states from denying any person "the equal protection of the laws." This clause has become the constitutional foundation for striking down laws and government actions that discriminate on the basis of race, gender, national origin, and other suspect classifications. The Supreme Court has developed a multi-tiered framework (*strict scrutiny*, *intermediate scrutiny*, and *rational basis* review) to determine when government classifications violate equal protection.

## you gotta know

### the clause and its origin
- The exact text: "nor shall any State ... deny to any person within its jurisdiction the equal protection of the laws."
- Part of the Fourteenth Amendment, ratified in 1868 during Reconstruction to protect the rights of freed slaves.
- Applies only to state action, not purely private discrimination (the state action doctrine).

### the three-tier scrutiny framework
- *Strict scrutiny* (highest protection): applies to classifications based on race, national origin, and fundamental rights. The government must show a *compelling government interest* and that the law is *narrowly tailored* to achieve it. Almost always strikes down the law.
- *Intermediate scrutiny* (medium protection): applies to gender and other quasi-suspect classifications. The government must show an *important governmental objective* and that the classification is *substantially related* to that objective.
- *Rational basis review* (lowest protection): applies to all other classifications (e.g., age, wealth). The law need only be *rationally related* to a *legitimate government interest*. Rarely strikes down laws.

### landmark cases
- *Plessy v. Ferguson* (1896): upheld "separate but equal" under equal protection (overturned in 1954).
- *Brown v. Board of Education* (1954): struck down racial segregation in schools under strict scrutiny.
- *Loving v. Virginia* (1967): struck down miscegenation laws as violating equal protection.
- *Craig v. Boren* (1976): established that gender classifications receive intermediate scrutiny.
- *Reed v. Reed* (1971): first case striking down a law on gender equal-protection grounds.
- *Romer v. Evans* (1996): struck down a Colorado constitutional amendment discriminating against LGBTQ people under rational basis review.

### modern applications
- The clause has been applied to strike down racial discrimination, gender discrimination, disability discrimination, and discrimination based on sexual orientation and gender identity.
- Affirmative action programs are evaluated under strict scrutiny and have recently been restricted by the Supreme Court.
- The clause protects not just political equality but economic and social equality in contexts involving state action (e.g., public accommodations, voting, education).
