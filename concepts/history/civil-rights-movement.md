# Civil Rights Movement

## summary

The Civil Rights Movement (primarily 1950s–1960s, with roots in earlier decades and continuing effects) was the sustained campaign by African Americans and allies to end racial segregation and discrimination in American law and society. Led by figures like *Martin Luther King Jr.* and *Malcolm X*, it employed nonviolence, civil disobedience, litigation, and political pressure to challenge *Jim Crow* laws, desegregate institutions, and win voting rights. The movement transformed American law, politics, and culture, though structural racism and inequality persist.

## you gotta know

### roots and early organizing

- The movement had deep roots: the *NAACP* (founded 1909) challenged segregation through litigation; *A. Philip Randolph* organized the *March on Washington for Jobs and Freedom* (1941) to demand integrated defense industries; the 1950s saw mounting legal and political challenges to segregation.
- *Plessy v. Ferguson* (1896) had enshrined "separate but equal" as constitutional law; the landmark *Brown v. Board of Education* decision (1954) declared segregation in schools unconstitutional, though enforcement would be slow and fiercely resisted.
- The *Montgomery Bus Boycott* (1955–56), sparked by *Rosa Parks*' refusal to give up her seat to a white passenger, lasted 381 days and ended bus segregation; *Martin Luther King Jr.*, a young minister, emerged as a national leader of nonviolent resistance.

### nonviolent direct action

- King's strategy drew on *Mahatma Gandhi*'s philosophy of *satyagraha* (truth force); the movement employed *sit-ins* (students refusing service at segregated lunch counters), *freedom rides* (mixed-race buses challenging segregation in interstate travel), and marches to confront segregation directly.
- The *March on Washington for Jobs and Freedom* (28 August 1963) drew 250,000 people to the Lincoln Memorial; King's *"I Have a Dream"* speech became the movement's most iconic articulation, imagining a nation where people are judged by "content of character" not color.
- The *Selma to Montgomery marches* (March 1965) protested voting discrimination; the violent police response (*Bloody Sunday*, 7 March 1965, when state troopers attacked marchers) shocked Americans and catalyzed federal action on voting rights.

### legislation and federal intervention

- The *Civil Rights Act of 1964*, passed under President *Lyndon B. Johnson*, prohibited discrimination in public accommodations, employment, and education; it was the broadest civil rights legislation since Reconstruction (1865–77).
- The *Voting Rights Act of 1965* suspended literacy tests and authorized federal election monitors in jurisdictions with histories of discriminatory voting; it had immediate impact in the South, where Black voter registration surged.
- *Fair Housing Act* (1968) prohibited discrimination in housing sales and rentals, completing the legislative trilogy of 1960s civil rights law; it passed days after King's assassination.

### radicalization and black power

- By the mid-1960s, the movement fragmented: while King pursued legislative gains and nonviolence, more radical figures (*Malcolm X*, *James Carmichael*, *Huey Newton*) advocated *Black Power*—self-determination, pride in black culture, and willingness to defend against violence.
- *Malcolm X*, a spokesperson for the *Nation of Islam* until 1964, criticized King's integration goals and nonviolence as accommodationist; his assassination (21 February 1965) shocked the nation and left the movement's philosophical direction contested.
- *Urban uprisings* (Watts, 1965; Newark, Detroit, 1967; post-King assassination, 1968) reflected Black anger at continuing poverty and police brutality, even as civil rights laws passed; the *Kerner Commission* (1968) documented systemic racism and inequality.

### cultural and social impact

- The movement transformed American consciousness about race; it redefined Black identity from shame to pride; *soul music*, *black studies* programs, and African American cultural assertion flourished as expressions of Black power and dignity.
- The movement inspired other social movements: the *women's liberation* movement, *Chicano rights*, *Native American activism*, and *LGBTQ+ rights* adopted the movement's tactics and moral framework of equality and dignity.
- The movement won concrete legal and political gains: desegregation of schools (though de facto segregation persisted), integration of public accommodations, expanded Black political representation (number of Black elected officials surged), and expanded civil rights protections.

### legacy and ongoing struggles

- Legally, the civil rights laws of the 1960s transformed American society; courts struck down remaining Jim Crow statutes; schools and workplaces integrated (unevenly); Black political participation grew.
- Yet structural racism persisted: *de facto segregation* (through housing discrimination, redlining, white flight) maintained residential separation; wealth gaps remained vast; police brutality continued to disproportionately target Black Americans.
- The movement's legacy remains contested: conservatives celebrate its achievement of colorblind law; progressives note that legal equality did not produce material equality; ongoing movements (*Black Lives Matter*, voting rights activism) continue struggles initiated in the 1960s.

## context

The Civil Rights Movement stands as one of the twentieth century's defining moral and political achievements. It challenged American law and conscience directly, forcing the nation to reckon with its contradiction between democratic ideals and racial oppression. The movement's nonviolent discipline and moral clarity—embodied in King—offered a vision of American redemption; its radicalization (Black Power, urban uprisings) reflected the limits of nonviolence and gradual change in the face of structural inequality. The movement's cultural production—music, literature, visual art—remains vital. The movement's legislative victories were real but incomplete: law changed faster than society, custom, or wealth distribution. The Civil Rights Movement thus remains both monument to what organized, determined people can achieve and reminder of how deeply structural racism runs, persisting despite legal prohibition.
