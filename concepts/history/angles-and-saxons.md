# Angles and Saxons

## summary

The *Angles* and *Saxons* were Germanic peoples who migrated to Britain in the 5th and 6th centuries, progressively conquering Roman territories and establishing the foundation of English civilization. Their settlement established the seven competing Anglo-Saxon kingdoms that would dominate Britain for nearly five centuries, creating the cultural and linguistic roots of modern England.

## you gotta know

- Migrated from what is now Germany and Denmark to Roman-held Britain after the empire's departure, progressively conquering and settling the eastern and central regions.
- Established rival kingdoms including *Northumbria*, *Mercia*, *Wessex*, *Essex*, *Kent*, *Sussex*, and *East Anglia*, collectively called the *Heptarchy*.
- Gradually displaced or absorbed the native *Romano-British* population, establishing Anglo-Saxon culture, law, and the Old English language as dominant.
- Maintained pagan religious practices longer than most Germanic peoples until missionaries like *Augustine of Canterbury* (sent by Pope Gregory) converted them to Christianity beginning around 600.
- *Mercia* and *Wessex* became the dominant powers, with *Wessex* eventually uniting the kingdoms under *Alfred the Great* (reigned 871–899) and his successors.
- Developed sophisticated legal codes and administrative systems that blended Germanic custom with surviving Roman practices.
- The Anglo-Saxon period (roughly 450–1066) became the foundation of English law, language, literature, and identity, ending only with the Norman Conquest.

## connections

- [[britain]] — their primary settlement and the land they conquered and transformed.
- [[heptarchy]] — the seven rival kingdoms they established and fought over for centuries.
- [[wessex]] — the kingdom that eventually unified Anglo-Saxon Britain under *Alfred*.
- [[alfred-the-great]] — the West Saxon king who achieved the first major Anglo-Saxon unification.
- [[mercian]] — one of the dominant kingdoms that vied with Wessex for supremacy.
