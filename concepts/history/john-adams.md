## summary

John Adams (1735–1826) was the second President of the United States and a principal architect of American independence. A lawyer, political philosopher, and diplomat, he shaped the ideological foundations of the Revolution, drafted the *Declaration of Independence*, and stewarded the young republic through the Quasi-War with France. His presidency (1797–1801) was marked by tensions between federalism and anti-federalism and by his shift away from the revolutionary generation toward caution.

## you gotta know

- Massachusetts lawyer and early opponent of British authority; rallied opposition to the Stamp Act and became prominent in Massachusetts colonial politics.
- Served on the Continental Congress and was instrumental in drafting the *Declaration of Independence* (1776); articulated the philosophical case for independence based on natural rights and popular sovereignty.
- Served as a diplomat during and after the Revolutionary War; negotiated the *Treaty of Paris* (1783) ending the war; later served as minister to France, Britain, and the Dutch Republic.
- Championed the *Constitution* at the ratification convention and supported the creation of a strong federal government; advocated for separation of powers and checks and balances.
- Served as the first Vice President under George Washington (1789–1797), a role he found frustrating; became president in 1796 after Washington's retirement.
- As president, navigated the *Quasi-War*, an undeclared conflict with France (1798–1800) over neutral shipping rights, avoiding full war but destabilizing politics.
- Signed the *Alien and Sedition Acts* (1798), federal laws restricting free speech and targeting foreign-born and opposition press; these laws became symbols of Federalist overreach and were widely condemned.
- Lost his re-election bid to Thomas Jefferson in 1800; the transfer of power to the opposition party was contentious, but Adams's commitment to constitutional processes preserved the transition.
- Lived until 1826, long enough to see the republic endure; died on the 50th anniversary of the Declaration of Independence.
