# XYZ Affair

## summary

The *XYZ Affair* was a diplomatic scandal of *1797–1798* in which French diplomatic intermediaries demanded bribes from American envoys sent to negotiate peace. When President *John Adams* exposed the extortion by publishing the dispatches with coded names (*X*, *Y*, *Z*) instead of the French officials' real identities, the scandal outraged Americans and pushed the nation to the brink of war with *France*.

## you gotta know

- President *John Adams* sent three envoys to *Paris* in *1797* to negotiate settlement of trade disputes and French seizures of American merchant ships.
- French *Foreign Minister* *Charles Maurice de Talleyrand* sent three intermediaries to demand a bribe of *250,000 French livres* (approximately *$50,000*) and a *$10 million* loan to *France* before serious negotiations could begin.
- The American envoys refused and reported the extortion to the government; when *Adams* published their dispatches, he used the letters *X*, *Y*, and *Z* to conceal the intermediaries' identities.
- Public outcry was intense; the phrase "Millions for defense, not one cent for tribute" captured American indignation and became a rallying cry against French arrogance.
- The scandal precipitated the *Quasi-War* (*1798–1800*), an undeclared naval conflict between the U.S. and *France*, damaging Franco-American relations for years.
- Diplomatic negotiation resumed after *Napoleon* took power in *1799*; the *Convention of 1800* formally ended hostilities.
- The affair demonstrated that even young America could resist foreign coercion and helped cement national unity around principles of sovereignty and honor.
