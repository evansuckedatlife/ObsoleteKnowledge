# Hall-Mills Case

## summary

In September 1922, the bodies of *Reverend Edward Hall* and his lover *Eleanor Mills*, a choir singer, were found murdered on a rural road in New Jersey. Hall's wife, *Frances Hall*, and her brothers were tried for the crime; circumstantial evidence and witness inconsistencies made the trial sensational but inconclusive. Though acquitted, the case remained controversial, epitomizing 1920s anxieties over sexuality, respectability, and justice.

## you gotta know

- *Reverend Edward Hall*, a prominent Protestant minister, and *Eleanor Mills*, a 34-year-old choir singer and mother of two, were found shot and stabbed to death on September 16, 1922, in De Russey's Lane near New Brunswick, New Jersey.
- Hall had been having an affair with Mills; both were married to other people. The murders occurred in a pastoral setting, initially suggesting a pastoral or moral crime.
- *Frances Hall*, Edward's wife, quickly came under suspicion; she claimed ignorance but was widely believed to have orchestrated or participated in the murders out of sexual jealousy and wounded pride.
- Frances's two brothers, *Willie Stevens* (simple-minded) and *Henry Stevens*, were also tried; the prosecution suggested Frances and Henry were co-conspirators.
- The trial (1926) featured conflicting eyewitness testimony, disputed timelines, and limited physical evidence; the defense exploited weaknesses in the state's case and raised questions about Frances's mental state.
- All three defendants were acquitted in December 1926; the verdict left the public deeply skeptical, as circumstantial evidence suggested guilt but fell short of legal proof.
- The case became emblematic of class privilege's power in the justice system: Frances Hall's social standing, the ambiguity of evidence, and the defense's courtroom skill combined to evade conviction despite widespread belief in her involvement.
