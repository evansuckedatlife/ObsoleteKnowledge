# Louisiana Purchase

## summary

The Louisiana Purchase was the 1803 acquisition by the United States of the vast Louisiana Territory from *France* for $15 million (approximately $300 million in 2020 dollars). The deal, negotiated by President *Thomas Jefferson's* envoys *Robert Livingston* and *James Monroe* in Paris, roughly doubled the size of the United States, extending the nation's borders from the Mississippi River westward to the Rocky Mountains. The purchase was pivotal to American westward expansion and made possible the *Lewis and Clark Expedition*, which explored the newly acquired lands.

## you gotta know

- France, under Napoleon Bonaparte, had recently acquired the Louisiana Territory from Spain and sought to rebuild a North American empire.
- The French controlled the port of New Orleans, a vital outlet for American commerce on the Mississippi River; the Spanish, who had held it before France, had restricted American trade.
- Jefferson authorized his envoys to offer to purchase New Orleans and the Mississippi delta for $10 million; France, needing funds for wars in Europe and having abandoned its colonial ambitions in the Americas after the Haitian Revolution, offered the entire Louisiana Territory.
- The purchase price was $15 million, financed by loans from British and Dutch banks and later repaid by the U.S. government.
- The territory included present-day Louisiana, Arkansas, Missouri, Iowa, North Dakota, South Dakota, Nebraska, Oklahoma, Kansas, and parts of Colorado, Minnesota, and Montana.
- The purchase sparked a constitutional debate: Jefferson believed a constitutional amendment was required to authorize the purchase, but Congress approved it quickly without an amendment.
- The Lewis and Clark Expedition (1804–1806) was immediately commissioned to explore the new territory and map a route to the Pacific Ocean.
- The purchase became a watershed moment in American imperialism and continental expansion, encouraging the doctrine of *Manifest Destiny*.
