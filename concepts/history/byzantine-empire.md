# Byzantine Empire

## summary

The Byzantine Empire was the eastern continuation of the Roman Empire, centered on Constantinople (modern Istanbul), that endured for over a thousand years from the 4th century until its conquest by the Ottoman Turks in 1453. Emerging from the late Roman Empire's division, it became the longest-lasting medieval state in Europe, serving as a crucial bridge between the classical world and the Renaissance. The empire preserved Greek and Roman learning, developed Eastern Orthodox Christianity, and created a sophisticated bureaucracy and military tradition that profoundly influenced European and Asian civilizations. Its capital, Constantinople, was the wealthiest and most cosmopolitan city of the medieval world.

## you gotta know

### founding and identity

- The empire was formally established when *Constantine I* moved the Roman capital to *Constantinople* in 330 CE, though it did not become the sole surviving Roman state until the Western Roman Empire's fall in 476 CE.
- Byzantium adopted *Greek* as its official language and *Orthodox Christianity* as its state religion, distinguishing it from the Latin Western tradition.
- The empire was ruled by a sophisticated *bureaucracy* divided into military themes (administrative-military units) and civil provinces, with emperors claiming direct descent from Augustus and Rome.

### cultural and religious significance

- The *Hagia Sophia*, built by Justinian I in the 6th century, was the empire's supreme architectural achievement and remained the largest cathedral in Christendom for nearly a thousand years.
- Byzantine monks and scholars preserved *Greek and Latin manuscripts*, copying countless texts that would otherwise have been lost to Western Europe during the medieval period.
- The *Icon Controversy* (8th–9th centuries) deeply divided the empire over the veneration of religious images, eventually establishing icons as central to Orthodox practice.

### military and political reach

- Under *Justinian I* (527–565 CE), the empire reached its greatest territorial extent, reconquering much of the Mediterranean including North Africa, Italy, and Spain, though these gains proved unsustainable.
- The empire developed *Greek Fire*, a secret incendiary weapon used to defend against Arab and Viking naval attacks, maintaining naval superiority for centuries.
- Byzantine emperors navigated constant wars against the *Sassanid Persians*, *Arab Caliphates*, *Crusaders*, and *Ottoman Turks*, adapting their military and diplomatic strategies across centuries.

### decline and fall

- The empire suffered catastrophically during the *Fourth Crusade* (1204), when Latin Crusaders sacked Constantinople and established a Latin Empire, fragmenting Byzantine power for nearly 60 years.
- In 1453, Ottoman sultan *Mehmed II* breached the walls of Constantinople with cannon fire, ending the empire after more than 1,100 years and marking the symbolic end of the medieval period.

## context

Byzantium occupies a paradoxical place in Western memory: often overshadowed by the more celebrated Roman and Ottoman empires, yet profoundly influential on both. It was the crucible in which Orthodox Christianity crystallized, the archive where classical knowledge survived the medieval darkness, and the cultural powerhouse that transmitted civilization to Eastern Europe and the Rus. Its fall to Ottoman cannons in 1453 echoed through Renaissance Europe, spurring the age of exploration and the printing revolution. The empire's thousand-year stability, despite constant external pressure and internal religious turmoil, remains one of history's most striking achievements.
