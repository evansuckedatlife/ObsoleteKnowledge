# Anti-Masonic Party

## summary

The Anti-Masonic Party was an early U.S. third party of the 1828–1834 era, formed in response to public outcry over alleged Masonic privilege and secrecy, particularly following the 1826 murder of a man who threatened to publish an exposé of Freemasonry. Though short-lived, the party pioneered the national nominating convention and later merged into the Whig Party, making it a significant precursor to modern party organization and the second major-party system.

## you gotta know

- The party originated in western New York in the late 1820s, emerging from anti-Masonic sentiment triggered by the abduction and murder of William Morgan, whose unpublished manuscript criticizing Masonic oaths threatened Masonic secrecy.
- When local authorities appeared to protect Masons accused of Morgan's murder, public fury galvanized a political movement against Masonic influence in American law and government.
- The Anti-Masonic Party held the *first* national nominating convention in American history (1831), establishing the institutional practice that became standard in both major parties.
- Presidential candidate William Henry Harrison (also the National Republican/early Whig nominee) won significant support in 1832, appealing to voters hostile to perceived aristocratic secret societies.
- The party's success lay partly in its focus on secret societies as threats to democratic transparency; it aligned with broader Jacksonian-era suspicion of elite privilege and backroom dealing.
- By the mid-1830s, most Anti-Masonic voters migrated to the new Whig Party, which offered a broader anti-Jackson coalition; the party dissolved as a distinct entity by 1840.
