# Assassination of Mohandas Gandhi

## summary

On January 30, 1948, *Mohandas Gandhi*, the spiritual leader of India's independence struggle and pioneer of nonviolent resistance, was shot and killed by *Nathuram Godse*, a Hindu nationalist extremist. The assassination occurred just months after India gained independence from British rule and as communal violence between Hindus and Muslims was ravaging the subcontinent. Gandhi's death became a symbol both of the costs of nonviolence and of the dangers of religious extremism to democratic societies.

## you gotta know

### The Assassination
- Shot on January 30, 1948, in Delhi, India, at the home of his host *Birla House*.
- Assassin: *Nathuram Godse*, a Hindu nationalist who opposed Gandhi's message of Hindu-Muslim unity and his support for Pakistan.
- Godse fired three bullets at close range; Gandhi died within minutes, his final words reportedly *"Hai Ram"* ("Oh God").
- The assassination occurred during the Hindu festival of Holi, a time of celebration that was darkened by the killing.

### Political Context
- India had gained independence just months earlier in August 1947; the partition into India and Pakistan had triggered massive communal violence.
- Gandhi was fasting and preaching nonviolence and Hindu-Muslim harmony, which infuriated Hindu extremists who blamed him for Pakistan's creation.
- Godse belonged to the Rashtriya Swayamsevak Sangh (RSS), a paramilitary Hindu nationalist organization opposed to Gandhi's pluralism.
- Gandhi had survived earlier assassination attempts and refused personal security, believing in his cause absolutely.

### Aftermath
- Godse was tried, convicted, and executed in 1949; the RSS was banned temporarily.
- Gandhi's death unified India in grief and shocked the world, elevating his moral status internationally.
- The assassination vindicated Gandhi's nonviolent philosophy by making violence against him appear monstrous and futile.

## context

Gandhi's assassination represents a tragic irony: the man who had wielded nonviolence as a weapon against an empire was cut down by violence rooted in religious nationalism. The event stands as a watershed in postcolonial history, exposing the fragility of secular, pluralist visions in societies torn by communal identity. It also demonstrates the paradox of principled nonviolence: Gandhi's refusal of protection was consistent with his philosophy but made him vulnerable to zealots. In global memory, Gandhi's death became inseparable from his life—the final testament to his beliefs.
