## summary

*Bostock v. Clayton County* (2020) is the Supreme Court decision that extended Title VII of the Civil Rights Act of 1964 to prohibit employment discrimination based on sexual orientation and gender identity. The 6–3 ruling, written by Justice Neil Gorsuch, held that discrimination on these bases is necessarily discrimination "because of sex" and therefore illegal.

## you gotta know

- Decided June 15, 2020 by a 6–3 margin, with Justice Neil Gorsuch writing the majority opinion.
- Consolidated three cases: *Bostock v. Clayton County* (termination of gay employee), *Altitude Express v. Zarda* (fired because of sexual orientation), and *Harris Funeral Homes v. EEOC* (termination related to gender identity).
- Held that discrimination based on sexual orientation or gender identity necessarily involves discrimination "because of sex," violating Title VII regardless of the employer's motive.
- Gorsuch used textualist reasoning: the statute's language "because of sex" covers causation, and discrimination against same-sex or transgender employees inherently involves sex.
- Chief Justice Roberts and Justice Alito dissented, arguing the decision rewrote a statute Congress had never intended to cover sexual orientation or gender identity.
- Immediately protected millions of LGBTQ workers from workplace discrimination in all jurisdictions, making it a major civil rights victory.
- Did not address religious exemptions, leaving open questions about how the decision interacts with religious freedom claims.
