# Nez Perce

## summary

The *Nez Perce* (Nimíipuu, "the People") are a Plateau nation historically inhabiting the Pacific Northwest (present-day Oregon, Washington, and Idaho), renowned for their salmon fisheries, distinctive appaloosa horse breeding, and Chief *Joseph*'s legendary resistance and retreat across 1,100 miles during the *Nez Perce War* (1877).

## you gotta know

- Sahaptian-speaking people; name "Nez Perce" (Pierced Nose) is French, though the Nez Perce did not universally practice nose piercing—the name may derive from a misunderstanding.
- Developed sophisticated salmon-fishing economies along Columbia River tributaries; constructed sophisticated weirs and preservation techniques; gathered roots and hunted game.
- Bred distinctive appaloosa horses, known for their spotted coats and endurance; mastered mounted hunting and warfare through equestrian skills unmatched in the region.
- *Chief Joseph* (Hinmaton-Yalaktit) led the Wallowa band's desperate resistance when forced from their ancestral Wallowa Valley; the *Nez Perce War* (1877) saw them retreat over 1,100 miles toward Canada, repeatedly defeating pursuing U.S. forces before surrender near the Canadian border.
- Joseph's surrender speech—"I will fight no more forever"—became iconic; he was exiled to Oklahoma, then Kansas, then finally allowed to return to the Colville Reservation in Washington.
- Today the Nez Perce Nation is based on the Nez Perce Reservation in north-central Idaho; language revitalization and traditional cultural practices continue.
