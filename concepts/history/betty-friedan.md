# Betty Friedan

## summary

*Betty Friedan* (1921–2006) was an American feminist writer whose *The Feminine Mystique* (1963) ignited second-wave feminism by exposing the unhappiness of middle-class American housewives and the cultural mythology that defined women solely through domestic roles. She co-founded the National Organization for Women (NOW) and became one of the most visible faces of the women's liberation movement.

## you gotta know

- Published *The Feminine Mystique*, which identified the "problem that has no name" — the dissatisfaction of educated women confined to domestic life — and sold over 1 million copies.
- Argued that women's identity and fulfillment required work, education, and independence, not conformity to the feminine ideal of the 1950s.
- Co-founded the National Organization for Women (NOW) in 1966 and served as its president, mobilizing millions of women around workplace discrimination, reproductive rights, and equal protection.
- Organized "strike for equality" demonstrations in 1970, demanding equal pay, childcare, and abortion access, bringing thousands of women into the streets.
- Expanded feminism beyond suffrage and voting to encompass sexuality, work, motherhood, and identity — the core concerns of second-wave feminism.
- Later life included tension with more radical feminists over her focus on equality within existing systems versus transformative social change.
