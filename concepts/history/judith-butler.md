# Judith Butler

## summary

*Judith Butler* (born 1956) is an American philosopher whose *Gender Trouble* (1990) fundamentally transformed feminist and queer theory by arguing that gender is not an identity but a repeated performance. Her concept of *performativity* — that we enact gender through bodily gestures and speech — opened pathways for understanding gender as malleable and non-binary.

## you gotta know

- Published *Gender Trouble: Feminism and the Subversion of Identity*, which challenged the idea that gender is an expression of a stable inner self.
- Introduced the concept of *performativity*, showing that gender is constituted through repeated citation of norms, not discovered or expressed from within.
- Argued that the category "woman" itself is contested and unstable, and that feminist appeals to "women's identity" risk reinforcing the very norms that oppress.
- Extended her analysis to the politics of recognizability: whose lives count as grievable, whose deaths matter, how power operates through frames of legibility.
- Her work became foundational to queer theory, transgender studies, and contemporary feminist scholarship, though also contested and difficult.
- Continues writing on ethics, violence, and the conditions under which human lives are recognized as livable and valuable.

## connections

- [[gender]] — she established it as constructed through performative repetition, not biological destiny.
- [[queer-theory]] — her work opened space for non-binary and fluid understandings of gender and sexuality.
- [[performativity]] — the core concept through which she theorizes gender and social norms.
- [[philosophy]] — she brought poststructuralist and psychoanalytic thought into conversation with feminist theory.
