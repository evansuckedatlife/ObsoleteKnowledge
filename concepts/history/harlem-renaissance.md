# Harlem Renaissance

## summary

The *Harlem Renaissance* was a cultural and intellectual awakening of African Americans centered in Harlem, a neighborhood of New York City, primarily during the 1920s and 1930s. African American writers, musicians, visual artists, and intellectuals created a dynamic new culture celebrating Black identity, heritage, and artistic achievement. The movement produced literary masterpieces, revolutionary music (jazz and blues), and visual art that challenged racist stereotypes and asserted African American cultural pride, simultaneously establishing the foundation for the modern Civil Rights Movement.

## you gotta know

- The *Great Migration* (1910–1970) brought millions of African Americans from the rural South to northern industrial cities, with Harlem emerging as the largest African American community, creating a critical mass of talented artists and intellectuals.
- The *Harlem Renaissance* began around the early 1920s and reached its height in the mid-to-late 1920s, though creative ferment continued through the 1930s and early 1940s, encompassing literature, music, theater, visual art, and intellectual discourse.
- *Langston Hughes*, a poet and writer, became the movement's poetic voice, celebrating African American vernacular, jazz rhythms, and folk traditions in poetry that was both intellectually sophisticated and accessible to ordinary people.
- *Zora Neale Hurston*, an anthropologist and writer, collected African American folklore, recorded vernacular speech, and produced novels celebrating Black Southern rural culture and female independence, most famously *Their Eyes Were Watching God* (1937).
- *Claude McKay*, *Countee Cullen*, *James Weldon Johnson*, and *Jessie Faucet* produced poetry and prose exploring African American identity, racial pride, and the complexities of living under Jim Crow segregation.
- *Duke Ellington* and *Louis Armstrong* revolutionized music: jazz became the defining musical form of the era, blending African American blues, ragtime, and improvisation into an art form that conquered American and eventually world culture.
- The *Cotton Club* in Harlem epitomized the era's cultural paradox: a nightclub featuring African American musicians and dancers, yet owned by white entrepreneurs and restricted to white patrons—exemplifying segregation's contradictions.
- The movement championed the *New Negro*—intellectuals' assertion of African American agency, dignity, and cultural achievement in contrast to degrading stereotypes; the concept became central to Black Pride and later Civil Rights ideology.
- *Harlem arts salons* and intellectual gatherings—often hosted by wealthy white patrons (a phenomenon called the "Negro vogue")—created spaces for cross-racial cultural exchange, though on unequal terms reflecting broader white patronage and control.
- The movement produced visual art and sculpture celebrating African and African American aesthetics, drawing on modernist styles while asserting cultural authenticity and pride.
