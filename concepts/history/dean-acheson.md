# Dean Acheson

## summary

*Dean Acheson* was Secretary of State under President Harry S. Truman from 1949 to 1953, a consequential figure in Cold War diplomacy who articulated the containment doctrine, managed the Korean War, and expanded NATO. A skilled lawyer and diplomat, Acheson believed in American power wielded through alliances and international law, yet his tenure was shadowed by accusations of having "lost" China to communism—a charge that defined the bitter domestic politics of his era.

## you gotta know

- Served as Secretary of State during the Korean War (1950–1953), advising President Truman on military strategy and diplomatic endgame.
- Delivered the *National Security Council Report 68* (NSC-68, 1950), which argued for a massive rearmament of the West to contain Soviet expansion.
- Expanded *NATO*, integrating West Germany into the alliance (1955, formally), a decision that alarmed the Soviet Union and solidified the Cold War division of Europe.
- Advocated the *Domino Theory*—the belief that if one nation fell to communism, others would follow—justifying American interventions across Asia and the developing world.
- Defended the American position in China's loss to communism, though his critics blamed his department for insufficient support of the Nationalist government.
- Believed diplomacy should always be backed by credible military force; wrote influential memoirs on the Korean War and Cold War strategy.

## connections

- [[harry-s-truman]] — the president under whom Acheson served; their partnership shaped the Korean War response.
- [[george-marshall]] — his predecessor; Acheson inherited and expanded Marshall's Cold War vision.
- [[korean-war]] — the defining crisis of his tenure; he advised on both military and diplomatic fronts.
- [[nato]] — he oversaw its expansion and integration during the critical early Cold War years.
