## summary

*Wickard v. Filburn* (1942) upheld federal agricultural regulation under the Commerce Clause, ruling that even wheat grown wholly for personal consumption on a farm substantially affects interstate commerce and may be regulated by Congress. The decision dramatically expanded federal regulatory authority over economic activity.

## you gotta know

- Roscoe Filburn, an Ohio farmer, planted more wheat than allowed under *New Deal* agricultural quotas and claimed he grew the excess for personal use, not interstate commerce.
- The Court ruled that the aggregate effect of many farms growing excess wheat for personal consumption affects the national wheat market and interstate commerce.
- Justice *Robert Jackson* wrote that even wholly intrastate, non-commercial activity can be regulated if it "substantially affects" interstate commerce.
- The decision effectively abandoned the *direct/indirect effects test*, replacing it with an aggregate-effects standard vastly expanding federal power.
- Became the foundation for virtually all post-1942 federal economic regulation, including labor, civil rights, environmental, and consumer protection laws.
- Marked the end of the *Lochner era*'s limits on federal commerce power; the Commerce Clause no longer meaningfully constrains Congress.

## connections

- [[new-deal]] — FDR's agricultural and economic programs that prompted the case.

- [[lochner-era]] — the restrictive era on federal commerce power this case ended.
- [[federalism]] — the balance of state and federal power shifted decisively federal.
