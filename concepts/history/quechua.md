# Quechua

## summary

The *Quechua* are the largest indigenous people of South America, inhabiting the Andean highlands across Peru, Bolivia, Ecuador, Colombia, Chile, and Argentina. They are the direct descendants of the Inca Empire and speak Quechua languages, maintaining rich traditions of highland agriculture, weaving, and social organization while navigating the legacies of Spanish colonization and ongoing marginalization.

## you gotta know

- Inhabit the *Andes* mountains and surrounding regions across multiple South American nations; largest concentrations in Peru and Bolivia.
- Speak *Quechua* languages (multiple varieties including Quichua and Kichwa), descended from the *Inca lingua franca*; over 8 million speakers remain, making it the most widely spoken indigenous language in the Americas.
- Are direct descendants of the *Inca Empire*, which they ruled until Spanish conquest (1532); Quechua culture preserves continuities with Inca traditions in agriculture, governance, and spirituality.
- Practice *terrace farming* on steep Andean slopes, cultivating potatoes, quinoa, maize, and other Andean crops; developed sophisticated agricultural knowledge and preservation techniques like *chuño* (freeze-dried potatoes).
- Create distinctive woven textiles (*aguayos*, *ponchos*, *chullos*) using traditional techniques and natural dyes; textiles encode identity, community affiliation, and regional variation.
- Maintain *ayllu* (community) organization, a system of reciprocal labor exchange and kinship-based social units predating and surviving the Inca; still organizes many rural Quechua communities.
- Practice *animism* blended with Catholicism, revering *Apus* (mountain spirits) and *Pachamama* (earth mother); traditional ceremonies honor these sacred relationships.
