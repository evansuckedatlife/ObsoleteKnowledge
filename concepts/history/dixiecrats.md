# Dixiecrats

## summary

The Dixiecrats, formally the States' Rights Democratic Party, were Southern Democrats who split from the national Democratic Party in 1948 to oppose President Truman's civil rights initiatives. Running on a platform of rigid racial segregation and states' rights, South Carolina Senator Strom Thurmond carried the Solid South (winning 39 electoral votes) but failed to prevent Truman's reelection, marking the beginning of the South's long realignment toward the Republican Party.

## you gotta know

- The party formed after President Harry Truman desegregated the military and endorsed a federal civil rights agenda, policies the Southern Democratic establishment viewed as a betrayal.
- Strom Thurmond, a South Carolina segregationist, won the party's presidential nomination and carried Alabama, Arkansas, Louisiana, Mississippi, and South Carolina—the only four of the Solid South states to break Democratic ranks.
- The Dixiecrats won 2.4% of the popular vote and 39 electoral votes, the strongest third-party electoral showing since the Bull Moose Party in 1912.
- The party platform defended racial segregation as constitutionally legitimate under the Tenth Amendment and opposed federal intervention in Southern social and racial affairs.
- Though the Dixiecrats themselves dissolved after 1948, the movement's constituency—white Southern voters—gradually migrated to the Republican Party over the 1960s and beyond (the "Southern Strategy").
- The Dixiecrat defection was the first crack in the Solid South, foreshadowing the Republican takeover of the South by the 1980s.
