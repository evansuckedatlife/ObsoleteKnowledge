# Congress of Vienna

## summary

The Congress of Vienna (1814–1815) was the diplomatic conference that reshaped Europe after the fall of Napoleon, bringing together the major European powers to negotiate a new continental order. Led by key statesmen including *Prince Metternich* of Austria, *Tsar Alexander I* of Russia, and British and Prussian diplomats, the Congress established principles of legitimacy (restoring deposed monarchs), balance of power (preventing any single nation's dominance), and conservative reaction against revolutionary ideology. The resulting treaty system—including the Treaty of Paris and various bilateral agreements—redrew the map of Europe, reorganized Germany and Italy into loose confederations, and established frameworks for international diplomacy that persisted throughout the 19th century. The Congress represented the triumph of the established order over revolutionary change and inaugurated the Concert of Europe, a system of great-power cooperation to maintain stability.

## you gotta know

### prelude and participants

- After *Napoleon's* defeat and abdication in 1814, the victorious powers—Austria, Russia, Prussia, Britain, and restored France—convened to settle European affairs and establish lasting peace.
- *Prince Klemens von Metternich*, Austria's foreign minister, dominated negotiations, representing the conservative principle that legitimate monarchies (not republics or reformed states) should govern Europe.
- *Tsar Alexander I* of Russia, the most powerful military victor, sought territorial gains and dominance; Britain, represented by *Viscount Castlereagh*, sought to maintain balance and prevent any single power's hegemony.

### principles and outcomes

- The Congress enshrined the principle of *legitimacy*—restoring the old ruling families to their thrones, including the Bourbons to France, and eliminating republics and revolutionary states.
- The Conference established the *Balance of Power* as the guiding principle of European diplomacy, creating buffer states around France (Belgium and the Netherlands united, German Confederation strengthened) to prevent French revanchism.
- The *Polish-Saxon Crisis*—disputes over territorial compensation—nearly broke the negotiations but was resolved through compromise, with Russia acquiring territory in Poland, Prussia gaining Saxony, and Austria gaining Italian territories.

### territorial reorganization

- Germany was reorganized from Napoleon's simplified Confederation of the Rhine into the loose *German Confederation* (1815–1866), comprising 39 separate states, a structure designed to prevent German unification and dominance.
- Italy remained fragmented under the Vienna settlement: the Papal States were restored, Austria controlled northern Italian territories, and various kingdoms (Naples, Sardinia, Papal States) maintained independence.
- The *Low Countries* (Belgium and the Netherlands) were united under *King William I* in an attempt to create a strong buffer state against France, though this arrangement proved unstable and dissolved in 1830.

### the Concert of Europe and legitimacy

- The Congress established the *Concert of Europe*, a system of great-power cooperation whereby the major powers would consult before taking action affecting European stability.
- Conservative reaction dominated: the Congress opposed *nationalism*, *liberalism*, and *socialism*, supporting absolute monarchies and the suppression of revolutionary movements.
- The Treaty of Paris (1815) imposed indemnities on France but treated it relatively leniently, intending to reintegrate it into the European system rather than humiliate it (unlike the harsher 1919 Treaty of Versailles).

### limitations and legacy

- The Vienna settlement ignored nationalist aspirations, particularly in Germany, Italy, and Eastern Europe, sowing seeds for conflicts and unification movements throughout the 19th century.
- The Congress achieved remarkable durability—Europe experienced no general war (unlike the Napoleonic Wars' constant conflict) from 1815 to 1914, though regional conflicts persisted.
- The system's emphasis on legitimacy and conservative order was challenged by the *Revolutions of 1848* and by the emergence of nationalist and unification movements, eventually leading to the unifications of Germany (1871) and Italy (1871) contrary to Vienna's wishes.

## context

The Congress of Vienna was a watershed moment in the history of diplomacy and international relations, establishing many principles that continue to guide state behavior. It represented the triumph of old-order conservatism over revolutionary change, yet its very success at creating a stable international system was undermined by the forces it sought to suppress—nationalism and liberalism. The Congress's careful balance-of-power logic prevented general European war for a century, an remarkable achievement, yet its resistance to national self-determination created instabilities that would eventually explode. The Congress established the template for great-power diplomacy and concert-of-powers systems that the world would repeatedly try to recreate (the League of Nations, the United Nations). Metternich's vision of stability through monarchical legitimacy and conservative order became the blueprint for 19th-century European politics, even as it faced mounting pressure from nationalist and democratic movements. The Congress of Vienna marks the end of the Napoleonic era and the beginning of the Concert of Europe, making it a crucial turning point in modern history.
