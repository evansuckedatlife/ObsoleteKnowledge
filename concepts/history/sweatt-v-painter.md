# Sweatt v. Painter

## summary

*Sweatt v. Painter* (1950) unanimously ruled that Texas violated the *Equal Protection Clause* by denying admission to its law school to *Heman Sweatt*, a Black applicant, and offering him a hastily created all-Black law school instead. Chief Justice *Fred Vinson*'s decision held that the separate facilities were inherently unequal in educational quality and prestige, chipping away at the "separate but equal" doctrine and laying the groundwork for *Brown v. Board of Education* four years later.

## you gotta know

- *Heman Sweatt* was denied admission to the University of Texas Law School solely on grounds of race; Texas created a separate law school for him.
- Chief Justice *Vinson* found the new school patently unequal in physical facilities, faculty, library, and prestige—a finding easy for courts to verify and replicate.
- The decision did not explicitly overrule *Plessy*, but it applied "equal" so stringently that true separation became legally impossible.
- Sweatt's case demonstrated the practical futility of "separate but equal" in graduate and professional education, where intangible factors matter.
- Part of the NAACP's strategic litigation campaign that culminated in *Brown*; Thurgood Marshall argued the case.
- Preceded *McLaurin v. Oklahoma State Regents* (1950), another decision stripping away *Plessy*'s foundation.
