# Tinker v. Des Moines

## summary

*Tinker v. Des Moines* (1969) held that students do not shed their constitutional rights at the schoolhouse gate, protecting students' right to wear black armbands in school to protest the Vietnam War. The decision established that schools may regulate student speech only when it would materially and substantially disrupt the educational process.

## you gotta know

- In 1965, Des Moines high school students Mary Beth and John Tinker wore black armbands to school in protest of American military involvement in Vietnam.
- School officials suspended the students for violating a hastily adopted no-armband policy.
- Justice *Abe Fortas* wrote that wearing armbands is *symbolic speech* protected by the First Amendment.
- Schools can restrict student speech only if it causes or threatens material and substantial disruption to school operations—a high bar.
- The Court recognized that students retain constitutional rights and that schools cannot simply silence unpopular viewpoints.
- Became the foundation for student speech protection; subsequent cases have carved exceptions (Hazelwood for school publications, Morse for drug-related speech).
