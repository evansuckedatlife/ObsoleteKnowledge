## summary

The First Battle of Bull Run (July 21, 1861), also called the First Battle of Manassas, was the first major land battle of the American Civil War, fought in northern Virginia near the creek of that name. Union General Irvin McDowell's Army failed to break through Confederate General Pierre Beauregard's defensive lines, resulting in a Confederate victory and the dramatic Union retreat. The battle shattered Northern illusions of a quick and easy war, demonstrating that the Confederacy would fight with determination and that the conflict would demand massive resources and sacrifice.

## you gotta know

### Background
- Lincoln under political pressure launched the offensive to prove Union military competence and capitalize on perceived Confederate weakness.
- McDowell, though outnumbered initially, believed he could overrun Beauregard before Confederate reinforcements arrived from the Shenandoah Valley.
- Beauregard deployed Confederate forces in a strong defensive position along Bull Run.

### The Battle
- Union forces began with promise, moving around the Confederate left flank in the morning.
- Confederate reinforcements, notably *General Joseph E. Johnston's* army from the Valley, arrived via the Manassas Gap Railroad, a crucial logistical advantage.
- The turning point came when Confederate reserves counterattacked; a fresh regiment led by *Thomas J. Jackson* held firm, earning him the sobriquet "*Stonewall*" Jackson.

### Aftermath
- Union forces broke and retreated chaotically toward Washington, pursued by Confederate cavalry.
- Northern civilians watching the battle from nearby hills were caught in the rout, adding to the spectacle of defeat.
- The battle ended hopes of a 90-day war and prompted Lincoln to call for 300,000 additional volunteers.

## context

Bull Run became a symbol of the Union's unreadiness and the Confederacy's fighting spirit. The battle revealed that victory required disciplined armies, superior strategy, and far greater losses than anyone anticipated. For the North, it meant a grim realization: total war with mobilization of the entire economy and society. For the South, it gave false confidence—a small victory that bred overconfidence about eventual triumph despite the Union's material superiority. The battle's shock value accelerated both sides' preparation for a prolonged conflict.
