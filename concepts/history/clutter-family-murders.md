# Clutter Family Murders

## summary

On November 15, 1959, the *Clutter family*—a wealthy, respected Kansas farming family—were murdered in their home by two drifters, *Richard Hickock* and *Perry Smith*, who believed the family had a safe containing large sums of money. The killings and subsequent investigation became the subject of Truman Capote's groundbreaking 1966 non-fiction novel *In Cold Blood*, which pioneered the "true crime" genre and raised questions about motivation, determinism, and American violence.

## you gotta know

- On November 15, 1959, *Herb Clutter* (a well-to-do farmer), his wife *Bonnie*, and their two youngest children, *Nancy* (16) and *Kenyon* (15), were murdered in their Holcomb, Kansas farmhouse.
- *Richard Hickock*, a two-time ex-convict, and *Perry Smith*, a troubled itinerant, committed the murders after learning (falsely) from a former cell-mate that Clutter kept a large safe filled with cash.
- Finding no safe and little money, the two killed all four family members in a methodical fashion—shooting the parents and two teenagers to eliminate witnesses.
- Police tracked Hickock and Smith to Mexico, where they were arrested after attempting to flee the country; both confessed under interrogation.
- The trial (1960) in Kansas was relatively straightforward; both men were convicted and sentenced to death. The case itself was legally routine but psychologically compelling.
- Hickock and Smith were executed by hanging on April 14, 1965, in Kansas; their executions marked the end of the case but not its cultural resonance.
- *Truman Capote*, a famous novelist, spent years interviewing Hickock, Smith, investigators, and townspeople; his book *In Cold Blood* (1966) revolutionized the non-fiction novel form and became a foundational true-crime text, raising enduring questions about murderer psychology and the American frontier.
