# Treaty of Tordesillas

## summary

The *Treaty of Tordesillas* (1494) partitioned the non-European world between *Spain* and *Portugal*, drawn at the behest of Pope Alexander VI to prevent conflict between the two Iberian powers over colonial spoils. A meridian line drawn 370 leagues west of the Cape Verde Islands gave Portugal eastern territories (Africa, India, Brazil) and Spain the western hemisphere (the Americas). The treaty codified European imperialism and shaped global colonialism for the next four centuries.

## you gotta know

### Historical Context & Origins
- Signed on *June 7, 1494*, only two years after Columbus's first voyage to the Americas.
- Issued by the papal bull *Inter Caetera* (1493), which gave Spain dominion over newly discovered lands, prompting Portuguese objection.
- No non-European powers were consulted; the treaty assumed European right to partition the globe.

### The Line of Demarcation
- Ran roughly *370 leagues* (approximately 1,100 miles) west of the Cape Verde Islands, modified by negotiation.
- *Spain* claimed all lands west of the line—effectively the entire Americas; *Portugal* claimed lands to the east.
- The vague boundary caused centuries of dispute; the line's inexactness allowed Portugal to claim *Brazil* (discovered 1500, which extends east of the original line).

### Territorial Division & Consequences
- *Portugal* secured control of Africa, India, and Brazil—the prime sources of spices, gold, and enslaved labour.
- *Spain* gained dominion over Central America, South America (except Brazil), the Caribbean, and Mexico—vast territories with silver, gold, and indigenous populations to exploit.
- The treaty excluded France, England, and the Netherlands, spurring them to colonise unguarded territories and raid Spanish/Portuguese shipping.

### Impact on Colonialism & Slavery
- Legitimised European conquest under a veneer of papal authority and bilateral agreement.
- Facilitated the *Atlantic slave trade*: African captives flowed to American plantations under the treaty's framework.
- Drew the geopolitical boundaries—linguistic, cultural, religious—that define Latin America and Africa today.

## context

The Treaty of Tordesillas epitomises the Age of Exploration's hubris: two Catholic powers, armed with papal authority and possession of the compass and caravel, divided a world they barely knew. The treaty was less a negotiated compromise than a division of spoils between two competitors, excluding rivals (France, England) and utterly disregarding indigenous peoples. The line proved impossible to enforce precisely in practice—disputes erupted for centuries, especially over Brazil—and the weakness of the demarcation spawned a century of privateering and colonial wars. Yet the treaty's logic—that discovery granted possession, that cartographic division was binding, that indigenous inhabitants had no rights—became the template for all European colonialism. The languages, religions, and political systems of Latin America and Africa still bear the imprint of which side of the Tordesillas line they fell upon. It is a foundational treaty not because it worked, but because it expressed a principle (European supremacy) that structured the modern world.
