# Bataan Death March

## summary

Following the fall of the Bataan Peninsula in the Philippines in April 1942, Japanese forces conducted a brutal forced march of American and Filipino prisoners of war, during which approximately 6,000 to 10,000 soldiers died from starvation, disease, and brutal treatment. The *Bataan Death March* became one of the most notorious war crimes of the Pacific campaign and a symbol of Japanese brutality.

## you gotta know

- Campaign occurred January–April 1942 on the Bataan Peninsula near Manila; American garrison under General Edward King surrendered April 9, 1942.
- Approximately 76,000 American and Filipino troops surrendered; Japanese forces under General Masaharu Homma began forced march toward prison camps.
- Prisoners marched 104 km over six days in tropical heat with minimal food, water, and medical care; Japanese soldiers killed stragglers and those too weak to continue.
- Death toll: estimated 6,000 Americans and 15,000 Filipinos died; Japanese military refused to acknowledge the atrocity for decades.
- Survivors testified to systematic beatings, executions, disease, and starvation; physicians were prevented from administering medical care.
- General Homma was executed for war crimes in 1946; several subordinates also tried and convicted.
- Event galvanized American hatred of Japan and determination to defeat them; fulfilled MacArthur's vow to return to liberate the Philippines.
