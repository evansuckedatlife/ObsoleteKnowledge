# Battle of Cannae

## summary

The *Battle of Cannae* (216 BC) was the most catastrophic Roman military defeat in history, where *Hannibal Barca* of Carthage executed a masterful double envelopment that annihilated a Roman army nearly twice the size of his own. The loss of roughly 50,000 Roman soldiers demonstrated the fragility of Rome's power and remains a textbook example of tactical brilliance.

## you gotta know

- Fought in 216 BC near the town of Cannae in southern Italy during the *Second Punic War*.
- *Hannibal* commanded approximately 50,000 Carthaginian troops; the Romans fielded roughly 80,000 under consuls *Varro* and *Paullus*.
- Hannibal formed a crescent-shaped center with strong flanks, intentionally weakening his middle to draw in Roman reserves.
- As the Roman center pushed forward, Hannibal's flanks wheeled inward, then closed to rear, trapping the Roman army in a complete encirclement.
- Roman soldiers, unable to maneuver or flee, were slaughtered. Estimates place Roman casualties at 50,000–70,000 killed or captured.
- Hannibal's forces suffered only around 6,000 casualties, a kill ratio of roughly 8:1.
- Despite this victory, Hannibal could not capture Rome or neutralize Roman recovery; Rome eventually prevailed through superior resources and will.
