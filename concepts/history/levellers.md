# Levellers

## summary

The **Levellers** were a radical democratic and egalitarian movement in England during the English Civil War and Interregnum (1645–1653), advocating for universal male suffrage, equal representation, religious toleration, and the abolition of feudal privileges and hereditary titles. Emerging from the New Model Army's rank-and-file soldiers and London artisans, the Levellers sought to democratize the English Revolution and to extend its benefits beyond the parliamentary gentry to common people. Though suppressed by *Oliver Cromwell* and eclipsed after the Restoration of 1660, the Levellers' radical vision—the idea that political authority derives from the consent of all men—profoundly influenced later democratic movements, including American republicanism and nineteenth-century British reform.

## you gotta know

- The movement took its name from their critics' accusation that they sought to "level" society by eliminating hereditary privilege and social hierarchy; Levellers embraced the label ironically.
- Key figures included *John Lilburne*, a soldier-turned-agitator whose writings on natural rights and manhood suffrage became foundational texts; *William Walwyn* and *Richard Overton*, radical pamphleteers and ideologists.
- The *Agreement of the People* (1647–49), a Leveller manifesto, proposed a written constitution granting Parliament to representatives elected by all adult men (excluding servants and paupers); it was revolutionary for its era.
- Levellers mobilized through the New Model Army, where soldiers' councils debated political questions alongside military strategy; the *Putney Debates* (1647) featured Leveller officers and *Thomas Fairfax*'s Grandees arguing over suffrage and representation.
- Cromwell initially tolerated Leveller influence but grew hostile as they demanded radical redistribution of power; he suppressed them through arrests, trials, and executions; *John Lilburne* was repeatedly imprisoned.
- The Restoration (1660) and the establishment of the constitutional monarchy ended Leveller activity, but their ideas persisted in radical networks and philosophical literature, influencing later democratic theory.

## connections

- [[english-civil-war]] — the Levellers emerged as a faction within the revolutionary upheaval, seeking to radicalize the Revolution's aims.
- [[oliver-cromwell]] — the military leader who initially collaborated with Levellers but ultimately suppressed them to consolidate power.
- [[republicanism]] — the Levellers articulated a vision of a republic based on consent and equal representation, distinct from aristocratic or monarchical models.
- [[chartism]] — nineteenth-century Chartists drew on Leveller precedent; both movements demanded universal suffrage and represented working-class political consciousness.
- [[thomas-hobbes]] — contemporary philosopher whose *Leviathan* partly responded to radical egalitarian arguments circulating during the Interregnum.
