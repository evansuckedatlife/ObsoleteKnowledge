## summary

The French Revolution (1789–1799) was a cataclysm that destroyed the old feudal and absolutist order in France and reshaped European civilization. Beginning as a financial crisis and popular uprising for constitutional reform, it escalated into radical upheaval: the storming of the Bastille, the abolition of feudalism, the execution of the king, and the *Reign of Terror*, during which tens of thousands died on the guillotine. Though revolutionary governments fell and Napoleon ultimately seized power, the Revolution's legacy—the ideas of citizenship, human rights, national sovereignty, and secular law—became irreversible and inspired democratic and nationalist movements across Europe and beyond.

## you gotta know

### crisis and uprising

- France faced bankruptcy and famine; the calling of the *Estates-General* (1789) to levy taxes sparked widespread unrest among the common people (the *Third Estate*) and lower nobility.
- The storming of the *Bastille* (July 14, 1789), a royal prison in Paris, became the Revolution's symbolic moment and is celebrated as France's national day; it signaled that the populace had the power to defy the crown.
- The *Declaration of the Rights of Man and Citizen* (August 1789) proclaimed universal principles of liberty, equality, and popular sovereignty, rejecting the divine right of kings.

### radicalization and terror

- The *Reign of Terror* (1793–1794), led by *Robespierre* and the *Committee of Public Safety*, executed thousands of perceived enemies of the Revolution, including King *Louis XVI* (1793) and Queen *Marie-Antoinette* (1793).
- The Revolution abolished feudalism, serfdom, and corporate privileges; created a secular civil code; and tried (with mixed success) to de-Christianize France through revolutionary cults.
- Radical factions like the *Jacobins* under Robespierre and the more moderate *Girondins* clashed over the pace and aims of reform; Robespierre's Jacobins gained control and purged rivals.

### legacy and aftermath

- *Thermidor* (July 1794) saw Robespierre's downfall and execution, ending the Terror; the subsequent *Directory* (1795–1799) stabilized the republic but proved weak and unpopular.
- *Napoleon Bonaparte* seized power in the *Coup of 18 Brumaire* (November 1799), establishing the Consulate and eventually the Empire; he preserved revolutionary legal reforms while discarding democratic governance.

## context

The French Revolution was history's first sustained attempt to rebuild society from first principles: to demolish absolute monarchy, corporate privilege, and feudal hierarchy and erect in their place a nation-state of equal citizens governed by written law. Its violence and eventual failure to sustain democracy contrast sharply with its immense influence: the concepts of citizenship, national identity, human rights, and secular law that it championed became permanent. The Revolution's phases—idealistic, radical, terroristic, reactionary—have become the template for understanding all subsequent revolutions. For quiz bowl, it is the crucible in which modern politics is forged: ideology, mass participation, terror, and the machinery of the state collide in ways that continue to shape the world.

## connections

- [[robespierre]] — the architect of the Terror and symbol of revolutionary excess.
- [[napoleon-bonaparte]] — the general who ended the Revolution and preserved its legal reforms.
- [[marie-antoinette]] — the queen whose execution symbolized the Revolution's radical break with the past.
- [[declaration-of-the-rights-of-man-and-citizen]] — the manifesto of universal human rights and popular sovereignty.
- [[reign-of-terror]] — the climactic period of violence and mass execution.
- [[enlightenment]] — the intellectual tradition that provided the Revolution's philosophical foundation.
- [[romanticism]] — the artistic and literary movement that emerged partly in reaction to the Revolution's rationalism.
- [[the-marriage-of-figaro]] — the play by *Beaumarchais* that satirized aristocratic privilege, foreshadowing revolutionary themes.
- [[edmund-burke]] — the British philosopher whose *Reflections on the Revolution in France* became the manifesto of conservative reaction.
- [[mary-wollstonecraft]] — whose *A Vindication of the Rights of Woman* applied revolutionary principles to gender equality.
