# Ostrogoths

## summary

The *Ostrogoths* were the eastern branch of the Gothic peoples, ruled by *Theodoric the Great*, who conquered and governed Italy from 493 to 526 and built a kingdom that preserved Roman institutions under Germanic leadership. Their reign was a high point of post-Roman order, but they fell to Byzantine reconquest after two decades of warfare that devastated the Italian peninsula.

## you gotta know

- *Theodoric the Great* was raised in Constantinople and became known for his sophistication, founding a kingdom that respected Roman law and culture while maintaining Gothic military strength.
- Ruled Italy for more than three decades, claiming legitimacy from the Eastern Roman (Byzantine) Emperor while governing independently.
- Commissioned the *Mausoleum of Theodoric*, an imposing circular monument in Ravenna that stood as a symbol of the kingdom's grandeur and blend of cultures.
- Suppressed religious dissent harshly; his execution of the Roman senator *Boethius* became infamous as a symbol of cultural conflict between Roman and barbarian values.
- Faced increasing tension between the Catholic Roman population and Arian Gothic rulers, a religious divide that destabilized the kingdom.
- Conquered from the *Huns* (who had controlled them as tributaries) and in turn defeated the *Visigoths* in regional conflicts.
- Their kingdom fell to *Justinian I's* reconquest (535–554), a brutal Gothic War that left Italy depopulated and weakened; the Ostrogoths effectively ceased to exist afterward.

## connections

- [[theodoric]] — the founder and most celebrated king of the Ostrogothic realm.
- [[visigoths]] — fellow Germanic people and rivals for dominance in the Mediterranean.
- [[huns]] — originally overlords of the Ostrogoths before being defeated by *Attila's* successors.
- [[byzantine-empire]] — conqueror of the Ostrogothic kingdom and claimant to restored imperial authority.
- [[boethius]] — victim of Theodoric's religious intolerance and author of the Consolation of Philosophy.
