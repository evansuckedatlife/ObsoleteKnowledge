# Cross of Gold

## summary

William Jennings Bryan's electrifying address at the 1896 Democratic National Convention, *"You shall not press down upon the brow of labor this crown of thorns, you shall not crucify mankind upon a cross of gold,"* became the defining statement of American Populism. The speech catapulted an unknown delegate to the presidential nomination and articulated the grievances of farmers and workers against the gold standard and industrial monopoly.

## you gotta know

- Delivered at the *1896 Democratic National Convention* in Chicago on *July 8, 1896*.
- Bryan's central image: *"You shall not crucify mankind upon a cross of gold"* — yoking monetary policy to Christian martyrdom.
- Attacks the gold standard as a tool of the wealthy, arguing free coinage of silver would aid farmers and workers.
- Bryan rejects the idea that *"the man who is employed for wages is as much a businessman as his employer"* — asserting the dignity of labor.
- The speech ignited the convention; Bryan won the nomination on the first ballot despite being a relative unknown.
- Though Bryan lost the presidential election to McKinley, the speech exemplified Populism's moral vocabulary: *"we have petitioned, and our petitions have been scorned."*
- The phrase *"cross of gold"* entered the language as shorthand for oppressive economic systems.
