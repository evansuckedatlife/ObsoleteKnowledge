# Roe v. Wade

## summary

Roe v. Wade (1973) recognized a constitutional right to abortion under the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment and struck down state laws criminalizing abortion. Jane Roe (a pseudonym for Norma McCorvey), a pregnant Texas woman, sued the state seeking to invalidate the Texas abortion law criminalizing abortion except to save the mother's life. The Supreme Court, in a 7-2 decision, ruled that the Constitution protects a woman's right to choose abortion, but that this right is not absolute and must be balanced against the state's interests in protecting potential life. Justice Blackmun's majority opinion introduced the *trimester framework*: during the first trimester, the state cannot restrict abortion; during the second, the state may regulate abortion to protect maternal health; during the third, the state may prohibit abortion except when necessary to preserve the life or health of the mother. Roe became one of the most consequential and controversial Supreme Court decisions, remaining the constitutional foundation for abortion rights for nearly 50 years until its overturn in 2022.

## you gotta know

### The Case and Dispute
- *Jane Roe's situation*: In 1969, Norma McCorvey (using the pseudonym Jane Roe), a pregnant Texas woman, sought an abortion. Texas law criminalized abortion except when necessary to save the mother's life.
- *The suit*: Roe sued Henry Wade, the Dallas District Attorney, seeking a declaration that the Texas law was unconstitutional and an injunction preventing its enforcement.
- *Constitutional question*: Does the Constitution protect a woman's right to have an abortion, and if so, under what circumstances may states restrict it?
- *Prior law*: States had traditionally criminalized abortion; only a handful of states had legalized it before Roe. The case was one of the first to reach the Supreme Court on abortion rights.

### Justice Blackmun's Framework
- The Constitution protects a woman's right to choose abortion, derived from the Due Process Clause's protection of liberty and from the implied right to privacy (*Griswold v. Connecticut*, 1965).
- This right is not absolute; states have legitimate interests in protecting potential life and maternal health.
- The Court adopted a *trimester framework* to balance the woman's rights and state interests:
  - **First trimester**: The state cannot restrict abortion; the decision belongs entirely to the woman and her physician.
  - **Second trimester**: The state may regulate abortion to protect maternal health (e.g., require certain procedures, qualifications of the physician, waiting periods).
  - **Third trimester**: The state may prohibit abortion entirely, except when necessary to preserve the life or health of the mother.
- *Viability* (the point at which a fetus can survive outside the womb, roughly 24 weeks) marks the line between the second and third trimesters.

### The Dissents
- Justice White's dissent: the Constitution provides no textual basis for a right to abortion; the majority is imposing its own judgment about abortion policy.
- Justice Rehnquist's dissent: abortion is not as historically rooted a right as freedom of speech or marriage; states should be allowed to regulate it as they see fit.
- Both dissenters predicted that Roe would become a lightning rod for abortion politics and that no middle ground would be found.

### The Political and Cultural Storm
- Roe sparked a nationwide backlash from anti-abortion activists and a surge in abortion politics.
- Conservative states passed restrictions (waiting periods, parental consent laws, limits on when abortion could be obtained) designed to survive Roe but make abortion harder to access.
- Abortion became central to presidential elections, Supreme Court nominations, and national politics.

### Evolution and Erosion
- *Planned Parenthood v. Casey* (1992) reaffirmed Roe's "central holding" but replaced the trimester framework with an *undue burden* standard: states could regulate abortion at any stage, so long as regulations did not place a substantial obstacle to pre-viability abortion.
- This allowed states to impose waiting periods, parental involvement laws, and other restrictions that reduced abortion access without technically overturning Roe.
- *Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization* (2022) overturned Roe, returning abortion regulation entirely to the states; the decision sparked a new round of political conflict.

### Legacy and Reversal
- Roe stood for nearly 50 years, making it one of the Court's most durable decisions on personal liberty.
- The decision was controversial throughout; polls consistently showed Americans divided on abortion, and the decision did not settle the question (unlike some Court rulings).
- Roe's reversal in 2022 returned the nation to a patchwork of state laws, with some states banning abortion and others protecting it broadly.

## context

Roe v. Wade represents both the height and fragility of the Court's protection for unenumerated rights. The decision rests on the implication of privacy from the Due Process Clause—the same reasoning that protects contraception (*Griswold*) and same-sex relations (*Lawrence v. Texas*). By creating a constitutionally protected right to abortion, Roe removed the issue from democratic resolution and placed it on the Court. For supporters, Roe vindicated women's autonomy and equality; for opponents, it usurped the democratic process and threatened potential life. The trimester framework was Blackmun's effort to find a compromise: women get the first trimester entirely; states get the third; the second is shared. Yet the framework satisfied no one; it was attacked as arbitrary, unmoored in law, and as judicial legislation. When *Casey* replaced it with the undue burden test and *Dobbs* overturned Roe entirely, the instability of abortion's constitutional status was exposed. Roe exemplifies the inherent tension in using the Constitution to resolve contested moral questions: absent a clear textual foundation, the Court's rulings depend on the composition of the bench and can be reversed when the Court's politics shift. The absence of a stable constitutional answer has left abortion politics unsettled for 50 years and continuing.
