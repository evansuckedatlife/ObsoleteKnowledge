# Corinth

## summary

*Corinth*, perched on the isthmus connecting mainland Greece to the Peloponnese, was a wealthy maritime and commercial hub that rivaled Athens and Sparta in influence and prosperity. Its position made it an invaluable gateway for trade and military movement, and its cultural achievements—particularly in pottery and architecture—shaped early Greek art.

## you gotta know

- Located on the isthmus between mainland Greece and the Peloponnese, controlling the only land passage between them and commanding two harbors (*Lechaeum* and *Cenchreae*).
- Dominated trade routes and became the wealthiest city-state, establishing colonies across the Mediterranean (Sicily, the Adriatic, Egypt).
- Hosted the *Isthmian Games*, one of the four great Greek athletic festivals held every two years, celebrating *Poseidon*.
- Pioneered *Corinthian pottery*, featuring distinctive black-figure decoration, and exported ceramics across the known world.
- Developed Corinthian architecture, distinguished by elaborate capitals with acanthus leaves—the most ornate of the three classical orders.
- Home to the *Corinthian Order* of columns and a sanctuary of *Aphrodite* atop the acropolis that was famed for its courtesans.
- Destroyed by *Rome* in 146 BCE as punishment for resisting Roman dominance, only to be refounded a century later as a Roman colony.

## connections

- [[athens]] — rival city-state and occasional ally.
- [[sparta]] — another major power contending for supremacy.
- [[medusa]] — slain by Perseus, who came to rule Corinth according to some accounts.
- [[jason]] — leader of the Argonauts, sometimes said to have ruled Corinth.
- [[poseidon]] — deity honored at the Isthmian Games held in Corinth's honor.
