# Abbasid Caliphate

## summary

The *Abbasid Caliphate* (750–1258 CE) was the longest-lived Islamic dynasty, founded by the revolutionary Abbasid family and centered in Baghdad, which became the cultural and intellectual heartland of the Islamic world. The Abbasids patronized science, mathematics, medicine, and philosophy at the legendary *House of Wisdom*, but their power fragmented as regional dynasties claimed independence, until the Mongol conquest ended their rule.

## you gotta know

- Established *Baghdad* in 762 CE as the new capital, a planned city that became the largest and most cosmopolitan metropolis of the medieval world.
- Inaugurated the *House of Wisdom* (*Bayt al-Hikma*) in the 9th century, where scholars translated Greek, Persian, and Indian texts, preserving classical knowledge and advancing mathematics, astronomy, and medicine.
- Shifted power from Arab aristocrats to a Persian-influenced bureaucratic elite, promoting non-Arabs and fostering a more inclusive Islamic identity centered on religious learning rather than Arab kinship.
- Produced towering intellectual figures: *Al-Ghazali* in philosophy and theology, *Al-Razi* and *Ibn Sina* in medicine, *Al-Khwarizmi* in mathematics and algorithms.
- Gradually lost territorial control as regional dynasties—*Fatimid*, *Seljuk*, *Mamluk*—carved out autonomous zones, leaving the Baghdad caliphs symbolic rather than imperial authority by the 11th century.
- Destroyed by the *Mongol siege of Baghdad* in 1258 CE under *Hulagu Khan*, which devastated the city and ended the caliphate, though it continued in exile in *Cairo* under Mamluk protection.
