# Treaty of Nanking

## summary

The *Treaty of Nanking* (1842) ended the *First Opium War* between *Britain* and *China*, signed aboard a British warship in Nanking (Nanjing). The treaty forced *China* to cede *Hong Kong* to Britain, open five "treaty ports" to foreign trade, pay a massive indemnity, and grant *Britain* most-favoured-nation status. It marked the beginning of the "Unequal Treaties" era, in which Western powers extracted concessions from a weakening China, humiliating the once-dominant empire and accelerating its decline.

## you gotta know

### War & Context
- The *First Opium War* (1840–1842) was fought because China tried to halt British opium smuggling; Britain invaded to force markets open.
- The Qing dynasty, militarily outmatched by British naval and industrial superiority, capitulated after losing major cities.
- Signed on *August 29, 1842*; opened the floodgates for further Western incursions into China.

### Territorial Losses
- *Hong Kong* ceded "in perpetuity" to Britain—Britain's gateway to China and later a global financial centre.
- *China* forced to recognise Britain's sovereignty over an Asian territory, an unprecedented humiliation for the imperial power.
- Later treaties (1860, 1898) extended British concessions and added other Western powers' territories.

### Economic & Trade Concessions
- Five "treaty ports" (*Guangzhou, Amoy, Fuzhou, Ningpo, Shanghai*) opened to British trade, ending China's monopoly over foreign commerce.
- *China* forced to pay an indemnity of 21 million silver dollars—crippling for the imperial treasury.
- Britain granted "most-favoured-nation" status: any benefits granted to other powers automatically extended to Britain.
- Trade restrictions on opium removed; British merchants flooded China with opium, destroying millions of Chinese lives.

### Sovereignty & Extraterritoriality Framework
- British subjects in China exempt from Chinese law (extraterritoriality)—a principle extending to other Western powers via later treaties.
- No Chinese representative signed the treaty; it was imposed unilaterally, establishing the "Unequal Treaties" template.
- China forced to treat Britain as an equal power, reversing the Qing system of tributary relations.

## context

The Treaty of Nanking is a watershed in Chinese and world history: it marks the moment China ceased to be the world's dominant power and became an object of Western exploitation. For the Qing dynasty, the treaty was catastrophic—it revealed military obsolescence (Chinese junks vs. British steel ships and artillery), economic vulnerability (a massive foreign debt), and loss of sovereignty (extraterritoriality). For Britain and the West, it vindicated the use of force to pry open markets; the "Opium Wars" demonstrated that industrial military technology could overcome continental empires. The treaty spawned a century of similar treaties (French, American, German, Russian) all extracting concessions from a weakening Qing. The humiliation and unequal terms burned deep in Chinese nationalist consciousness, becoming a rallying cry for revolution (the Boxers, Sun Yat-sen, the communists) and a defining grievance of 20th-century China. The treaty thus not only partitioned China among Western powers but also seeded the ideological currents that would eventually expel them. Hong Kong's cession, finally reversed in 1997, symbolises China's century of subjugation and its 21st-century resurgence.
