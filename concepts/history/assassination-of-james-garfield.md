# Assassination of James A. Garfield

## summary

On July 2, 1881, President *James A. Garfield* was shot by *Charles J. Guiteau*, a delusional office-seeker angered by his rejection for a diplomatic position. Garfield died 79 days later from complications of the wound and infection, making him the second American president to be assassinated. The assassination exposed the dangers of the patronage system and catalyzed reform of the civil service, transforming how the federal government hired its officials.

## you gotta know

### The Shooting
- Shot on July 2, 1881, in the Baltimore and Potomac Railroad Station in Washington, D.C.
- Assassin: *Charles J. Guiteau*, a failed lawyer and office-seeker suffering from delusions of importance.
- Guiteau fired two shots; one passed through Garfield's arm, the other lodged near his spine.
- Garfield was initially conscious and seemed likely to recover, but died on September 19 from a combination of the wound and medical complications (likely infection introduced by repeated probing).

### Guiteau's Motive
- Guiteau had petitioned Garfield for the position of consul to France and, upon rejection, became consumed with resentment.
- He believed he had been instrumental in Garfield's election and was owed a position by the patronage system.
- Guiteau's delusions suggest he suffered from mental illness; he claimed he was acting in the best interest of the Republican party.
- He was arrested immediately and executed by hanging in 1882.

### Political Consequences
- The assassination sparked public outrage at the patronage system, which had enabled unqualified men like Guiteau to roam the halls of power seeking appointments.
- Congress passed the *Pendleton Act* in 1883, creating a merit-based civil service and reducing the power of patronage politics.
- Vice President *Chester Arthur*, himself a product of the patronage machine, surprisingly supported civil service reform.
- The event marks the beginning of the transition from 19th-century spoils politics to modern bureaucratic governance.

## context

Garfield's assassination was as much a commentary on American political institutions as on Guiteau himself. The shooting illustrated how the patronage system—a fixture of 19th-century politics—created a class of supplicants and malcontents with access to power but no legitimate claim on it. Unlike the assassinations of Lincoln (political ideology) or Kennedy (contested motives), Garfield's death was the act of a deranged individual enabled by a broken system. Yet paradoxically, his death led to the repair of that system. In quiz bowl and American political history, the Garfield assassination stands as a turning point from the spoils era to civil service professionalization—a small price paid for a large institutional reform.
