# Election of 1968

## summary

Richard Nixon's comeback victory in 1968 over Vice President Hubert Humphrey ended a generation of Democratic dominance and marked a decisive turn to the right. Nixon won 43.4 percent of the popular vote and 301 electoral votes, carrying the South and suburbs while Humphrey, hamstrung by Johnson's unpopular war in Vietnam, managed only 42.7 percent. George Wallace's American Independent campaign captured 13.5 percent and 46 electoral votes from the South, crystallizing the realignment: the traditional Democratic coalition had fractured over race, war, and social disorder.

## you gotta know

### The Three-Way Contest
- *Richard Nixon* campaigned as the "law and order" candidate, promising to end the Vietnam War through "peace with honor" while cracking down on urban crime and protests.
- *Hubert Humphrey*, the sitting vice president, was crippled by his association with Lyndon Johnson's Vietnam escalation; he could not break free without repudiating his president.
- *George Wallace*, the Alabama governor and segregationist, captured nearly 14 percent of the popular vote—the strongest third-party showing since 1912—running against "pointy-headed intellectuals" and civil rights expansion.

### The Vietnam War and Social Upheaval
- The Tet Offensive (January 1968) had turned American public opinion decisively against the war; by spring, Johnson withdrew from the race.
- The assassinations of Martin Luther King Jr. (April) and Robert Kennedy (June) further destabilized the country and energized backlash politics.
- Urban riots, antiwar protests, and student unrest created a sense of social crisis that favored law-and-order messages.

### The Realignment
- Nixon's "Southern Strategy" explicitly courted white southern Democrats alienated by civil rights; the South began its shift toward the GOP.
- Humphrey's narrow loss (0.7 percent nationally) masked a deeper Democratic fracture: the working-class and ethnic voters who had built the New Deal Coalition were shifting toward Nixon and Wallace.

## context

The 1968 election marked the final rupture of the New Deal Coalition. The civil rights movement, which had been embraced by liberals in 1964 (Goldwater's crushing defeat), had become a liability in 1968 as urban unrest and busing controversies mobilized white backlash. The Vietnam War, escalated under Kennedy and massively expanded under Johnson, had shattered liberal consensus and radicalized young voters. Wallace's strong third-party showing proved that there was a significant constituency for outright opposition to civil rights and federal overreach. Nixon's appeal—a promise to restore social order, disengage from Vietnam (while appearing tough on communism), and govern from the center-right—proved victorious. The election inaugurated an era of Republican dominance in presidential politics (the GOP won 5 of 6 elections from 1968–1988) and the so-called "Reagan realignment" that still shapes American politics.
