# Battle of Shiloh

## summary

The Battle of Shiloh (April 6–7, 1862), fought near a small Methodist church in southwestern Tennessee, shocked both North and South with its casualty toll. Confederate General Albert Sidney Johnston surprised Union General Ulysses S. Grant's encamped army on the first day, nearly driving it into the Tennessee River. However, Union reinforcements under Don Carlos Buell arrived on the second day; the Confederate counteroffensive faltered, and Johnston died from wounds suffered in battle. The engagement cost each side roughly 10,000 casualties in just two days—more than all previous battles combined—and marked Americans' first confrontation with the scale of modern industrial warfare.

## you gotta know

### Background
- Grant's army was waiting for Buell's reinforcements at Pittsburg Landing, believing no immediate Confederate threat existed.
- Confederate commanders, seeking to strike before the Union could consolidate forces, planned a surprise assault.
- The attack achieved tactical surprise; Southern forces crashed through the Union camps at dawn on April 6.

### The Battle
- Union forces, disorganized and scattered, fell back; the sector called the *Hornet's Nest* saw desperate hand-to-hand fighting.
- Johnston was killed in the late afternoon; *General Braxton Bragg* assumed command and pressed the attack.
- Buell's reinforcements began crossing the river on the evening of April 6; Grant's line, though shattered, held near the landing.

### Aftermath
- On April 7, the Union counterattack pushed the Confederates back off the field; they retreated southward.
- Total casualties exceeded 24,000; the two-day toll stunned both North and South and signaled that the war would be far longer and bloodier than anticipated.
- Grant's reputation was momentarily tarnished; Lincoln defended him, but it was clear that victory would demand greater strategic skill.

## context

Shiloh was the first battle to demonstrate the horrific potential of massed infantry armed with rifled muskets. The casualty count—nearly double the total of the Revolution—awakened both sides to the mechanized nature of nineteenth-century warfare. For the Union, Shiloh justified grant's eventual promotion; his refusal to panic and his willingness to fight again the next day became his trademark. For the Confederacy, the loss of Johnston, one of Lee's few peers as a general, was irreplaceable. The battle opened the Western Theater campaign that would ultimately fracture the Confederacy.
