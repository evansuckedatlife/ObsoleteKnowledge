# Nathanael Greene

## summary

*Nathanael Greene* was the commander of the Continental Army's southern department and one of the war's most brilliant strategists. Though he won few pitched battles, he executed a masterful campaign of harassment and supply disruption that exhausted British forces in the Carolinas and Georgia, forcing them back toward the coast.

## you gotta know

- Took command of the southern theater in 1780 after the American collapse at *Charleston*, inheriting a war of attrition.
- Divided his small force into mobile units that avoided decisive engagement while attacking British supply lines and isolated garrisons.
- Won a strategic victory at *Guilford Courthouse* (March 1781) by fighting and retreating in good order, inflicting so many British casualties that General *Cornwallis* was forced to withdraw toward the coast.
- Deliberately sacrificed the *Catawba* and *Pee Dee* rivers regions, trading space for time to gather supplies and recruits while the British army grew weaker.
- Reclaimed *Charleston* and *Savannah* by siege and maneuver without major battles, demonstrating that control of territory lay not in one decisive clash but in grinding British supply chains to a halt.
- Never defeated in the field, despite being outnumbered; his attrition strategy proved the template for asymmetric warfare.

## connections

- [[george-washington]] — the commander-in-chief who gave Greene operational freedom despite political pressure to fight.
- [[horatio-gates]] — his predecessor in the southern theater, whose defeat at *Camden* left Greene with a depleted force.
- [[cornwallis|Charles Cornwallis]] — his primary British antagonist in the South, worn down by Greene's mobile strategy.
- [[american-revolution]] — the southern campaign Greene waged became a model for irregular warfare.
- [[southern-campaign]] — the theater that shifted the war's balance in the Patriots' favor.
