# Assassination of Anwar Sadat

## summary

On October 6, 1981, Egyptian President *Anwar Sadat* was shot and killed by members of the militant Islamic group *Egyptian Islamic Jihad* during a military parade in *Cairo*. The assassination was motivated by Sadat's peace treaty with *Israel*, his suppression of Islamic activism, and his alignment with the West during the Cold War. The event marked a turning point in Middle Eastern politics, exposing the rise of Islamist opposition to secular Arab nationalism and the dangers of isolated leadership.

## you gotta know

### The Assassination
- Occurred October 6, 1981, during a military parade celebrating the anniversary of the 1973 Yom Kippur War in Cairo.
- Perpetrators: *Egyptian Islamic Jihad*, a militant Islamist organization later linked to Al-Qaeda.
- Assassins fired from the reviewing stand; Sadat was struck multiple times and pronounced dead within the hour.
- The attack also killed several senior military and government officials and wounded others in the stands.

### Political Motivation
- Sadat's 1979 *Camp David Accords* with *Israel*, brokered by U.S. President *Jimmy Carter*, made him a pariah in much of the Arab world.
- Islamic militants opposed his secular, Western-aligned government and saw the peace with Israel as a betrayal of Arab and Islamic solidarity.
- Sadat had imprisoned thousands of Islamic activists and Coptic Christians in the months before his death as part of a crackdown on dissent.
- The assassins claimed to be defending Islam and Arab dignity against imperialism and Zionism.

### Consequences
- *Hosni Mubarak*, Sadat's vice president, succeeded him and ruled Egypt for 30 years, maintaining the Israeli peace and an authoritarian system.
- The assassination vindicated the warnings of Arab nationalists and Islamic activists who had opposed the Camp David Accords.
- It demonstrated that Islamist militancy, not secular pan-Arabism, was becoming the dominant ideological force in the Middle East.
- The event was a harbinger of the rise of modern militant Islamism throughout the region.

## context

Sadat's assassination marks a critical inflection point in Middle Eastern history: the moment when Islamic political opposition became violent and capable of striking at the highest levels of state power. While Sadat had moved Egypt away from Soviet alignment toward the American sphere and toward peace with Israel—achievements of historic magnitude—he had also isolated himself politically by suppressing dissent and ignoring the rising power of Islamic sentiment. The assassination exposed the limits of imposed peace and revealed that a leader's personal security cannot be guaranteed if large segments of the population feel betrayed by his policies. In quiz bowl and modern history, the event is inseparable from the rise of Islamist movements, the Israeli-Palestinian conflict, and the Cold War's end in the Middle East.
