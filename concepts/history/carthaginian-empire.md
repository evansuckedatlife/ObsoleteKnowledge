# Carthaginian Empire

## summary

The Carthaginian Empire (814–146 BCE) was the Mediterranean's dominant naval and commercial power before its confrontation with Rome, built by Phoenician colonists who established a trade-based empire stretching from North Africa to Spain and the western Mediterranean islands. Unlike territorial empires that governed conquered lands directly, Carthage maintained a maritime and commercial network—controlling trade in tin, amber, and agricultural products—and relied on mercenary armies rather than citizen soldiers. At its peak under *Hannibal* (247–183 BCE), the famous general who nearly destroyed Rome during the *Second Punic War* (218–201 BCE), Carthage threatened Roman dominance; however, Rome's superior resources and manpower, combined with Hannibal's strategic isolation from home support, eventually defeated Carthage. The *Third Punic War* (149–146 BCE) ended in Carthage's complete destruction—its inhabitants enslaved or killed and the city razed—making it the ancient world's most thorough annihilation of a rival power.

## you gotta know

### Phoenician Founding and Commercial Empire
- *Carthage* was founded around 814 BCE by *Phoenician* colonists from *Tyre* (in modern Lebanon) as a trading post and eventually became a mercantile empire controlling the western Mediterranean's commerce.
- Unlike Rome, which conquered and governed territories, Carthage maintained a *maritime empire* based on trade monopolies, naval bases, and tributary relationships with merchants—a model vulnerable to direct military challenge.
- The city was ruled by merchant oligarchs and occasionally by a powerful general (*suffete*, the highest office)—a republic, like Rome, but one where wealth trumped political participation.

### Hannibal and the Punic Wars
- *Hannibal* (247–183 BCE), the son of *Hamilcar Barca*, took command of Carthage's Spanish territories and began preparations for a second war with Rome over dominance in the Mediterranean.
- The *First Punic War* (264–241 BCE) saw Rome defeat Carthage and seize Sicily, Sardinia, and Corsica; the *Second Punic War* (218–201 BCE) was Hannibal's attempt to destroy Rome by invading Italy from Spain.
- Hannibal's *Punic* *War march* took him from Spain through southern Gaul, over the Alps (losing many elephants), and into Italy with 50,000 troops; he defeated Rome repeatedly at *Cannae* (216 BCE)—considered the most brilliant tactical victory in history—and at the *Trebia River* (218 BCE).
- Despite his undefeated record in Italy, Hannibal could not take Rome itself, could not secure sufficient reinforcements from Carthage, and faced Roman recovery under *Scipio Africanus*, who invaded North Africa and defeated Hannibal at *Zama* (202 BCE), ending the war.

### Decline and Destruction
- After the *Second Punic War*, Carthage was economically exhausted and stripped of overseas territories, reduced to a regional North African power paying tribute to Rome.
- The *Third Punic War* (149–146 BCE) broke out over Roman suspicion of Carthaginian revival; Rome's general *Scipio Aemilianus* besieged Carthage, starved it into submission, and ordered the complete destruction of the city—a shocking act that demonstrates Rome's fear of Carthaginian revival.
- The site remained uninhabited for decades; later, Rome established a new *Carthago* as a colonial city, but the Carthaginian Empire had utterly ceased to exist.

### Legacy and Comparison
- Carthage represents an alternative model of ancient empire: commercial, naval, and mercenary-based rather than territorial and citizen-based. Rome's model ultimately proved superior because citizen armies fight more desperately than mercenaries and because control of land (not just trade) grants long-term stability.
- Hannibal remains history's most celebrated losing general because his tactics (the double envelopment at Cannae) set the template for military genius and his strategic problem (insufficient support from home) remains eternally relevant.

## context

Carthage embodies a persistent ancient and modern tension: the confrontation between a land empire and a maritime commercial power. Athens vs. Sparta, Spain vs. Portugal (in the Age of Exploration), and Britain vs. France (in the 18th century) replayed the Carthage-Rome dynamic. Rome's total destruction of Carthage was unusual in its harshness; most ancient empires absorbed defeated rivals or reduced them to vassals, but Rome feared Carthage's mercantile wealth and maritime capacity enough to obliterate it. This fear was justified: within a century of Carthage's fall, Rome's own civil wars would threaten its stability (the *Punic Wars* had severely drained Rome's resources too), suggesting that Carthage, if given a reprieve, might have recovered. For quiz bowl, Carthage tests knowledge of Hannibal's tactics (Cannae, the double envelopment), the three Punic Wars, and the contrast between commercial and territorial empires.
