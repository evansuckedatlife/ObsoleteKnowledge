# Alexander Hamilton

## summary

*Alexander Hamilton* was a Founding Father of the United States whose bold financial and political vision shaped the young nation's economic system and constitutional structure. A self-made immigrant from the Caribbean, he rose from poverty to become George Washington's trusted aide, a delegate to the Constitutional Convention, the chief architect of American financial policy as the first Secretary of the Treasury, and one of the three principal authors of the *Federalist Papers*. His rivalry with Thomas Jefferson defined American politics in the 1790s; his legacy remains divisive, celebrated as the creator of American economic power and condemned for his monarchist sympathies.

## you gotta know

- Born in the British West Indies in 1757 (or 1755—records are unclear), illegitimate and orphaned, he educated himself and emigrated to New York as a teenager, working his way up through hard work and writing.
- Served as George Washington's aide-de-camp during the Revolutionary War, forming a deep bond with Washington and absorbing military and political leadership firsthand.
- Drafted *The Federalist* essays (with Madison and Jay) in 1787–88 to argue for ratification of the *U.S. Constitution*, providing the most authoritative commentary on constitutional intent and design; these essays remain the definitive guide to understanding the founders' thinking.
- Proposed at the Constitutional Convention the notion of a powerful executive (a "president for life," in his notes, later tempered) and a bicameral legislature, sketching the framework for a strong central government.
- As Secretary of the Treasury (1789–1795) under Washington, established the nation's financial credibility by assuming state war debts into federal debt, creating a national bank, and establishing a federal mint—building the institutional foundation of American capitalism.
- Advocated for tariffs to protect American manufacturing, the creation of a merchant marine, and federal investment in infrastructure, laying out a proto-industrial vision that later Republicans would embrace.
- Clashed repeatedly with Secretary of State Thomas Jefferson over the scope of executive power and the role of commercial interests in government, a rivalry that crystallized into nascent Democratic-Republican and Federalist parties.

## connections

- [[george-washington]] — his mentor and president, whose trust and patronage made Hamilton's rise possible; he was Washington's closest advisor.
- [[thomas-jefferson]] — his great rival in Washington's cabinet, representing agrarian democracy against Hamilton's commercial republic.
- [[aaron-burr]] — his political nemesis and eventual killer; they faced off in a duel in 1804 that killed Hamilton at 47.
- [[james-madison]] — his collaborator on the Federalist Papers, later a political opponent.
- [[federalist-papers]] — the essays whose co-authorship established his as the premier voice explaining constitutional authority.
- [[american-revolution]] — the cause he served as Washington's aide, experience that shaped his vision of centralized power.
- [[u-s-constitution]] — the document he advocated for and interpreted as expansively as possible to build federal strength.
- [[first-bank-of-the-united-states]] — the institution he created to anchor the nation's financial system.
