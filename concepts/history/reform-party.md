# Reform Party

## summary

The Reform Party, founded by Texas billionaire Ross Perot in 1995, was a centrist third party focused on federal deficit reduction, campaign finance reform, and fiscal responsibility. Running Perot as its presidential candidate in 1996, the party achieved 8.4% of the popular vote, the strongest third-party showing since Theodore Roosevelt's 1912 Bull Moose campaign, but fractured over personality conflicts and declining interest in deficit politics after the late-1990s economic boom.

## you gotta know

- Perot first ran in 1992 as an independent, winning an extraordinary 18.9% of the popular vote by emphasizing the deficit and critiquing the two-party duopoly; his strong performance energized supporters to formally establish the Reform Party for 1996.
- The party's platform prioritized reducing the federal deficit, eliminating the national debt over a fixed timeline, and implementing campaign finance reform and term limits for Congress.
- Perot's 1996 Reform Party candidacy netted 8.4% of the popular vote and no electoral votes, appealing to fiscally conservative voters alienated from both parties but not achieving durable organizational strength.
- Perot was a charismatic but erratic candidate whose charts-and-graphs populism (popularized through infomercials) appealed to voters fed up with conventional politics and concerned about national fiscal health.
- The party quickly declined after Perot's loss, factionalizing over whether to seek a moderate Republican or a progressive populist direction; by 2000, it was factionalized and moribund.
- Donald Trump's 2016 and 2020 campaigns drew some former Reform Party voters, but the party itself never revived; it exemplifies how charisma-dependent third parties often collapse when their founder departs.
