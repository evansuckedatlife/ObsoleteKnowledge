# Theodoric

## summary

*Theodoric* (c. 454–526), known as *Theodoric the Great*, was the king of the Ostrogoths who conquered Italy in 493 CE and established the Ostrogothic kingdom, ruling as the nominal successor to the Roman emperors. Raised as a hostage at the Byzantine court in Constantinople, he synthesized Germanic warrior culture with Roman administrative tradition, attempting to preserve Roman civilization while governing as a barbarian king. Though his reign is often remembered as a brief moment of stability in the chaotic post-Roman West, it ultimately failed to survive his death, giving way to the Byzantine reconquest and the complete disappearance of the Ostrogothic kingdom.

## you gotta know

- Born around 454, son of the Ostrogothic king Theodemir; sent as a young hostage to the court of the Byzantine Emperor *Zeno*, where he was educated in Greek language, court etiquette, and Roman governance.
- Returned to lead the Ostrogoths and, with Byzantine sanction and military support, invaded Italy in 488 CE, defeating the Germanic king *Odoacer* who had deposed the last Western Roman Emperor in 476.
- Conquered the Italian peninsula and established himself as king (rex) of the Ostrogoths, ruling from his capital at Ravenna (476–493), nominally recognizing Byzantine sovereignty while wielding independent power.
- Governed using Roman institutions: maintained the Senate, appointed Roman magistrates, preserved the tax system, and kept the Roman army structure largely intact—attempting to present himself as a continuation of Roman rule rather than a barbarian replacement.
- Married *Audofleda*, sister of the Frankish king *Clovis*, creating a matrimonial alliance that reflected his ambition to operate at the highest level of Mediterranean politics.
- Commissioned the construction of churches, palaces, and public works, including the mausoleum at Ravenna that bears his name, demonstrating his commitment to Roman cultural continuity.
- Hired the philosopher *Boethius* (author of the *Consolation of Philosophy*) and his son *Symmachus* as chief administrators, cultural bridges between Germanic rule and Roman civilization.
- Grew increasingly suspicious of the Byzantine court and his own Italian-Roman elite, executing both Boethius and Symmachus near the end of his life, eroding the delicate balance he had maintained.
