# Election of 1876

## summary

The 1876 election between Republican Rutherford B. Hayes and Democrat Samuel Tilden produced the most disputed result in American history until 2000. Tilden won the popular vote by a decisive margin and held the edge in electoral votes, but disputed returns from the three remaining reconstructed southern states threw the outcome into chaos. A specially created Electoral Commission, controlled by Republicans, awarded all contested electors to Hayes in exchange for the Compromise of 1877—which ended Reconstruction and abandoned freed slaves to southern white rule.

## you gotta know

### The Disputed Results
- *Samuel Tilden* won the popular vote (51 percent) and carried 184 electoral votes, needing only one more for victory.
- *Rutherford Hayes* held 165 electoral votes; twenty electors from South Carolina, Florida, and Louisiana were disputed.
- Each state sent conflicting returns: Republican canvassing boards submitted one set; Democratic authorities submitted another—fraud and intimidation likely on both sides.

### The Electoral Commission and the Compromise
- Congress created a 15-member Electoral Commission (8 Republicans, 7 Democrats) to adjudicate the disputes.
- The Commission voted 8-7 on every disputed return, always for Hayes, awarding him the presidency by one electoral vote (185-184).
- The backroom deal: Hayes would end federal protection for freed slaves in the South, withdraw the last occupation troops, and allow southern whites to regain political control.

### The Outcome and Legacy
- Hayes became president despite losing the popular vote—a setback for democratic legitimacy.
- Reconstruction ended; the "Solid South" emerged as a Democratic stronghold, solidifying white southern power for nearly a century.

## context

The 1876 election exposed the fragility of Reconstruction-era democracy in the South. Republicans had relied on federal troops and Black voters to maintain political control in the former Confederate states, but northern will to sustain that occupation was exhausted. The election became a bargaining opportunity: Hayes's southern supporters—mostly railroad magnates and "New South" industrialists—wanted federal investment and autonomy; northern Republicans wanted to move past Reconstruction. The result was Reconstruction's death and the birth of Jim Crow. The compromise also established a precedent: disputed elections could be resolved by political negotiation rather than law, making electoral contests more volatile. The 1876 election thus represents the moment when the promise of Reconstruction—equality before law for all Americans—was sacrificed to sectional reunion.
