# Separation of Powers

## summary

Separation of powers is the constitutional principle that divides governmental authority among three distinct branches—the legislative (Congress), executive (Presidency), and judicial (courts)—to prevent the concentration of power in a single entity. Enshrined in *Articles I, II, and III* of the U.S. Constitution, the doctrine was championed by Enlightenment philosophers like *Montesquieu* and is considered foundational to American democracy. Each branch is given distinct powers and the ability to check the others' authority, creating a system of balanced government intended to protect liberty and prevent tyranny.

## context

Separation of powers is more than a constitutional structure—it is a foundational theory of democratic governance, repeated in nearly every modern constitution. The Framers drew on both ancient and Enlightenment sources to create a system deliberately designed to make governing difficult, distributing power horizontally to prevent executive tyranny (the fear after British monarchy) and vertically to preserve federalism. Court cases from *Youngstown Sheet & Tube v. Sawyer* to *United States v. Nixon* to *City of Arlington v. FCC* continue to refine the boundaries between branches and their respective powers. Understanding separation of powers is essential to understanding why American government is often gridlocked and slow.

## you gotta know

### the three branches
- *Legislative branch* (Congress, Articles I): power to make laws, control federal spending, declare war, confirm judges and cabinet members.
- *Executive branch* (President, Article II): power to enforce laws, command the military, negotiate treaties, appoint judges and cabinet members, veto legislation.
- *Judicial branch* (Courts, Article III): power to interpret laws, adjudicate disputes, and declare laws unconstitutional.

### checks and balances
- The President can *veto* legislation, but Congress can override a veto with a two-thirds supermajority.
- Congress confirms the President's judicial and cabinet appointments; the Senate ratifies treaties.
- The courts can strike down laws and executive actions as unconstitutional (*judicial review*).
- Congress can impeach and remove the President, judges, and other federal officials.
- Congress controls the judiciary's structure and budget.

### philosophical origins
- *Montesquieu's* 1748 work *The Spirit of the Laws* articulated the separation of powers doctrine.
- *Federalist Papers* #47–#51 (by *James Madison*) defended separation of powers as essential to republican government.
- The doctrine built on earlier English constitutional tradition (e.g., *Magna Carta*, English Bill of Rights).

### key cases
- *Marbury v. Madison* (1803): established judicial review, a core check by the judiciary.
- *Youngstown Sheet & Tube Co. v. Sawyer* (1952): limited presidential power during the Korean War; held that the President cannot take property without constitutional or statutory authorization.
- *United States v. Nixon* (1974): established that the President has executive privilege but not absolute immunity from judicial process.
- *Myers v. United States* (1926): delimited Congressional power to restrict the President's removal authority over executive officers.
