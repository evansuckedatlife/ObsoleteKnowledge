# Peterloo

## summary

The **Peterloo Massacre** occurred on August 16, 1819, in Manchester, England, when British cavalry and militia charged into a peaceful crowd of 60,000 to 100,000 political reformers gathered to hear orator *Henry Hunt* speak in support of parliamentary reform and working-class representation. The name, a dark joke alluding to the Napoleonic Battle of Waterloo, was coined by reformers to describe what they saw as a military attack on civilians exercising the right of assembly. Between 15 and 18 people died in the immediate aftermath, with dozens more wounded; the event became a catalyst for the nascent labor and reform movements and prompted the government's repressive *Six Acts*.

## you gotta know

### The Meeting
- The reform rally took place on St. Peter's Field in Manchester, a manufacturing center with a large working-class population suffering from industrial depression and excluded from political representation.
- *Henry Hunt*, a radical orator from London, was invited to address the crowd; attendees included workers, their families, and supporters of the *Hampden Clubs*, organizations advocating democratic reform.
- The assembly was peaceful and orderly; banners called for universal suffrage and the repeal of the *Corn Laws*; the crowd was unarmed.

### The Charge
- Local magistrates, alarmed by the size and political character of the gathering, ordered the cavalry and militia to disperse the crowd and arrest Hunt.
- Soldiers, many of them inexperienced and nervous, charged the densely packed crowd on horseback with sabres drawn.
- In the chaos, people were trampled and slashed; women and children were knocked down; the crowd had limited routes to escape.

### The Aftermath
- Estimates of deaths ranged from 15 to 18; hundreds were injured; Hunt and other organizers were arrested and charged with sedition.
- The crowd dispersed in panic; bodies and the wounded were left on the field; the shocking scene was reported in newspapers across Britain.
- Trials of the organizers resulted in acquittals or light sentences; the magistrates and military officers faced no prosecution.

### Political Consequences
- The government responded with the *Six Acts* (1819), repressive legislation targeting public assemblies, seditious speech, and the radical press.
- Peterloo energized the reform movement rather than suppressing it; the event became a rallying cry for advocates of universal suffrage and workers' rights.
- The massacre was commemorated annually by reformers and became a founding myth of the labor movement in Britain.

### Legacy
- Peterloo contributed to the eventual passage of the *Great Reform Act* of 1832, which expanded the franchise.
- The event demonstrated that British military and magistrates would use force against peacefully assembled civilians; it hardened the conviction among reformers that the system had to be overthrown rather than petitioned.

## context

Peterloo occurred at the intersection of post-Napoleonic anxiety, industrial social dislocation, and the emergence of modern political consciousness. The British ruling class feared that popular demonstrations, inspired by French revolutionary precedent, would spiral into insurrection; military officers and magistrates acted preemptively, striking at the heart of lawful assembly itself. The massacre revealed a profound contradiction in British governance: the Crown claimed to defend liberty and order, yet attacked unarmed citizens exercising fundamental rights. For contemporaries, Peterloo was shocking precisely because Britain considered itself a civilized nation with the rule of law; yet cavalry had charged women and children at a political rally. The event galvanized the reform movements of the nineteenth century and became a foundational text in the theory of civil disobedience and state violence.
