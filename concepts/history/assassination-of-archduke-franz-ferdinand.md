# Assassination of Archduke Franz Ferdinand

## summary

On June 28, 1914, *Archduke Franz Ferdinand*, heir to the *Austro-Hungarian* throne, was shot and killed in *Sarajevo* by *Gavrilo Princip*, a Bosnian Serb member of the revolutionary group *Black Hand*. The assassination triggered a chain of diplomatic crises and military mobilizations that led to *World War I* within weeks, making it arguably the single event with the greatest geopolitical consequences in modern history.

## you gotta know

### The Assassination
- Occurred June 28, 1914, in *Sarajevo*, Bosnia-Herzegovina.
- Assassin: *Gavrilo Princip*, age 19, a Bosnian Serb nationalist opposed to Austro-Hungarian rule.
- Franz Ferdinand was shot while riding in an open-topped motorcar with his wife, *Sophie, Duchess of Hohenberg*.
- Both the archduke and his wife were killed; the chauffeur was also struck.

### The Black Hand & Political Context
- Princip was a member of *Black Hand*, a Serbian nationalist secret society dedicated to liberating Slavic lands from Austro-Hungarian control.
- The assassination was part of a coordinated plot; multiple would-be assassins had been positioned along the motorcade route.
- Franz Ferdinand's motorcade actually escaped an earlier bomb attempt that same morning before Princip's gun succeeded.
- Princip was captured immediately and died in prison in 1918.

### Path to World War
- Austria-Hungary issued a harsh ultimatum to *Serbia*, blaming it for the assassination.
- Serbia's defiance triggered Austro-Hungarian mobilization; Russia mobilized to defend Serbia; Germany sided with Austria-Hungary; France sided with Russia.
- The alliance system and rigid mobilization schedules turned a regional Balkan crisis into a general European war by August 1914.

## context

The assassination of Franz Ferdinand holds a unique place in historical consciousness: it is the moment when the *ancien régime* of European empires, monarchy, and balance-of-power politics collides with nationalist fervor and begins to collapse. The date June 28, 1914, marks the boundary between the long nineteenth century and the catastrophic twentieth. No other assassination has triggered such immediate, inexorable geopolitical consequences—within six weeks of Princip's shots, millions were mobilizing for war. The event also highlights the role of contingency: the archduke's motorcade took a wrong turn that morning, putting Princip directly in its path after several earlier assassination attempts failed. In quiz bowl, it is the canonical example of how a single violent act—the act of one person—can reshape the world.
