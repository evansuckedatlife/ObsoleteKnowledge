# Dred Scott v. Sandford

## summary

*Dred Scott v. Sandford* (1857) was the Supreme Court's most infamous decision before the Civil War, ruling that enslaved African Americans had no legal standing to sue in federal courts and that Congress could not ban slavery in the territories. Chief Justice Roger Taney held that African Americans, whether enslaved or free, could not be citizens of the United States, intensifying sectional conflict and pushing the nation toward rupture.

## you gotta know

- Dred Scott was an enslaved man whose owner, John Emerson, had taken him to live in free territory; Scott sued for his freedom on grounds of his residence.
- Chief Justice *Roger Taney* ruled that Scott had no right to sue because he was not a citizen—that at the nation's founding, African Americans were viewed as "having no rights which the white man was bound to respect."
- The Court struck down the *Missouri Compromise*, holding that Congress had no authority to prohibit slavery in federal territories.
- The decision denied citizenship to all people of African descent, enslaved or free, undermining the legal status of free Black people in the North.
- Enraged Republicans and abolitionists; deepened the sectional divide and is widely seen as precipitating events leading to the Civil War.
- The decision was effectively overturned by the *13th Amendment* (abolishing slavery) and the *14th Amendment* (granting citizenship to all born in the U.S.).
