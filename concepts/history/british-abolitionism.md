## summary

**British Abolitionism** was the political and moral movement to end slavery throughout the British Empire, driven by a coalition of religious groups, middle-class reformers, and working-class sympathizers. Emerging as a significant force in the late eighteenth century, the movement achieved a historic victory with the passage of the *Slavery Abolition Act of 1833*, which abolished slavery throughout the Empire. British abolitionism was the nineteenth century's most successful reform movement, demonstrating the power of moral persuasion, public opinion, and organized pressure; it inspired similar movements worldwide and established Britain's self-image as a moral guardian of Christian civilization.

## you gotta know

- *William Wilberforce*, an MP and devout Christian, led the parliamentary campaign against slavery from 1787 onward; his persistence through decades of defeat exemplified abolitionist commitment.
- The movement united unlikely allies: Quakers and evangelical Christians (citing religious principle), political radicals (arguing for universal human rights), economists (contending slavery was economically inefficient), and working-class activists (viewing slavery as an extreme of class exploitation).
- Abolitionists organized mass petitions, public meetings, and boycotts of slave-produced sugar; women, excluded from formal political participation, became crucial organizers of boycotts and fundraising.
- The 1807 *Slave Trade Act* abolished the slave trade but not slavery itself; abolitionists continued agitation for another 26 years until the *Slavery Abolition Act of 1833* passed, compensating slaveholders for their "property."
- The compensation of £20 million (roughly 40% of government revenue) to slaveholders rather than the enslaved revealed the priorities of even "progressive" reform; enslaved people received freedom but no restitution.
- British abolition was achieved peacefully through legislation, contrasting with the violent American Civil War; however, the compensation structure ensured slaveholders profited from the transition.
