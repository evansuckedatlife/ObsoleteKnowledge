# Election of 2000

## summary

George W. Bush's disputed victory over Al Gore in 2000 hinged on a 537-vote margin in Florida and a 5-4 Supreme Court decision that many viewed as a constitutional violation. Gore won the national popular vote by 540,000 votes but fell short in the Electoral College; Florida's contested results were halted by the Court's *Bush v. Gore* decision, which stopped the recount and handed the presidency to Bush. The election exposed the Electoral College's democratic deficit, the problem of ballot design and voting systems, and the Court's apparent willingness to enter presidential politics.

## you gotta know

### The Candidates and the Close Results
- *Al Gore*, Clinton's vice president, won 48.4 percent of the popular vote nationally but lost the Electoral College by four votes (271–266).
- *George W. Bush*, the Texas governor, won 47.9 percent of the popular vote but claimed Florida's 25 electoral votes on the basis of a contested recount.
- Florida's election was incompetently administered: the "butterfly ballot" in Palm Beach County confused thousands of voters; overvoting and undervoting were rampant.

### The Recount Chaos and Legal Battle
- Florida law required a recount when the margin was less than 0.5 percent; the recount exposed hanging chads, pregnant chads, and thousands of ambiguous ballots.
- Different Florida counties used different standards for counting; by late November, Bush held a 537-vote margin.
- Gore's campaign sought a hand recount of disputed counties; the Florida Supreme Court ordered one to proceed, setting a deadline of December 12.

### The Supreme Court Intervention
- The U.S. Supreme Court, in *Bush v. Gore* (2000), halted Florida's recount on Fourteenth Amendment equal protection grounds, finding that varying counting standards violated equal protection.
- The decision was 5-4 along ideological lines; critics argued the majority was result-oriented, choosing an unprecedented ground to decide the case.
- Bush won Florida's 25 electors and the presidency.

## context

The 2000 election crystallized anxieties about the American electoral system's adequacy for the 21st century. The Electoral College, already an anachronism that had enabled Bush to win despite losing the popular vote by 0.5 percent nationally, was compounded by antiquated voting technology and inconsistent ballot design. The Supreme Court's intervention was extraordinary: the Court had traditionally deferred to states on election procedures, and *Bush v. Gore*'s equal protection rationale was novel and narrow enough that Chief Justice Rehnquist's own concurrence suggested he had doubts. The decision seemed to many Americans like the Court substituting its judgment for the voters'—a violation of democratic principle. Yet the Court's decision would not be undone; Florida would remain in the Bush column, and the presidency would be decided by judicial rather than democratic means. The election also proved that the system of "checks and balances" could fail when partisanship overwhelmed institutional independence: the Court's conservative majority saw Bush as the preferable candidate and acted accordingly.
