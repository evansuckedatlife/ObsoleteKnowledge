# Elizabeth Cady Stanton

## summary

*Elizabeth Cady Stanton* (1815–1902) was an American women's rights pioneer whose *Declaration of Sentiments* at the 1848 Seneca Falls Convention became the manifesto of the women's movement. She combined moral argument with radical political analysis, challenging not only women's disenfranchisement but also property law, marriage law, and religious doctrine.

## you gotta know

- Drafted and presented the *Declaration of Sentiments* at the Seneca Falls Convention (1848), modeled on the Declaration of Independence but listing grievances against men's laws and customs.
- The declaration's demand for women's suffrage was controversial even among women's rights supporters and was the only resolution not unanimously passed at Seneca Falls.
- Co-founded and led the National Woman Suffrage Association with *Susan B. Anthony*, developing the movement's strategy and ideology for 50 years.
- Wrote extensively on women's equality, religious critique, and the interconnection between women's rights and labor rights, abolition, and temperance.
- Did not live to see the Nineteenth Amendment ratified; died in 1902, 18 years before women gained the federal vote.
- Late in life, collaborated with *Susan B. Anthony* and *Matilda Joslyn Gage* on *The History of Woman Suffrage*, ensuring the movement's record survived.

## connections

- [[susan-b-anthony]] — her closest colleague, together defining the first wave of American feminism.
- [[seneca-falls-convention]] — the founding event of the women's rights movement, where she articulated the demand for equality.
- [[womens-rights]] — framed it not as a special cause but as a demand for full citizenship and rational governance.
- [[abolitionism]] — she and other women abolitionists recognized the parallel between slavery and women's legal subordination.
- [[lucretia-mott]] — fellow organizer of Seneca Falls and collaborator in the early movement.
