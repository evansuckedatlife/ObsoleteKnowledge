## summary

The Battle of Antietam (September 17, 1862), also known as the Battle of Sharpsburg, was the bloodiest single day in American military history. Fought along Antietam Creek in Maryland, it pitted Robert E. Lee's Confederate Army of Northern Virginia against George McClellan's Union Army of the Potomac. Though tactically inconclusive, the battle was a strategic Union victory: Lee's invasion of the North was halted, and the Union was able to claim victory enough to issue the preliminary *Emancipation Proclamation*, reshaping the war's purpose from restoration of the Union to abolition of slavery.

## you gotta know

### Background
- Lee invaded Maryland to gain supplies, draw Union forces away from Virginia, and seek foreign recognition.
- McClellan obtained a copy of Lee's battle plans but failed to exploit the advantage decisively.
- The Confederate army was split across two separate positions, vulnerable to a concentrated Union attack.

### The Battle
- Fighting erupted at multiple points: the *Sunken Road* (Bloody Lane) saw fierce hand-to-hand combat; Burnside's Bridge was a focal point of northern sector fighting.
- Union forces suffered from lack of coordination and McClellan's overcautious strategy, even as they held numerical superiority.
- Casualties were staggering: approximately 12,000 Union and 10,000 Confederate soldiers killed, wounded, or missing in a single day.

### Aftermath
- Lee withdrew across the Potomac, ending his first invasion of the North.
- McClellan failed to pursue aggressively, disappointing Lincoln and leading to his eventual replacement.
- Lincoln issued the preliminary Emancipation Proclamation, effective January 1, 1863.

## context

Though neither army achieved a decisive tactical victory, Antietam fundamentally changed the war's moral and diplomatic dimensions. By halting Lee's invasion and allowing Lincoln to frame the conflict as a struggle for human freedom, the battle deterred European powers from recognizing the Confederacy and elevated the Union cause. The horrifying casualty count—and those that followed—began shifting American consciousness about the cost of war. Photographers like Mathew Brady documented the carnage, making Civil War suffering visible to Northern civilians for the first time.
