# Daniel Chester French

## summary

*Daniel Chester French* (1850–1931) was an American sculptor who dominated the Beaux-Arts tradition in the United States, creating monumental public sculptures and monuments that defined American civic identity. His calm, dignified figures in marble and bronze—from the *Minute Man* to the *Lincoln Memorial* statue—embodied classical restraint and democratic ideals.

## you gotta know

- Sculpted the *Minute Man* (1874), a bronze militia member poised for action, commemorating the opening battle of the American Revolution near Concord, Massachusetts.
- Created the seated *Lincoln* (1920) within the *Lincoln Memorial*, a 19-foot marble figure of profound quiet dignity that became an icon of American democracy.
- Designed the *Republic* (1893), a gilded bronze colossus for the World's Columbian Exposition, a 65-foot goddess symbolizing American triumph.
- Master of *Beaux-Arts classicism*—trained in Paris, he brought European academic tradition to American monuments.
- Prolific creator of public monuments, memorial statues, and architectural sculpture across the United States.
- Maintained a studio in Stockbridge, Massachusetts, where he produced works that balanced public grandeur with intimate expressiveness.
