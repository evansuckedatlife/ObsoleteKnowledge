# American Gothic

## summary

*American Gothic* is Grant Wood's 1930 painting of a farmer and his daughter standing before a Gothic Revival farmhouse, their expressions stern and suspicious. This spare, iconic image has become the archetypal symbol of rural American character and values, endlessly parodied and reinterpreted as a statement on isolation, tradition, and provincial America.

## you gotta know

- Painted in 1930, displayed at the Art Institute of Chicago; won a bronze medal at the Carnegie International and immediately became an American cultural icon.
- Depicts a farmer holding a pitchfork and a young woman in a simple white dress with a Gothic farmhouse (featuring a distinctive pointed window) in the background.
- The figures' expressions are closed and wary; the composition is rigorously frontal and austere, emphasizing social distance and emotional reserve.
- Wood drew on his Iowa upbringing and admired Northern European Renaissance painting (especially Flemish techniques) to create a style he called "Regionalism."
- The work can be read as satirical commentary on rural conservatism, or as a sincere celebration of American agrarian character—ambiguity that has fueled decades of debate.
- Became so iconic that it spawned countless parodies, homages, and reinterpretations, becoming a shorthand for "heartland America" in visual culture.
