# Bauhaus

## summary

*Bauhaus* (Building House) was a German design school and movement (1919–1933) that unified art, craft, and industrial design under a single philosophy: function, form, and beauty are inseparable. Founded by architect *Walter Gropius*, it integrated painting, sculpture, typography, and architecture while emphasizing honest materials, geometric simplicity, and the notion that good design democratizes beauty by making it accessible through mass production.

## you gotta know

- Founded in Weimar, Germany, in 1919; *Walter Gropius* assembled a faculty of avant-garde artists including *Paul Klee*, *Wassily Kandinsky*, and *László Moholy-Nagy*.
- Core principle: *"form follows function"*—objects should be designed from their purpose outward, free from applied ornament, celebrating the inherent beauty of material and construction.
- *Bauhaus* integrated color theory (*Kandinsky*), geometric composition (*Klee*), and typography (*Herbert Bayer*) into a systematic pedagogy taught through studios and workshops.
- Produced influential graphic design, furniture (*Marcel Breuer's tubular steel chairs*), pottery, textiles, and architectural principles that became foundational to modernism.
- Relocated from Weimar to Dessau in 1925, then to Berlin in 1932, under increasing political pressure; closed by the Nazi regime in 1933 as "degenerate."
- Faculty and students emigrated globally, spreading Bauhaus principles to America and elsewhere; its influence on 20th-century design is immeasurable.

## connections

- [[walter-gropius]] — founder and principal theorist of Bauhaus pedagogy.
- [[paul-klee]] — faculty member who integrated color and form theory into Bauhaus teaching.
- [[wassily-kandinsky]] — faculty member who developed theory of abstract color and composition.
- [[de-stijl]] — Dutch contemporary movement; both pursued geometric abstraction and integrated design, though Bauhaus emphasized function more.
- [[constructivism]] — Russian movement; Bauhaus shared abstraction and design integration but emphasized universal beauty and craft over state propaganda.
