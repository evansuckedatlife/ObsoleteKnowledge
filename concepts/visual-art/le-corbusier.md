## summary

*Le Corbusier* (1887–1965), born Charles-Édouard Jeanneret, was a Swiss-French architect who championed modernism and the machine aesthetic. He advocated for rational, grid-based design and the standardization of living spaces, famously coining the term *machine for living in* and reshaping cities through radical urban planning proposals.

## you gotta know

- Wrote *Toward a New Architecture* (1923), a manifesto arguing that buildings should be designed with the precision and efficiency of machines.
- Designed the *Villa Savoye* (1931) near Paris, a pristine white modernist villa elevated on pilotis (concrete supports), embodying his "Five Points" of architecture.
- Advocated the *Modulor*, a system of proportions derived from human measurements, to standardize building dimensions and create harmony through mathematics.
- Proposed radical urban plans like *Ville Contemporaine* (a utopian city for 3 million) and later *Plan Voisin* for Paris, driven by grid logic and zoning separation.
- Pioneered concrete (*béton brut*) as the primary material, celebrating its raw, unfinished texture in designs like *Ronchamp Chapel* (1955), a sculptural masterpiece.
- Developed the concept of *Unité d'Habitation*, a residential building integrating shops, schools, and communal spaces within a single massive block.
- Influenced urban modernism globally, though many of his unrealized city plans remain controversial for their scale and disregard for existing urban fabric.

## connections

- [[frank-lloyd-wright]] — opposing vision: Wright pursued organic integration, Corbusier pursued rationalist abstraction.
- [[walter-gropius]] — shared commitment to modernism and functional design; both shaped international style.
- [[mies-van-der-rohe]] — parallel minimalist; shared "form follows function" but with different expressions.
- [[brutalism]] — his raw-concrete aesthetic became the foundation of the brutalist movement.
