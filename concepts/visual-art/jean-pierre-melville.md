# Jean-Pierre Melville

## summary

*Jean-Pierre Melville* was a French director who prefigured the New Wave through his cooler, more economical style—spare dialogue, long silences, and a visual aesthetic influenced by American film noir. His crime thrillers and Resistance dramas influenced Godard and Truffaut while remaining distinct from their avant-garde concerns, grounded in genre pleasures and the romance of the outlaw.

## you gotta know

- Pioneered an independent, studio-free mode of filmmaking in the 1950s, presaging the New Wave's low-budget autonomy.
- Directed *Le Samouraï* (1967), a glacial crime thriller starring Alain Delon as a perfectionist hitman—an exercise in cool style and silence that became an international cult icon.
- Made *Bob le Flambeur* (1956), a heist film with American noir DNA but French sensibility; influenced countless crime directors who followed.
- Known for subdued performances, minimal dialogue, and attention to clothing and gesture over psychological exposition.
- Fascinated by ritual and codes of honour—thieves, soldiers, assassins—as structures that replace conventional morality.
- Composed many scenes with non-diegetic voice-over, a technique he inherited from American noir and deployed with Gallic restraint.
- Remained a singular figure, neither New Wave nor classical; younger directors (Godard, Truffaut) cited him as a precursor.
