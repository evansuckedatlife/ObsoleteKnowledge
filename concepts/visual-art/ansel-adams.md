# Ansel Adams

## summary

*Ansel Adams* was an American landscape and environmental photographer renowned for his dramatic black-and-white photographs of the American West, particularly Yosemite National Park. He developed the *Zone System*, a sophisticated method for metering light and controlling exposure and printing tone, and became an evangelist for photography as a fine art equal to painting and sculpture.

## you gotta know

- Pioneered the *Zone System*, a methodology for translating a scene's tonal range into a final print through careful metering, exposure, and darkroom technique—still taught to photographers today.
- Created iconic images of *El Capitan*, *Half Dome*, and *Moonrise, Hernandez, New Mexico*; his monumental landscapes conveyed wilderness majesty and environmental fragility before environmentalism became mainstream.
- Founded the *Group f/64* (1932), a collective of photographers advocating for sharp focus, rich tonal detail, and fine prints—a rejection of pictorialism and a defence of photography's unique aesthetic.
- Wrote seminal technical texts: *The Negative*, *The Print*, and *The Camera*—treatises on craft that remain authoritative and accessible to both amateurs and professionals.
- Also served as a conservationist, using his photographs to advocate for wilderness preservation, particularly in California's Sierra Nevada.

## connections

- [[alfred-stieglitz]] — earlier fine-art photographer whose championing of photography's artistic legitimacy paved the way for Adams's career and philosophy.
- [[eadweard-muybridge]] — predecessor whose rigorous technical control of photography inspired Adams's systematic approach to exposure and printing.
- [[henri-cartier-bresson]] — contemporary street photographer whose "decisive moment" aesthetic contrasted with Adams's meditative landscape approach but shared his artistic ambitions.
