## summary

*Vitruvian Man* is Leonardo's iconic drawing from circa 1490 depicting a perfectly proportioned male figure superimposed on both a circle and a square, rendered in ink on paper. The work embodies the Renaissance marriage of art and mathematics, illustrating the Roman architect Vitruvius's belief that the human body contains ideal geometric proportions and represents a microcosm of the universe.

## you gotta know

- A pen-and-ink drawing on paper, approximately 34 × 24 cm (13.4 × 9.4 inches), accompanied by Leonardo's annotations in mirror writing describing the proportions.
- Shows a man with arms and legs extended to simultaneously inscribe both a circle (with the navel as center) and a square (with the genitals as base), representing the harmony between celestial and earthly forms.
- Based on Vitruvius's *De architectura*, in which the Roman theorist described ideal human proportions: the height equals the armspan, the face is one-tenth of the height, and so forth.
- Leonardo's notes in the margins detail these proportions with precision, demonstrating his obsessive study of human anatomy and his belief that beauty and geometry are inseparable.
- The drawing has become emblematic of Leonardo himself and the Renaissance ideal of humanistic mastery—a symbol appearing on the euro coin and in countless cultural references.
- Leonardo conducted extensive anatomical dissections to verify and refine the proportions, treating the work as both artistic vision and scientific investigation.
- The figure strikes a balance between static geometry and organic life, representing the Renaissance tension between mathematical idealism and embodied reality.
