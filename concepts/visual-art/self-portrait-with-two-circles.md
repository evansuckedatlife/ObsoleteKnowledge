## summary

Rembrandt's *Self-Portrait with Two Circles*, painted in 1665 near the end of his life, shows the aging artist standing before a canvas bearing two concentric circles, a symbol of perfect geometry and artistic aspiration. The work is a meditation on mastery, mortality, and the artist's enduring pursuit of transcendent form amid personal hardship.

## you gotta know

- Rembrandt paints himself at the easel, palette and brush in hand, gazing outward with dignified, almost defiant composure.
- The two circles on the canvas behind him have invited allegorical interpretation: perfect geometric forms, the limits of human achievement, or the divine aspiration that drives creation.
- Rembrandt's face is heavily shadowed; his clothing is plain and worn, belying his eminence—a reflection of his personal decline into financial difficulty in his later years.
- Measures approximately 114 by 94 centimetres and is housed in the Iveagh Bequest at Kenwood House in London.
- He executed over 100 self-portraits across his lifetime—a unique commitment to the form that tracks his artistic evolution and personal aging.
- The two circles remain cryptic; some scholars suggest they represent the earth and heaven, the material and ideal, or simply the artist's solitary creative labour.
