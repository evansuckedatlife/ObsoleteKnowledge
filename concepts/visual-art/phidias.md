# Phidias

## summary

*Phidias* (c. 500–432 BCE) was the preeminent sculptor of ancient Greece, directing the sculptural program of the *Parthenon* during Athens' Golden Age. His colossal chryselephantine (gold-and-ivory) statues of gods set the standard for monumental divine representation that influenced Western sculpture for millennia.

## you gotta know

- Directed the overall artistic vision of the *Parthenon* (447–432 BCE), supervising the architectural sculptures including the pediments, metopes, and frieze.
- Designed the *Athena Parthenos* (c. 438 BCE), a 40-foot-high chryselephantine statue housed within the Parthenon, renowned for its majesty and detailed ornamentation.
- Created the *Statue of Zeus at Olympia* (c. 435 BCE), one of the *Seven Wonders of the Ancient World*, another gold-and-ivory colossus whose description inspired awe for 800 years.
- Master of *contrapposto* and idealized human anatomy—his figures embodied the classical ideal of perfect proportion and heroic beauty.
- Established the sculptor as an intellectual leader equal to architects and philosophers, shaping Athenian cultural policy under *Pericles*.
- Fell victim to political persecution (c. 430 BCE) and likely died in exile, though his legacy outlasted his contemporaries.
