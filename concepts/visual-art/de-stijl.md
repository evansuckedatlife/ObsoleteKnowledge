# De Stijl

## summary

*De Stijl* (The Style) was a Dutch art movement (1917–1931) that pursued universal harmony through pure geometric abstraction, reducing form to primary colors (red, yellow, blue), black lines, and white space. Founded by *Piet Mondrian* and *Theo van Doesburg*, it applied rationalist principles across painting, sculpture, architecture, and design, seeking timeless, impersonal beauty as a bridge between art and everyday life.

## you gotta know

- Founded in 1917 in the Netherlands during World War I; the journal *De Stijl* (founded 1917) was the movement's publication and intellectual nexus.
- *Piet Mondrian's* mature abstract paintings—grids of black lines dividing fields of primary color and white—became the iconic visual language of the movement.
- Aesthetic principles: elimination of all curved lines; reduction to primary colors plus black, white, and gray; asymmetrical balance; the belief that geometric abstraction expressed universal truth.
- *Mondrian* believed this language could transcend culture and individual taste, creating a visual esperanto accessible to all humans.
- *De Stijl* extended into architecture (*Gerrit Rietveld's Utrecht Chair*, the *Rietveld Schröder House*) and furniture design, making it a total design philosophy, not just painting.
- The movement dissolved in the early 1930s as fascism rose and its utopian vision faltered; Mondrian emigrated to Paris and later New York, continuing his abstract practice.

## connections

- [[piet-mondrian]] — founder and the movement's most recognizable figure; his late works define geometric abstraction.
- [[theo-van-doesburg]] — co-founder and theorist; editor of *De Stijl* journal.
- [[constructivism]] — Russian contemporary; both pursued abstraction as utopian order, though with different political aims (communism vs. universal harmony).
- [[bauhaus]] — German movement that shared De Stijl's integration of art and design but embraced functionalism alongside abstraction.
- [[abstract-expressionism]] — later movement influenced by Mondrian's abstraction, though gestural rather than geometric.
