# Campbell's Soup Cans

## summary

*Campbell's Soup Cans* is Andy Warhol's provocative 1962 installation of 32 silk-screened canvases, each depicting a slightly different variety of Campbell's condensed soup cans at their commercial size. This work brazenly elevated mass-produced commodity packaging to fine art, crystallizing Pop Art's embrace of American consumer culture and mass reproduction as worthy subjects for artistic meditation.

## you gotta know

- First exhibited in 1962 at the Ferus Gallery in Los Angeles; now at the Museum of Modern Art, the work launched Warhol to fame and became the iconic emblem of Pop Art.
- Each of the 32 canvases depicts a single Campbell's soup can rendered in commercial silk-screen printing—the same technique Warhol had learned from his graphic design background.
- The cans are presented frontally, isolated, and at life size, mimicking the appearance of merchandise displayed in a supermarket or on a shelf.
- Warhol selected Campbell's soup as his subject partly because he ate it every day for lunch, seeing in it a democratic symbol of American convenience and mass accessibility.
- The work transgressed the boundary between "low" commercial art (advertising, packaging) and "high" fine art, provoking outrage from critics but fascinating viewers.
- Pioneered Warhol's technique of using mechanical reproduction and serialization to interrogate authenticity, originality, and the artist's hand in an age of mass production.
