# Jacques Tati

## summary

*Jacques Tati* was a French filmmaker, actor, and mime who created a singular comedy of observation, using elaborately designed sets and silent-film timing to expose the absurdities of modern life and technological "progress." His character *Monsieur Hulot* became cinema's great comic archetype of the man perpetually at odds with an increasingly sterile, automated world.

## you gotta know

- Created and played *Monsieur Hulot*, a bowlegged, pipe-smoking protagonist whose bemused incompetence with modern machinery drives films exploring mid-century modernism's human cost.
- Directed *Playtime* (1967), an audacious live-action cartoon set in a vast mock-modern Paris built on soundstages—shot in 70mm and 1:1.66 ratio, a technical marvel and a financial disaster.
- *My Uncle* (1958) contrasts the cluttered, organic chaos of a working-class neighbourhood with the antiseptic sterility of a modernist villa and its labour-saving gimmicks.
- Worked primarily in comedy without dialogue—or with barely audible mumbling and sound effects—returning cinema to the silent tradition of Chaplin and Keaton.
- Filmed in long takes and wide shots, allowing the viewer to scan the frame and discover gags independently; his compositions are dense with simultaneous action.
- His films took years to complete due to meticulously designed sets and locations; *Playtime* was in production for over a decade.
- Remained adamantly auteurist, refusing studio interference and maintaining total control over form and meaning.

## connections

- [[charles-chaplin]] — precursor in silent comedy; Tati inherited and modernised his philosophy.
- [[buster-keaton]] — another influence in physical comedy and compositional sophistication.
- [[jean-luc-godard]] — contemporary New Wave radical; both rejected conventional narrative.
- [[modernism]] — his critique of 1950s–1960s design and lifestyle.
