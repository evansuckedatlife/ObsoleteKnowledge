# Dada

## summary

*Dada* was an anti-art, anarchist movement born during World War I (roughly 1916–1924) that rejected reason, logic, and aesthetic convention in a spirit of absurdist rebellion. Emerging simultaneously in neutral cities (Zurich, Berlin, New York), it weaponized nonsense, chance, and readymades against what Dadaists saw as the rationalist folly that had plunged the world into carnage, using irreverence and chaos as tools for cultural critique.

## you gotta know

- Founded at the *Cabaret Voltaire* in Zurich in 1916 by exiles fleeing World War I; *Hugo Ball*, *Tristan Tzara*, and *Emmy Hennings* performed sound poetry and absurdist theater.
- *Marcel Duchamp's* *Fountain* (1917)—a porcelain urinal signed "R. Mutt"—epitomizes Dada's provocative stance: the artwork is the gesture, not the object's aesthetic merit.
- Technique: chance operations, collage, photomontage (*Hannah Höch*), nonsense poetry, and readymades—deliberately embracing accident and ugliness over craft.
- *Cabaret Voltaire* performances featured simultaneous poetry and sound; *Dada 3*, *Der Dada*, and other periodicals spread the movement's manifestos.
- Branches in Berlin (political, photomontage-heavy), New York (Duchamp, *Man Ray*), and Paris (literary, proto-Surrealist); the movement was international and decentralized.
- Dada explicitly rejected the notion of unified aesthetic or style; its only coherence is rebellion, making it more a *spirit* than a formalized movement.

## connections

- [[marcel-duchamp]] — creator of the readymade; Dada's most philosophically influential figure.
- [[hugo-ball]] — co-founder and performer at Cabaret Voltaire.
- [[hannah-hoch]] — pioneer of photomontage and German Dada's visual language.
- [[expressionism]] — contemporaneous European movement; Dada shared its rejection of convention but embraced absurdity rather than emotion.
- [[surrealism]] — direct descendant; Surrealists inherited Dada's embrace of the unconscious but systematized it under Breton's manifestos.
