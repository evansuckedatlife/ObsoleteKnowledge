# Nicéphore Niépce

## summary

*Nicéphore Niépce* was a French inventor who created the first permanent photograph using a process called *heliography* around 1822. Though he did not live to see photography's commercial success, his pioneering work on light-sensitive materials and camera obscura techniques directly enabled Louis Daguerre's later triumph.

## you gotta know

- Produced *View from the Window at Le Gras* (circa 1822), considered the oldest surviving permanent photograph, taken with an 8-hour exposure through a camera obscura onto bitumen-coated pewter.
- Used *bitumen of Judea*, a light-sensitive tar-like substance, as his recording medium—it hardened when exposed to light and could be washed away to create an image.
- Coined the term *heliography* ("light-writing") for his process, which required extremely long exposures, rendering it impractical for portraiture.
- Partnered with *Louis Daguerre* in 1829 to refine his process; after Niépce's death in 1833, Daguerre continued alone and perfected the faster, more practical daguerreotype.
- Worked in rural Burgundy, France, largely in isolation, making his achievements even more remarkable given the lack of scientific community around him.

## connections

- [[louis-daguerre]] — his business partner who commercialised and refined heliography into the daguerreotype.
- [[william-henry-fox-talbot]] — contemporary English inventor pursuing similar goals independently, leading to the calotype process.
- [[alfred-stieglitz]] — later photographer whose aesthetic concerns contrasted sharply with the technical focus of the Niépce-Daguerre era.
