## summary

*Louis Daguerre* was a French painter and inventor who pioneered the daguerreotype, the first commercially viable photographic process, in 1839. His silvered copper plates produced sharp, detailed images that captivated the public and launched the age of practical photography, though each plate was a unique original rather than a reproducible negative.

## you gotta know

- Developed the *daguerreotype* process: exposing a silvered copper plate to light and developing it with mercury vapour to reveal a detailed photographic image.
- The French government purchased his patent in 1839 and released it to the world as a gift to humanity, accelerating global adoption of photography.
- Produced *Boulevard du Temple*, often cited as the first photograph to contain a human being—though the exposure was so long (10–15 minutes) that moving subjects vanished.
- Each daguerreotype was a single, unreproducible positive image; no negatives meant every plate was an original artwork, which boosted its appeal to wealthy collectors.
- His earlier career as a diorama painter—theatrical illusionists showing scenes through painted backlit panels—influenced his vision of capturing visual reality.
- Daguerreotypes dominated portraiture in the 1840s–1850s before being eclipsed by cheaper wet-collodion and albumen print processes.

## connections

- [[nicephore-niepce]] — his business partner; Niépce's heliography was the conceptual foundation for the daguerreotype.
- [[william-henry-fox-talbot]] — English rival who invented the calotype (negative-positive process), a competing early photography method.
- [[eadweard-muybridge]] — later pioneer who used photography to study motion and anatomy.
- [[alfred-stieglitz]] — late 19th-century photographer who elevated photography to fine art, moving beyond the mere documentation Daguerre enabled.
