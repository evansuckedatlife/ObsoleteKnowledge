# Eadweard Muybridge

## summary

*Eadweard Muybridge* was a British photographer famous for pioneering *motion photography*—using sequences of rapid sequential photographs to decompose and analyse movement. His work in the 1870s–1880s bridged still photography and cinema, revealing the mechanics of animal and human locomotion in unprecedented detail.

## you gotta know

- Conducted the famous *horse-in-motion* experiment (1877–1878) commissioned by Leland Stanford to settle a bet: are all four hooves of a galloping horse airborne simultaneously? Answer: yes. The series of photographs shocked the art world and contradicted centuries of painted convention.
- Created *Animal Locomotion* (1887), an encyclopedia of 781 sequential photographs of humans and animals in motion—walking, running, jumping, climbing, dancing—arranged in high-speed sequences.
- Invented an early form of motion-picture technology: sequential photos printed on a spinning disc (*zoopraxiscope*) that, when rotated before a light source, created the illusion of continuous motion, a precursor to cinema.
- His motion studies revolutionised scientific understanding of anatomy and biomechanics; artists, dancers, and engineers studied his plates to understand how bodies actually move versus how tradition depicted them.

## connections

- [[alfred-stieglitz]] — contemporary photographer focusing on aesthetic and fine-art concerns, while Muybridge pursued scientific documentation.
- [[ansel-adams]] — later landscape photographer whose technical mastery of exposure and printing descended from Muybridge's rigorous experimental approach to light.
- [[dorothea-lange]] — Depression-era documentarian whose social-realist photographs also pursued truth through careful sequential composition.
