# Louis Sullivan

## summary

*Louis Sullivan* (1856–1924) was an American architect and theorist who pioneered the modern skyscraper and championed ornamental design in industrial architecture. His philosophical principle—*form follows function*—became the guiding maxim of modernism, though his own work richly integrated organic ornament with structure.

## you gotta know

- Designed the *Wainwright Building* (1891) in St. Louis, one of the first skyscrapers to express vertical structure as aesthetic principle, establishing the template for American tall buildings.
- Created the *Guaranty Building* (1896) in Buffalo, featuring a terracotta facade with flowing organic ornament, demonstrating how industrial buildings could achieve beauty.
- Coined the phrase *form follows function*, rejecting historical revival and arguing that building design must emerge from its purpose.
- Designed ornamental systems incorporating Art Nouveau influence—flowing, naturalistic patterns integrated into terra cotta, metalwork, and carved detail.
- Founded the *Chicago School* with Dankmar Adler (his partner), developing the steel-frame skyscraper while rejecting the historical pastiche of eastern architects.
- Mentored *Frank Lloyd Wright* as a draftsman, profoundly influencing Wright's approach to organic design and structural honesty.
- Wrote extensively on architecture (*Kindergarten Chats*, *A System of Architectural Ornament*), developing the theoretical foundations of modernism.

## connections

- [[frank-lloyd-wright]] — direct apprenticeship; Wright absorbed Sullivan's philosophy of integrating form with nature and function.
- [[art-nouveau]] — his ornamental language drew from and influenced the movement, though applied to steel and terra cotta.
- [[chicago-school]] — founder of the movement that developed the modern skyscraper.
