# Jean Renoir

## summary

*Jean Renoir* was a French master filmmaker of the 1930s–1960s, the son of Impressionist painter Pierre-Auguste Renoir, who synthesised realism, humanism, and visual poetry into films of extraordinary warmth and social insight. His masterpieces *Grand Illusion* (1937) and *The Rules of the Game* (1939) stand as two of cinema's greatest achievements, balancing popular narrative with formal sophistication.

## you gotta know

- Made *Grand Illusion* (*La Grande Illusion*, 1937), an anti-war film about escaped POWs that transcends national boundaries through acts of human sympathy and music.
- Created *The Rules of the Game* (*La Règle du jeu*, 1939), widely considered one of cinema's supreme works—a sprawling ensemble piece capturing French society on the eve of war.
- Pioneered deep focus and moving camera, using the full spatial depth of the frame to orchestrate human action rather than relying on cuts.
- Known for generosity toward his actors; his films are distinguished by performances of rare authenticity and empathy.
- Spent the war years in America, where he continued directing before returning to France to reestablish his legacy.
- Adapted texts including *The Lower Depths* and *Toni*, bringing literary and theatrical sensibility into the cinema.
- His influence spans generations; he is sometimes called cinema's greatest humanist for his refusal to sentimentalise or judge his characters.
