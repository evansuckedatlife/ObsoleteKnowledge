# The Potato Eaters

## summary

Vincent van Gogh's 1885 *The Potato Eaters*, painted in Nuenen in northern Brabant, depicts a family of peasants gathered around a table in dim lamplight, eating a meagre meal of potatoes. The work is van Gogh's early masterpiece—a study in earthy colour, social empathy, and the dignity of labour rendered through expressive brushwork and psychological intensity.

## you gotta know

- Van Gogh spent weeks sketching and studying the Potato Eaters family in their cottage, returning repeatedly to capture the right moment and emotional truth.
- The painting's palette is deliberately dark and muted—browns, ochres, and sombre greens—to convey both the poverty of the scene and the spiritual gravitas van Gogh perceived in peasant life.
- Five figures lean inward around a table illuminated by a single hanging lamp; the light models their weathered faces with an almost Rembrandtian chiaroscuro.
- Measures approximately 82 by 114 centimetres; multiple versions and studies exist, including a lithograph.
- Van Gogh called it "the best thing I have ever done" at the time; it sold only after his death, when critics recognised its emotional and formal power.
- Housed in the Van Gogh Museum in Amsterdam, the work marks van Gogh's evolution from dark Realism toward the colour-saturated Post-Impressionism he would later embrace.

## connections

- [[vincent-van-gogh]] — his early masterpiece and a turning point toward expressive form.
- [[post-impressionism]] — demonstrates the movement's emotional intensity and departure from Impressionist light.
- [[peasant-realism]] — reflects van Gogh's deep sympathy for rural labour and the marginalised.
