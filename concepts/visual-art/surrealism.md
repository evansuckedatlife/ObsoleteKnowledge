# Surrealism

## summary

*Surrealism* emerged in the 1920s as a movement devoted to unleashing the unconscious mind, drawing on Freudian psychoanalysis and Dada's irreverence to create dreamlike, irrational imagery that bypassed rational thought. Anchored by *André Breton's Surrealist Manifesto* (1924), it spanned visual art, literature, film, and sculpture, aiming to revolutionize human perception and social consciousness through systematic disruption of logic.

## you gotta know

- *André Breton*, a poet and essayist, published the *Surrealist Manifesto* in 1924, setting the movement's philosophical foundation: automatic drawing and writing to access the unconscious.
- *Salvador Dalí* pioneered *paranoiac-critical method*—inducing paranoid states to generate dreamlike, hallucinatory imagery—exemplified by melting clocks and distorted architecture.
- *Max Ernst* developed *frottage* and *grattage*, rubbing and scraping techniques to produce unexpected textures and forms that bypassed conscious control.
- *Magritte*, *Miró*, and *Tanguy* created Surrealist works ranging from meticulous photographic dreamscapes to biomorphic abstractions and automatist abstraction.
- Organized exhibitions and reviews (*La Révolution Surréaliste*) from Paris; the movement expanded across Europe and reached the Americas, influencing Dada successor generations.
- Surrealist principles extended into fashion, advertising, film (*Un Chien Andalou*, 1929), and photography, making it a cultural juggernaut.

## connections

- [[andre-breton]] — founder and chief theorist of Surrealism.
- [[salvador-dali]] — the movement's most commercially successful and visually recognizable figure.
- [[max-ernst]] — innovator of automatic and frottage techniques.
- [[dada]] — direct precursor; Surrealism inherited Dada's anti-logic stance but systematized it philosophically.
- [[abstract-expressionism]] — later movement influenced by Surrealist automatism and unconscious creation.
