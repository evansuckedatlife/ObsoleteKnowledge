# Cubism

## summary

*Cubism* is an early-20th-century revolutionary art movement that deconstructed forms into geometric shapes and multiple viewpoints, showing objects from many angles simultaneously on a flat surface. Pioneered by *Pablo Picasso* and *Georges Braque* around 1907–1914, it rejected realistic perspective and fundamentally altered how artists and viewers understood the relationship between representation and abstraction.

## you gotta know

- *Les Demoiselles d'Avignon* (1907) by Picasso is often cited as the first Cubist painting, featuring distorted female figures fragmented across an angular, disorienting composition.
- The movement splits into two main phases: *Analytical Cubism* (1908–1912) pushed fragmentation to the point of near-abstraction, with muted earth tones and barely legible forms; *Synthetic Cubism* (1912–1914) reintroduced color and collage elements, papier collé.
- Key technique: showing multiple perspectives of an object simultaneously—a guitar seen from front, side, and inside at once—collapsing traditional single-point perspective.
- *Juan Gris* refined Cubism into a more geometric, almost mathematical formalism, prioritizing grid structures and clarity over Braque's and Picasso's expressionism.
- Exhibited prominently at the 1911 Salon d'Automne in Paris and the 1913 Armory Show in New York, shocking conservative audiences and scandalized the press.
- Profound influence on architecture, design, and sculpture; Cubist principles rippled into Dada, Constructivism, and later movements.

## connections

- [[pablo-picasso]] — co-founder and the movement's most recognizable figure.
- [[georges-braque]] — co-founder who developed papier collé and collage within Cubism.
- [[juan-gris]] — codified Cubism's geometric logic into a rational system.
- [[fauvism]] — color-driven precursor that rejected naturalism but predated Cubism's geometric deconstruction.
- [[constructivism]] — Russian movement that absorbed Cubist fragmentation and applied it to propaganda and design.
