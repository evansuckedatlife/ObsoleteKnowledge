# Frank Lloyd Wright

## summary

*Frank Lloyd Wright* (1867–1959) was an American architect who pioneered organic architecture—a philosophy that integrated buildings seamlessly with their natural surroundings. His low-slung, horizontally-striped designs and open floor plans revolutionized residential and civic architecture, making him one of the most influential architects of the 20th century.

## you gotta know

- Designed *Fallingwater* (1935), a residential masterpiece built over a waterfall in Pennsylvania, exemplifying his organic architecture principles.
- Created the *Guggenheim Museum* (1959) in New York, a spiral building that defies conventional architecture and prioritizes flowing interior space.
- Developed the *Prairie House* style in early 1900s Chicago—low-pitched roofs, deep overhangs, and horizontal bands of windows mimicking the Midwestern landscape.
- Championed open floor plans and the integration of interior and exterior spaces, reducing walls and barriers.
- Articulated the concept of *organic architecture*: buildings should grow naturally from their sites, materials, and purposes rather than impose geometry upon them.
- Established *Taliesin West* in Arizona, a school and workshop where apprentices learned through hands-on design and construction.
- Known for distinctive detailing: cantilevered terraces, art-glass windows, and brickwork that expressed the material's inherent structure.

## connections

- [[walter-gropius]] — both pioneered modernism, though from different philosophies (Wright's organic vs. Gropius's functionalist).
- [[le-corbusier]] — contemporary visionary; Wright rejected Corbusier's machine aesthetic as inhumane.
- [[bauhaus]] — parallel movement emphasizing craft and design integration, though Wright worked outside the school.
- [[mies-van-der-rohe]] — minimalist contemporaneous with Wright; their divergent approaches shaped 20th-century architecture.
