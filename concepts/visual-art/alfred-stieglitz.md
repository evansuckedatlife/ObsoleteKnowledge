# Alfred Stieglitz

## summary

*Alfred Stieglitz* was an American photographer and tastemaker who championed photography as a fine art in the early 20th century. Through his galleries, journals, and exhibitions—particularly at *Gallery 291*—he promoted both his own evocative, symbolist-influenced work and that of peers like Georgia O'Keeffe, establishing photography's legitimacy alongside painting and sculpture.

## you gotta know

- Ran *Gallery 291* (1905–1917) in New York, the first major gallery dedicated to photography and avant-garde modern art, showcasing works by photographers and painters including Picasso, Rodin, and Cézanne.
- Edited and published *Camera Notes* (1897–1902) and *Camera Work* (1903–1917), influential journals that reproduced fine photographic prints and essays arguing for photography's artistic merit against those who dismissed it as merely mechanical.
- Photographed *The Steerage* (1907), a composition of passengers on a ship's deck arranged in geometric patterns; he called it his masterpiece and it became an icon of photography as visual poetry, not mere documentation.
- Pioneered *pictorialism*, initially, then shifted toward modernist abstraction and pure photography—crisp focus, strong geometric compositions—rejecting soft-focus romanticism.
- Married Georgia O'Keeffe and created a famous series of intimate photographs of her that blurred boundaries between portraiture, abstraction, and sexuality, challenging conventions of the medium.

## connections

- [[ansel-adams]] — later photographer and disciple who credited Stieglitz's advocacy for photography's artistic legitimacy as foundational to his own career.
- [[henri-cartier-bresson]] — contemporary European photographer whose "decisive moment" philosophy reflected Stieglitz's influence on treating photography as a form of visual poetry.
- [[dorothea-lange]] — Depression-era documentarian whose socially conscious work differed from Stieglitz's aestheticism but inherited his technical rigour and artistic ambition.
