# The Arnolfini Portrait

## summary

Jan van Eyck's 1434 *The Arnolfini Portrait* depicts a wealthy merchant and his wife in an elaborately furnished domestic interior, capturing a moment of apparent marriage vows or betrothal. The painting's exquisite detail, symbolic depth, and ambiguous narrative have made it one of the most scrutinised works of Northern Renaissance art.

## you gotta know

- Shows Giovanni Arnolfini (a merchant from Lucca active in Bruges) and his wife in their richly appointed chamber, surrounded by symbols of fertility, fidelity, and prosperity.
- The single candle burning in the chandelier above them, the dog at their feet, the mirror, and scattered oranges have all been interpreted as emblems of marriage, witness-bearing, and divine blessing.
- Van Eyck's signature appears above the mirror as a legal notarisation: "Jan van Eyck was here"—suggesting the artist as witness to the event.
- Measures approximately 82 by 60 centimetres and is housed in the National Gallery in London.
- The painting's meaning is debated: was it a marriage contract, a betrothal, a fertility prayer, or simply a portrait of a couple?
- Its hyper-detailed realism—every texture, reflection, and symbol rendered with obsessive precision—helped establish the reputation of oil painting as the supreme medium.
