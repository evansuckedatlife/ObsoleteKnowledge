# The Milkmaid

## summary

Vermeer's *The Milkmaid*, painted circa 1658–1660, shows a domestic servant pouring milk into a bowl with such concentration and dignity that the humble task becomes almost sacramental. The work exemplifies Vermeer's genius for infusing everyday Dutch life with profound quiet beauty and masterful chiaroscuro.

## you gotta know

- Depicts an anonymous housemaid in a simple blue and yellow striped dress, pouring milk from a pitcher into a ceramic bowl on a table.
- Vermeer's light—spilling from an unseen left window—models the woman's face, hands, and the milk itself with extraordinary naturalism and warmth.
- At approximately 46 by 41 centimetres, it is a relatively small cabinet painting, designed for intimate domestic viewing.
- Housed in the Rijksmuseum in Amsterdam, where it is one of the most beloved works of the Dutch Golden Age.
- The composition's stability, the focus on a moment of quiet absorption, and the rich earthy palette create a meditation on labour and dignity.
- Vermeer signed only around 35–40 paintings in his lifetime and left nearly all of them undated; *The Milkmaid* is not signed.
