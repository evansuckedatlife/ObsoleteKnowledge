# François Truffaut

## summary

*François Truffaut* was a French New Wave director and influential film critic who championed the auteur theory and turned it into praxis through intimate, character-driven films about human vulnerability and memory. His breakthrough *The 400 Blows* (1959) established him as the movement's humanist pole, opposite Godard's formalism, and he sustained a career of elegant, melancholic explorations of love, regret, and childhood.

## you gotta know

- Wrote for *Cahiers du cinéma* where he developed the auteur theory—the claim that a film's director is its primary creative author, akin to a novelist's authorship.
- Directed *The 400 Blows* (*Les Quatre Cents Coups*, 1959), a semi-autobiographical coming-of-age film introducing actor Jean-Pierre Léaud and the New Wave aesthetic.
- Created the *Antoine Doinel* series across four films (1959–1979), following the same character through adulthood—a radical act of longitudinal narrative cinema.
- Known for direct, handheld camera work, natural lighting, and scenes shot in real locations rather than sets.
- Made *Day for Night* (1973), a film about filmmaking itself—a late-career meditation on cinema's illusory nature.
- Frequently worked with cinematographer Raoul Coutard and composer Georges Delerue, creating a recognisable visual and sonic palette.
- His final film, *Confidentially Yours* (1983), remains tender and formally playful despite his declining health.
