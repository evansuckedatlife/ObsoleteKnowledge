## summary

*Andrea Palladio* (1508–1580) was a Venetian architect whose rational interpretation of classical Roman design established principles of proportion and symmetry that dominated architecture for centuries. His villas and civic buildings became templates for *Palladianism*, an international architectural movement that influenced neoclassical design through the 18th and 19th centuries.

## you gotta know

- Designed the *Villa Rotonda* (c. 1567) near Vicenza, an iconic symmetrical villa with a domed rotunda and classical portico on all four sides, embodying ideal proportions and harmony.
- Created the *Basilica of Vicenza* (1546–1617), a civic building wrapping a medieval structure in classical colonnades that reconciled antiquity with medieval forms.
- Published *I Quattro Libri dell'Architettura* (Four Books of Architecture, 1570), systematizing classical orders and proportional systems, becoming the most influential architectural treatise of the Renaissance.
- Designed numerous Venetian villas for wealthy merchants, establishing the *Palladian villa* typology: symmetrical, classically porticoed, integrated into agricultural estates.
- Studied Roman ruins and treatises (*Vitruvius*), extracting principles of proportion and order that he applied to contemporary buildings.
- Pioneered the use of the classical portico as the dominant facade element, adopted globally and becoming central to neoclassical architecture.
- His work influenced Thomas Jefferson, Christopher Wren, and nearly every 18th-century architect, becoming the foundation of Palladianism.

## connections

- [[renaissance]] — defining architect of the mature Renaissance; his rational classicism advanced the movement's ideals.
- [[classical-architecture]] — revived and systematized classical principles; his treatises codified classical language for centuries.
