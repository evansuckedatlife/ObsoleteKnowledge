# Guernica

## summary

*Guernica* is Pablo Picasso's monumental 1937 anti-war mural painting depicting the bombing of the Basque town of Guernica during the Spanish Civil War. Rendered in fractured, anguished forms and stark black-and-white palette, it stands as one of the most powerful artistic responses to violence and suffering ever created, transforming personal tragedy into universal protest.

## you gotta know

- Completed in 1937 for the Spanish Pavilion at the Paris International Exposition, commissioned in response to the bombing of Guernica by Nationalist forces.
- Measures over 11 feet tall and nearly 27 feet wide, a monumental scale that amplifies its emotional and political force.
- Depicts fragmented human and animal forms—a weeping mother, a bull, a horse pierced by a lance, dismembered limbs—arranged in cubist distortion to convey chaos and agony.
- Uses only black, white, and gray (no color), emphasizing the starkness of death and the totality of loss.
- The bull and horse are ambiguous symbols—sometimes read as Spain and fascism, sometimes as brutality and innocence, or as matador and victim.
- Became an iconic symbol of artistic opposition to war, and inspired countless anti-war artworks across the 20th century.
