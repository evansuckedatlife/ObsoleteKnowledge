# Jean-Luc Godard

## summary

*Jean-Luc Godard* is a Swiss-French filmmaker and theorist who became the most radical voice of the French New Wave, radically deconstructing narrative cinema through jump cuts, direct-to-camera address, and self-reflexive form. His early masterpiece *Breathless* (1960) broke every rule of conventional filmmaking and redefined what cinema could be, though his later work grew increasingly experimental and political.

## you gotta know

- Directed *Breathless* (*À bout de souffle*, 1960), the definitive French New Wave film that introduces abrupt jump cuts and an anti-hero protagonist played by Jean-Paul Belmondo.
- Pioneered the jump cut—sudden, unmotivated editing within scenes—as a signature technique that shattered continuity conventions.
- Wrote for *Cahiers du cinéma*, the influential film journal where New Wave directors nursed their auteur philosophy before making films.
- Known for fragmentary, essayistic narratives; his films often interrupt their own stories to digress into philosophy, literature, or formal experimentation.
- Later work grew increasingly political, embracing Marxist themes and renouncing conventional cinema as complicit in bourgeois ideology.
- Frequently cast Jean-Paul Belmondo and Anna Karina (his second wife), developing deep creative partnerships.
- Continued making films into the 2020s, remaining one of cinema's most obstinate experimenters.

## connections

- [[francois-truffaut]] — fellow Cahiers critic and New Wave pioneer; the two represented opposite poles of the movement (Godard formal, Truffaut humanist).
- [[agnes-varda]] — contemporary experimenter with narrative form and documentary blending.
- [[jean-pierre-melville]] — precursor to the New Wave whose cool, economical style influenced Godard's approach.
- [[nouvelle-vague]] — the movement he helped define and then pushed beyond its own boundaries.
- [[anna-karina]] — actress and second wife; their collaborations define much of his 1960s work.
