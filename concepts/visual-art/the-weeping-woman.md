# The Weeping Woman

## summary

*The Weeping Woman* is Pablo Picasso's 1937 portrait of a fragmented female figure with distorted features, clutching a handkerchief, her face a cubist anatomy of anguish. Painted during the Spanish Civil War as a sequel to *Guernica*, it transposes collective suffering into intimate psychological pain through violent formal distortion.

## you gotta know

- Created in 1937 shortly after *Guernica*, during the Spanish Civil War; Picasso used the weeping woman as a symbol of both personal and national grief.
- The figure's face is fractured into multiple planes and viewpoints—an eye, nostril, and mouth displaced and reassembled in a non-naturalistic configuration.
- Bold primary colors (yellow, blue, red) and black lines collide across the canvas, creating visual dissonance that mirrors emotional turmoil.
- The woman holds or wraps herself in a patterned handkerchief, a small domestic detail juxtaposed against the abstract violence of the form.
- Picasso returned to the motif of the weeping woman in multiple works after 1937, treating it as a vehicle for emotional and political expression.
- Exists in several versions and sketches, reflecting Picasso's tendency to explore themes obsessively across many works.
