# The Garden of Earthly Delights

## summary

Hieronymus Bosch's monumental triptych *The Garden of Earthly Delights*, painted circa 1490–1510, presents a fantastical panorama of paradise, earthly pleasures, and infernal punishment across three hinged panels. Its surreal imagery, teeming with hybrid creatures and grotesque delights, has invited endless allegorical interpretation and defines the Northern Renaissance imagination of the fantastic.

## you gotta know

- A triptych measuring approximately 2.2 by 3.9 metres when fully opened, painted on oak panels with meticulous detail and vivid colour.
- The left panel depicts the *Garden of Eden* with the creation of Eve and mysterious hybrid creatures.
- The central panel—the "garden of earthly delights"—swarms with naked figures engaged in sensual pleasures, feasting, courtship, and play among impossible architecture and giant fruits.
- The right panel shows *Hell*—a terrifying landscape of torture, fire, and punishment, ruled by a hybrid demon-figure on a throne.
- Housed in the Prado Museum in Madrid since the 18th century, the painting's true meaning remains debated among scholars.
- Bosch's intricate weaving of flora, fauna, and human forms influenced Surrealist artists in the 20th century.
