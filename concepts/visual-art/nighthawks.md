## summary

*Nighthawks* is Edward Hopper's 1942 quintessential American painting depicting a handful of solitary figures in a late-night diner bathed in fluorescent light, surrounded by empty streets. It captures urban isolation and melancholy with spare realism, becoming an iconic meditation on loneliness within the modern city.

## you gotta know

- Painted in 1942 during World War II, now at the Art Institute of Chicago; widely considered Hopper's masterpiece and one of the most recognizable American paintings.
- Shows three patrons (a couple and a lone man) and a server in a corner diner lit by bright interior light, while the street outside is dark and deserted.
- The composition uses sharp geometric forms (the triangular diner window, harsh shadows, rectangular street) to emphasize isolation and the barrier between interior and exterior worlds.
- Hopper's figures are introspective and disconnected from one another, despite occupying the same space—a visual metaphor for emotional isolation even among people.
- The painting is carefully composed to exclude any sign of life outside the diner, heightening the sense of a microcosm sealed off from the world.
- Has become a cultural touchstone for American melancholy and urban alienation, referenced endlessly in literature, film, and popular imagination.
