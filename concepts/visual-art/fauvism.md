# Fauvism

## summary

*Fauvism* was a short-lived but electrifying early-20th-century movement (1905–1910) that liberated color from descriptive purpose, deploying bold, non-naturalistic hues and loose brushwork to express emotion and light directly. Led by *Henri Matisse* and *André Derain*, it scandalized Paris's Salon d'Automne in 1905, where critic Louis Vauxcelles derided the artists as "*fauves*" (wild beasts), a label they embraced.

## you gotta know

- The 1905 Salon d'Automne debut in Paris caused uproar; critics called the exhibition a room of madmen for its violent, unrealistic color schemes.
- *Matisse's Woman with a Hat* (1905) exemplifies the style—a portrait where the sitter's face and background are rendered in clashing yellows, oranges, and greens unrelated to nature.
- Primacy of pure color over form: artists used flat areas of intense color, often straight from the tube, prioritizing emotional impact over optical accuracy.
- *André Derain* and *Matisse* painted landscapes where grass is purple, water is orange, and sky is pink—color as liberated expression rather than description.
- Fauvist innovations in the decorative arts, particularly through *Matisse's* textile and mural work, influenced design and interior spaces.
- The movement peaked around 1908 and dispersed as members moved toward Cubism, Expressionism, or other abstraction; *Matisse* continued a lifetime exploration of color and form.

## connections

- [[henri-matisse]] — the movement's principal figure and most enduring artist.
- [[andre-derain]] — co-founder whose landscape work exemplified wild color.
- [[cubism]] — emerged almost simultaneously; both rejected naturalism but via different formal strategies.
- [[expressionism]] — shared Fauvism's emotional intensity and non-naturalistic color; German Expressionism drew from Fauvist boldness.
