# The Night Watch

## summary

*The Night Watch*, Rembrandt's monumental 1642 group portrait, depicts an Amsterdam militia company mustering in a shadowed courtyard with dramatic spotlighting and theatrical energy. Its unconventional composition—figures in motion rather than static formation—revolutionised the group portrait genre and remains one of the most analysed paintings in art history.

## you gotta know

- Commissioned as a group portrait for the Amsterdam civic guard, paid by the *Militia Company of Captain Banning Cocq*.
- Measures nearly 3.8 by 4.5 metres—immense and designed to dominate a wall of the militia's headquarters.
- Originally titled *The Night Watch* because it was so darkened by varnish and grime that 18th-century viewers assumed it depicted a nighttime scene; modern restoration revealed bright daylight.
- Rembrandt abandoned symmetrical formality; figures overlap and crowd the frame dynamically, with a young drummer and a girl in golden-yellow dress as striking focal points.
- Its dramatic use of light and shadow—a hallmark of Rembrandt's style—isolates the captain and his lieutenant while plunging others into relative darkness.
- Hung in the Amsterdam Museum; during World War II it was rolled up and hidden to protect it from Nazi looting.
