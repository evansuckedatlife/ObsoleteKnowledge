# Caravaggio

## summary

*Caravaggio* is an Italian Baroque painter celebrated for his dramatic use of chiaroscuro—violent contrasts between deep shadows and vivid light—and his unflinching realism that brought sacred subjects into ordinary taverns and streets. His revolutionary technique and turbulent life made him foundational to Baroque art, though his violent temperament left him perpetually at odds with patrons and the law.

## you gotta know

- Pioneered *chiaroscuro* (sharp light-shadow contrast) to create psychological intensity and direct emotional force in religious scenes, influencing nearly every Baroque painter who followed.
- Painted *The Calling of St. Matthew* in a tavern, depicting Christ entering a dive bar to summon a tax collector—sacred narrative rendered in grimy, lived-in reality.
- *The Conversion of St. Paul* and *St. Peter's Crucifixion* present martyrdom from shocking, undignified angles; he was commissioned to paint altarpieces that shocked rather than consoled.
- His *Judith Beheading Holofernes* gives the biblical widow an urgent, muscular physicality; often considered a self-portrait of moral ambiguity.
- Fled Rome in 1606 after killing a man in a brawl; spent his final years in Naples, Malta, and Sicily, painting while evading capture, his style growing darker and more anguished.
- Worked directly on canvas with minimal preparation, painting *alla prima* (wet-on-wet), making revisions rare and execution visible—a method that impressed and unsettled his peers.
- Died young (around 37) in obscurity on a beach, possibly of malaria or infected wounds, nearly forgotten until scholars rediscovered his influence in the 20th century.

- [[rembrandt-van-rijn]] — likewise mastered light and shadow to explore human interiority and moral complexity.
- [[artemisia-gentileschi]] — followed his dramatic chiaroscuro and directness; painted boldly violent biblical and mythological scenes.
