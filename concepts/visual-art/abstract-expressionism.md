# Abstract Expressionism

## summary

*Abstract Expressionism* was an American art movement (roughly 1943–1960s) that combined abstraction's non-representational form with Expressionism's emotional intensity and gestural energy, emphasizing the artist's physical act and inner feeling. Centered in New York, *Jackson Pollock*, *Mark Rothko*, and *Willem de Kooning* created monumental canvases that asserted American cultural dominance after World War II and established New York as the successor to Paris as the world's art capital.

## you gotta know

- *Jackson Pollock's* drip paintings—flung, poured, and splattered across large canvases—exemplify *action painting*, treating the canvas as an arena for physical gesture and unconscious expression.
- *Mark Rothko's* large color-field paintings—vast fields of floating color—pursued transcendent, spiritual experience through chromatic interaction and scale.
- *Willem de Kooning* merged abstraction with fragmented figuration, his gestural brushwork conveying psychological turbulence and the struggle to represent the human form.
- Surrealist automatism and Freudian psychology informed the movement; artists sought authentic emotional expression freed from social convention or rational control.
- The paintings were monumental in scale, demanding physical presence and immersion; galleries and museums designed spaces around them, elevating the art's experiential dimension.
- Cold War geopolitical dimensions: the U.S. government and institutions promoted Abstract Expressionism as proof of American cultural freedom against Soviet Socialist Realism.

## connections

- [[jackson-pollock]] — the movement's most iconic figure; his drip technique became synonymous with action painting.
- [[mark-rothko]] — pioneer of color-field abstraction; his spiritual ambitions elevated abstraction beyond formalism.
- [[willem-de-kooning]] — master of gestural abstraction; his merging of figure and abstraction opened multiple paths.
- [[surrealism]] — direct precursor; Abstract Expressionists inherited automatism and the unconscious but pursued pure abstraction rather than dreamlike imagery.
- [[de-stijl]] — earlier geometric movement; Mondrian's later work in New York influenced some Abstract Expressionists toward grid-based abstraction.
