# Expressionism

## summary

*Expressionism* was an early-20th-century movement (1905–1925, with roots earlier) that prioritized subjective emotion and inner psychological states over objective reality, using distorted forms, intense colors, and energetic brushwork to convey feeling. Centered in Germany and Austria, it produced works of angular discomfort and existential dread, influencing graphic art, sculpture, and film.

## you gotta know

- *Die Brücke* (The Bridge) and *Der Blaue Reiter* (The Blue Rider) were the two major German Expressionist groups, both centered in different cities but sharing emotional intensity and formal innovation.
- *Edvard Munch's* *The Scream* (1893, though painted before the movement coalesced) became the iconic image of Expressionist angst—swirling, unsettling form expressing psychological disturbance.
- Characteristic style: jagged, sometimes crude brushwork; garish, clashing colors; distorted human anatomy; dark subject matter (anxiety, alienation, sexuality, urban decay).
- *Egon Schiele*, *Oskar Kokoschka*, and *Emil Nolde* created intensely introspective or grotesque figurative work that embraced ugliness as truth.
- Expressionist principles extended into theater, film (*The Cabinet of Dr. Caligari*, 1920), and graphic design, making it a movement across media.
- Sharply rejected by Nazi regime as "degenerate" in the 1930s; many works were confiscated or destroyed.

## connections

- [[die-brucke]] — first major German Expressionist group.
- [[der-blaue-reiter]] — second major German group, more spiritual and abstract in orientation.
- [[edvard-munch]] — precursor figure whose work embodied Expressionist psychological intensity.
- [[fauvism]] — shared non-naturalistic color and emotional directness but pursued different formal paths.
- [[dada]] — both rejected conventional aesthetics; Expressionism's anguish fed into Dada's nihilism.
