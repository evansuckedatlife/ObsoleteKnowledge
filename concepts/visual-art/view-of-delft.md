# View of Delft

## summary

Vermeer's *View of Delft*, painted circa 1660–1661, is his only known cityscape—a serene panorama of Delft's skyline reflected in the Schie River after rain. Its atmospheric handling of light, sky, and urban architecture has made it a cornerstone of landscape painting and a meditation on the transience of weather and human settlement.

## you gotta know

- Depicts the real southern face of Delft, with the Schiedam Gate and tower of the New Church (Nieuwe Kerk) rising above a foreground of boats and figures on the waterfront.
- Painted from across the river; the low vantage point and calm water create an almost mirror-like reflection that emphasises the composition's geometric stability.
- The painting captures the moment after a rainstorm; sunlight breaks through clouds, illuminating the city's red-tiled roofs and pale facades with extraordinary subtlety.
- Measures approximately 98 by 117 centimetres and is housed in the Mauritshuis museum in The Hague.
- The work invites comparison to 17th-century Dutch landscape painting traditions, yet Vermeer's restraint and atmospheric precision set it apart as uniquely his own.
- Long considered a supreme achievement of landscape art, influencing Impressionists in the 19th century.
