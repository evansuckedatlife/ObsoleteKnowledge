## summary

The *Mona Lisa* is Leonardo's most celebrated portrait, painted circa 1503–1519, depicting Lisa Gherardini, a Florentine merchant's wife. Her enigmatic expression and the revolutionary sfumato technique—a soft, smoke-like blending of tones—established the work as the pinnacle of Renaissance portraiture and have made it perhaps the most famous painting in Western art.

## you gotta know

- Painted using oil on poplar wood, approximately 77 × 53 cm (30 × 21 inches), with meticulous attention to atmospheric perspective.
- Features the sitter's calm, mysterious smile—the *Mona Lisa smile*—which has captivated viewers for over five centuries and spawned countless interpretations.
- Employs sfumato to an unprecedented degree: the subtle gradations of tone create a dreamlike quality without harsh outlines, blurring the boundary between figure and landscape.
- The background landscape behind Lisa is dreamlike and imaginary, with winding paths and a bridge, rendered in cooler tones that recede into misty distance.
- Leonardo likely carried the work with him to France, and it now hangs in the Louvre Museum, Paris, where it attracts millions of visitors annually.
- The painting's fame was amplified by the 1911 theft by an Italian handyman, Vincenzo Peruggia, whose crime made it a global cultural icon.
- Demonstrates Leonardo's mastery of composition: the pyramidal form of the figure, the direct gaze, and the balanced asymmetry all draw the viewer's eye.
