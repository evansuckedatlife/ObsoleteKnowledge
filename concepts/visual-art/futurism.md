# Futurism

## summary

*Futurism* was an Italian movement (1909–1944) that celebrated speed, machinery, violence, and modernity as antidotes to what its founder *Filippo Tommaso Marinetti* saw as the deadweight of the past. Through visual art, sculpture, poetry, and manifestos, Futurists glorified dynamism, war, and technological progress, producing electrifying compositions that attempted to capture movement and energy on static surfaces.

## you gotta know

- *Marinetti's Founding Manifesto of Futurism* (1909) declared that "beauty exists only in struggle" and "war is the world's only hygiene," shocking and energizing the European avant-garde.
- Visual style: diagonal lines, fragmentation, and simultaneous representation of movement—influenced by Cubism but more intent on conveying speed and kinetic energy rather than multiple viewpoints.
- *Umberto Boccioni's* *Dynamism of a Dog on a Leash* (1912) exemplifies the style: blurred forms suggesting violent motion, muscle, and velocity.
- Celebrated the *automobile*, the airplane, and the machine as agents of liberation; industrial forms and metallic surfaces dominate the imagery.
- Futurist ideals (strength, youth, velocity, violence) were later co-opted by Fascism; many Futurists supported Mussolini, tying the movement to problematic political history.
- Though numerically small and artistically heterogeneous, Futurism's manifestos and provocations profoundly influenced Constructivism, Dada, and later avant-gardes.

## connections

- [[filippo-tommaso-marinetti]] — founder and chief ideologue of Futurism.
- [[umberto-boccioni]] — visual artist who pioneered Futurist painting and sculpture techniques.
- [[cubism]] — shared fragmentation and simultaneity but pursued different philosophical ends (Cubism: perception; Futurism: dynamism).
- [[constructivism]] — Russian movement that absorbed Futurist energy and formal innovations while rejecting its violent ideology.
- [[dada]] — emerged partly as a rejection of Futurism's celebration of war.
