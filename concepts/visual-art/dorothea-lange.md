# Dorothea Lange

## summary

*Dorothea Lange* was an American documentary photographer best known for her work documenting the Great Depression and Japanese-American internment. Her empathetic portraiture and unflinching social realism established photography as a powerful instrument for social justice and human dignity, influencing documentary photography and photojournalism for generations.

## you gotta know

- Created *Migrant Mother* (1936), the iconic image of a Dust Bowl woman with her children—a photograph that became synonymous with Depression-era hardship and demonstrated photography's capacity to move public opinion.
- Worked for the *Farm Security Administration* (1935–1942), documenting rural poverty, migrant labour camps, and economic displacement across America; her images shaped New Deal policy and public consciousness.
- Photographed Japanese-American internment camps during World War II, capturing the dignity and resilience of families forcibly detained; her work remained largely suppressed until decades after her death.
- Developed a philosophy of *participatory observation*—she spent time with her subjects, built trust, and allowed intimacy rather than treating subjects as abstract specimens of hardship.
- Later worked internationally in the Middle East and Asia, bringing her humanist lens to global inequality and social change.

## connections

- [[walker-evans]] — fellow FSA photographer whose stark, formally composed documentary images represented a complementary approach to Depression-era documentation.
- [[henri-cartier-bresson]] — contemporary whose "decisive moment" philosophy and humanist street photography paralleled Lange's ethical commitment to subjects.
- [[alfred-stieglitz]] — predecessor whose championing of photography's artistic legitimacy created the cultural precedent for treating documentary work as fine art.
