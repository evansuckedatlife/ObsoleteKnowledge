## summary

Pablo Picasso (1881–1973) was a Spanish painter and sculptor who became the most influential artist of the twentieth century. Co-founder of Cubism and a master of multiple artistic styles—from his melancholic Blue Period to Surrealism to Abstract Expressionism—Picasso fundamentally changed how art could represent reality. His prolific output, political activism, and restless innovation made him synonymous with modernism itself and the radical rupture between representational and non-representational art.

## context

Picasso's genius lay not in perfecting a single style but in inventing styles wholesale and then moving beyond them. He lived through the century's greatest upheavals—Modernism, world wars, fascism—and his art reflected that turbulence. *Guernica* became the twentieth century's most powerful anti-war statement. His willingness to fragment, distort, and reimagine the human figure opened the door to all modern and contemporary art that followed. For quiz bowl, Picasso is the watershed: the boundary between old art history and modern; he appears everywhere from Blue Period melancholy to Cubist abstraction to political symbolism. His name is synonymous with creative revolution.

## you gotta know

- Born in Málaga, Spain in 1881; showed prodigious artistic talent from childhood and trained classically under his father, a painter and professor.
- Created works in the *Blue Period* (1901–1904), painting melancholic figures in predominantly blue tones—poor, suffering, and isolated subjects—reflecting his own early struggles in Paris.
- Co-founded *Cubism* around 1907, along with Georges Braque, fragmenting objects into geometric planes and showing them from multiple viewpoints simultaneously, dismantling centuries of single-point perspective.
- Painted *Les Demoiselles d'Avignon* (1907), depicting five nude female figures with flattened, distorted bodies and clashing colours—a manifesto work that shocked Paris and inaugurated Cubism.
- Developed *Analytical Cubism* (1908–1912), reducing objects to near-abstract arrangements of planes and facets; and *Synthetic Cubism*, reintroducing colour and collage elements.
- Created *Guernica* (1937), a massive mural painting depicting the bombing of the Basque town of Guernica during the Spanish Civil War—a raw, fragmented cry against violence that remains modernism's greatest political artwork.
- Worked extensively in sculpture, ceramics, and printmaking; his sculptural works pioneered assemblage and the use of found objects as art.
- Passed through *Surrealist* and *Neoclassical* phases, constantly reinventing his approach while maintaining his distinctive, instantly recognisable artistic voice.
- Maintained a fraught romantic life, often painting his partners (including Françoise Gilot and Dora Maar); many of his works are intimate chronicles of desire, jealousy, and possession.
- Lived to age 91, producing over 50,000 works—paintings, sculptures, prints, ceramics—making him one of the most prolific artists in history.

## connections

- [[cubism]] — co-founder and the movement's defining figure; revolutionised how form and space could be represented.
- [[guernica]] — his most powerful political work, embodying modernism's engagement with historical trauma.
- [[les-demoiselles-d-avignon]] — the breakthrough work that shocked Paris and launched Cubism.
- [[the-weeping-woman]] — showcases his Surrealist phase and emotional expressionism.
- [[joan-miró]] — a fellow Spanish modernist who emerged from similar currents; they shared the Spanish modernist vision.
- [[henri-matisse]] — his great contemporary and rival, representing colour-based abstraction against Picasso's form-based approach.
- [[african-art]] — a major influence on his early modernist work, especially in *Les Demoiselles*; he collected African masks and sculpture.
- [[modernism]] — the defining artist of the movement; his work marks the rupture from representation to abstraction.
- [[analytical-cubism]] — the austere, near-abstract phase he pioneered.
