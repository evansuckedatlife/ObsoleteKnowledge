## summary

*Louis Lumière* and his brother *Auguste* were French inventors and filmmakers who pioneered cinematography and the motion-picture camera, creating some of cinema's first narrative films—brief, observed moments of everyday life (*L'Arrivée d'un train en gare de La Ciotat*, *La Sortie de l'Usine Lumière*). Though not filmmakers in the narrative sense, their technological and aesthetic innovations established cinema as a viable medium for capturing life and distributing images worldwide.

## you gotta know

- Invented the *Cinématographe*, a portable camera-and-projector combined, superior to Edison's Kinetoscope in portability and commercial viability.
- Filmed and distributed *L'Arrivée d'un train en gare de La Ciotat* (1896), a 50-second shot of a train approaching the camera—legend holds audiences were startled by the approaching locomotive.
- Produced *La Sortie de l'Usine Lumière à Lyon* (1894), a minute-long film showing workers and bicycles leaving their father's factory—among the first narrative footage.
- Dispatched camera operators around the world to film local scenes, creating the first global cinema distribution network and archiving life across the globe.
- Never pursued narrative cinema; their films remained documentary actualities, brief observed moments, privileging camera placement and light over storytelling.
- Their thousands of films create a visual archive of the 1890s—street scenes, colonial expeditions, family moments—a priceless anthropological record.
- Established cinema as a commercial medium and international art; though surpassed by narrative innovators, they remain foundational to cinema history.
