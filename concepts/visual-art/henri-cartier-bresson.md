# Henri Cartier-Bresson

## summary

*Henri Cartier-Bresson* was a French photographer and photojournalist who pioneered the philosophy of the *"decisive moment"*—the idea that photography captures a single, unrepeatable instant that crystallises meaning and emotion. His compact *Leica* camera and street photography style defined 20th-century photojournalism and influenced generations of photographers worldwide.

## you gotta know

- Developed the concept of the *"decisive moment"* (*l'instant décisif*)—the precise split-second when form, gesture, and composition align to create a photograph of maximum emotional and narrative impact.
- Used the *Leica* rangefinder camera, a small, fast, unobtrusive tool that allowed him to move through crowds and capture candid moments impossible with larger press cameras of his era.
- Co-founded *Magnum Photos* (1947), a cooperative photography agency that established the model for modern photojournalism and allowed photographers to own and control their work.
- Photographed revolutions, wars, and everyday life across Europe, China, India, and the Americas; his images in publications like *Life* and *Paris Match* defined visual journalism for the post–World War II era.
- Brought humanist sensibility to documentary photography: subjects were never exploited or staged, but rather captured in moments of unguarded authenticity.

## connections

- [[alfred-stieglitz]] — earlier fine-art photographer whose artistic vision and gallery advocacy created the cultural space in which Cartier-Bresson could position photojournalism as art.
- [[dorothea-lange]] — American documentarian of the Depression era whose social conscience paralleled Cartier-Bresson's humanism, though her idiom was the static portrait.
- [[ansel-adams]] — contemporary whose rigorous technical mastery contrasted with Cartier-Bresson's spontaneous, intuitive approach.
