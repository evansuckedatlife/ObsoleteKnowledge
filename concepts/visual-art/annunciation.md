# Annunciation

## summary

Leonardo's *Annunciation*, painted circa 1472–1475 in Florence, depicts the angel Gabriel approaching the Virgin Mary to announce that she will bear the Christ Child. Executed early in Leonardo's career with oil and tempera on poplar panel, the work demonstrates his emerging mastery of perspective, gesture, and psychological subtlety, transforming the traditional religious scene into a moment of intimate surprise and grace.

## you gotta know

- Oil and tempera on poplar wood, approximately 98 × 217 cm (39 × 85 inches), housed in the Uffizi Gallery, Florence.
- Shows Mary seated at a lectern, turning in surprise from her reading as the angel approaches from the left, their spatial separation spanned by interlocking gazes and gestures.
- Employs mathematical linear perspective with the receding wall and garden creating spatial depth, drawing the viewer's eye through the composition toward a distant landscape.
- The angel's extended arm and staff point toward Mary with deliberate geometry, while her hand expresses hesitation and receptive openness—a dialogue in gesture rather than words.
- The garden setting, with flowering plants rendered with botanical precision and roses symbolizing Mary's purity, grounds the sacred event in observed natural detail.
- Leonardo captures the moment before knowledge—Mary's expression combines wonder, uncertainty, and dawning comprehension, a psychological subtlety rare in earlier *Annunciation* paintings.
- The work shows influences from Florentine tradition while announcing Leonardo's innovations: his soft modeling of light, his attention to narrative psychology, and his spatial mastery.
