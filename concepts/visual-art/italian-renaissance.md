# Italian Renaissance

## summary

The Italian Renaissance (fourteenth to sixteenth centuries) was a cultural and artistic movement originating in Italy that marked the rebirth of classical learning and values and fundamentally transformed European art, literature, and philosophy. Centred in Florence, Venice, and Rome, it produced innovations in perspective, anatomy, and the human figure that broke decisively with medieval tradition and established the foundations of modern Western art. The movement's embrace of individual genius, scientific observation, and the dignity of human life defined the modern world.

## you gotta know

- Emerged in Florence in the fourteenth century as scholars and artists rediscovered classical Greek and Roman texts, philosophy, and aesthetic principles after centuries of medieval tradition.
- *Linear perspective* was formalised by Filippo Brunelleschi and theorised by Leon Battista Alberti, allowing artists to represent three-dimensional space on a two-dimensional surface with mathematical precision.
- Pioneered *oil painting* as a dominant medium (adapted from Flemish techniques), which allowed for unprecedented luminosity, layering, and subtlety impossible with tempera.
- Celebrated *anatomical accuracy* achieved through direct observation of the human body, including dissection, allowing artists to render figures with unprecedented naturalism and dignity.
- Produced the *High Renaissance* (circa 1495–1520), the period's culmination, when Leonardo, Michelangelo, and Raphael created their greatest works in Florence, Rome, and Milan.
- Was centred on *Florence* and patronage by the *Medici family*, whose wealth and taste shaped the movement and allowed artists to work with unprecedented resources and freedom.
- Revived *classical motifs* from ancient Greece and Rome—mythology, philosophy, proportions—integrating them with Christian subject matter in a synthesis unique to the Renaissance.
- Established the concept of the artist as *intellectual and genius*, not merely a craftsman—a radical elevation that gave artists social status and authority over their work's meaning.
- Extended beyond Italy across the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries to transform art throughout Europe, making Florence and Rome the centre of artistic innovation.

## connections

- [[leonardo-da-vinci]] — the quintessential Renaissance genius; embodies the movement's fusion of art, science, and philosophy.
- [[michelangelo]] — Leonardo's greatest rival, representing competing Renaissance ideals; sculptor and painter of incomparable power.
- [[raphael]] — the High Renaissance master whose harmony and grace balanced Leonardo's empiricism and Michelangelo's power.
- [[botticelli]] — early Renaissance painter whose mythological works *The Birth of Venus* and *Primavera* defined Renaissance aesthetics.
- [[perspective]] — the mathematical innovation that allowed Renaissance artists to represent space authentically.
- [[humanism]] — the philosophical foundation that elevated human potential and dignity, driving Renaissance values.
- [[classical-art]] — the ancient Greek and Roman models that Renaissance artists revived and built upon.
- [[florence]] — the birthplace and primary centre of the Italian Renaissance.
- [[medici-family]] — the patrons whose wealth and taste shaped Renaissance art and ideas.
- [[the-renaissance]] — the broader European movement of which the Italian variant was the originator and model.
