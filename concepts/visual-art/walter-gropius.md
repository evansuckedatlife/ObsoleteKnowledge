# Walter Gropius

## summary

*Walter Gropius* (1883–1969) was a German architect and educator who founded and directed the *Bauhaus*, the most influential art and design school of the 20th century. His vision of uniting art, craft, and industry through systematic design education revolutionized architectural practice and design philosophy, establishing principles of *functionalism* and *modernism* that shaped contemporary aesthetics and the built environment worldwide.

## you gotta know

- Founded the *Bauhaus* in *Weimar*, Germany (1919), an experimental school integrating fine art, craft, architecture, and industrial design under one pedagogical umbrella with the motto "form follows function."
- The *Bauhaus* taught that *good design* should be democratic, accessible, and honest to materials; beauty emerged from utility and rational structure, not ornament—a radical break from *Art Nouveau* and historicism.
- Gropius assembled a faculty of modernist luminaries: *Wassily Kandinsky*, *Paul Klee*, *László Moholy-Nagy*, and others, who taught students that art was not separate from everyday life but essential to it.
- Advocated *functionalism* in architecture: buildings should express their purpose through clear, undecorated form; structure and utility determine aesthetic; materials (steel, glass, concrete) should be honestly expressed.
- As an architect, designed the *Bauhaus school building* itself (1925–1926 in Dessau), a masterpiece of modernist principle: clean lines, geometric volumes, extensive glazing, and functional transparency.
- The *Bauhaus* was suppressed by the *Nazi regime* in 1933 (Nazis despised modernism as "degenerate"); Gropius emigrated to the United States, where he taught at Harvard and influenced American modernist architecture.
- Legacy extends through generations of architects and designers who learned Bauhaus principles; his integration of art, industry, and education remains foundational to contemporary design education.
