# The Persistence of Memory

## summary

*The Persistence of Memory* is Salvador Dalí's iconic 1931 Surrealist painting featuring melting pocket watches draped across a barren landscape, exploring the fluidity of time and the logic of dreams. Small in size but immense in influence, it has become the most recognizable symbol of Surrealism and a meditation on the relativity of temporal experience.

## you gotta know

- Painted in 1931, just 24 inches by 32 inches, yet has achieved disproportionate cultural presence as the quintessential Surrealist image.
- Depicts three soft, melting watches and one rigid pocket watch covered in ants, set against a dreamlike landscape with cliffs and distorted forms.
- The soft watches suggest the malleability of time in the unconscious mind, contrasting with the rigidity of measured, rational time (represented by the hard watch).
- Dalí termed his method "paranoiac-critical," a technique for accessing the unconscious by deliberately inducing paranoid-hallucinatory states to paint dream-logic.
- The landscape includes a distorted humanoid form (often read as a self-portrait of Dalí asleep) and a cliffside suggesting the coast near Dalí's home in Catalonia.
- Now housed at the Museum of Modern Art in New York; it has become so iconic that it is endlessly parodied and referenced in popular culture.
