## summary

*Ludwig Mies van der Rohe* (1886–1969) was a German-American architect and a master of minimalism who distilled buildings to their essential elements. His philosophy—*less is more*—defined International Style modernism through glass-and-steel pavilions and open-plan spaces that prioritized clarity and elegance.

## you gotta know

- Directed the *Bauhaus* school (1930–1933) before fleeing Nazi Germany, later establishing American modernism at the Illinois Institute of Technology.
- Designed the *Barcelona Pavilion* (1929) for the Spanish Exposition, a floating glass and marble structure that redefined the concept of architectural transparency and spatial flow.
- Created the *Farnsworth House* (1951) in Illinois, a glass box elevated on steel supports, demonstrating how a dwelling could dissolve boundaries between interior and exterior.
- Pioneered the *glass-and-steel curtain wall*, enabling the design of the modern skyscraper (influencing designs globally, including *Seagram Building* in New York).
- Articulated the phrase *less is more*, emphasizing reduction to essential structural and aesthetic elements rather than ornament.
- Championed *universal space*—open floor plans without fixed walls, allowing flexible adaptation to inhabitants' needs.
- His Chicago buildings (including *Lake Shore Drive Apartments*) became templates for postwar urban renewal, establishing the glass tower as an icon.

## connections

- [[le-corbusier]] — contemporary modernist; shared industrial aesthetic but Mies favored more refined, austere minimalism.
- [[walter-gropius]] — co-directed Bauhaus; both shaped 20th-century design education and modernism.
- [[frank-lloyd-wright]] — competing vision; Wright rejected Mies's machine-like purity as soulless, championing organic integration instead.
- [[bauhaus]] — institutional anchor of his career and influence on post-war architecture globally.
