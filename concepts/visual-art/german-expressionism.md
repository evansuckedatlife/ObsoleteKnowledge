## summary

German Expressionism (early twentieth century, circa 1905–1930) was an avant-garde movement in German-speaking Europe that prioritised emotional intensity, raw feeling, and psychological truth over realistic representation. Centred on two main groups, *Die Brücke* (The Bridge, founded 1905) and *Der Blaue Reiter* (The Blue Rider, founded 1911), it used bold colours, distorted forms, and violent brushwork to convey inner states and spiritual or social anxiety. The movement anticipated many modern and contemporary art movements and was suppressed by Nazi ideology as "degenerate."

## you gotta know

- Emerged in reaction to Impressionism and other aesthetic movements, rejecting the idea that art should merely record light and sensation in favour of expressing deep psychological and emotional truths.
- *Die Brücke* (The Bridge), founded in Dresden in 1905, emphasised raw emotion, primitive influences, and the artist's subjective vision; key members included Ernst Ludwig Kirchner, Emil Nolde, and Karl Schmidt-Rottluff.
- *Der Blaue Reiter* (The Blue Rider), founded in Munich in 1911, was more spiritually oriented, exploring colour theory and abstraction; key figures included Wassily Kandinsky, Franz Marc, and Paul Klee.
- Used *intense, often clashing colours*—not to describe nature but to convey feeling; reds, oranges, and greens might appear unnatural, chosen for emotional rather than optical truth.
- Applied *distortion, exaggeration, and angular forms* to human figures and faces, making bodies twisted or anguished, reflecting inner turmoil and rejecting classical ideals of beauty.
- Drew inspiration from *African and non-Western art*, primitive sculpture, and medieval German woodcuts, seeing in them a spiritual authenticity lost to modern bourgeois culture.
- Was deeply tied to German culture and politics of the Weimar period; the movement absorbed anxiety about war, modernity, and social disintegration, making much of its content psychologically turbulent.
- Was officially condemned by the Nazi regime as "degenerate art" (*Entartete Kunst*) beginning in 1933; many works were confiscated or destroyed, and artists were silenced or exiled.
- Influenced Existentialism, Abstract Expressionism, and modern art broadly, establishing distortion and emotional authenticity as legitimate artistic strategies.

## connections

- [[expressionism]] — the broader European movement of which German Expressionism was the most radical and influential variant.
- [[die-bruecke]] — the pioneering Berlin-based group that inaugurated German Expressionism's raw emotional intensity.
- [[der-blaue-reiter]] — the Munich-based group that explored colour theory and abstraction within an Expressionist framework.
- [[ernst-ludwig-kirchner]] — the defining figure of Die Brücke, whose angular, anguished figures embodied the movement's aesthetic and psychology.
- [[emile-nolde]] — a Die Brücke member whose intense colours and religious imagery fused primitivism with spiritual seeking.
- [[edvard-munch]] — Norwegian artist whose *The Scream* (1893) anticipated German Expressionist themes of anxiety and psychological distress.
- [[distortion-in-art]] — the central formal strategy of the movement, deployed for emotional rather than representational effect.
- [[emotion-over-representation]] — the core principle that inner feeling trumps external accuracy.
- [[modernism]] — the broader avant-garde movement within which German Expressionism was a radical vanguard.
